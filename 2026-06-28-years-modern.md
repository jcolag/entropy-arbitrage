## The Maybe-One War

Not too long ago as an aside, I pitched the idea that we all currently live through a---the only, really, at least until this mess ends---global war.  This approach has a couple of parts to it, all varying degrees of fringe interpretations of history, but I think that the idea holds for explanatory purposes, even if not useful as a history lesson as such.

### A Hundred Years Plus

In this way of looking at history, we might start the narrative with the [Glorious Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glorious_Revolution) in England, 1688, replacing James II with Mary II and her husband William III of Orange.  William saw an opportunity to make a name for himself as a defender of the Protestant movement against (Catholic) French expansion under Louis XIV.  The narrowest view of history says that this tumbles right into the [Nine Years' War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nine_Years%27_War), which puts larger wheels in motion.  For example, the Nine Years' War includes Ireland's [Williamite War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Williamite_War_in_Ireland) still concerned about the revolution and [King William's War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King_William%27s_War) across the Atlantic, which itself many historians see as a minor part of the [French and Indian Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_and_Indian_Wars), which becomes an important aspect of how I see this.

In parallel to those events, the [Enlightenment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment) movement starts.  As I have described it (multiple times) before, you have a couple-dozen writers spending their free time in still-new coffeehouses[^qn8uPQ], sending each other letters about what the world might look like if society chose policies based on how they would affect their countries and adjust as more information came in, instead of always relying on the idea that some supernatural force enchanted certain bloodlines to make decisions.

[^qn8uPQ]:  Some historians consider this aspect of the Enlightenment at least mildly important, because prior to importing tea and coffee and its culture, especially *boiling the water* and killing any bacteria in it, Europeans mostly consumed alcohol---the predecessor of the middle- and upper-class obsession with European beer, wine, cider, and spirits cultures---because alcohol also kills most microorganisms, and you needed to do one or the other to have a beverage that won't make you sick, until modern water treatment systems come along.  Over the course of a few decades, then, Europeans went from people hydrating with beer, wine, or cider, to hydrating with sterilized and caffeinated water in often ritualized manners.

As the action of the Nine Years' War continued, it sparked other wars.  That expanded the stage globally and increasingly saw factions using the rhetoric and philosophy of the Enlightenment, most notably the [Carnatic wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carnatic_wars) in India and the [Seven Years' War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War) in India and North America.  Most historians will tell you something like the latter looking something a lot like a first world war itself, in all but the name.

As those conflicts "ended," they sparked the so-called Atlantic Revolutions, Corsica in 1755, [Pontiac's Rebellion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pontiac%27s_War) around the Great Lakes, the United States, France, Haiti, Colombia and the rest of Latin America, Greece, and more through the mid-nineteenth century in the so-called [springtime of the peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolutions_of_1848), with a bunch in between that I probably forgot.

We started with a relatively minor revolution that played on the French-English rivalry, and got over a century-and-a-half of interconnected wars around the world that *remade* a lot of the world in important ways.  Where once the Western Hemisphere governed itself on the basis that God pulled aside some complete jerks a thousand years ago to tell them that they and their descendants owned the entire world and everything in it---and no, the non-chosen among you can't see any documentation on this deal, for proof denies faith or something, so shut up and pay your taxes---we now had and still have a regime where any legitimate nation worth anybody's attention either has a constitutional democracy *or* money pouring in from around the world to buy oil.  If you don't fall into one of those categories, you can maybe make headlines for a few days if you threaten to build a nuclear missile or throw a few tourists in prison on imagined espionage charges, but otherwise nobody cares about you.

You might increasingly see historians refer to this stretch as the [Second Hundred Years War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Hundred_Years%27_War), though to maintain the narrative of England versus France with Spain also in there sometimes, they ignore a lot of India and stop the clock after the Napoleonic Wars, excluding the South American revolutions despite the clear connections.  It seems like a framing designed to quietly talk around my point of a global fight between authoritarians and more progressive movements, but it does provide the entry.

### More Civility

If you have much exposure to the United States, then you know where this next "phase" probably goes.

In the United States, we had two (broadly speaking) major conflicts following the [Bolivarian Revolutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_American_wars_of_independence) (name since used more to refer to modern Venezuela), the more organized ethnic cleansing and genocide against the Native Americans and our [Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Civil_War), where the conflicts became directly and overtly about which classes of people---not nationalities, but whether individuals of a specific type could participate in society---should have a right to self-determination.

Oh, and the [Oyster Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oyster_Wars).  We can't forget about the Oyster Wars, even though they don't *quite* fit the story.

Meanwhile, the British Empire, where the Sun never set, had trouble keeping the lights on, so to speak, with crises in South and Southeast Asia, Iran, and Africa, more waves of colonial independence movements, plus the [Opium Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opium_Wars).  The French committed their own ethnic cleansing in Algeria.  The Russians had internal ethnic conflicts.  Apart from the Opium Wars, the Chinese gangs stirred up trouble in Southeast Asia in the [Haw Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haw_wars) and had the [Taiping Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiping_Rebellion) to deal with closer to home.  The Ottoman Empire started to see their territory shrink from revolutions, alongside [conflicts with Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_Russo-Turkish_wars).  The [Great Game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Game) worked a lot like the Cold War, though with fewer concerns about setting up legitimate-looking proxies.

Following up on the Atlantic Revolutions, the [Armenians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armenian_national_movement) and [Romanians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanian_War_of_Independence) finally got in on the independence action, too.

Notice the through-line, here, the sides for and against imperialism.  I want to come back to the United States Civil War, though.

I forget where I've heard it, but at least historian [Don H. Doyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Don_H._Doyle) has argued that, much like the global scope of the Seven Years' War (or Second Hundred Years' War), our Civil War *nearly* became a world war.  Shortly prior to the Civil War, you see, the [Knights of the Golden Circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knights_of_the_Golden_Circle) secretly assembled to upend the [Missouri Compromise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Missouri_Compromise) by creating a *whole bunch* of slave-states south of the Mason-Dixon Line by conquering Mexico, most Central American countries, and most Caribbean islands.

Yeah, the first person to consider granting statehood to Puerto Rico wanted to do so (after conquering it) to get more slavery-friendly officials in Congress...

Anyway, that plan didn't vanish when the war broke out, because the pro-slave forces[^qMA-mw] still wanted more land to farm with forced labor.  And by the way, don't forget about the [Confederados](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confederados), taking advantage of another couple of decades of slavery, but also scattered groups in Mexico, and (now) Belize, creating tiny colonies throughout the hemisphere.

[^qMA-mw]:  While I undoubtedly have done so in the past, I don't see much value in validating the failed rebellion by using *their* name for it, as if they actually achieved independence and gained official recognition from other countries.  They rose up against the United States to preserve the right to own other humans---South Carolina specifically cited "an increasing hostility on the part of the non-slaveholding States to the Institution of Slavery" as the primary reason for secession---and got shut down.  That doesn't make a country.

That certainly doesn't make for a *world* war, though, barely even a full hemisphere.  Let's speculate a bit, based on what little we know about international relations of the day, on how things might've otherwise panned out.

What might have turned this global?  The [Cotton Famine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lancashire_Cotton_Famine), caused by the inability for Southern labor camps to get their raw cotton to market, causing skyrocketing textile costs globally could've done it and almost did it.  The slaveholding rebellion understood this, dispatching Louisiana's John Slidell and Virginia's James M. Mason to Paris and London---in what we know as the [Trent Affair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trent_Affair) for their capture on the way across the ocean---to emphasize that breaking the United States blockade from inside or out would solve the problems of both the institution of slavery and Europe's clothing industry.  Both England and France really wanted to wait until they could back a clear winner, but economic motivation has changed other minds, and Slidell believed that he had Napoleon III convinced other than the continued influence of (pro-United States) Foreign Minister Edouard Thouvenel.

One imagines that, had Slidell and Mason convinced England and France to take a side, then Russia---already openly supporting the United States politically and maintaining a presence in US ports---would almost certainly have felt the need to get more directly involved.  The Ottoman Empire also seems to have supported the United States economically.  Those potentially spur Prussia and Austria.  And when you have all the major Western powers involved, you almost certainly get troops from and friction in the colonies, not to mention naval battles to prevent troops and/or supplies arriving for the other side.

That also starts connecting some of the conflicts that I mention above.  The Opium Wars give China a potentially strong interest in seeing the British and French fail, for example, whether by throwing them out or interceding in the war, too.  You also have the Tokugawa shogunate dealing with an economic crisis and cholera epidemic, largely because the United States largely forced the isolationist government to open its ports.

Like World War I, you can see how quickly this could spiral out of control.  And here, we didn't need an assassination so much as a better PowerPoint deck in Paris.

### Labor Pains

Starting around this time and stretching through the First World War, don't forget that you also have the rising labor movement.  If that doesn't seem connected, then get out your red yarn for the murder-wall, because **The Communist Manifesto** comes out---extending the ideas of the Enlightenment from government to commerce---right as that whole Springtime of the Peoples breaks out its series of European revolutions.  And even if we didn't have contemporary statements to the contrary, you can't deny that the labor movement wants doesn't take its inspiration from the budding socialist movement.  Workers of the world, unite, and so forth, right?

And oh, does that not seem like part of a war to you?  Well, then you might want to review your labor history, because workers often went on strike, and management responded---if they couldn't destroy the movement by evicting the organizers from the company towns and conspiring to ban them from ever working in the industry again---called in the police[^49a129], state militia, and mercenary groups posing as detective agencies---often led by former military officers whose side lost the Civil War, by the way---units to violently break the strikes.

[^49a129]:  When people talk about police unions not making sense as unions, they mean that the police have rarely sided with labor.  As I've said since almost the beginning of the blog, policing protects property from humans.  They'll shoot someone in the back for shoplifting and strangle them in the street for competing with a store without a permit, but won't intervene in a school shooting, because the job demands protecting property and seeing people as a threat.

And at times, unions engaged in quieter, never-condoned-by-leadership violence, most often blocking people from crossing the picket line or targeting key support for owners.

In cases like the [Haymarket Affair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haymarket_affair), opportunistic violence---neither the protesters nor the government had anything to gain from throwing a bomb, so I assume that the escalation had nothing to do with the protest itself---spiraled out of control to the tune of fifteen deaths and dozens of injuries *after* the explosion.

And yes, capitalism *also* came out of the Enlightenment.  And if you have some familiarity with **The Wealth of Nations**, you can also see this as both a refinement and another instance of this same fight.  Where capitalists read about specialization and surplus labor and concluded that some mysterious force (ahem:  inherited wealth) demanded that they and only they harvest the surplus for themselves, the labor movement (via socialists pointing out that you burn out a system by running it non-stop without maintenance) demanded a cut of the profits for the people generating the surplus and some of the decision-making power.  And the capital classes, inherently authoritarian and autocratic, decided that justified violence.

You'll even notice that some of these *individual* conflicts became so long and violent that they get called wars of their own.  In the United States, we have the [Coal Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal_Wars) and [Colorado Labor Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colorado_Labor_Wars) among quite a few others.

### No Asterisks

Getting back to the more conventional wars, I bring this all up because, not long after the last batch of international conflicts and as the labor wars play out, with little time for peace anywhere with smaller conflicts, we get the two "formal" world wars, the second now familiarly framed as authoritarianism versus liberty.  Out of the Second World War, we get the Cold War, similarly framed as authoritarianism versus liberty, though with both sides using similar framing and both probably on the wrong side of things.  And now we have...well, look around, and you see the same kinds of wars.

I contend, then---and for absolute clarity, *only* I say this, as far as I have ever found---that this all constitutes one massive world war that started in the 1680s.  Probably not *every* local conflict figures into this model, and often you'll see authoritarian governments fight each other seemingly for the purpose of cracking down on opposition and dissent at home, such as the current/recent conflict in Iran and the Cold War, but like the Second Hundred Years' War remade the world into a space where only people-power and petrodollars matter politically, the last century has solidified that we fight wars mainly over this one ideology, not really resources or fear of the Other.

### Getting to the Point

Mostly, though, I wanted to make the point that, with the *excuse* of war, the Enlightenment took only a few decades to go from an interesting idea circulating among few enough people who could fit into a single room to remaking most of the world order.  And refined over the decades since, the movement has stayed relevant and powerful, even if the original vision failed to include the vast majority of people in its scope.

Equality works really well, it turns out.

### Leading the Witness

As Gawker reported in 2011, Roger Ailes spent a chunk of 1970 pushing [A Plan For Putting the GOP on TV News](https://www.gawkerarchives.com/5814150/roger-ailes-secret-nixon-era-blueprint-for-fox-news), a memo demanding a far-right (television) media outlet to push slickly produced interviews with Nixon-friendly politicians directly to local stations to air to fill time without needing to worry about pesky questions about effects of policies or the like.  The proposed Capitol News Service didn't happen, but Ailes soon launched the privatized (Coors-financed) Television News Incorporated.  Eventually, running a cable station cost less than distributing ~~political ads masquerading as~~ prepackaged news across the country, so a lot of that experience went to launching Fox News after some [practice developing tabloid-style magazine shows](https://www.salon.com/2013/10/19/the_birth_of_fox_news/) and sports coverage.  In short, the media didn't sufficiently support Republican policies, therefore somebody needed to pull the media to the right while stoking resentment against those other outlets.

And speaking of pulling the media to the right, it seems worth mentioning that deliberate right-wing media, sadly, frames the conversation. The Ailes model, however, calls it unfair liberal biased to accept where the majority of people sit, and demands that every mention of what they call a "liberal" idea---something that most people believe---gets balanced by an alleged "conservative" idea.  If you interview an expert in the well-documented dangers of depriving gender-affirming care to teenagers, then you also need at least one bigot (but really many) ignorantly rambling about their imagined version of scripture, or the right will decry you as biased, even though a significant majority of people don't actually see it that way and actual numbers prove whatever they say false.

If you like the "marketplace of ideas" metaphor, then the Ailes model demands that society heavily subsidize his ideas so that they can stay on the market, even though they don't work and nobody really likes them.  Think of them as the CFL lightbulbs of intellect:  Low-quality, widely disliked, and probably hazardous, but still on the market because a handful of laws mandated them before LED-based bulbs made economic sense and stores can sell the CFLs with higher profit margins...

### Corporatocracy

In parallel to Ailes trying to remake the media, future Supreme Court Justice [Lewis Powell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lewis_F._Powell_Jr.) wrote the legal strategy, the so-called *Powell Memo*.  It detailed how progressive ideology had swept the country, resulting in...well, *progress*, and how that seems pretty bad if you want to operate in an oligarchy.  In his opinion, Republicans needed to prioritize---wait for it---placing groomed ideologues on the Supreme Court, a majority willing to dismiss precedent, common interpretations of the Constitution, and Congressional intent to rule in favor of business interests and against democracy.  And what did he want most out of his fantasy Supreme Court?  He wanted to get rid of that pesky new campaign finance law preventing companies from buying politicians outright.

If you want a deep-dive into how this has played out, by the way, I'd highly recommend David Sirota's [**The Master Plan**](https://the.levernews.com/master-plan/) podcast.  I find the popular culture references grating, but the actual meat of the series tells as much of the story as they've pieced together.

### And Guns, Too

I almost forgot about the *other* major contemporary push to remake the country, the gun lobby and the Second Amendment.

It might seem hard to believe, but prior to the 1970s, you could probably make an actual list of every person who believed that the [Second Amendment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution) granted individuals the right to own and carry weapons.  Instead, spurious comma in the text aside, everybody---including the most right-wing Supreme Court Justices---read it as protecting the *states* from Washington dissolving state militias.  Every once in a while, someone would ramble about a "Right to Revolt"---usually in the same breath as a term like "The War of Northern Aggression"---but like I said, they made up an absolute fringe for insisting that individuals had a private right to overthrow the government recognized by the government.

That started to change in (once again) the 1970s, when the [Revolt at Cincinnati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolt_at_Cincinnati) ousted an NRA leadership dedicated (as the organization had dedicated itself for its century-long existence) to gun training and safety, in favor of exactly those aforementioned extremists.

Politicians, especially Republicans, blinked when confrontations happened, and the "NRA Mark-II" had its foothold, to the point that the official GOP platform in 1980 asserted the right *of citizens* to "bear arms" and an opposition to federal gun registration.

You can get a [more thorough overview](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/how-nra-rewrote-second-amendment) of this change, including wildly misinterpreting historical documents and misidentifying writers to support the NRA's new crusade.  But you probably know the broad outlines.  The Clinton administration let the right wing play to the "coming for your guns" idea, and that has cascaded to the current Supreme Court making decisions based on whether states in the 1780s ever *explicitly* banned mowing down a village with bump stocks while high on kratom, the so-called [history and tradition](https://yalelawjournal.org/essay/history-and-traditions-equality-problem) test created in [*New York State Rifle & Pistol Ass'n v. Bruen*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_State_Rifle_%26_Pistol_Ass%27n_v._Bruen), their abbreviation, not mine.

They act like money---specifically, the *oil* money, which as I mentioned seems like the only thing that makes a non-democratic country relevant to anybody---has started to run out, and with it, their power.

People with power operate deliberately, either because they see the value of getting it right and have the time, or because they want others to see them casually using their power.  The insecure hastily attack, hoping that it puffs them up to *look* powerful, in hopes of not needing to risk a challenge.

This *hurts*  people, and those numbers increase, but it also exposes their weakness, because the most vulnerable people in society wouldn't pose a threat to powerful people...

### Reforms

By the way, not really relevant to the overall post, but since I brought some of it up, I might as well get into people talking about fixing this.

Because of the long-term plan that got us here, like I see the far-right as laughable even when dangerous, I also don't take it particularly seriously whether this politician or that supports court expansion or similar patches to the current situation.  Unless you look at our current situation as the result of a half-century-long project, adding four Justices to the Supreme Court only turns the clock back a bit, as they pour money into making sure that Republicans win the presidency whenever a Justice might retire or die.

Likewise, the long-running idea of publicly funding political campaigns seems like it goes nowhere in the current situation, because billionaires will still spend what amounts to unlimited amounts.  Your three-dollar donation added to your tax payment won't make Super PACs go away.

I don't mean that we shouldn't reform the courts or elections or anything else, because we absolutely should.  Instead, I mean that the country needs a longer-term strategy than only treating the most pressing symptoms of the broader disease.  Even if somebody declared that we should look at overturning or ignoring [*Marbury v. Madison*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marbury_v._Madison) itself[^Jmc2EL]---without which, the Supreme Court wouldn't matter much to our lives---that doesn't do more than bandage up a broken leg to send the athlete back into the game.

[^Jmc2EL]:  Remember that case whenever people talk about "unprecedented" actions.  More than two hundred years ago, the Supreme Court rewrote the Constitution by assigning themselves the authority to decide the constitutionality of laws.  And everybody shrugged, pretending that nothing else made sense.

We need systemic change that doesn't only try to reset the clock but carefully disassembles the infrastructure that pushes their authoritarianism.

For example, if you want to reform campaign finance, aim for banning *all spending* on political campaigns.  I suggest this for two reasons.  First, between public events, local media interviews and debates, and social media (with the caveat that the big platforms will hide dissenting voices), no politician should need to spend money to get their message out if it resonates.  And second, such a ban would immediately turn every candidate into a watchdog monitoring every other candidate's campaign for evidence that can stop their campaign.  But also, that needs to happen *while* expanding the courts, expanding the size of Congress, prosecuting people working to destroy democracy, protecting and expanding voting rights, seizing excessive wealth, legalizing abortion, making everybody in the supply chain responsible for every gun death, impeaching and/or forcibly retiring the corrupt Supreme Court Justices, and probably a hundred other steps, because the system works against us on all those levels.  Changing one can tie them up fighting it with the rest of the system, providing openings to push through more, but the *goal* needs to cover the entire system, not rolling back a few bad things and going "back to normal."

*Normal* saw us as a threat to neutralize, remember.

## Get It Together

Putting these two incidents---the (maybe) single global war and the fifty-year reactionary backlash---together for contrast, I suspect that you can see where my hope comes from.  In fifty years, back when communications involved waiting for ships to deliver the mail, questions such as "what would a more fair world look like?" set the world on (fairly literal) fire fighting for that fairer world.  In fifty years, with nearly modern technology, powerful and well-coordinated people pouring untold money and energy into remaking the world have...undone about ten years of progress and broken a bunch of things, and their movement already seems on the verge of collapse.

[Theodore Parker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodore_Parker) seems to have presented an assessment of this idea first, though you'll recognize it from the many more famous people who have quoted and paraphrased him in progressive generations.

> I do not pretend to understand the moral universe, the arc is a long one...I cannot calculate the curve and complete the figure by the experience of sight; I can divine it by conscience. But from what I see I am sure it bends towards justice.

Where Parker claimed to not understand it, we have our examples that let us start to try to understand.  And as I see it, doing the right thing---treating others with respect and protecting the most vulnerable among us---lets us stand together by building relationships instead of hierarchies.  We can "compound the gains" that we make, because making people's lives better enables them to better fight for the right thing.

Authoritarianism doesn't do any of that.  It lashes out and, when it gets attackers (investigators, critics, disappointed constituents) to back off, it lashes out again, because it doesn't have another move.

Therefore, with fifty years and untold money pouring into their plots to remake the United States into a mercantile colony under their ownership, they haven't succeeded.  Even a literal insurrection and aggressive attempt to disassemble the bureaucracy hasn't broken the system.  They got a lame duck president who managed to build monument to poisonous algae *killing* ducks, as if the Fairy with Turquoise Hair turned the metaphor into a real boy.

This shows a better future, *but* it also shows why it feels like such a struggle to get it:  Having a good idea doesn't win the day by itself.  Look at how these two stories evolve.  Yes, the start of the Enlightenment sounds like your social media feed with people still circulating that picture of a disgruntled Bernie Sanders in his heavy coat, but everything else happened because people *acted*.  They didn't always act in the best ways---such as the world still mostly not forgiving Haiti for its revolution---but they pressured governments in solidarity, and that cascaded into democratic revolutions that persist to today.  The Ailes/Powell project, by contrast, *comes out of* an era where people pressured the government in such volume that it forced even the Nixon administration to create the Environmental Protection Agency, sign campaign finance reform, and more (on the heels of pressuring the Johnson administration to support everything that we credit them with) not because Nixon secretly had the country's best interests at heart, but because fighting progress would've cost him.

### How (Not) To

And what lesson did we, the people, learn from those battles?  Oh, while the wealthy planned and organized, *we* all learned to hate engaging with politics and media, clearing the way for Ailes and Powell to create a world where pointing out that the flu vaccine probably would've prevented the [Air Force flu outbreak](https://www.npr.org/2026/06/20/nx-s1-5864519/flu-outbreak-tests-new-pentagon-vaccine-policy), seeing people as (ahem, for example) [endowed by their Creator with certain unalienable Rights](https://www.archives.gov/founding-docs/declaration-transcript), believing that [the Government of the United States of America is not, in any sense, founded on the Christian religion](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Tripoli) but that people who brand themselves with Christianity really should maybe [treat the foreigner as they treat the native, and even themselves](https://www.biblegateway.com/verse/en/Leviticus%2019%3A34)[^Rt9aKi], or using terms like [intersectionality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersectionality) gets you branded as dangerously partisan, but you can always [recycle supply side economics](https://www.business.rutgers.edu/business-insights/supply-side-economic-theory-powers-trump-tax-plan) or the like as a "centrist."

[^Rt9aKi]:  I paraphrased that one, because translations that use the plainest modern English tend to identify "the foreigner" (or related term) in the previous line, they almost all separate treat-as-natives and treat-as-yourself as separate things, and the second-person ("you") made it difficult to fit any of them into the paragraph's flow.

In other words, we win when we stand together and support each other, whereas capital wins when we declare victory and assume that somebody else will fix the remaining problems.  Stop waiting for someone else to do it.  Remember (secret third story) that, when we all stopped going to work and shopping at the start of 2021, the same sorts of people who enjoy comparing food assistance programs with [feeding vermin](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/sc-lt-gov-andre-bauer-compares-helping-poor-to-feeding-stray-animals/) turned around and unilaterally voted to send everybody stimulus checks so that money would start flowing again.  And when we went back to (at least online) jobs and stores, they immediately started trying to claw that money back.

They keep showing us the power that we have, and we need to start paying attention.

Oh, and for those a bit distracted, the post takes its title from the Walt Whitman poem, which I realized after doesn't have an entirely different premise.

> Years of the modern! years of the unperform'd!  
> Your horizon rises, I see it parting away for more august dramas,  
> I see not America only, not only Liberty's nation but other nations preparing,  
> I see tremendous entrances and exits, new combinations, the solidarity of races,  
> The earth, restive, confronts a new era, perhaps a general divine war,  
> No one knows what will happen next, such portents fill the days and nights;  
> Years prophetical! the space ahead as I walk, as I vainly try to pierce it, is full of phantoms,  
> Unborn deeds, things soon to be, project their shapes around me,  
> This incredible rush and heat, this strange ecstatic fever of dreams O years!  
> Your dreams O years, how they penetrate through me! (I know not whether I sleep or wake.)  
> The perform'd America and Europe grow dim, retiring in shadow behind me,  
> The unperform'd, more gigantic than ever, advance, advance upon me.

And as we come on the two hundred fiftieth anniversary of our Declaration of Independence, I can't really think of a better sentiment.
