## Previously...

I initially outlined the project [in this post]({% post_url 2020-01-02-trek-00 %}), for those falling into this from somewhere else. ## Emissary

As we shift gears from **The Next Generation**, this phase of the project feels highly experimental.  In a lot of ways, after all, **Deep Space Nine** got here first, with entire episodes that specifically talk about international relations or the security state, making me wonder how much I can contribute beyond picking out the lines and doing the insipid social media "THIS {% emoji index pointing up %}" thing for forty-odd minutes per week.  But also, even though I didn't finish this series when it aired, I *really* enjoyed it at the time and when I finally watched the full seven seasons, so if you come here for my occasional meltdowns about the terrible writing, I either can't guarantee that you'll get that until we finish here and start **Voyager**, or I'll start to find the bad writing personally insulting as I realize that I never noticed the massive flaws before...

Anyway, to get back into the habit of setting the context, the introduction happens in parallel to the early scenes of [*The Best of Both Worlds*, part 2]({% post_url 2023-11-09-best-both-worlds-part-2 %}), but the main body of the episode takes place shortly after [*Chain of Command*, part 2]({% post_url 2025-01-30-chain-command-2 %}) while Picard recuperates from getting aggressively quizzed[^kkkbxP] on arithmetic and interior design.

[^kkkbxP]:  It surprises me, in retrospect, that I didn't point out that a smarter captain would have insisted on seeing no more than *three* lights, to force the Cardassian to negotiate.  "Every time you electrocute me, I deduct one light.  Not so smug now, I see..."

 > **LOCUTUS**: Resistance is futile. You will disarm your weapons and escort us to sector zero-zero-one. If you attempt to intervene, we will destroy you.

Huh.  I didn't expect this, but this fits with what [**First Contact**]({% post_url 2025-12-25-first-contact-2 %}) told (will tell) us, that the Borg *might* not have wanted anything more than diplomatic relations---it doesn't stretch much to interpret the first part as requesting a peaceful solution---and Picard's influence sabotaged it with the threat, because he resented his job, as the film will later imply.

 > **CAPTAIN**: Red alert. Load all torpedo bays. Ready phasers. Move us to position alpha, Ensign.

I can't help noticing how much more diverse the *Saratoga* crew looks, compared to every other ship that I can remember.  We see multiple non-humans, multiple non-white performers, and women.  And because the franchise embraces and encourages social progress...they'll kill almost all of them immediately.  {% emoji eyeroll %}

 > **SISKO**: Help me! Jennifer, hold on.

To get this out of the way, I always love watching [Avery Brooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avery_Brooks) do his thing.  When this aired, I don't think that anybody who I knew *actually* watched **Spenser:  For Hire**, but everybody recognized Brooks as his character Hawk, who got his own---fairly impressive, if dated and far too short---spinoff, **A Man Called Hawk**, and I and many other people *wanted* to watch the parent show specifically because of Brooks, but it probably conflicted with something with a more comprehensible concept than using a prepositional phrase as a profession.

That all said, the awful "help me" line-read never fails to make me giggle.

 > **SISKO**: Jake, there you are. How are they biting?
 >
 > **JAKE**: Small fries. Threw them back. Want to go for a swim?

It seems worth noting the optics, here.  Simulation or not, fishing, especially from a bridge---a place that looks public, rather than a boat or a dock off a palatial estate---codes them as middle- or lower-class.  And assuming that Jake didn't go to the costume shop, the undone overalls read as lower-class.  That stands in sharp contrast to the equestrians, skiers, fencers, ballroom dancers, and so forth that we have seen so far, arguably making this the first hint that we've seen of Starfleet officers who don't come from excessive wealth.

And it occurs to me that might connect to the diversity that we saw on the *Saratoga*.

 > **JAKE**: Is that it?

Not relevant to anything here, but to reminisce for a moment:  At college when this aired, I saw this in a packed room, and the reveal of the station and the credits absolutely floored the group.  People complained a bit about the slower pace of the theme, but that change seems like the entire point of the exercise.

I don't know if the analogy quite holds much water in terms of melodies, but unlike the bombastic, almost [John Philip Sousa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Philip_Sousa)-like qualities of **The Next Generation**'s opening credits, this swings closer to a composer such as [Aaron Copland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aaron_Copland), with its mournful initial voice giving an isolated feel, then building in other voices into more of a chorus.

Super-tangentially, thinking about this a few years ago led me to start thinking about the main theme music of each show as the protagonists' voices.  It doesn't take much to imagine Kirk whistling something inspired by [*Beyond the Blue Horizon*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beyond_the_Blue_Horizon_%28song%29) or its animated mirror-image, and Picard presumably goose-steps to his main theme.  Sisko has a *Fanfare for the Common Man* vibe, starting out feeling isolated, but the show builds in those other voices echoing how the show becomes much more of an ensemble cast quickly.  Looking ahead in their production schedule, you can imagine Janeway humming along to a [Richard Strauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Strauss)-inspired borderline tone poem, or Archer literally belting out to *Faith of the Heart*.

Would that idea withstand any scrutiny if we traveled back in time to interrogate the composers as they worked, or if I knew anything about musical composition?  Probably not, especially once we pass [the 2009 film]({% post_url 2026-02-05-star-trek-2009 %}) when everything needs to reference something that came before it.  Only **Discovery**'s theme really has a specific "voice" (that it didn't lift from an earlier show), and I don't think that it matches up to any of the characters.  Although you'll notice that **Strange New Worlds** takes the skeleton of the original theme and almost seems to want the form of the [*1812 Overture*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1812_Overture) or the more clichéd choice of Holst's [*Mars, the Bringer of War*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Planets), with the relaxed build to a recurring militant theme[^_vvNfj], rather than the original version's unending travel concept.

[^_vvNfj]:  Actually, returning to something that I've said before about the show wanting to reboot the original series, the **Strange New Worlds** theme actually most sounds to me like "what if Jerry Goldsmith rewrote the Alexander Courage theme for **The Next Generation** instead of revising his theme from **The Motion Picture**?"  For a more interesting precedent for this sort of work in the franchise, the expanded (or whatever they called it) score for **The Voyage Home** has an fun alternate overture track that adapts the original theme to Rosenman's neoclassical style, rather than updating his **Lord of the Rings** theme as we got in the film.

 > **O'BRIEN**: When my wife Keiko saw our quarters, she started talking about visiting her mother in Kumamoto. Sir, I wouldn't allow the boy to go roaming. We're still having some security problems.

Reinforcing the idea that humans live in their own ethnic enclaves, even on Earth, [Kumamoto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kumamoto) sits near the southeast corner of Japan.

And by the way, we'll talk about racism a lot from here on, I imagine, but notice that O'Brien specifically calls out "security problems," here.  He doesn't mention the dangerous debris, the missing equipment that probably means that somebody will fall out into the void, or getting in the way of the work crews.  No, he sees the civilians remaining in a recently liberated death-camp and worries about the *crime rate*, even though we'll find out in a couple of scenes that the station has its own law enforcement.

 > **O'BRIEN**: I'd like to ask the designer what he was thinking about when they built this place. I still haven't been able to find an ODN access. That's the Prefect's office up there.
 >
 > ...
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: The environmental controls in ops are stuck at thirty-two C. We're working on it.

I feel the need to point out that they have the *Enterprise* idling out in the parking lot, with allegedly Starfleet's best, but O'Brien can't find the (I assume) power outlets or thermostat.  How long has he had to make headway on this?

 > **O'BRIEN**: Sir, have you ever served with any Bajoran women?
 >
 > **SISKO**: No. Why?
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: I was just wondering, sir.

This exchange feels like it tells us so much about the Federation, doesn't it?

In context, O'Brien has worked with precisely *one* Bajoran woman[^Ir3AAC], and as far as we know, only during [*Disaster*]({% post_url 2024-06-13-disaster %}).  He talked down to her, and didn't like that she suggested saving the lives of hundreds of civilians instead of risking everybody to save a handful of people in engineering who might have already died, a far more benign version of the decision that they wanted us to cheer Picard for at the start of **First Contact**, by the way.  Anyway, O'Brien has chosen to paint all Bajoran women with this one interaction where she had the nerve to disagree with one of the main cast.

[^Ir3AAC]:  As I have probably mentioned before, in some parallel timeline where Michelle Forbes didn't have a real shot at a high-profile film career, this scene would've built to revealing Ro Laren's new gig, much like the Trill redesign that we see later hints strongly that they designed the role as Kamala from [*The Perfect Mate*]({% post_url 2024-10-03-perfect-mate %}).

And couple this with how we've seen O'Brien behave so far, such as his deep racism (of multiple varieties, no less) in [*The Wounded*]({% post_url 2024-01-25-wounded %}) and his sexism in [*Data's Day*]({% post_url 2024-01-18-data-s-day %}).  He seems to talk like this a lot, even after allegedly coming to terms with the racism, and it has gotten him promoted to this new position, rather than dishonorably discharged.

Anyway, nobody calls him on his crap here, either, even with a different Bajoran woman *right there*, dovetailing with all the times on **The Next Generation** that somebody has talked about Klingon or Vulcan or whatever personalities, continuing to build the case that people really do see ethnicities in this way.  And if you don't see the problem, replace "Bajoran" with literally any group of humans, with the same ominous tone intended, let's not forget, to get a laugh out of us.

And somewhat related to how the Federation sees this situation, as friend-of-the-blog [Shannon McMaster](https://dice.camp/@shannonmcmaster) (keen-eyed readers often find his profile listed among the Webmentions at the end of posts) pointed out a while back on Mastodon:  How the heck did the name "Deep Space Nine" not get flagged as highly offensive and condescending?  E.E. Smith used the phrase [deep space](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/deep_space)---analogous to the [deep sea](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/deep_sea) as the parts of the oceans where no sunlight filters through---to refer to anywhere not in Earth's (or sometimes the Sun's) gravity well.  But surely, once you have friendly relationships with people who live in other solar systems, you generalize the term to places outside *any* gravity well, and DS9 seems fairly firmly in a planet's gravity well, inside a star's gravity well.  The term only really starts to make sense if we assume that Starfleet[^ixXQSX] wanted to make a point of Bajor's lack of importance.

[^ixXQSX]:  Follow-up question, why does Starfleet have naming rights at all, here?  The Cardassians built the station, and it sure does seem like Bajoran property, now, with Starfleet only getting permission to squat there until the Provisional Government can decide whether to apply for Federation membership.

Second-anyway, I'll skip over some amazing lines, including most of the scene that this introduces.  Instead of chuckling with glee at every line, I'll instead direct attention to how Sisko handles it.  He accepts and acknowledges Kira's anger and asks how they can plan to do better, even after she tries to push him away.  We don't see any orders, defensiveness, or racist stereotypes.

In the scene following, I'll also skip through, rather than tell you what the primary cast did prior to this, other than to mention that we last saw Armin Shimerman as a (probably unrelated) Ferengi in [*Peak Performance*]({% post_url 2023-04-13-peak-performance %}).

 > **SISKO**: Major, there's a Ferengi legal tradition. It's called plea bargaining. I might let the boy go, but I want something in exchange from Mister Quark. Something very important.

I don't want to pick a fight with Sisko, but I don't *think* that [plea bargains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plea_bargain) work like this.  In our reality, this means that you admit guilt to some crime---not necessarily the crime that the trial cares about---in exchange for something from the prosecutor's office, such as dismissing the worst charges or pursuing lighter sentences.

By contrast, this feels more like a variety of [extortion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extortion), using the threat of an exaggerated charge to coerce concessions from the accused and their family.  And maybe the Ferengi do have something like that, but at least get the terms right...

 > **PICARD**: Commander. Yes, please, come in. Welcome to Bajor.

A tiny thing, but you might take note that Picard chooses to pronounce "Bajor" differently than everybody else does, "b'JORE." rather than "BAY-jore."  And sure, we might imagine that (as George Lucas allegedly did for the original *Star Wars* trilogy) a galactic-scale civilization would necessarily have different pronunciations for almost everything.  But I can't think of a single other instance where this franchise has treated alternative pronunciations of names as remotely legitimate, with [*The Child*]({% post_url 2022-11-24-child %}) even having Data make the point that mispronouncing a name means getting the name wrong[^k1HjCg].

[^k1HjCg]:  I sometimes feel like the "don't make fun of people who mispronounce words, because they probably read it" idea became toxic at some point, with people---particularly people who speak for a living, such as podcasters---deciding that meant that they didn't need to learn how to pronounce names.  To such people, let me give you explicit permission, then:  You can *look up* how to pronounce things as part of your preparation, rather than suggesting that people send corrections...

In any case, I never noticed this detail before, but it sets up the dynamic nicely, especially combined with his not helping O'Brien, suggesting that he has no real interest in the situation, no matter what he says.

 > **SISKO**: Yes, sir. We met in battle. I was on the *Saratoga* at Wolf 359.

While this bumps up against the boundaries of the writing, the Wolf 359 incident traumatized both Picard and Sisko, among many others, but it has only dawned on Picard in this scene that he *caused* the trauma for everybody else.  And it sounds like---despite nobody requiring him to actually go to therapy to deal with it---everybody else got far worse "treatment" for their post-traumatic stress than he did.  And only Picard got to make everybody else's trauma about his pain.

Maybe more importantly, Picard wants **nothing** to do with this, literally turning away to change the subject.  And I can't help comparing Picard's disinterest in his colleague's pain with the fascination with and apparent empathy for the alternate universe Riker in [*Parallels*]({% post_url 2025-08-07-parallels %}) who similarly needed to take command of his ship after watching many of his colleagues die in an equivalent battle.

 > **PICARD**: They've left the Bajorans without a means of being self-sustaining. The relief efforts we've been coordinating are barely adequate. I've come to know the Bajorans. I'm a strong proponent for their entry into the Federation.

...whatever they call their planet.

By the way, *why* does Picard want this so badly (but not so badly that he'd take the job), anyway?  If they have no resources remaining, then the relief efforts wouldn't stop.  I mean, OK, the relief efforts probably *would* stop as people stop worrying, but they wouldn't become any less necessary, so Bajor doesn't get anything out of it.

Does the Federation get something out of Bajor's entry?  Bajor can't offer anybody any resources if they don't have any.  Next week, they *will* have strategic value, but Picard shouldn't know about that yet, unless he read the script.  It seems doubtful that his handful of Bajoran acquaintances, who like Kira grew up fighting for freedom and independence, want the Federation governing them any more than Kira does.

I can think of three possible reasons, probably related.  First, Picard might see strategic value in Bajor's proximity to the Cardassians, seeing another war on the horizon as hinted in episodes such as *The Wounded*.  Second, and maybe most cynically, like his repulsive comment at the end of [*Ensign Ro*]({% post_url 2024-05-30-ensign-ro %}) that he "noticed qualities in [her] that could be harnessed, molded," he sees an army of former freedom fighters available to expand Starfleet's ranks.  And finally, in *Parallels*, Worf passed through a timeline where the Bajorans won their freedom somewhat earlier, defeated the Cardassians, and began attacking the Federation, so we could imagine Picard interested in their Federation membership because he wants control over their expansion.

Actually, a fourth possibility might feel the most consistent:  We'll eventually find out that the Bajorans have a decidedly ancient civilization, *Ensign Ro* hinting that their recorded history stretches back millions of years (since before humans stood upright), with space-travel that pre-dates even the overwhelming majority of *fictional* space-travel on Earth, such as in Kepler's [**Somnium**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somnium_%28novel%29).  It would fit entirely in Picard's character, then, to want a government in place that would allow him to pillage artifacts as an amateur archaeologist, the remaining resource that Bajor has.

 > **PICARD**: Your job is to do everything short of violating the Prime Directive to make sure that they are. I have been made aware by Starfleet of your objections to this assignment. I would have thought that after three years spent at the Utopia Planitia yards, that you would be ready for a change.

You can all feel the condescending attitude here, right?  In this line, Picard has (a) directed Sisko to skirt whatever ethical boundaries that he needs to, "short of violating the Prime Directive"---whatever that means, here---to quell Bajor's political strife; (b) disapproved of the idea of managing shipyards as a career; and (c) suggested that exile to recently liberated hostile territory on the border makes for a fun change from engineering work on Mars.

 > **PICARD**: Unfortunately as Starfleet officers, we do not always have the luxury to serve in an ideal environment.

Oh, and the guy who poured himself tea from a fragile ceramic pitcher (and didn't offer his guest any) in his gleaming ship literally looking down on the people who he has "come to know" wants to explain that sometimes you don't get cushy assignments?  Nice.  And that looking-down issue seems maybe intentional, because it not only links with the aforementioned likely mispronunciation of the planet's name, but also Sisko's note (which I didn't bother to include) that the Cardassians elevated the prefect's office above the operations center, so that everybody needed to look up with respect.

 > **SISKO**: I realize that, sir, and I'm investigating the possibility of returning to Earth for civilian service.

Notice Picard recoiling at the idea of somebody voluntarily leaving Starfleet.

 > **PICARD**: I'll look into it. In the meantime however---
 >
 > **SISKO**: In the meantime, I will do the job I've been ordered to do to the best of my ability, sir.
 >
 > **PICARD**: Dismissed.

And look how Picard shrinks, here, as Sisko tells him off.

To repeat some context that I've mentioned earlier in the project, when **TNG** launched, many fans didn't care for it, in a slightly different way than fans have objected to every other new part of the franchise.  Given my age, I couldn't discriminate well enough to realize it, but I gather that those fans saw many of the same issues that we have dug up over the past almost-four years.  Far from the "I will not kill today" striving for excellence of the original series, Picard tries to steam-roll over anybody who doesn't have his privileges.  By this point in watching **TNG**, I know that I had gotten sick of this "city on a hill" nonsense that I wrote about recently, and continued watching more out of inertia than anything else, joking that every cold open amounted to "should we create a disaster on our own, or should we order out, this week?"  Nobody on the *Enterprise* cares about anything or anybody outside their friend group, and I think that you can feel that.

Now, release **DS9** into this context, and notice what you see in this scene.  Yes, on the surface, you have two men trying to move on from their related trauma in different ways.  Then, below the surface, you have Picard showing himself as the same aristocratic jackass, but in front of somebody who absolutely does not have any patience for it.  And without the often-literal wide-eyed adulation of his sycophants[^dDRkoy], he buckles when told that, yes, some people do know how to do their jobs even when they don't get a fancy office with dainty ceramic knick-knacks, comfortable chairs, and potted plants.  And I don't think that it takes much to read this as telling us explicitly that better exists out there, and that people like Picard don't actually have much to offer behind the bluster.

[^dDRkoy]:  Riker's "oh, no, he didn't" face, for example.

At that point, to return to a question asked a few times before, most recently with **First Contact**, how does Picard still have his job?  He has debilitating post-traumatic stress that has endangered his crew multiple times, and now we see that he also doesn't have the trust or respect of his colleagues beyond the *Enterprise*.  Heck, he also shows time and again that he doesn't respect his superiors, even seeing them as targets in [*Conspiracy*]({% post_url 2022-11-03-conspiracy %}) because he vaguely suspected foreign influence.  What value does he have in his position that outweighs these problems?

On the writing side, friend-of-the-blog Mup has a post [digging into this scene](https://gius.uk/trek/DS9Pilot/) in particular, along with comments that I made trying to guess at the writers' intentions, based on other scenes.

 > **SISKO**: Please. My officers, the Bajoran engineers, all their families depend on the shops and services of this Promenade. If people like you abandon it, this is going to become a ghost town. We need someone to step forward and say, I'm staying, I'm rebuilding. We need a community leader, and it's going to be you, Quark.

Look at this, building an actual community.  Sure, with some emotional and some literal blackmail, but for the purpose of getting an oppressed population back on its feet without direct interference.

 > **SISKO**: I know this place. This is Gilgo Beach, where we met.

Unless another place by the same name exists that I couldn't track down, this happens about an hour by car from where I type this.  [Gilgo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilgo,_New_York) lies on the barrier beaches separating Long Island from the Atlantic Ocean, similar to more famous places such as Fire Island and Jones Beach State Park.

Notably, because of the way the glaciers deposited and formed Long Island, the enormous rock behind Jennifer through this scene absolutely does not exist.  Any large rocks got deposited along Long Island's north shore[^jT7Z1L]...or, at least, rocks of that size only got dropped by the glaciers.  Throughout the area, you'll actually find smaller rocks (human-sized, give or take) that don't make any geological sense, because colonial-era merchant ships would use local rocks as ballast until loading up raw materials to haul back to Europe.

[^jT7Z1L]:  In fact, along the so-called [Gold Coast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Shore_%28Long_Island%29), I can think of a few rocks that size, but none on a beach that looks like that.  Honestly, for the past thirty-plus years, the differences led me to assume that the episode meant some beach in California with a similar name until I searched the name for this post.

![Gilgo Beach in late 2016](/blog/assets/33241426201_1111b7bdb8_o.png "Facing east, for those of you who need to orient yourself.")

In reality, Gilgo Beach looks more like the above, some parts of the area having fine enough sand that it almost feels closer to a clay.

Depending on your age, you might know the Gilgo area from the highly publicized [serial murders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilgo_Beach_serial_killings) that started a few months after this episode aired and lasted for almost twenty years, with police still uncovering evidence.

 > **JENNIFER**: I'm afraid I don't accept drinks from strange men on the beach.

Think about what this throwaway line says, here.  In our time, women reject offers from strange men because of some combination of men assuming that acceptance implies some sort of obligation to return their affections in some way---and Sisko does go pretty far overboard in this scene; please don't treat women like this---or they worry that the man has doctored the drink.  *Maybe* Jennifer has some unrelated concern, but it doesn't seem likely.

 > **JAKE**: What?

We'll have a lot of opportunity to talk more about baseball---referring to Jake's glove, I mean---as the series progresses, but it seems worth noting that, like fishing at the start of the episode, the sport has strong "blue-collar" coding in the United States, and immigrants have historically embraced watching and playing in hopes of seeming "more American," an image in popular culture that largely ended around this time.  People started to see a decline in the sport's popularity through the 1980s, after all, with [*The Big Goodbye*]({% post_url 2022-08-04-big-goodbye %}) and [*Evolution*]({% post_url 2023-05-04-evolution %}) extrapolating that decline to suggest that the sport had become almost obscure by the twenty-fourth century.

 > **QUARK**: You won't like it. I love the Bajorans. Such a deeply spiritual culture, but they make a dreadful ale. Never trust ale from a god-fearing people, or a Starfleet Commander that has one of your relatives in jail.

Note the little reminder that foreigners don't trust the Federation.

 > **SISKO**: Three hundred twenty-eight, maybe. Did you tell him about that slug inside of you?

I suppose that we don't actually know how the Trill think of or talk about their...participants, I guess?  But I have to admit that calling it "that slug" sounds *extremely* racist.

 > **BASHIR**: I didn't want some cushy job or a research grant. I wanted this. The farthest reaches of the galaxy. One of the most remote outposts available. This is where the adventure is. This is where heroes are made. Right here, in the wilderness.
 >
 > **KIRA**: This wilderness is my home.
 >
 > **BASHIR**: Well, I...didn't mean.
 >
 > **KIRA**: The Cardassians left behind a lot of injured people, Doctor. You can make yourself useful by bringing your Federation medicine to the natives. Oh, you'll find them a friendly, simple folk.

Feel free to copy-and-paste almost everything that I said about the Sisko/Picard scene earlier down here, because this scene does almost exactly the same with newer characters, and tells us fairly clearly that Starfleet has a systemic problem.  And both those scenes go with the problem with the "Deep Space" nomenclature.  The Federation doesn't see these distant cultures as interesting or even as *people*, at best only supporting characters in a [~~white~~ human savior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_savior) story.

In the words of [Wilfred Scawen Blunt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilfred_Scawen_Blunt), *The White Man’s Burden, Lord, is the burden of his cash*.  And sure, Bashir probably doesn't read as "white" to any of us, but he absolutely reads as a rich kid...

You might recall that, due to our time-travel shenanigans, we actually met a slightly more established Bashir in [*Birthright*]({% post_url 2025-03-06-birthright-1 %}).  Back to the other part of the running theme, that episode featured everybody frustrated with the doctor taking random parts of the *Enterprise* for a project, something that their crew does routinely for personal projects.

 > **DAX**: Curzon.

For good news, the franchise has finally decided that it can accept a loose transgender allegory without telling us to find it repulsive.  For bad news, though, they still needed to make the only remotely (explicitly) queer character an alien who doesn't quite count until **Discovery** tried again.  I guess that you could count giving Sulu a husband in one of the reboot films, too, but "blink and you miss it" only barely counts.

 > **PICARD**: You know, yesterday, I called down here, and I asked for you without thinking. It won't be quite the same.

I can't help reading this scene as Picard trying to get a second chance at the meeting with Sisko, except with somebody who admires him. ## Conclusions

We got a lot, this time, no?  Even before the political-type material, we have actual leisure activities and swimsuit fashion.

### The Good

Though it only seems to appear on lower-status ships, Starfleet does display more diversity in its crews and leadership in this.  Those officers also have less patience for authoritarian lecturing and stand up for themselves well enough to shame the likes of Picard.

Sisko shows some empathy, both on the small scale of connecting with people and on the larger scale of seeding communities to build into larger support for the Bajorans.

### The Bad

We get evidence that supports the idea that Picard deliberately undermined any more peaceful intentions by the Borg.

We also see plenty of racism.  They reinforce the idea that humans live ethnically segregated.  O'Brien openly jokes about Bajoran women in front of Bajoran women.  Starfleet named somebody else's station as if they don't recognize the inhabited planet it orbits.  Picard mispronounces the name of the planet that he allegedly cares deeply about.  Starfleet also explicitly directs Sisko to manipulate the Bajoran government to their benefit.  Sisko refers to the non-humanoid Trill as what sounds like a slur.

Classism also follows behind the racism, with Picard sneering at ship construction and civilian work.  Picard also lectures Sisko on the importance of duty from his comfortable job.  Bashir dismisses Bajor as wilderness for him to tame.

Sisko, at least, seems highly careless with legal terminology, joining a continued confusion about the Prime Directive, and also willing to use children as bargaining chips.

They give a strong impression that their terrible approach to helping Picard recover from his trauma represents the best that the Federation has to offer.  And related, Picard never came to terms with the damage that he caused and refuses to engage with it.

We see at least (further) hints that men feel entitled to the affections of women.

Quark provides a reminder that the Federation doesn't have a good reputation beyond its borders.

### The Weird

Despite repeated assertions that the Federation has a strong desire to see Bajor join the Federation, nobody seems to have any explanation as to how either party would benefit from the relationship.
