## Previously...

## Progress

Off the station, this episode follows [*Frame of Mind*]({% post_url 2025-04-10-frame-mind %}) and comes shortly before [*Suspicions*]({% post_url 2025-04-17-suspicions %}), going from protecting Riker from a murder conviction to shock at the existence of Ferengi scientists.

Also worth mentioning, we probably won't get much out of the episode, given how infrequently our Federation-based cast even shows up, but I'd call it a definite crowd-pleaser.  You can't find a better stem bolt in this sector, after all.

 > **QUARK**: I thought I told you to cancel that order. Now what am I supposed to do with five thousand wrappages of Cardassian yamok sauce. They're the only ones who could stomach that swill.
 >
 > ...
 >
 > **QUARK**: You ordered it, you're going to pay for it. I'm going to take half of your paycheck every week for the next six years. From now on, no more Cardassian food in here ever.

Not Federation-relevant, I suppose, but this *probably* gives us some idea of pricing orders of magnitude in at least Federation-adjacent spaces.  We don't know what size they would use for "wrappages," or the relative value of yamok sauce as compared to, say, mayonnaise.  But---once again assuming that their years and weeks look mostly like ours---one wrappage has about the same worth as (almost exactly) one thirty-second of a server's weekly income.

For example, in the United States, the national minimum tipped wage where states haven't done better---we wouldn't expect Quark to pay his staff more than the absolute minimum---currently sits at $2.13 per hour.  If we go maybe-too-far and assume an eighteen-hour (twenty-six minus eight for a home life) schedule seven days per week, fifty-two weeks per year for six years, would give us about $8.39 per wrappage.  Checking local supermarket circulars for condiments (retail instead of wholesale, I realize, but the best that I can probably do), that comes in at significantly more than the ten ounce bottle of Sriracha, slightly more than a thirty ounce jar of super-mainstream mayonnaise, less than the forty-eight ounce jar or eight ounces of red miso paste, and much less than six ounces of chili crisp, about as fancy as my neighborhood gets, I guess.  If we want to assume more of a literal sauce, it closely matches the twenty-five ounce jars of marinara sauce.

No, I don't know why they classify miso as a condiment.  Somebody needs to review the episodes of our franchise so that we can understand our culture, I guess...

Anyway, napkin-math to get us an order of magnitude suggests that their economy probably doesn't look too different from ours, for reasons that we don't know.  It reminds me of [*The Apple*]({% post_url 2020-09-10-apple %}) and its adaptation suggesting that the Federation's currency in Kirk's time looked a lot like the United States dollar in 1967, except that since this aired, we have had decades of active wage suppression and inflation.

And yeah, it probably sounds like a bad sign that we compare neatly with the Ferengi economy, but they also had a shriveled, sex-obsessed leader handing out economic favors in exchange for bribes, whereas w---oh, dear.

 > Station log. Stardate 46844.3. With the help of the Federation, Bajor is about to commence its first large-scale energy transfer, the tapping of the molten core of its fifth moon, Jeraddo.

I can't claim any relevant expertise, here, and I guess that it doesn't have anything to do with the Federation except that Sisko hasn't screamed at them to stop, but this sounds obscenely reckless.  As I understand it, molten cores have a strong influence on magnetic fields, rotation, and stability.  Pulling energy out, cooling the core, changes those, which feels like something that you don't want to do in orbit around an inhabited planet.

 > **DAX**: They make him look kind of cute. That's odd. Sensors read a humanoid presence in fifteen-delta. Could be one of the project thermologists.

Again not really relevant, but this sort of thing always kept me from finding Dax interesting, trying to make such a big deal about her attraction to random characters, as if that inherently makes her transgressive or interesting...

 > **MULLIBOK**: If I came across a deposit of mineralized clay, what I'd do is just grind it up in my teeth. That way it enriched the soil. Baltrim, how about some of that wonderful wine here? What I'm telling you, Major. I tamed this place. Me.

You might recognize our crotchety settler as [Brian Keith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brian_Keith), who appeared pretty much everywhere, but people have mostly recognized him from the likes of the original **The Parent Trap** and **Family Affair**.

And despite his lack of lines, Baltrim, you might recognize as [Terrence Evans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrence_Evans), who'll appear again in a few seasons as another character, then an ambassador on **Voyager**, but popped up almost everywhere.

 > **NOG**: I would've bet anything that he would have known what they were.
 >
 > **JAKE**: I told you. We should've asked that freighter captain.

Can they not...look these things up?  Surely, somebody in the Federation or an allied power has set up at least the interstellar version of [McMaster-Carr](https://www.mcmaster.com/)---yeah, they sell everything, but everybody I know uses it primarily to find out if particular parts exist and in what range of measurements before shopping---if not a full encyclopedia.

And notice that, despite this apparent lack of access to information, literal children have shown more respect for people's time than Data did after a full Starfleet Academy education and years of professional experience.

 > **GUARD**: What about the other two?

And I believe this makes the last of the recognizable guest stars, [Daniel Riordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Riordan), who we actually last saw in [*Coming of Age*]({% post_url 2022-09-22-coming-age %}), and will later feature as Duras (as in Klingon House of...) in **Enterprise**, with plenty of other appearances around genre fiction.

 > **JAKE**: Land is good.

This feels like an admission that the Federation does, in fact, work on capitalist principles on some level.  If Jake understands the value of land---really that even if they can extrude anything out of a replicator, you can't manufacture a place to build housing and the like---then you probably don't get free housing, not even with random planets to colonize.  Contrast that to Nog, who even immersed in an ultra-capitalist culture and (as Sisko emphasized early on) a few years on Jake, doesn't understand the property rights involved in land ownership, believing that they only own a pile of dirt somewhere.

 > **BASHIR**: But sir, that isn't true.
 >
 > **SISKO**: Make it true, Doctor. Now, please.

Excellent.  Also, Bashir asking for prompting so that he can play along feels like it could come out of a **Bluey** episode.

 > **MULLIBOK**: There was this big, ugly tree.
 >
 > **SISKO**: Go on.
 >
 > **MULLIBOK**: Never mind.

I think that I've mentioned this before, because we definitely saw a scene like this early on in the series, but I love the "patiently listen while the other person tires himself out like a toddler" angle that Sisko takes with at least some people.

 > **SISKO**: I'm not saying it's simple, I'm saying it can't wait. Look, I understand you're used to sympathizing with the underdog. You've spent your life fighting to overcome impossible odds just like he's doing. But you have to realize something, Major. You're on the other side now. Pretty uncomfortable, isn't it?

Granted, I look back at this with 2020s experience, but it feels like a missed opportunity for the show never get deeper into this.  It sounds like Sisko speaks from personal experience that the uniform, and giving us some sense of his discomfort in Starfleet missions and why he finds it reasonable to stay for decades despite that discomfort might give us a better sense of values.

 > **SISKO**: When I first met you, Major, I thought you were hostile and arrogant. But I was wrong. Bajor needs you, and I need you. I like you, and I don't want you to be hurt. So, as a friend, I'm here to remind you that his fate is already decided. Yours isn't.

This probably has nothing to do with our purpose, here, beyond Sisko caring about people, and I may have mentioned this before, but I really love their relationship.  A lesser show would have let O'Brien play Iago to Sisko's Othello, making him paranoid about Kira's motives (as Sisko hints, here), or paired them off[^c-2s4-] in a creepy romance where she also sees him as an important religious figure.

[^c-2s4-]:  Not to invoke the spirits of the competition, but whenever the powers-that-be talk about potentially rebooting **Babylon 5**---which at this point also terrifies me as potentially happening under the banner of a combined Paramount-Skydance/Warner-Discovery---I worry that a streamlined version of the story walks right into the star-crossed romance between Delenn and the combined Sinclair/Sheridan/Valen who she also venerates, not noticing the worrying aspects of that idea.

Instead, from the start, they establish this as a fairly strong friendship where they look out for each other and support each other.

 > **KIRA**: That's because I was taking care of you all night.

A minor point, only tangentially related to the overall project, here, but it seems worth noting that Kira comfortably slept in (most of) her uniform, with no comments from the wise-ass about her odor, which strongly suggests that the rag-tag Bajoran militia, formally in existence for less than a year after a brutal extractive occupation lasting for decades, has higher-quality uniforms than Starfleet.  Either that, or Bajorans don't sweat, but that seems like something that would eventually come up.

 > **KIRA**: Saving your life.

Did he build his house out of *refined fuel*?  I've gone to barbecues where the person at the grill had a chimney starter, newspaper, lighter fluid, and more, and it still took much longer to get charcoal to catch fire than this house...

## Conclusions

Despite my asides, as mentioned at the top, we really don't get much out of the episode beyond the entertainment value.

### The Good

When we do see the Federation side of things, it mostly showcases Sisko's willingness to support his colleagues, even if that means bending an occasional rule.

### The Bad

We have returned to the age when, despite ubiquitous computers with galactic database access, nobody can figure out where to get definitions to words.

Sisko strongly implies that Starfleet has put him on the uncomfortable side of issues.

### The Weird

The Federation seems to have significantly more interest in real-estate prices than the Ferengi.

While occasionally still get reminders that the Federation makes uncomfortable, stifling uniforms, we find out that they apparently do so deliberately, with less-funded governments producing far better options.
