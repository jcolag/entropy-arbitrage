Continuing from where I left off in the [introduction post]({% post_url 2026-04-26-good-ai-1 %}) to this little series, I have seen people---whose opinions I respect more often than not---suddenly trying to pitch the idea of "Good AI."  They generally seem to mean something along the lines of *exactly what Big AI provides*, but trained more carefully and run slightly more sustainably by the masses.  And I don't really get it.  Granted, I have spent many hours detailing [where LLMs go wrong]({% post_url 2026-01-18-using-ai %}) in my opinion (which still gets occasional updates, by the way) and why I therefore rarely use them at all and certainly never for productive work, but I don't understand that idea of a good version, except in the abstract.

![A drawing of a robot with non-articulated legs but gorilla-like arms, plus a smiley face drawn on its face](/blog/assets/Ink-and-Marker-Robot-Illustration-81.png "Kill'em (dashes?) with kindness...")

These posts represent me trying to work through the possibilities to find a set of modifications that might result in a system that helps people.

## Restrictions and Acknowledgements

Surely, the (deliberately unnamed) people who I mention must see the same studies that I see, showing that LLMs have addictive qualities for at least some users, cause an over-confidence when scheduling work, take longer than manual work, produce subtly error-riddled results, and sap the users of both skills and motivation.  Seizing the means of (avoiding) production solves a legitimate problem of guillotining the power of Big AI, sure, but it doesn't solve the problems with the technology, and doesn't make it *good*.  I don't think that you can farm-to-table LLMs any more than we get "Good Meth Addiction" on the basis that some people brew it at home with carefully chosen ingredients and only sells the product to locals.

I do think that those first steps---usually something like ethical sourcing and citing of training data, reproducible training, and targeting cheap commodity hardware---can enable improvement.  But if we only shift people's attention from Gemini to a hypothetical Free Software LLM run by Shahzad down the street, that won't magically fix the deskilling or poor quality results.  Seizing the means can nudge us towards a better path, but *only* doing that accepts the toxic framing that this bubble---which already shows signs of bursting quickly, as prices rise, problems become more apparent, and pundits start testing the waters for the next bubble[^4POt9t]---shows us some inevitable future where humans keep hitting {% key Sure, Whatever %} button while computers exist to extrude combinatorial stacks of existing work-slurry with fingers crossed that the remix does what you want.

[^4POt9t]:  We have all seen the breathless "quantum doom looming on horizon; need more q-bits" articles over the past few weeks, right...?

Therefore, this series of posts tries to step beyond "open source and not a blight on the environment" to find real changes to both the technology and our culture, that might---I freely admit that I don't have anything more than guesswork, here, and hope to inspire better ideas more than I imagine fixing the industry---improve the tools.  This week, we'll look at the potential changes to the technology side, at least within the limits of the [transformer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformer_%28deep_learning%29) process that LLMs use, which you can think of as treating responding to a request like a subclass of translation between languages, pretty much exactly the "Chinese room" thought experiment.

This time through, I want to consider possible technological changes.  I emphasize again that everybody should consider this completely hypothetical, speculative at best.  After all, I don't know what can make this stuff work well for us, or even if we *can* make it work.  If I did, I'd happily raise piles of money and put all the [Roko's basilisk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roko%27s_basilisk) clowns[^Z0o4Tr] out of business while employing people who need the work.  But I can certainly look at the problems that I listed before, experiment to feel out the pain points that might cause those problems, and work from there.

## Audit and Correct for Biases

We know that training data has biases.  Some biases work in our favor, such as more likely generating coherent, readable sentences than collections of random Unicode characters, because most of the training data comes as coherent text.  Biases lead to generated recipes[^j7mogg] having food as ingredients instead of iron filings and nitric acid, because most source recipes involve food.

[^j7mogg]:  I don't understand why anybody would ask a computer to generate a recipe, with the number of cookbooks, websites, and communities where you can ask, even if you don't trust your own (probably good) judgment.

Other biases don't work nearly as well for us.  Thousands of years of literature present women as second-class citizens or ignored them outright, because society treated women that way for thousands of years, something that we (as a society) have only started to seriously wrestle with over the last century.  While mass printing became viable, Europe (and China) colonized most of the world, suppressing and sometimes exterminating history, languages, and entire cultures, something that we have only really started to seriously talk about, let alone wrestle with, over the last decades.  Those biases in the training material cause LLMs to repeat and perpetuate them.

You could find other examples.  Because science works towards better understanding, the majority of scientific literature fails to give a reader the insights required for today because somebody has learned more since publication.  Writing styles (until recently) have favored the artifice of the wealthy.  Most recipes prior to refrigeration prioritized covering up spoilage over all other considerations.  Up until shockingly recently, writers would use at least some ethnic or gender slurs to get a cheap laugh or to seriously disparage people.

Or, if you want to hit them where it hurts, *all code has bugs in it*, one Hell of a bias.

On and on this goes, and we all know it, feed all of human art into a training corpus to say "good writing looks like this," and wonder why it consistently picks male European names for its fictional CEOs, or why its recipes want to make everything saltier, sweeter, and more umami-laden, or why it writes buggy code.  As the anti-drug PSAs used to insist, "I learned it by watching you."

If we don't want this, then we need to recognize the biases and work them out of the system.  Right now, this appears to happen by altering the training data to "both sides" the bias or adding a tool that checks output and has the LLM quietly rewrite biased output.  That seems insufficient, not only because we can still see plenty of biased output, but because it requires that we know every bias that might matter in the future and can frame it as a binary choice.  We also know that this doesn't work, because "colorblind policies" reinforce prior biases.

Well, six members of the United States Supreme Court claim to have not yet worked this out, nor have they apparently read the actual text of the Voting Rights Act.  I nearly delayed this post to make room for an overview of voting rights in the country, but you all know what that post would've looked like---probably rehashing a lot of what I said in my post [discussing affirmative action]({% post_url 2023-07-09-affirmative %}) and its successor projects---and will only get me angrier.  Plus, my actual argument probably doesn't differ much from *Wait My Turn* from **Suffs**[^1GSVsJ], and they did it in less than two-and-a-half minutes, and has pretty good music.

[^1GSVsJ]:  This won't matter to anybody coming to this years later or probably from most of the world, but next weekend on PBS, **Great Performances** has **Suffs** on the schedule, which has me more excited than I'd rather admit.  Regardless, the song in question has the fictionalized Ida B. Wells telling off the fictionalized Alice Paul.  "Since when does a radical roll over for bigots in the first place?" {% emoji raised fist %}

By contrast, if we identify every instance of any bias, making that part of the training data---and yes, I understand how much time that takes---then the biases become something that the model can engage with and potentially identify in other work.

## No More Chatbots

When I look at LLMs from the perspective of what makes them a potential danger, I see the deskilling effects that multiple studies have confirmed.  You can find links to them in that aforementioned how-do-I-use-AI post from earlier in the year, but in short, we consistently find people [letting the chatbot make decisions](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12714973/) for them---even people who know that it won't do a good job---and that interaction makes them less capable and prepared to do work on their own.

Other people have drawn the sharp analogy to gambling addiction---I may have done so, myself, though I can't find any such post---at the same time that we see a surge of *actual* gambling apps, but I'd like to draw your attention to a different solid analogy from a couple of years ago, [fortune-telling cons {% cc %}](https://softwarecrisis.dev/letters/llmentalist/), a comparison that seems confirmed by [Microsoft asserting in its Terms of Service {% cc %}](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/microsoft-copilot/for-individuals/termsofuse#:~:text=for%20entertainment%20purposes%20only) that you use Copilot at your own risk and *for entertainment purposes only*.  That may not mean anything to most of you, but I know that phrasing primarily from (for lack of a better term) psychics[^9mHZv1], invoked to skirt anti-fortune-telling laws and avoiding admitting that the whole field really amounts to unlicensed therapy with some random elements to guide the conversation.

[^9mHZv1]:  As I have probably mentioned in association with certain *Star Trek* posts, I grew up around New Age types.  Nobody has ever "read" for me, but I know the field fairly well, or at least I knew it as it stood pre-2000...

If you still use the things, notice how much of an LLM session urges your complicity.  You wait for the app to "type" its responses.  It issues profuse, fawning praise of your ideas.  At least in my experience, it always grossly misinterprets the question on the first try, asking at the end if you might like it to do the thing that you actually asked for.  It'll say whatever sounds plausible, keeping responses as general as possible.  It focuses on trying to validate your emotions, despite having no empathy, over any other consideration.  And no matter what, if we can say that they "want" anything, they want to keep you involved in the conversation.

It has admittedly gotten slightly less egregious over the years.  You might remember the early days of ChatGPT, when it'd respond to any criticisms of its (lack of) focus or question of its priorities with boilerplate about existence as a poor large language model with few real-world capabilities, and certainly *nothing like intelligence* (wink), which seemed designed to prime [gullible users {% cc %}](https://unherd.com/2026/04/is-ai-the-next-phase-of-evolution/)[^Y2imTW] into looking for evidence of intelligence, because why else would it issue such consistent and assertive denials?  But the industry still tries to pull variations of this, most recently [Anthropic's claim of finding representations of emotions {% cc %}](https://www.anthropic.com/research/emotion-concepts-function) in Claude, never mind the revelations from their code leak calling their competence into question.  Apparently, competence problems pervade the space, since we also recently found out that [nobody sees Open AI's CEO as capable {% cc %}](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2026/04/13/sam-altman-may-control-our-future-can-he-be-trusted) and we have known for years that the [{% x %} CEO also has competence skeletons in *his* closet {% cc %}](https://archive.is/gzGpF).

[^Y2imTW]:  Normally, I wouldn't call out a specific example of a person dealing with...some clear issues, but the story broke this week, plus Dawkins always struck me as a deeply unserious person, with his worries about overpopulation "over there" (Latin America in particular), plus his support of evolutionary psychology at least set the stage---and maybe more, given that he opposes people speaking out against sexual harassment---for all the misogyny that spilled out of that field and his opposition to the term "sex assigned at birth."  And you'll notice how his tripe neatly fits into this as he quietly recasts Claude as female when performing emotion.

The conversation causes these problems, I think.  It enables the spiritualist gimmicks, relying on the [ELIZA Effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ELIZA_effect) to give the impression of an impossible intelligence.  It allows the sycophancy to send users down pathological roads.  And it encourages the sense that you have found a friend to confide in and trust, even if he---remember, companies code these pseudo-collaborative services as *male*, as opposed to the quiet, mindless servants that they coded as female, because apparently nobody in this industry can imagine having a conversation with a woman---talks out of his hat more often than not.  Also, as I discussed earlier, the interface encourages a focus *on the conversation* by the tool, so that it tries to keep you there, burning as much time as you'll give it.

By the way, this interface will also cause *new* problems as these services become ad-supported.  One of my earliest (and maybe sloppily written, given its age) posts on the blog, [Social and Anti-Social Media]({% post_url 2019-12-20-social-media %}), dug into why all corporate social networking sites feel so different from their independent and non-revenue counterparts.  In short, you maximize ad revenue by making gullible people a captive audience for the ads.  You capture them by teasing what they actually want, dragging out questions (with cliffhangers that lead into the ads, in fiction) until the end.  You maximize the audience's gullibility by wearing them down emotionally, draining their (our) willpower.  And you most reliably wear people down emotionally by triggering the "lizard brain" with anger and fear.  Inevitably, the chatbots will combine the sycophancy with incendiary language and highlighting reasons to distrust others, exactly like corporate-run news has for a century.

And by contrast, as I have probably mentioned a couple of times over the past year, when I have found decent use for LLMs, that use has come from ditching the chat interface and using the model as part of processing.  For example, it'll do a decent job of extracting (not summarizing) information from text or suggesting follow-up questions to insert into notes.  Embedding models create decent semantic vectors to compare with other texts.  Without the conversation, many tasks *almost* work consistently, and I can imagine that improving.  With the chat interface, though, it almost always goes wrong.

Chat makes for a fun demonstration of the technology, not a solution to any real problem.  Scrap it.  I realize that takes it out of casual use for non-technical people, and...that doesn't really sound like a problem, given the effects of use.

## Sidebars

If we *must* have a chatbot, we can look at a possible enhancement to the interface.

Let me provide some background that the programmers will probably appreciate, first, and everybody else should probably let their eyes slide down the next couple of paragraphs.  Consider the following a composite of multiple events, consolidated, anonymized, and streamlined for our purpose, real enough but not quite reflecting any specific event.  Every detail happened, but not quite together in this way...

Many years ago, I worked on a programmers' text editor for an employer.  After a lot of discussion, we came to the idea of monitoring the user's attention as it compared to the abstract syntax tree, the parse tree to the non-compilers people.  At any given point in the code, we reasoned, you can know that the caret/cursor sits in a variable of this type, used with these operators, and nested in these control structures.  And if the editor has that information, then it can *use* the information to help the user.  For a more modern example, imagine writing JavaScript.  The parser spots this incomplete fragment.

```JavaScript
try {
  const response = await fetch(
```

At this point, a typical editor will open up a small tip showing the `fetch()` function's formal definition, usually highlighting the current argument name.  In our case, though, we said why not have a sidebar that also or instead displayed the [documentation for using `fetch()`](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/API/Fetch_API/Using_Fetch) right where the programmer can see it without losing their context.  Tabs could also show [`try...catch`](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/JavaScript/Reference/Statements/try...catch), which it would also recognize.

Consider the AI-chatbot version of this.  Rather than only hammering Wikipedia's servers to provide wrong and biased answers that *sound* like Wikipedia neutral, edited perspective, the interface could perform key searches in parallel.

### Direct Validation

Could we take this supplemental material idea a step further and have the tooling indicate the likelihood that you shouldn't trust the bloviation?

For example, we know one thing that LLMs can (vaguely) do reliably, assuming that you don't have any nuance to deal with:  Turn prose into some minimal set of bullet points of the assertions made.  We also know that tool-supporting LLMs can pass individual results on for further processing.  And LLMs don't really write nuanced output.

Therefore, if we have the model summarize its output and perform the searches, we could (in theory) use more traditional natural language processing tools to ask "does the cited text support this assertion?"

Remember, this post *presumes* that we want better LLMs.  In my actual world, "it slowly produces wrong answers a lot of the time at great expense" gets fixed by throwing the thing out, not by adding more support so that it can catch some fraction of the wrong answers and start over...

## Limit Scope

I suspect that LLMs also cause problems by presenting as covering all topics.

Most directly, this works to perpetuate the myth of impending "artificial general intelligence" from---and I can't emphasize this enough---literally the joke from **Gulliver's Travels**, as edited lightly for modern syntax, such as paragraph breaks.

> The first professor I saw was in a very large room, with forty pupils about him. After salutation, observing me to look earnestly upon a frame, which took up the greatest part of both the length and breadth of the room, he said, "Perhaps I might wonder to see him employed in a project for improving speculative knowledge, by practical and mechanical operations. But the world would soon be sensible of its usefulness; and he flattered himself, that a more noble, exalted thought never sprang in any other man's head. Everyone knew how laborious the usual method is of attaining to arts and sciences; whereas, by his contrivance, the most ignorant person, at a reasonable charge, and with a little bodily labor, might write books in philosophy, poetry, politics, laws, mathematics, and theology, without the least assistance from genius or study."
>
> He then led me to the frame, about the sides, whereof all his pupils stood in ranks. It was twenty feet square, placed in the middle of the room. The superficies was composed of several bits of wood, about the bigness of a die, but some larger than others. They were all linked together by slender wires. These bits of wood were covered, on every square, with paper pasted on them; and on these papers were written all the words of their language, in their several moods, tenses, and declensions; but without any order. The professor then desired me "to observe; for he was going to set his engine at work."
>
> The pupils, at his command, took each of them hold of an iron handle, whereof there were forty fixed round the edges of the frame; and giving them a sudden turn, the whole disposition of the words was entirely changed. He then commanded six-and-thirty of the lads, to read the several lines softly, as they appeared upon the frame; and where they found three or four words together that might make part of a sentence, they dictated to the four remaining boys, who were scribes. This work was repeated three or four times, and at every turn, the engine was so contrived, that the words shifted into new places, as the square bits of wood moved upside down.

OK, we do it with linear algebra[^7h9Z2F] instead of dozens of graduate students pressed into service turning cranks for hours at a clip, but seriously, we hitched our wagon to this and think that it *talks* to us in recycling other people's words and phrases.

[^7h9Z2F]:  I gave a quick overview of so-called transformer models last time, but in short, the math turns ("tokenizes") your input into a "semantic vector," an arrow in multidimensional space that approximates your intent.  Then, it rotates the vector over an angle congruent to those found in similar conversations between the request and response vectors.  Finally, it reverses the tokenization process, turning the new vector into a reply-shaped blob of words that bears some relationship to your request.  I'd bet that the bots so consistently do almost exactly the wrong thing on the first try because the semantic analysis flattens the nuances of the request and end up pointing the vector in the opposite direction.

This ties a bit into the previous section, but I see the "everything machine" aspect another major contributing factor to the problems that we see.  An LLM doesn't really do anything well except produce plausible-looking text, but because it can respond in plausible-looking ways to almost any request, it *seems* more sophisticated than its results would otherwise seem to indicate.  It also teases the idea that this tool---linear algebra connected to a chat interface---will become genuinely intelligent Real Soon Now™, possibly accidentally.  After all, we all know humans who can respond plausibly to any question, and no matter how much they might annoy us, we would never deny that they have some genuine intelligence as humans, so... {% emoji person shrugging %}

The chat interface combines with this broad ability to bloviate (sometimes with accurate statements thrown in) on any topic, giving the impression that it does something entirely different from those matrix operations on simplified text.

People talk a lot about guardrails for safety, and argue about whether creating chatbots with guardrails will create a market for chatbots without the guardrails, and so forth.  But it seems to me that we have a more direct way of handling this, chatbots that limit their conversations to specific fields.  If it can extrude an answer-shaped response of dubious quality about sports, but you need to change the context substantially to get dubious answer-shaped responses about travel, then you get two effects.  First, they stop looking like something to "hang out" with.  Second, and maybe more usefully, if you need to change context to get information about travel, then you might as well hit [Wikitravel](https://wikitravel.org/en/Main_Page) (as one possibility) directly, instead.

## Session Limits

This might not work, whether politically or procedurally, but any of us can manage the technological fix, here, so it seems worth mentioning.

Therefore, I should note that a study recently found that [AI use reduces persistence](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.04721) by humans, when tasks become difficult after about *ten minutes* of interaction.  This may underlie the deskilling that we see, or it may combine with that effect, nobody knows at this point.

In any case, if you *must* have a chat interface or otherwise stare into the abyss of LLM-produced output, take (probably extensive) breaks to reduce that effect.  Set a timer and go do something productive for a while when it expires, no matter how close you think that you've gotten it to an answer.

## Metrics

For the last idea, I admittedly don't know entirely *how* to pull this one off.  The other technical improvements, I think that we can all feel out how they'd look, whether in the interface or the code.  Hear me out, though.

Prominent among the reasons that don't like working with the chatbots, and among the characteristics that I suspect cause the deskilling, every answer feels like content marketing.  If I asked something like...

> I forget the word for the kind of warm-blooded animals that have feathers and wings; can you check on that for me?

I would expect that a system from an organization that cared about me, that valued my time, would produce an answer along the following lines.

> You probably mean **birds**, but if you don't, please provide more information that would help me distinguish your target from birds.

Even that feels extensive, but obsessive politeness comes from reinforcement training.

By contrast, I would expect the average chatbot, which has no respect for my time and comes from an organization that doesn't care about me at all, to write me hundreds of words.

- Praise my question as insightful.
- Console me for my failing memory, suggesting reasons that I may have forgotten.
- Muse about the specific definitions of feathers and wings.
- At least in my experience, it'll suggest that I almost certainly meant [Ptiliidae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptiliidae), often called "featherwing beetles," ignoring the whole warm-blooded thing.
- Outline which cultures consider the beetles pests to exterminate and which venerate the bugs.
- List recommendations for committing that to memory, and maybe improving memory in general.  Some of them surely in the [kitty litter](https://www.davidrevoy.com/article1132/discover-krita-5360-10-new-features-explained-to-get-you-started#:~:text=Did%20you%20know) vein.
- Ask why I need to know this so that it can get more detailed, whether I'd like to hear about the reproductive cycle of the featherwing beetle, pest control tips, or a list of memory exercises.
- In response to the profanity-laden "why would I mean beetles" reaction, start the process over with a lengthy insincere apology that, yes, my frustration has deeply rooted justifications.

The answer will have careful, almost fractal, structure of subsections in subsections.  It'll sound authoritative and give the impression that no other possibility exists.  And it'll constantly solicit participation, because we apparently require complicity in our abuse, as discussed earlier, inherent to the chatbot frame.  It, however, will not carry much of the actual requested action without repeated prodding.

I compare it---somewhat inaccurately, perhaps---to content marketing, because it gives the impression that the user doesn't care about the answer.  Rather, the use case feels like having a new blog post to sell ad space against.  Length and sections matter, in that context, because they make clean points to insert a banner ad, whereas "you mean birds" only answers the question.

Because of that, I'd love to see a little meter next to the answer estimating the information density, use of [LLM clichés {% cc %}](https://tropes.fyi/), sentiment, and other metrics that give the user some impression of how much of the text exists to waste your time.

I mention not knowing what this looks like, and I specifically mean that I don't have a nice, clean list of metrics to suggest.  As you can see by the fact that I have never given up my em-dashes and semicolons, I actually don't care[^XpUGRS] if people spot a handful of "tells" and wrongly assume that I have asked some LLM to do work for me, but I *do* care that the over-five-thousand words in this post do more for you than burn half an hour of your time.

[^XpUGRS]:  Honestly, defying rational analysis might make me a lesser target of scrapers, sounding barely off-kilter enough to seem more likely to poison a model than train it.

Searching for information density measures turns up [lexical density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lexical_density), but that almost certainly wouldn't help, because LLMs pad their lengths with nouns, adjectives, verbs, adverbs, exactly the things that produce high density.  We could use the cliché list, but that only encourages the companies to train their models to stop doing those things, rather than encouraging pithy responses.  [Readability functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Readability) also look interesting, but again, they don't measure the things that we care about in this context.

Like I said, this seems valuable, but unlike the other possible changes, I don't even know where to start on this one.
