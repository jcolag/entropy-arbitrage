<i class="fas fa-square"></i> [Federated End-to-End Encrypted Messaging is Coming Soon](https://wedistribute.org/2026/02/fediverse-e2ee-coming/) from We Distribute

> Funding for this feature [was secured through](https://www.sovereign.tech/tech/activity-pub) the [Sovereign Tech Fund](https://www.sovereign.tech/), with leadership provided by the Social Web Foundation. Honestly, I don't care specifically about encrypted messaging on the Fediverse.  However, everybody trots out its lack as a massive criticism, as if *any* major multipurpose alternative secures messages.

{% embed https://www.eff.org/files/banner_library/keys-crossed-pink-starburst_0.png|Two old-style keys crossed in a circle with various red tones radiating out|false| %}

<i class="fas fa-square"></i> [How to Pick Your Password Manager](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/02/how-pick-your-password-manager) from EFF Deeplinks @eff@mastodon.social

> There are also a variety of third-party password managers, some paid, and some free, and some open source. Most of these have the advantage of letting you sync your passwords across a wide variety of devices, operating systems, and browsers. Here are four key things to look out for. First, when synchronizing between devices, your passwords should be encrypted end-to-end using a password that only you know (a “master” or “primary” password). Second, support for autofill can reduce the chance that you’ll get phished. Third, security audits performed by third parties can increase confidence that the software really does what it is designed to do. And finally, of course, random generation of unique passwords is a must.

I recently saw a couple of studies go by exposing security issues with some mainstream password managers, so this seemed worth sharing for the people who might need it.

While I can't recommend it for people who don't want to manage everything on their own, I use [pass](https://www.passwordstore.org/), which stores all my passwords as encrypted text files in a git repository.  It then has different applications that can use it on different platforms, all working from the same repository.  And because it uses standard encryption, even if all the software falls apart, I can still manually decrypt a file to get at the password.
