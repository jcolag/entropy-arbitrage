Oh, before we get going, for those out there who celebrate, have a happy Easter...

As many of you already know, I spent about fifteen years [teaching]({% post_url 2020-01-19-teaching %}) after hours, both as an adjunct professor handling graduate classes and as a local adult education instructor.  As such, even though I don't generally bring it up, I try to keep track of major education stories as they unfold.

![An underwater photo taken looking up to a large circular school of fish while the sun sparkles in the blue water. However, the artist has slightly distorted it with digital artifacts, and I have turned the image on its side](/blog/assets/Lone-Thomasky-Bits-aBaume-Distorted-Fish-School-2560x3612.png "My school had this feel, sometimes...")

And you all know as well as I do that the biggest education stories, these days, revolve around generative artificial intelligence, especially large language models.  I'll get there in a bit---no need to let AI suck the joy out of another weekend---but really, I mostly want to plant a flag in this post that we need to "re-take education" in general, in some sense, as we see it under siege from almost every direction.

Some of this, you've probably heard before.  Other parts feel new, at least to me.  But we'll start with one intersection of generative AI and education that causes pearl-clutching.

## Five-Paragraph Essays

We keep seeing stories about how students turn to ChatGPT or whatever they currently turn to, producing five-paragraph essays that teachers and professors can't distinguish from an authentic attempt at doing the work.  And as I have said before, yeah, that'll happen, because five-paragraph essays exist in precisely the space that large language models thrive:  Form over function, cleanliness over content, and...some third, funnier comparison.  Paragraph one, introduce your topic, usually with the educational equivalent of clickbait, a thesis statement, and then some guide to narrow the focus.  Paragraphs two through four, present and explain one piece of supporting evidence (one each) in favor of the thesis.  Paragraph five, summarize the evidence and restate the thesis, explaining---to use the LLMs' favorite phrasing---*why this matters*.

Such essays require no real thinking or understanding.

### Some Subversion

In college, especially for "write a paper on the story assigned for homework" versions of the essay, for a part-experiment and part-act of rebellion[^NoUSFO], I would routinely write these by randomly selecting three books off my shelves, opening to a similarly random page, and turning something about what I found on each of the three pages into "insights" into what I assumed the story talked about.  I would then skim the assigned story to find a thesis statement that *sounded* plausible (by picking phrasing that resembled the author's) to write my introduction and conclusion, and print that.  Then, I'd actually read the story, so that I could participate in class.

[^NoUSFO]:  I don't believe that any copies of those essays have survived.  Every so often, I find another pile of dusty, old, unlabeled 3½-inch diskettes, and haul out the USB floppy drive to see if they have anything of possible interest, but no luck so far.  If I ever do stumble across the files, you know that I'll almost certainly convert them to Markdown and drop them in a git repository somewhere...

If I remember correctly, I peaked with a paper that cited something like a conspiratorial [Nikola Tesla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nikola_Tesla) biography, [**Worlds in Collision**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Worlds_in_Collision), and [**The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Hitchhiker%27s_Guide_to_the_Galaxy_%28novel%29).  Or maybe I used a Sherlock Holmes story instead of the biography for that one?  And I remember writing the essay about one of the lesser Charles Dickens stories...but also maybe not, and *it doesn't matter*, because I would've written the same essay no matter the assignment.  And that uncertainty makes my point for me, that any fool could sleepwalk through writing these things, as long as the fool can sound coherent when stringing words together and knows the structure.

Do I recommend doing this?  No, definitely not.  To do this sort of thing well, you need to carefully walk the line between "creatively reinterpreting" the spirit of the project and showing disrespect for people trying their best to help.  You need to always keep the audience (the grader) in on the joke, without ever admitting to the joke, but always accepting the responsibility if they don't get it.  And it all needs to happen *quietly*, because more than half of any given class will almost certainly see it as an invitation to act like a jerk.  As such, no, don't do this, unless you already have a good read on how it'll turn out.

Rather than recommending this approach to school-work, I instead wanted to use the story to illustrate my point that it doesn't take *any* talent or intelligence to do this work.  Decades before LLMs, I probably came as close as you could get to getting fairly good grades by handing in essays in which I didn't know the topic or particularly care about it, as much [bibliomancy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bibliomancy) as academia.  Oh, the app that doesn't understand or care about things can do this, too?  Yeah, that tracks.

### DIY Futures

Speaking of bibliomancy, a lot of areas work like this, when you think about it.  I once had the idea for a Conspiracy Theory of the Day website, for example.  The server would get the date and events occurring on that date, and then search documents and encyclopedias for variations of the terms and similar numbers.  Put together the right number of out-of-context facts with loose syntactic connections, and you get prophetic-sounding twaddle that **They** don't want you to know.

Natural language processing at the time couldn't reliably handle finding, extracting, and rephrasing the relevant phrases, sparing the world of that release, but I think that the point still stands that we have plenty of "empty" writing, seeming profound because it follows some form, but not having anything to say beyond expressing that form.

## Fighting the Last War

Anyway, let's get back to the point of starting this post mentioning my teaching background.  I taught at a weird time, and that time might prove instructive for the current situation.  From the day I started until the day that the campus shut down, laptops and mobile phones became not only commonplace, but a critical tool to keep staff on-call after hours while they went about their business.

Increasingly, that meant coping with the reality that enough of the students might legitimately need to have laptops open and phones out---maybe jumping onto a group chat or the like---that any student could plausibly claim to need the same.  Other instructors in the graduate program bemoaned this, because how could you help the students keep their jobs (the employer often paying tuition, in those days) but prevent them from looking up answers?  If you want to go for full-on paranoia, you could even imagine a sufficiently motivated student calling the author of the textbook for advice.

### Different Testing

I had such students since the beginning, though, so...actually, if you read the bit about five-paragraph essays above, then you probably already have some idea where I want to go with this.  I always hated the idea of testing on facts, because remembering or forgetting details doesn't change the reality, and in any real setting, you look things up unless you know them cold, because misremembering or guessing can end in disaster.  My exams, then, *presumed* that you knew or could look up anything covered in class---I let students bring a single page of handwritten notes for the purpose[^wQYIt7]---and asked students to walk through how they'd use that information in practice.

[^wQYIt7]:  Why restrict them, especially with some people potentially wielding laptops?  I actually made the choice for their benefit, not to restrict them.  As a student, I used to have fun observing activity during open-book exams, because people struggled in rough proportion to how much material they brought.  At the most extreme, the "clever" frat-bro who rolled in a hand-truck of textbooks and copies of every member's graded exams for the last however-many years floundered and ultimately failed to complete the test, with too much in front of them.  Plus, writing by hand improves retention, so the students who followed my advice didn't actually need their notes on top of massively outperforming anybody who tried to cheat by scanning the textbook onto their laptops.

As a side effect, this seemed to "reset" many students.  Those (you know the demographic, here) accustomed to coasting by on "already knowing this stuff" suddenly needed to do more than repeat what the textbook said.  And all sorts of "problem" students came to life, because they finally had a space where expressing a coherent opinion mattered.

Every silver lining has its cloud, though.  It took *forever* and a ton of energy to grade these things, probably an average of five pages of hurriedly handwritten text from people who I had different opinions of.  That meant folding covers over to hide names, grading one question at a time, and shuffling the stack between questions, to minimize the chances of unfairly grading somebody who liked or disliked personally, or the chances that the person caught at the bottom of the pile would suffer from my exhaustion.  I would also write a "sample answer" for each question, something that I put together in a few minutes (to approximate not knowing the question in advance) that I would've given full credit for, as a rough guide.

### Dredging for Examples

Unlike my old essays, I *do* have all my old lecture notes, conveniently converted to (almost) Markdown.  And for a while, I have wanted to test my hypothesis by handing a handful of my old exam questions to an LLM.  Surely, their answers would fall into one of three categories, and I have a suspicion of which one.

- They could produce the "already know this stuff" style that I alluded to above.  Those students would try to hedge their answers in hopes of saying something *right* (as opposed to telling my what *makes* their position right and realizing that any single answer could work), and any justification would occur by analogy to a chosen existing system instead of working from the problem.
- Maybe they'll take the approach that I tended to use in my sample answers, picking something clinically and back the choices with whatever evidence fits.
- Finally, we could get something approximating the best answers that I tended to see, carefully analyzing the problem, making reasonable (not necessarily good, but viable) choices, and backing those choices based on their analysis.

Because we have started getting a new batch of think-pieces about professors trying to solve their generative AI woes, it seemed like a good time to try this out.  I'll use Claude as (I guess) the least-objectionable choice, these days.

We'll start with a question that I used in 2008 for a network-programming midterm exam.  My notes say that the class had more of an interest in architecture and management than in actually writing code, so this particular exam doesn't have any programming questions.

> Construct a protocol to conduct votes in a "secret ballot" style.  A user may submit a question, a list of answers, a list of authorized voters, and an expiration time.  Those authorized users may then vote from among the provided options.  When all authorized voters have responded, each of their clients should receive a message declaring the winner.

I explained the experiment in the query to prevent the thing from warning me about teaching and giving it the context of what the class would have covered before the exam.

### Sample Answer

First, let's look at (a cleaned-up version of) my sample answer, what I included in the following week's handout so that students didn't need to ask me what I actually expected in an answer.  If you don't work in software, imagine the "protocol" as a set of forms that people mail to each other, and you'll get fairly close to the right understanding of what I suggest.

> As an important part of this that we can probably mostly hand-wave for now, we have no chance of getting a system like this working without a high-level protocol like [HTTP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTTP) under it, some structure that allows the client and server to communicate consistently without any confusion what everybody sends and receives.  I might suggest something minimal, with three, possibly four, kinds of requests.  Three don't have much to them, one might.
>
> - Request the Question:  Perhaps the packet should begin with a `Q` followed by a username (and any credentials, but never mind that) for verification.  If the user information checks out, we need to return the question and any options.
> - Submit a Vote:  This looks similar, meaning that it needs some signifier to distinguish it.  We could start it with a `V` and follow it by the index of the selected vote.
> - Request a Result:  We may not need this, depending on the "real" architecture, but to poll the server for answers after the vote has elapsed, we may wish to send an `R` with the vote index, to have the results returned.  It should probably include the same authentication content as the Question Request, to maintain security.  This allows the flexibility for clients to either wait for the final result or poll until the result becomes available.
> - Submit a Question:  This becomes more complex.  We need to load the packet with the question, the valid users, and the valid answers.  Something HTTP-like or XML-like would work, or something more compact, with each kind of item above on a separate line, comma-delimited.
>
> The server then needs to issue one of the following responses:
>
> - Issue Question:  This packet should look identical to Question Submission, minus the list of valid users.  Send it in response to a Question Request packet.
> - Status:  Responding to a Vote or Question Submission, we should have some sort of status/error system to inform clients about a legitimate user, or that we already have another vote in progress, in cases where we might only allow one at a time.  The HTTP status line seems appropriate, with a status value and message.  If we reject the Question, add the expiration timestamp so that the user knows when the current vote ends.
> - Results:  We need to send the answer which received the largest number of votes.
>
> Otherwise, the "lead" user should send a Question Submission packet and wait for a Status message.  Other clients should issue a Question Request and receive the Question Issue.  After receiving a response from the user, send a Vote Submission, waiting for the Status message response.  After the Status, however, the client should stay connected until receiving the final Result packet.  If the connection times out, however, it should probably reconnect and begin issuing Result Requests.
>
> The server differs from generic servers in client management, really, where we need to preserve all client connections in some array/list.  When we have all the votes or run out of time, we iterate through the list of client connections and send the result.  Handle other packets as mentioned above.

I cleaned up some grammar, but not the content.  If I answered this today, I would probably overhaul some parts, and definitely replace [XML](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/XML) (the industry darling at the time) with something more compact and human-readable, and also probably make the protocol more verbose than single letters, but you can see the "shape" of the answer, I hope.  Address the problem.  Figure out what that means for the user-facing code.  Figure out what that means for the server, maybe flipping the order if the server seems more interesting.  And then work out how that affects an implementation.

It could have gone in different directions, such as storing a [hash value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hash_function) of the user and their vote as a way of allowing each user to verify their vote without allowing anybody to *see* the vote.  And a more implementation-oriented answer would want to focus more on verifiably counting the votes without exposing them, but this suffices.

### The Dread Machine

Now we'll look at Claude's version.

> #### A Secret Ballot Voting Protocol
>
> ##### Overview
>
> The protocol runs over TCP, using persistent connections between clients and a central voting server. TCP is appropriate here: we need reliable delivery (lost votes are unacceptable), and we have a defined set of participants rather than a broadcast audience. Messages are ASCII text lines, terminated by `\r\n`, in the style of SMTP or HTTP/1.0 — easy to debug with telnet and easy to parse.
>
> ___
>
> ##### Roles
>
> - **Proposer**: the client that creates the vote
> - **Voter**: an authorized participant (the proposer may also be a voter)
> - **Server**: maintains vote state, enforces rules, and distributes results
>
> ___
>
> #### Message Format
>
> Each message is a line of the form:
>
> `COMMAND [ARG1 [ARG2 ...]] \r\n`
>
> Multi-line data (answer lists, voter lists) are sent as a sequence of lines terminated by a line containing only `.` — again, familiar from SMTP.
>
> ___
>
> ##### Creating a Vote
>
> The proposer connects and issues:
>
> `VOTE "Should we order pizza?" 2007-11-15T17:00:00`  
> `alice@cs.example.edu`  
> `bob@cs.example.edu`  
> `carol@cs.example.edu`  
> `.`  
> `Yes`  
> `No`  
> `Maybe`  
> `.`
>
> Fields, in order: the `VOTE` command, the question in quotes, an expiration timestamp (ISO 8601), a `.`\-terminated list of authorized voter identities, and a `.`\-terminated list of answer options.
>
> The server replies with a unique vote ID and an acknowledgment:
>
> `OK VOTEID 8a3f9c`
>
> or an error:
>
> `ERR Invalid expiration time`
>
> ___
>
> ##### Authentication
>
> Voters connect and authenticate before doing anything else:
>
> `AUTH alice@cs.example.edu s3cr3tpassword`
>
> The server replies `OK` or `ERR`. Authentication is deliberately kept simple here; a real deployment might substitute a challenge-response scheme or TLS client certificates — but those are above the scope of this class so far.
>
> ___
>
> ##### Casting a Vote
>
> An authenticated voter requests the available votes:
>
> `LIST`
>
> The server returns one line per pending vote for which this user is authorized:
>
> `ITEM 8a3f9c "Should we order pizza?" 2007-11-15T17:00:00`  
> `END`
>
> The voter then casts their ballot:
>
> `CAST 8a3f9c 1`
>
> where the argument is the 1-based index of their chosen answer. The server replies `OK` or `ERR` (e.g., if the vote ID is invalid, the user already voted, or the vote has expired). The server records only the answer index — **not** which user submitted it — enforcing the secret ballot property. Because the server discards the voter→answer mapping after marking the voter as "has voted," neither the server operator nor any later audit can reconstruct who voted for what.
>
> ___
>
> ##### Result Notification
>
> The server watches two conditions:
>
> 1. **All voters have responded**: immediately broadcast results to all still-connected authorized clients.
> 2. **Expiration time reached**: broadcast results to all connected authorized clients, noting any non-voters.
>
> The broadcast message:
>
> `RESULT 8a3f9c "Should we order pizza?"`  
> `WINNER 2 "No" 2`  
> `TALLY 1 "Yes" 1`  
> `TALLY 2 "No" 2`  
> `TALLY 3 "Maybe" 0`  
> `END`  
>
> `WINNER` names the option with the most votes (in a tie, the server picks the lowest-index tied option — a defined tiebreak rule avoids ambiguity). `TALLY` lines give all counts so clients can display a full breakdown.
>
> For clients that have disconnected before results are finalized, the result is stored server-side so the client can retrieve it on next connection via a `RESULTS` command — similar to how email is held for offline recipients.
>
> ___
>
> ##### Summary of Commands
>
> | Sender | Command | Purpose |
> | --- | --- | --- |
> | Proposer | `VOTE` | Create a new vote |
> | Client | `AUTH` | Authenticate |
> | Client | `LIST` | Enumerate pending votes |
> | Client | `CAST` | Submit a ballot |
> | Server | `OK` | Success acknowledgment |
> | Server | `ERR` | Error with description |
> | Server | `RESULT` | Broadcast final results |
> | Client | `RESULTS` | Retrieve stored results |
>
> ___
>
> ##### A Few Design Choices Worth Noting
>
> - **TCP over UDP**: the reliability requirement and the stateful authentication/session model make TCP the clear choice. There's no meaningful benefit to UDP here.
> - **ASCII over binary**: debuggability during development outweighs the small efficiency gain of binary encoding for a protocol at this scale.
> - **Voter identity vs. ballot secrecy**: the server knows _who has voted_ (to track completion) but not _how they voted_. This mirrors a real paper ballot: the poll worker checks your name off the list, but your folded ballot goes anonymously into the box.
> - **Persistent connection for push notifications**: because the server needs to push `RESULT` messages asynchronously, clients maintain their TCP connection. This is simpler than polling and avoids the latency of clients discovering results late.

Yikes, even *after* I removed the line praising this as a fun exercise.

### Comparisons

I had my suspicions about which direction that this would swing, but didn't think that it'd go so far in that direction.  I hope that you notice the difference, even if you only skimmed it.  The LLM chose a polished, "professional-looking" presentation instead of explaining its intent **as instructed**.  It read the question and landed on a goal of penning an Internet [Request for Comments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Request_for_Comments), rather than showing any comprehension of the relevant material.  It writes *for posterity*, despite having nothing to say, while not caring about the purpose (social contract?) of a question like this, that we want to start a discussion.  Software work centers on making trade-offs, so if we don't have those discussions because the first answer looked authoritative, then we don't know what trade-offs we plan to make.

Its answer doesn't run much longer than mine (seven hundred words instead of five hundred), but it breaks everything into sections *and* breaks its "document" into fake-pages, which feels exhausting for such a short piece.  And when it asserts that "voters connect and authenticate before doing anything else," for example, it doesn't provide any reason to manage "sessions" instead of having the client authenticate with each request.  It might have a great reason for all we know[^PfCsNY], but would rather cosplay as a brainy student who has read ahead in the textbook, as in the challenge/response and TLS certificate comment, providing code segments as if they clarify anything from the text, and trying to coin jargon ("tiebreak" and "debuggability" stand out as trying desperately to sound smart).

[^PfCsNY]:  More likely, it pattern-matched the problem to something like e-mail and copied the IMAP protocol or something similar.  But the possibility of a fantastic reason does exist, and I don't want to give the impression that I don't like the answer because it came to a different conclusion than I did.

It reminds me of, back when I tutored math students, programmable calculators got many of them in the bad habit of reading the question, entreating the spirits of the Dark Box for a while, and then writing a single-digit number on the board with a box around it.  Not only did they invariably get the answer wrong, but by not showing their work, I had no idea where they needed help.  In the same way, when a student answered along the lines that you see in the LLM answer---and many in that first category of student *loved* to dazzle with professional structure and detail in hopes of distracting from their own non-answers---I couldn't give advice on what they might want to review to shore up their understanding, only give them a poor grade and move on.

Part of me wanted to try this experiment a few more times on other subjects---I have plenty of exam questions for algorithms, programming languages, databases, operating systems, and a few other classes---but I don't know if I can tolerate any more of that without breaking out into hives.  Plus, this trial makes my point well enough, that we only need AI-detection algorithms and rules preventing usage if we assign work more aligned with the LLM skill-set than human capability.

## Broader Themes

These results also *maybe* give some insight into why study after study shows that adults using LLMs in their work get worse at their jobs while students who use it don't develop those skills at all.  By presenting the formalized and professionalized structure as a substitute for reasoning and argument, and when people approve of that content-free work, it signals that nobody values the actual skill in a task, only the presentation.  And we know that, to paraphrase [Campbell's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campbell%27s_law), when people judge work by metrics, the metrics *replace* the work as the goal, so it makes sense to stop valuing the valuable part of the job.

I don't really want this to *only* become another post warning about AI, though.  Again, I presented my solution to cheating with laptops above, and verified that it still mostly works today, with the same caveats of taking more work from the instructors.  To me, that largely solves that part of the problem, if anybody wanted to listen to me:  Stop asking students to fill voids of specific shape with meaningless words or to repeat factual information, because they'll absolutely cheat, and you can't tell who did, because the computer does a fantastic job of those two things.  It hasn't changed in at least thirty years, and it probably didn't change much in the thirty years before that, or the thirty years before those; only the shape of the cheat-enabling technology has changed, not the problem, and not the solution.

That gets to where this post probably needs to go, then.  Why do we keep getting this wrong?  We all needed to deal with this sort of nonsense as a student.  Nobody likes to do it.  Nobody likes to slog through reviewing and grading it.  Multiple choice tests post-1970 tests at least have the (weak) justification that the instructor can take a nap while the machine grades a stack of papers, and maybe that speed and scalability outweighs the lack of learning happening.  But the rest of this nonsense?

## What Does It Do

I have the fragment of a post in my drafts-folder speaking about this more globally, but we really need to ask ourselves what we *want* out of education.

Year after year, powerful people talk about how the world needs to replace this curriculum or that form of testing, and we toss a coin to decide which we'll take their advice on.  And then we wonder why students don't do better by some new or old metric, teeing up the next powerful person to recommend a change.

Never, in any of that, have I heard anybody describe a *goal* for the education system.  And if we don't have a goal, something that we want to give every student who passes through and/or society at large, then we'll have a heck of a time getting to that goal.

Throughout my life, school has served a variety of purposes.

- Mass childcare; from morning to mid-afternoon on weekdays, nobody needs to care what minors over the age of five or six have gotten up to, because somebody sent them to school.
- Socialization; children learn to deal with each other, even the creeps.
- Social stratification; even though we like to imagine schools as mostly average students with occasional niche interests that cause them to excel, in reality, by high school, we generally have advanced classes populated consistently by the privileged, minimal classes for the less privileged, and everybody else, with only occasional overlap.
- Vocational training; students learn enough to cope with office jobs.
- Cultural enrichment; students learn a bit of everything, and sometimes inspiration strikes them.
- Civic pride; bringing the entire age cohort together fosters a group identity.
- College preparation; many classes teach skills to help get into a decent college.

I have probably missed at least a few, but the list raises questions.  Do we want these?  Should we prioritize any of these over the others?  Should we get rid of a few?  Does some unified theory exist that encompasses a few of them?  When I say that schooling needs a goal, I mean that list gets in the way of actual reform, because it shows that schools mostly...exist.  They do whatever they did when we went to school, which it got from continuing what happened when our parents went to school, and then grandparents, and then as far back as our families might have attended.

### Vulnerabilities

I have some beliefs[^8UVrH1], but my personal preferences don't make goals.  And that lack of goals helps to explain the persistence of vestigial rituals such as those aforementioned five-paragraph essays.

[^8UVrH1]:  Personally, in the long and storied tradition of childless white men mucking around with public education, I would focus on what I call cultural enrichment and socialization---showing young people as many options as possible and forcing them to get along---and make everything else subservient to those two goals.  But like I said, "childless white man."

We don't know what we want, but we know that, at some point in history, someone (maybe even a smart person) brought in the idea of the five-paragraph essay to judge how well students...did...something.  I don't know.  I do know that the form means that any dolt can grade them---look for the thesis statement, check that each of the middle paragraphs supports the thesis, and that the paragraph uses the middle paragraphs to confirm the thesis---and the form sounds science-y, I guess, so it probably has some legitimate use.  Right?  We can all see that line of reasoning, because nobody has any way of saying "OK, but assigning the essays gets us further away from The Goal."

And this also might explain how technologies like LLMs so quickly infiltrate academia.  Without a tangible goal, you can only argue about the badness of cheating and whether the new technology qualifies as cheating at all.  If you say "this technology violates the entire purpose of schooling," then you need to back that up with a purpose that *everybody in the room* agrees with.  If you don't get full agreement, then you have proven that it probably doesn't violate anything.  You end up sounding like the people who railed against spell-checkers and calculators, wringing their hands that students would never learn to balance a checkbook[^ykyDQj].

[^ykyDQj]:  You...add the numbers, right?  I never saw the point.  Either more money goes in than comes out and balancing can wait, or more comes out than goes in, and you have far bigger concerns than the accuracy of addition.  However, I do note that nobody complained about banks having *websites* where you can look at your transaction history, something that literally does make balancing a checkbook obsolete, since they've already done it.

## What Next

Like other posts in this vein over the years, I don't have complete answers---if I did, I'd go implement them instead of nibbling at the edges---but hope that sketching out a vague shape of a solution can inspire people to take the next steps, and so on, until we actually have solutions.

First, as you can probably guess, we need that solid vision of what we want schooling to actually do.  I might have it on my list of things that school actually does.  I may have overlooked it on the list.

Then, we need to start thinking about everything *in* schooling and how it relates to that goal or those goals.  If they help get closer to the goal, then improve them.  If they don't, maybe discard them.

Last year, I started at this from a different angle, [building a parallel system]({% post_url 2025-05-04-30m-free-education %}), reproducing the motivation to learn even the stuff that a student doesn't enjoy, the exposure to a wide variety of topics, the social connections that become professional connections, the credentials, and a progressive curriculum.  In some ways, it might make more sense to start here, not because it has more impact, but because we know *how* to find and collect interesting material, and only need to put in the labor to do it.
