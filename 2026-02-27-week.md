>

Pardon me if I've said this before, probably multiple times, but if you find Mastodon difficult to deal with because you have different relationships with different people, you can tame it to a great extent with lists.  I have about half a dozen lists, right now.  For example, I have the people who I genuinely know from outside the Internet, because I don't want to miss whatever they have going.  The Cohost list gives me some sense of preserving that community.  I keep the news outlets corralled.

I don't yet, but you *could* take the people who you feel obliged to follow but don't find interesting and put them on their own list.  Check the box to remove them from your main feed, then...never look at the list, and they become much quieter.

Anyway, as people identify themselves with a like/favorite/whatever, I can click through to their profile and make sure that I have them on the right list.

{% embed https://images.jacobinmag.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/19110740/GettyImages-1158051420.jpg|Gloria Cruz (left) and Jack (no last name given) talk with newsmen on July 14, 1970, about why the Young Lords took over the old nurses' residence at Lincoln Hospital in the Bronx|false| %}

<i class="fas fa-square"></i> [Reassessing the People’s Hospital in the Bronx](https://jacobin.com/2026/02/bronx-lincoln-hospital-young-lords/) from Jacobin

> Lincoln Detox, which began as the People’s Program following the November occupation and was later incorporated into the hospital system with city funding after negotiations with administrators, treated addiction not as an individual moral failure but as inseparable from poverty, racism, housing instability, policing, and economic neglect. It hired people in recovery as peer counselors. It offered childcare. It paired medical treatment with political education. It pioneered acupuncture protocols that would later spread nationally. At its height, it served roughly 1,600 people a month.

It came a bit before my time, but I (vaguely) remember people still arguing over the People's Hospital all through my childhood, to give you some idea of its influence on the public imagination.
