## Recipes

Technically, I suppose that I could have inserted this in last week's post when I gave it a first try, especially since that post didn't have much heft to it at all, but I didn't care for how it turned out, so I wanted to give it another shot.

{% cook 1|Air Fryer Bread %}
Combine @flour{1%cup}, @yeast{1%tsp}, @salt{1/4%tsp}, and @sugar{1/2%tsp} in a bowl.

Stir in @water{1/2%cup} (about 110°F/43 C) and @olive%oil{1%Tbsp} to get a shaggy dough.  If it sticks, add a teaspoon or so of flour.  If it seems too dry, add a teaspoon or so of water.

Knead the dough for ~{5%min} until elastic and smooth.  Form it into a ball and let it rise in the mixing bowl, covered with a towel for ~{15%min}.

Reshape the dough if desired, and move it to the air fryer basket on a sheet of parchment paper.

Set the air fryer for 330°F (165 C) and bake for ~{15%min}, or golden brown on top and a hollow sound when tapped.  Check later in the bake and cover the top with foil if it browns too fast.

Flip the loaf and bake for another ~{5%min} or until the underside doesn't look and feel raw.

Let rest for ~{10%min}.
{% endcook %}

A smart person would probably use instant or rapid-rise yeast, but I still have a significant amount of the active dry yeast that I bought from Costco something like twenty years ago and threw in the freezer, and so went with that.  I imagine that sourdough starter would also work, but I haven't developed that kind of discipline...

A handful of things might warrant clarification, especially for non-bakers.

- Bet on needing to adjust the baking times.  Even normal ovens vary wildly, so small, cheaply made convection ovens probably vary even more.  Check before the fifteen-minute timer expires (knock lightly on the crust with a fingernail or utensil to see if you get a hollow sound) and make your best judgment.  The same goes for the underside, where I'd bet that my device does a worse job of getting hot air underneath than most.
- To get warm water, you could get away with the tap if you don't need to filter your local water, but I used an electric kettle to boil the minimum and mix it with cold water, slightly less hot than cold.  If you go too hot, it kills the yeast; too cold, and the yeast doesn't metabolize the sugar fast enough.
- At this size, kneading takes almost no space or technique.  I picked the dough up, pushed in opposite directions with each hand (analogous to pushing along a counter), folded the stretched result over, and turned the folded dough to repeat the process, so one could walk a bit instead of hunching over a counter.  Again, if it sticks, it needs a bit more flour mixed in, anyway.

The mini-loaf requires a bit of care when slicing to not flatten the slices into what looks like soft biscotti---the crust feels somewhere between a buttermilk biscuit and a baguette with a soft interior---but the slices held up fairly well for what I wanted.  I made myself an eggplant sandwich, as in eggplant slices that I broiled and froze a few weeks ago, then reheated in a pan, plus crushed garlic and Parmesan cheese, and a snack of the "heel."  The next morning, it became toast for peanut butter.  And a second dinner buttered the remaining (probably too much) on the side of whatever I actually had as the meal.  The second loaf became bread for a bowl of spinach and peanuts, toast with eggs, and so forth.

Importantly, this means that I can throw together bread without firing up the oven, and also without needing to *plan* to have bread.  Normally, I make no-knead half-loaves, and usually leave the dough to sit for almost twenty-four hours (mixing the dough after dinner on the previous night), great for putting minimal effort in, but bad for spontaneously deciding that I want bread.  This recipe sits squarely in "that'd make a good sandwich if I bought bread" territory, mostly baking as I otherwise cook.

## Mini-Server, Part 25

Maybe worth mentioning, I dropped [YaCy](https://yacy.net/) from my server, because it throws an error on searching, which feels like it maybe defeats the purpose.  I might normally consider investigating, but the project has added ChatGPT as its official support, and the forum has an entire thread where the project lead excitedly talks about using AI saving so much trouble, and how it does a pretty good job if you ignore all the problems. {% emoji person facepalming %}

It feels like a huge shame, because I don't believe that we have *any* other independent, non-niche[^sxOdOd] search index, but I can't help assuming that they imagine pushing towards using LLMs to make choices, not away.

[^sxOdOd]:  Yes, I know that the personal-site search engines exist, for example, but that doesn't help for general searches where I *might* need somebody's blog, but might also need corporate documentation, a product, or a social media post.

For "posterity," I should note that these problems come at a fairly bad time.  While I don't generally use Google, most of you know that they recently announced their nosedive, doing what they can to avoid having users leave their site.  Somehow, they envision themselves as a "destination" where you stare at ads while it pretends to give you useful information.  Alongside this, I notice that Duck Duck Go's search results have gotten worse over the past year or so, presumably due to the search providers that they use restricting access, meaning that every other "Silicon Valley search, but without the ads" service will probably get worse, too.  That makes this a terrible time for YaCy to want to integrate LLMs *and* have quality issues.  Maybe [mwmbl](https://mwmbl.org/) has a fully independent index?  I haven't investigated them more than superficially.

Also, I managed to get [Beehive](https://github.com/muesli/beehive) running on the x86-based server after utterly failing to get it to run properly on a Raspberry Pi.  Notably, it does need some command-line arguments to get it to work remotely.

```
beehive -bind "0.0.0.0:8181" -canonicalurl "http://server-name.local:8181"
```

I'll need to read more to see if the "canonical URL" can let it serve from a subfolder---as in `http://server-name.local/beehive`, rather than port 8181---at some point.  And I'll need to review the actual features, to see if it can automate anything that I would actually do.  It seems nice, though, and I have had it on my list of projects to try out for a while...
