## Battle Lines

For context, the franchise hadn't aired anything since [*Vortex*]({% post_url 2026-05-14-vortex %}), but this then aired shortly before [*The Chase*]({% post_url 2025-04-03-chase %}).

And continuing a bit of a trend, don't expect much out of this episode, since we spend what I'd call far too much energy on the situation on the novel planet.  Back in the mists of history, shows like this needed to establish themselves as "serious science fiction" before people would except a main cast with some mild diversity.  You know, completely unlike the reaction to **Discovery** and **Starfleet Academy**... {% emoji eyeroll %}

 > **SISKO**: So they wanted us to find these.

In fact, that seems...important, maybe?  The Cardassians trashed the station when they left after decades of hauling out every resource that they could extract from the planet.  I see no chance that they created this for any reason *other* than stir up rivalries for the incoming government.

 > **O'BRIEN**: You might want to warn Kira before she sees her file.

Look, I love the idea that billions of Bajorans all believe that they each played such a pivotal role in the resistance that their every mention in official files would include paragraphs of raging profanity.  However, given the breakdown of O'Brien's lines so far, I can't see this as anything other than a follow-up to his "have you ever *served* with any Bajoran woman...?" question in [*Emissary*]({% post_url 2026-02-26-emissary-1 %}).

 > **SISKO**: Kai Opaka. Why would she be here?

This seems like the third episode in a row treating clear situations like some arcane mystery.  Gosh, why *would* the Bajoran religious leader want to spend time at the facility nearest the location from which the majority of her religion's supernatural lore derives?  Like, if you provably found the [Garden of Eden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garden_of_Eden) at your local elementary school playground, you're going to expect some prominent Abrahamic religious leaders to show up from time to time.  Think that, but *far* more intense.  It actually surprises me that they don't have constant traffic through the place.

Also, she seems fun, so maybe pipe down and enjoy the visit?

Speaking of which, did I point to the late [Camille Saviola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camille_Saviola)'s background during the pilot?

 > **SISKO**: I didn't want to keep you from your duties, Doctor.
 >
 > **BASHIR**: It's a slow day.

Did he...patiently wait for somebody to acknowledge his potential involvement?  Awkward, but I didn't expect character growth this early...

That said, I won't bother to quote the boring couple of lines, but you might notice that Bashir hasn't grown in his character's other main dimension, in that he treats this as a fun excursion to bond with the Kai, rather than indulging the closest thing to a local head of state in what amounts to a religious pilgrimage.

 > **O'BRIEN**: I do. I have a little girl, ma'am.
 >
 > **OPAKA**: Yes. Would you give this to her for me?

Does this necklace ever show up again?  I ask, because it seems important beyond the in-episode foreshadowing.  From a character perspective, you'd think that the single person who (as the pilot told us) the overwhelming majority of Bajorans respect and choose to follow would provoke more of a reaction from even O'Brien than a weak "party on" smile as he acts like she handed him a novelty rubber chicken.  Longer term, though, the family will become the faces of colonial (cultural) genocide for the season finale, and you might think that "but we honor the Kai, not least because the Kai honored our daughter" feels like it could start to defuse that problem, or at least an attempt that could've backfired.

More to the point, though, I don't believe that we ever see it again, making me wonder if O'Brien tossed it in a crate somewhere, on the basis that he didn't want some alien necklace influencing his daughter or something.

To put it all another way:  What the Hell does this scene have to do with anything, other than weakly foreshadowing that Opaka doesn't plan to return?  We could've spent more time on the Cardassian files, had the Kai grab some earlobes, had some antics at the bar...

 > **KIRA**: Not at all, Opaka. You honor us with your presence.

This strikes me as...interesting, I guess?  Probably not important, but Sisko seems mildly annoyed at Kira jumping in, even though she probably does have a right to jump in, given that the Bajoran government has a massive stake in seeing the Kai happy, *and* you probably can't stop her from going full-on fan-girl.  But it might foreshadow Sisko's arc of trying to distance himself from Bajoran religion.

 > **SISKO**: The other side of the galaxy, to be precise. The Gamma quadrant is seventy thousand light years from Bajor. It would take our fastest starship over sixty-seven years to get here.

He says this with the clarity that sounds like they'll quiz us on the factoid later, or that we'll need to slog through an entire seven-year series where the plot will revolve around that data point plus or minus a few thousand light years...

 > **KIRA**: Point-three-five light years, bearing two-two-nine mark four-one.

I almost said that it almost sounds criminal not to investigate at that distance and direction---twenty-two degrees (probably) clockwise and forty-one degrees up comes pretty close to right in front of them---but I should do the math, since Sisko kindly gave us a benchmark to work with.  If the fastest ship would take sixty-seven years to travel seventy thousand light-years, then that comes to a bit over a thousand years (slightly less than 1,045) light-years per year, or a bit less than three light-years per day.

First and unexpectedly, this seems absurdly low.  As I have mentioned before, in [**First Contact**]({% post_url 2025-12-18-first-contact-1 %}), Picard tells/will tell Sloane that the Federation includes a hundred fifty planets across eight thousand light years.  If Picard meant that as a diameter, then it takes about eight years to traverse the Federation, shipping, travel, and so forth, which seems to not match anything that we've seen.

More relevant to this line, though, thirty-five hundredths of a light year would take three hours in each direction.  As I've mentioned before, a "runabout" traditionally means a powered boat seating four-to-eight people meant more for agility than speed or distance, meaning that it doesn't move as fast as those "fastest ships."  In twenty-first century recreational sports, think of the class of boat that you'd picture pulling water-skiers, not the "cigarette boat" that you'd see used by TV smugglers.  But it also seems weird that Sisko doesn't point this out---well over a six-hour diversion---as an added reason not to drag the Kai along for the day.

 > **KIRA**: Shields are down. Forward thrusters are gone. We're losing power.

Maybe they should stop taking these things through the wormhole.  I mean, sure, it'd probably feel embarrassing to pay for trips on other ships going through, but [*Vortex*]({% post_url 2026-05-14-vortex %}) last week similarly indicated that they didn't build them to deal with any unexpected hassle.

 > **ODO**: But they're nearly three hours overdue.
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: Three and a half.

It leads to their eventual rescue, but it seems shocking that nobody thought to send a message back to the station to announce their maybe-a-day-or-two side-excursion.  OK, sure, we probably have some episode where they'll explicitly deploy some cross-wormhole communication gadget that they don't have yet, but they could surely send one of those probes, at least.

 > **O'BRIEN**: Constable, tell them we're doing the best we can, okay? Runabout pad C.

Hey, why does *O'Brien* get to call Odo "constable"...?

 > **DAX**: Have the Bajorans station a ship on the Gamma side, we'll stay in close touch by subspace.

Never mind about the cross-wormhole communication thing, because apparently they already could've sent a message directly...

 > **SHEL-LA**: Our first visitors. You're not all the same race.

You might recognize Shel-La as a fairly young [Jonathan Banks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jonathan_Banks), probably mostly known then for comedy and increasingly as calculating criminals.

 > **O'BRIEN**: Not even a transponder signal. If they went down, we should at least get something from their transponder. The warp eddy has traces of meson particle emissions. That makes it a Starfleet power reactor.

In nature, cosmic rays striking matter will generally emit some mesons, but they decay quickly into electrons and neutrinos or photons, so I don't know if I buy that they detected a bunch, hours later.

 > **DAX**: An uncharted binary star system.

Didn't it have *three* stars when Bashir described it...?

 > **BASHIR**: His readings are the same as Opaka's. Virtually every cell in his body shows signs of this biomechanical presence. Now somehow it has literally brought him back to life.

I mean...he claimed to have brought someone back from the dead personally, shortly before [*The Passenger*]({% post_url 2026-04-23-passenger %}), so I don't know if we can trust his "literally[^0-okPa]," there.

[^0-okPa]:  Totally unrelated but a story that I never get to tell, probably about a decade ago, I went for a walk on a small local beach on an overcast day, and overheard a teenager announce to their friends that they had *literally* never seen clearer skies.  No laughs, so I don't think that they meant it as a joke, so the moment still haunts me because I have no idea how they got there, even assuming new and exciting definitions that mean non-literal things.  And I feel similarly about Bashir's line, here.

By the way, we apparently still need to answer this question.  For example, if you can [restore "some functions to intact brains from deceased donors"](https://www.science.org/content/article/not-alive-not-dead-disembodied-human-brains-used-drug-testing), can you actually call the donor "deceased"?  And if you can't call them deceased, can you call them donors...?

 > **SISKO**: Major, this is not our---

Kira doesn't need to follow the Prime Directive, though, does she?

 > **SISKO**: We'd be willing to transport all of you away from here if that's what you want.

Wait, what happened to "not our war," like ten seconds ago?  It seems like the right call to me, but I also don't see how it differs legally from Kira's offer.

 > **O'BRIEN**: What the hell would they be doing here anyway. It's a waste of time. They could be twenty light years from here, and we wouldn't have the first clue.

For those of you who missed my arithmetic fugue state earlier, it would take them at *least* a week to travel twenty light years, probably several, so they probably couldn't have gotten that far unless he spent a hilariously long time reinventing the tare button for his fancy compass.

Don't look at me like that.  The *episode* brought all this up, not me.  Sisko had an entire, carefully enunciated line telling us how fast their ships travel.

 > **BASHIR**: Isn't that a bit like assisting a jailbreak?
 >
 > **SISKO**: I don't need you to interpret the prime directive for me, Doctor.

It does seem worth raising the issue, though, even if they come to the consensus that they should actually do this.

 > **BASHIR**: Yes, of course. I only meant---
 >
 > **SISKO**: Whatever crimes they've committed they've paid for a hundred times over. They've been altered somehow and been abandoned by their world. I believe the Federation would recognize them as separate and unique.

Well, wait, now he has me *opposing* this plan.  If they qualify as separate from their home culture and unique, then the Prime Directive seems to absolutely apply, because the law appears to want to protect the uniqueness of indigenous groups.  Sisko has turned this from a defensible jailbreak to literal ethnic cleansing, somehow, interfering with a pre-warp culture to remove them from their homes...

 > **ZLANGCO**: So, you are the leader of the aliens.

Less recognizable, but if Zlangco rings a bell---other than a name that sounds like an evil corporation from a cartoon---[Paul Collins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Collins_%28actor%29) plays him, *best* known for voicing John Darling in Disney's **Peter Pan** adaptation as a child (so you probably don't recognize him *from* that), but with a smattering of other credits, such as the Treasury Secretary in **Dave** at around this time.

 > **SISKO**: The Federation is made up of over a hundred planets who have allied themselves for mutual scientific, cultural and defensive benefits. The mission that my people and I are on is to explore the galaxy.

Wait, since when?  I could've sworn that they assigned him to lead administrative duties on the station so that the Bajoran provisional government doesn't tear itself apart dealing with the optics of keeping a former death camp running.  Sure, he can't tell them that he took the day off to hang out with Space [Pope Joan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pope_Joan) and only reluctantly agreed to check out the [OTB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Off-track_betting) feed---reading between the lines that the satellites streamed a bunch of statistical data earlier but not since---but it feels like a huge exaggeration to suggest that he explores the galaxy for a living...

 > **DAX**: Is there any way for us to get a comm. line through the field?
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: Well, I've got one percent to work with, don't I?

Do you get to *choose* which one percent gets through, though?  I don't think that you do...

 > **SISKO**: But those satellites will come after you if you get too close. They're armed. One of them cut us down without any trouble at all.
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: Yeah, we figured as much.

Who talks to the boss like that?  "Yeah, only a complete fool would get caught in this kind of trap."  Maybe this explains why he went from an ensign, sometimes a lieutenant, early in **The Next Generation** to a non-commissioned officer here...

 > **DAX**: Maybe a photon would destroy one of the satellites.

Sure, and maybe that *kills everybody* by shutting down their nanites.

 > **BASHIR**: Commander, I find myself caught in a moral dilemma. As much as I am dedicated to the preservation of life, I wonder if we shouldn't help these people end this torture.

While it does seem worth raising this option, or literally raising the option of beaming them off the planet to kill them for that matter, given Sisko's insistence on their becoming a novel and unique culture, it *does* sound a lot like genocide.  Why does this century dig genocide so much...?

I can't find a video, so please forgive any errors in my memory, but a while back, Maria Bamford had a bit about talking through her darker thoughts with her therapist.  It (as you would expect) escalates, until she talks about wanting "to genocide" random individuals who inconvenienced her.  This always feels like that, but less charming.

## Conclusions

As mentioned, the episode spends most of its energy on navigating the plot, so we don't have too much to work with.

### The Good

Bashir has started learning to read the room.

### The Bad

Somehow, they don't see anything interesting in the Cardassians leaving behind high-level files with little to no security.

Maybe related, they don't seem to understand the fragility of their runabouts.  And similarly, they don't call home when they have a day-long detour on the agenda.

We continue to see what at least rhymes with O'Brien's racism.

Likewise, all the Federation citizens seem completely disinterested in Bajoran culture.  Sisko initially treats the Kai---whose support he knows that the Federation needs---like an inconvenience, then seemingly bristles at Kira's devotion.  O'Brien acts like a gift from her to his daughter mostly inconveniences him.  Even Bashir acts more like he got invited on a road trip than a symbolic activity.

O'Brien and Bashir also seem bad at their jobs, making assertions that require an in-universe redefinition of plain-English words to make sense.  O'Brien also implies that Sisko and Kira acted gullibly by letting the satellites attack their runabout.

To the extent that the episode has something to say about culture, though, it all lands on the Prime Directive.  Somehow, they can't interfere in a prison gang war despite the danger to themselves, but they can offer to relocate the entire population.  Yet Sisko defends his decision specifically in a way that sounds like the ethnic cleansing of a unique population, seemingly the literal thing that the Prime Directive wants to avoid.

Bashir, out of nowhere, also casually suggests genocide as an option, reprogramming the healing nanites to let the prisoners kill each other, after discovering that the jailbreak won't work.

### The Weird

Sisko, at least, seems to pitch his role as equivalent to exploring the galaxy, not handling paperwork at a decommissioned labor camp turned spaceport.
