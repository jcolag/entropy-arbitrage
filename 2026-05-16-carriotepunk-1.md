## Carriotepunk

The book's page has the following description.

> Double récit sur le thème de l’errance, Carriotepunk est une nouvelle qui se promène sur des territoires post-capitalistes fictifs, où éoliennes à tambours et panneaux solaires alimentent les communautés qui ont fait de l’entraide une règle fondamentale. Un moment de lecture sous forme de voyage paisible, en suivant d’un côté le parcours d’un enfant recueilli sur la route, et en parallèle celui d’une jeune femme nomade qui a choisi d’aller se rendre utile de l’autre côté de la montagne, avec un chariot solaire pour bagage.
{: lang="fr" }

In English, that translates to this.

> Carriotepunk is a double story on the theme of wandering, and it tells a new story that travels through fictional post-capitalist territories, where drum wind turbines and solar panels feed communities that have made mutual aid a fundamental rule. A moment of reading in the form of a peaceful journey, following on one side the journey of a child gathered on the road, and in parallel that of a young nomadic woman who chose to go to make herself useful on the other side of the mountain, with a solar cart for luggage.

I saw the author announce the first seven chapters in rapid succession in March, but it doesn't look like it has moved since then.  For now, then, we'll need to look at it as an introductory short story, and hope that the author hasn't forgotten the rest of the story.

Oh, and as usual, for the sake of expediency, I chose to read this via machine translation, so I can't guarantee that some nuance or depth won't get lost.

## What Works Well?

These chapters sketch out some aspects of a world that feels more thoroughly planned than many of the stories that we've seen setting up similar post-collapse societies.

For the people looking for this sort of thing, I'd argue that this comes closer to the spirit of solarpunk than I have generally seen, too.  We start with the off-handed comment that something forced society to get rid of money, but then we get the broad outlines of necessities covered by some distributed mechanism (to describe later, I suppose), with anybody wanting to engage more deeply than that joining cooperatives or exchanging hours of service as currency.

## What Works...Less Well?

{% include lesswell.md %}

Much of the story so far feels scattered.  For one example that stands out, we have a scene where one of our protagonists (Amarille) spends a few minutes with teenage tinkerer Gloria.  Somewhere in this conversation, which seems to include introductions between people who already know each other, somebody asks about Doctor Baboune, and...the name never comes up again.  Does that refer to Amarille's full name?  Does Gloria have a nickname?  Do they both know someone else by that name?  Some languages have used a similar term for a baboon (no *e* at the end), but French uses *babouin*, so it doesn't work as that sort of joke, leaving me pretty much lost.  And like I said, that mostly works as the quickest example to explain, but similar incidents happen elsewhere.

Similarly, we have several incidents where the book builds to some large event, only to blow off anything that might have happened there.  It often feels like it would rather rush us to the next scene than finish the current scene.

## Opportunities

I don't see anything, beyond leaving polite and encouraging comments.

## What's Adaptable?

We get a handful of names that I can't find elsewhere, and so presumably represent new places, such as the Télécom cooperative networking villages, the White Forest with petrified trees, Acratia, the village of Rivebraux with its assembly, and so forth.  And some items don't get specific names, but will presumably become important later in the story, such as Amarille's electric cart, the octagonal ring that she gives Gloria, and the bioluminescent mycelium lighting the assembly space.

## Six Years Hence:  Jathia's Wager

Continuing the look back at the early Free Culture Book Club posts, six years ago today, we talked about [**Jathia's Wager**]({% post_url 2020-05-16-jathia %}), the (probably) **Matrix**-inspired meditation on...something that I couldn't really identify.

To my knowledge, nobody has really touched the film since its release, beyond occasionally mentioning it as available for adaptation.  Personally, I *nearly* started writing a story to contribute to anthology, and that might have used the film as a springboard, but the overall project seems to have vanished shortly after the announcement and I didn't get further than collecting stories that might serve as a viable basis, so that doesn't count.

Since the post, however, I found another Free Culture project by the same creator, the **Mob Mentality** "series," only two episodes (and an alternate pilot), which I can't seem to find online anymore.  When I start revisiting creators, I'll put more effort into tracking that down, assuming that I don't already have it archived somewhere waiting for upload.

Looking back at the behind-the-scenes of the post itself, part of me wonders...what possessed me to cover two films back-to-back?  Since then, I think that I have taken care to vary the medium as much as possible.  And the dearth of Free Culture (fictional) films led to worries early on about trying to scrape up the occasional short story.  On the other hand, maybe this presents another sign that I didn't expect the posts to happen regularly for six years, and so I dropped whatever seemed convenient.  Or maybe I figured that asking people to invest in actual reading that early on would drive more people away than it convinced to read any of the posts...

And a lot like I said about **Orang-U** last week, I would probably never call **Jathia's Wager** a *good* film, but I "appreciate the hustle," as they say, and imagine that an audience exists for it to connect with.  However, I also don't believe that anything about it stands out nearly as much as the placeholder commercials, which I don't think that any other project has attempted[^qZLAdU], though Terry Hancock [speculated on formalizing sponsorship](https://jcolag.codeberg.page/qco/170-advertising-revenue.html) shortly after the film's release.

[^qZLAdU]:  I have old notes on a possible comic book that would run ads for other Free Culture properties, but (a) that differs substantially from using ads to fund the project, and (b) I gave up on that idea because I could only track down *one* project out there that actually created plausible advertising assets at the time, the soon-to-close [Wikinews](https://en.wikinews.org/wiki/Wikinews:Wikinews_ads).  Several projects have made various branding assets available to (usually student) groups to create advertising for events, but almost nobody has the equivalent of a full-page ad or a poster (let alone a commercial, for anybody considering this in video) ready to go.
