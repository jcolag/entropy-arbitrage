## Privilege

Anyway, to start somewhere, when we talk about male privilege, you probably already know this, but it doesn't mean that all men live lives of luxury.  In this context, privilege means that, in most situations, equalizing for other factors, men will have better outcomes on average.  Applying for a job, men will have an advantage in getting hired over equally qualified women, for example.

This doesn't mean that men don't have problems that derive from sexism, though, and that brings us to the start of our big problem:  Right-wing movements and agents can use these legitimate problems to attract attention.  They quickly point out that few shelters for the victims of abuse accept men.  They bemoan how men feel an obligation to provide for their families.  You can't shut them up about how men make up the overwhelming majority of on-the-job deaths.  All valid problems, and we all know the litany of dozens of others.  If we wear bright colors, if we take care of children not directly related to us, and so forth, people see those and more as a warning sign or other problem.

## Bait and Switch

The political right loves to talk about this class of problems for (I would argue) two reasons.

- For once, it gives them something legitimate to talk about instead of imagining an invasion around every corner or trying to pitch the idea of an aristocracy again.
- It also makes the topic feel toxic to everybody who doesn't also believe in supply side economics or covering up industrialized child abuse or whatever.

When you read that paragraph above, for example, you probably grew uncomfortable as I started talking about shelters and workplace deaths, wondering if you stumbled into the wrong blog.  Because the political right talks about the fictional oppression of white men all the time, legitimate problems start to sound like fake "wedge issues" to rile men up.

This then feeds itself.  Oh-ho, they shout to their followers, notice how the liberals won't talk about this, because they don't care about you.  And then they work on radicalizing those young men in their audience instead of solving *literally any* of their problems.

Suitably conned, those followers become the movement's foot-soldiers, convinced that those issues (again, remember, real issues, here) will get solved soon, but *first* they have a few other chores to take care of.

## Solving Problems

Anyway, when you fill in the gaps of these "reverse sexism" problems, as feminism strives to do, then you start to see that you solve the problems for men by fighting systemic sexism against *women*.  The right still hasn't quite decided if it will [accept women's suffrage {% cc %}](https://msmagazine.com/2025/08/13/pete-hegseth-women-right-to-vote-pastor-suffrage-19th-amendment/), so you know that they won't get behind fixing the wage gap or---gasp!---treating women like actual humans with internal lives of their own.  As such, they don't want to hear about this "fixing men's problems by treating women as equals" sorcery, and certainly don't want *you* hearing about it, because you might realize that fixing it could work.

As an analogous situation, consider the recent right-wing war on [Critical Race Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_race_theory).  CRT says that you can't understand a culture like the United States, especially its power dynamics, without first understanding the centrality of race---a social construct, not a fixed biological trait---in the minds of the most powerful.  If you don't understand that the powerful *deploy the stereotypes* of Black people to prevent their advancement, then little about the economy, policing, incarceration, or voting systems make any sense.  And we can fix these institutions for everybody if we dismantle that racism at the heart of it all, and can't if we don't.  But the political right doesn't *want* to fix those things[^gLG7bB], so they want to wipe out the intellectual framework that enables us to see it all, a first step towards dismantling it.

## The Name Game

I know.  Really, I get it.  Why call it *feminism*, if it helps everybody at once?  And I could give you a handful of possible explanations to varying degrees of satisfaction.

From a historical standpoint, what we think of as feminism today grew from the progressive marginalization of women in the Civil Right's Movement.  Like so many other pre-intersectional movements, the Civil Right's Movement in the United States developed a "one advancement at a time" mentality, and so decided that they could happily use women's labor for the advancement of *racial* equality, then maybe get around to gender equality later.  The women's suffrage movement largely did the same thing to the non-white folks who supported them.  And you can still hear people disparaged as "white feminists" for not embracing intersectionality and implying that other groups need to "wait their turns."  The term feminism, then, underscored that it wouldn't leave women behind.

Thinking descriptively, we fix many of our problems through the root cause of gender equality, *by raising the status of women* to that of men.  The term identifies it as a school of thought centering on (something regarding) women.  As a parallel, consider environmentalism, which refers to the process (minimizing and reducing human-driven environmental damage), but has far broader goals (health, sustainable economies, and more).

In terms of focus, related to the descriptive approach, the name discourages the aforementioned complete crackpot solutions guaranteed to make things worse, such as fixing gender inequality through promoting [tradwife](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tradwife) lifestyles[^As6PTw].  Think in terms of the people who refuse to engage with a certain activist movement because they'd rather say that "all lives matter."  When they do that, they hide the actual problem in their semantic games of refusing to single out a population under siege, erasing that Black people generally have more medical problems, not to mention a higher likelihood of fatal encounters with armed people and the criminal justice system.

[^As6PTw]:  Should you, as a woman, learn and practice classic domestic skills?  Sure.  You should as a man, as well.  I cook, sew, do my laundry, and so forth, myself, not because it fits some obsolete gender role, but because the ability to do them makes your life better *and* you'll probably enjoy at least some of those practices.  You should also learn to fix things, write, play music, speak in front of a crowd, write software, exercise, and more.  Empower yourself and normalize life as a generalist.

And for my final pitch for the term...stop trying to overthink this.  You dust by sweeping up particulate matter from a surface, but you *also* dust by scattering particulate matter (snow, powdered sugar, etc.) on a surface.  We park on driveways and drive on parkways.  They picked the word.  Live with it.

I raise the name for another reason, though.

## Gender Norms

Think about the root of the resistance that many men have in embracing feminism as a route to their own freedom and health.  After living in a world where people mock the idea of using products for the "wrong" gender or even unisex products---a world increasingly crammed with absurd products declaring themselves *for men* so that we poor, benighted guys finally have society's permission to drink water or use a cotton swab or whatever---the idea of involvement with a socio-political movement with what looks like feminine "branding" terrifies them.

This also gets back to the idea of hegemonic gender roles.  If you can't bring yourself to apply a feminist lens to fixing problems, then you can *instead* walk away from gender conformity[^UKDmNM] and opt out of the toxic bits.  Among the many obnoxious reasons that the political right wants to stomp out any traces of transgender people living their lives, it most prominently scares them that we might all learn that literally nothing stops you from ditching gender norms in your life.  Or, rather, literally nothing stops you, except for the existential dread from imagining all the forces that might stop you or might harass you.  By persecuting transgender people, they want to send the message that a man babysitting children or wearing a skirt instead of shorts should (in their eyes) make that man suspicious.
