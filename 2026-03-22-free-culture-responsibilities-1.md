"It took him long enough to get back to this," I hear you say.  And...yes.  It did.  I don't know what to tell you.  Other things took priority...

![A logo representing Free Culture, featuring a sketch of three definitely generic modular brick toys stacked as a corner](/blog/assets/117878252_3c00463c07_o.png "I considered an alternate logo for this post, but I still don't hate this so much...")

To get back on track, as I mentioned increasingly far back, somebody reached out to me asking a question that took me off guard.

To wit.

> What's Free Culture?

Indeed, looking back on those first years of this blog, I somehow never touched on that.  This embarrassed me quite a bit, since I often try not to sink into jargon.  I *have* talked about [the movement and its failings]({% post_url 2024-06-02-free-culture-movement %}) and [why I care so much]({% post_url 2023-06-25-free-culture %}) about the idea, not to mention spending every Saturday morning trying to strong-arm you into checking out some project via the [Free Culture Book Club](/blog/tag/book-club), yes.  But it doesn't look like I bothered to define the idea or how I see it until starting this series of posts last year.

## Recap

I kicked off that process of defining the idea back in September with an [elevator pitch for Free Culture]({% post_url 2025-09-21-define-free-culture-1 %}), defining the idea in terms of the four freedoms from the Free Software movement.

- The freedom to experience the work as you wish, without restriction
- The freedom to study the work, and to create derivative works that say what you want
- The freedom to redistribute copies so you can help others
- The freedom to distribute copies of your modified versions to others without restriction

I also talked a bit about why this might matter to us, especially in an age of constant media consolidation.  After that, I tried to use historical quotes about art and cooperation to outline a modern [philosophy of Free Culture]({% post_url 2025-10-05-free-culture-philosophy %}) not reliant on opposition to copyright or other individualistic, libertarian positions that coincidentally uphold tropes like abusive lone geniuses.  I summarized the results with this minimal statement.

> Humans need to create and share, creating culture; Free Culture sees this as an inalienable right.

This oversimplifies away the idea that we make art to connect with others---our inspirations, our peers/audience, and those who we ultimately inspire---because everybody has something to contribute to the culture, but for less than a hundred characters, it probably passes muster.

After that, I tried to outline the [history of Culture Freedom]({% post_url 2025-10-26-define-free-culture-history %}) from what we now call fan-fiction, to fights over copyright, to the movements that set up the mechanisms by which we can ensure that at least *our* parts of the culture remain free.

Finally, before puttering out before the holidays, I started digging into the [mechanisms of Free Culture]({% post_url 2025-11-16-free-culture-mechanics %}) and a deeper [look at licensing]({% post_url 2025-11-23-free-culture-licensing-2 %}).  You can think of those as the current and aspirational (respectively) mechanisms *of* the mechanisms of Free Culture.

This time through, I want to talk a bit about our responsibilities, starting with our responsibilities as fans and---pardon the expression---consumers.

## Taking Responsibility

You might (rightly) ask why we should ever have any responsibilities for media that we enjoy.  After all, when Disney or Universal release something that you love, they'd prefer that you treat it like a stereotypical estranged parent after a divorce, stopping by occasionally to shower the kids (the media) with gifts (money), we called it the "Disney Dad" during my childhood, but otherwise keep out of it.  Enjoying the media should suffice, no?

And yet, despite corporate culture only wanting us as passive consumers, you don't need to stretch far to find fans volunteering sometimes extensive effort for their preferred media properties, everything from discussing the ideas to creating derivative art---and yes, you can even find complicity on the blog, most notably the [*Star Trek* analysis](/blog/tag/star-trek)[^5F2O0t] on Thursday nights, to the tune of about a million words, so far---which also serve to promote the brand to their friends and/or audiences.

[^5F2O0t]:  A choice, by the way, that feels increasingly uncomfortable.  Even as I enjoy the material more and become better accustomed to the "voice" of the posts, having Paramount owned and progressively controlled by an aspiring fascist warlord makes it far more worrying to introduce new people to the franchise.

By contrast, Free Culture wants your involvement.  As I mentioned, we (broadly speaking) see that we create cultural works to *connect* with people, and want to pass that connection along.

And I should note that "passive consumption" has all sorts of problems, even in corporate circles.  Streaming, I think, has exposed this to many people.  The big services will cancel and even remove shows if they don't produce the right metrics that show them that they have a hit that will improve subscriber numbers in some way.  If you don't know the metrics well enough to game them---I don't know about now, but at least at one point, Netflix counted accounts that completed a new season of a show in three days after release as the primary signal[^hljg5W] of whether to make more---then you have no influence at all.  And if people do start to understand the metrics, then the company will change them to prevent a smaller audience from gaming them.

[^hljg5W]:  For another classic metric for renewal where viewers functionally have no control, almost all ad-supported media looks at sponsorship to make decisions.  If a company sees a greater increase in sales than it costs them to run commercials during a show, then the network will happily renew the show.  If companies don't, then they pull their ads, and the network drops the show.  And it takes significant organization to swing that in the narrow window when everybody makes those decisions.

For this post, then, I want to talk about that involvement, and the different forms that it might take, ideally (but maybe not always, depending on how you measure things) from least to greatest effort.  Or to put it another way, what makes us *good consumers* of Free Culture?

## Speak Out

If we think about "the least that we can do," then if you find something that you enjoyed, then **tell people about it**.  Seriously, it probably took you some effort to find the thing, right?  And other people will probably enjoy it, right?  Why, then, would you want it to take as much effort for them to find it?

Let's debunk (dismiss?) some myths before leaving it at that.

No, you won't offend anybody by recommending that they check something out.  It always seems like an ironic worry, when we can point to multiple entire media outlets dedicated to making sure that you know about releases from million- and trillion-dollar corporations, even though those corporations could spring for advertising.  I mean, the day that some hypothetical producer inevitably has James Bond team up with Elle Woods to stop Hannibal Lecter, every media outlet will not only give you their "takes" on every single bit of news to dribble out of the studio, but also announce every change to the schedule, even though MGM---owned by Amazon, remember---could afford to hire people to walk door-to-door explaining it, to say nothing of covering it in **The Washington Post**, running ads on Amazon itself, stuffing pamphlets into every box they ship, or offering a discount on AWS for any website running there that shows their ad.  You telling friends or followers that you liked some Free Culture comic, which absolutely can't afford to draw the kind of attention that a major media outlet can, doesn't go too far.

Even if you only have the big corporate social media sites to talk about these things, you'll probably find somebody who'll find it interesting.  It can often seem expedient to dismiss everybody on the platform as some lazy, mainstream-friendly individual with no taste.  But...*you* use it, right?  Otherwise, you wouldn't worry about not fitting in.  And people use social media because of the people there, not because they love Mark Zuckerberg's aesthetic of the day or whatever.  People, then, will have a wide variety of interests, and might also love the obscure thing that you love.

At the opposite end of the spectrum, also don't take for granted that everybody already knows about what you found.  After many influxes of people from decaying commercial sites, you can no longer guess that everybody who you know on the Fediverse uses Linux and thinks deeply about public licenses.  Increasingly, the average person there really only wanted a Twitter replacement.  And given that my "book club" blog posts still occasionally get responses on Mastodon about the discovery, I guarantee that at least some people probably haven't seen what you did.  They *might* know about the couple of most popular Free Culture projects, but even then, not everybody unless you only associate with Free Culture zealots.

And critically, the individual and small-team creators making Free Cultural works don't typically have the resources to get the word out on projects on their own.  Some creators pitch their work on their social media accounts, but that doesn't help get the works in front of *new* people who might become fans if given the chance.

### Spread, How-To

How do you accomplish this?  I have a blog, myself, so I dedicate one post per week to telling readers about something that I have found.  Social media also works, indicating that you checked the thing out, liked it, and why, and providing a link.  Specifying the license also probably wouldn't go amiss.  For another angle, you might go with a comparison to a more famous property, "like an [Insert Big Franchise], but..."

Also, outright telling your friends about things works.  You almost certainly talk to them about movies and television shows.  Depending on your friend group, books and comics might also get on that list.  You can sneak some independent media in there.

## Spend Money

Speaking of people not having resources, did you know that it costs money to...like, survive under capitalism?  It totally does.

Look, I hate this part of the conversation.  Every time that I see a new *We Need to Pay Open Source Maintainers*, I cringe, because turning collaborative volunteer infrastructure work into a day job sounds horrible for everybody involved.  And that goes double for artsier projects.

However, like I said, people living in society need to pay bills, and shockingly few creditors accept songs, funny stories, or video games as payment.  Briefly, *I* ran a business that accepted quirky gifts from overseas from people who couldn't pay with a credit card, but the brevity probably indicates how well that worked out, especially after accounting for shipping costs.  Anyway, paying bills requires money.

Do you want a Free Culture where people can only create if they come from wealth and/or have no responsibilities for anybody else?  Those wealthy and single people have plenty of time to churn out what we could charitably call art.  Somebody who needs to work multiple jobs to make ends meet or needs to take care of somebody doesn't.

If you have the means, then, and they've set up the infrastructure to accept payments, support your favorite creators with actual money that they can spend.

### Budgeting

And if you don't think that you have the means, take a look at the media that you do spend money on.  Do you complain about "too many streaming services," because you "need" to subscribe to all of them to watch your shows?  That costs, what, over a hundred dollars per month, going into the pockets of billionaires[^6Wv-BR]?

[^6Wv-BR]:  And given how much Internet infrastructure Amazon owns, most of that money probably goes into the pockets of Jeff Bezos after the company that you think you paid covers its AWS bill.

As my personal rule---admittedly, as much to convince me to cancel what I didn't love as to motivate me to support people, so it may not work for everybody---no less than the amount of our payments to big businesses should go to comparable independent creators.  Think of it as atoning.  If twenty bucks go to Netflix (for example) every month, then you surely have that much more in the budget to send to people who'll use it for something more productive than tipping a security guard on their super-yacht.  And if you don't have that much more money, do you *really* get that much value out of Netflix that it comes first in your budget?

I live on the extremes, here, but if anybody wants to know, since I instituted that rule in my life, the subscriptions have vanished, and I believe that my entire media budget (except for PBS Passport, technically a gift exchange rather than a subscription) now goes to Free Culture projects.  I do still "consume" commercial media, but I do so through the library-based services, the occasional physical media purchase, PBS, and the occasional ad-supported service.

## Contribute Labor

We already touched on this a bit for helping publicize your favorite works, but as part of Free Culture, you can---often, though not always---find ways to help.

Nobody gets everything right, after all.  Typos sneak their ways into texts.  A diagram might not show as clear a depiction of the topic as desired.  Translations can bring the work to another audience, and almost any translation can benefit from another pass.  Some projects solicit help prior to release.

This doesn't apply to every project.  Often, a book will get "thrown over the wall" under a pseudonym, with no way of contacting the creator to get a new edition or an editable form that you can use to make your own edition.  And certain kinds of works---anything with human performers, for example---take a lot of effort to correct and distribute again.  But *frequently*, it can apply.

Notably, we start to deviate from general "help your favorite indie performer," at this point.  You can certainly help publicize somebody's self-published book or a non-label album or whatever, no matter the license, and you can pay the creators.  You *should* do those things.  Free Culture licensing, however, accepts and encourages contributing a translation or editing in ways that an ordinary author or other artist probably couldn't accept without thinking about legal consequences for the work.

Not every creator thinks this through, but a commercial project that accepts fan contributions doesn't *own* that contribution.  That means that, if you translate an author's book "for them," they can't publish it without your permission, because they don't own the translation.  And you can't publish it without their permission, because you don't own the underlying content.  Worse, if they commission a separate translation that they would own so that they don't need to involve you, that potentially opens them up to legal arguments about whether the new translation comes too close to yours.  Television shows famously reject ideas from outsiders for similar reasons, that a fan might come up with an idea similar to something already in the pipeline, which could lead (and has historically led) to ugly legal fights.  Contributions to a Free Culture projects, though, implicitly come under compatible terms.

### Howdy

This certainly varies from creator to creator, but even checking in to compliment somebody's work can often go a long way to providing some support.  And yes, I would consider this labor.

Personally, I *appreciate* it when people contact me with compliments for something on the blog or another project, but I also often find it slightly uncomfortable, especially in those rare cases when it comes out of nowhere.  As such, I will say that it probably helps to say something in addition to the compliment---one person pointed to someone whose writing I might enjoy, for example---to give the recipient more to engage with.  And also, don't grump if the response doesn't feel right, because it takes a surprising amount of practice to take a compliment.

Again, speaking from my own experience, it took me a long time to learn that you respond to compliments with "thank you," rather than dismissals or wondering why anybody would say such a thing.  Years ago, somebody told me that I looked good in a new haircut, and I swear that I couldn't come up with anything more *coherent*, let alone polite, than "huh, weird."

Oh, and also apply leniency to responses, because a lot of compliments can come off as creepy, depending on identity issues.  For example, since we still have Women's History Month happening, head over to the social media page of pretty much any female journalist on local television.  Pick the market, pick the platform, and pick the journalist.  Then read the comments.

No matter what they post---their report on a ten-car pileup, a murder, the effects of recent weather, a picture with their spouse and kids, opening up about a struggle with depression, or their outfit for the day---you'll find a congruent collection of well-meaning sexist dolts dishing out compliments.  Often, you'll find something like "good morning.  What a beauty" on *every post*.  If you luck out, you'll see pervasive use of diminutive names, as if the correspondents think that they have some personal relationship.

Don't do that.  Remember where the parasocial boundaries actually sit.

### The LLM Elephant in the Room

When I say "contribute labor," above, I mean your own.  Unless the maintainer of a project has explicitly said otherwise, don't throw generative AI output at them.

First off, don't you think that, if they wanted a substandard filler in the project, they'd type the prompt themselves?  You don't have exclusive access to these things, you realize, nor have you stumbled across some obscure secret technology.  Trust me, everybody already knows how to use them.

More than that, though, if it doesn't fit perfectly, you have given the project the gift of *homework* to figure out if they can salvage anything of your "contribution."  Invariably, this means doing all the work independently, so you really haven't done anything but shove yourself into the process.

Also, though, you can't guarantee that the LLM didn't plagiarize somebody else's work.  I have written elsewhere that you can make a good argument that *training* an LLM qualifies as Fair Use, even if it infringes on somebody's copyright.  You might have grounds to argue that supplying an LLM as a service still qualifies as Fair Use, even if that infringes copyright somewhere.  But if somebody uses infringing output, that absolutely does not fall under Fair Use, opening up the project to lawsuits.

Contribute your own effort, in other words, not a no-effort result that you can't explain or justify.

## Participate

Here, we dive even deeper into activities that only really work, or at least unambiguously help, in Free Culture spaces.  You can *make* fan art for anything---and as I pointed out back in October, the modern/recognizable form of that tradition goes back as far as publishing does---but most creators feel uncomfortable with you taking control of "their" narrative.  And even the creators who appreciate it can change their minds at any time, and abruptly turn against such projects.

Since I already raised the specter of the franchise in this post, after a planned fan film raised over half a million dollars then announced future funding rounds, and started putting together an all-star cast, Paramount sued for copyright infringement after decades of "benign neglect" over *Star Trek* fan fiction.  They managed to settle to avoid driving more wedges between the studio and fans, but then devised a set of rules that future productions need to follow, including length, trademark, source, budget, staffing, content, profit, and other restrictions.

Free Cultural works, however, grant you unconditional permission to do whatever you want to do with the work in advance.  If you want to film a big-budget, star-studded adaptation of something, then you only need to comply with the public license.  With Free Culture works, that means (as previously discussed) some combination of crediting your source, highlighting your changes, making your version available under an equivalent license, and providing the material required to reconstruct your version if somebody wants to change it.

By the way, since we get to nicely juxtapose the two situations, notice how even the most rigorous[^EGe2Z3] public license differs from Paramount's fan film rules.  Paramount seeks to reserve complete freedom for itself while also protecting its reputation, whereas Free Culture licenses seek to protect the part of your audience who wants to adapt your work as you adapted somebody else's.

[^EGe2Z3]:  I almost used the word "restrictive," here, violating my rule.  Licenses *let* you do things, so by definition, they can never restrict, only refuse to extend a full panoply of privileges.

Anyway, to get back to the point, not only can you create these adaptations and extensions, but you *should*, if it inspires you to create.  As I said multiple times in earlier posts in this series, Free Cultural works at least partly belong to everybody, so more such works enrich everybody.

## Disentangle

For the last item on this list---at least for now---let's take participation and adaptation a step further.  I could summarize the idea that I want to present, here, but given that another (fictional) source does a fantastic job of half-jokingly getting to what I have in mind, I might as well quote the passage in its entirety.

With the explanation that the author in this case uses ze/zem/zir pronouns for a hypothetical person, consider the following philosophy of *literary disentanglement*.

> Did you think that "Death of the Author" was as bad as it could get? Well, you were wrong. Literary disentanglement picks up where Death of the Author left off.
>
> If Death of the Author says that the creator cannot comment on his completed work, then we find that there is one more step that we can take. The author's commentary on zir work is made of zir words, just as zir work is. Both are products of zir mind. Where the Death of the Author falls short is in supposing that there is any difference between the two: the work itself is as valid as the author's commentary. Thus, it is just as invalid.
>
> Death of the Author assumes that the author is incapable of properly commenting on zir work and yet capable of bringing it forth as it should be in the first place. Disentanglement theory recognizes that ze can do neither. The work that the author produces is flawed as the commentary. The process of disentanglement is the process of perfecting it: the critic carefully analyzes the work to excise those words, paragraphs, chapters, and even characters that do not truly belong in the work and grafts material that should have been there from the beginning but were not, and this on account of the incompetence of the author.
>
> This new, perfected version of the work, the work as it should have been all along, is what the critic comments upon. The author may protest that it is not what ze wrote, but one does not favor a child's scribble over the very sun that it depicts. Even so the critic does not waste zir time with the half-formed marble block but sets zir sights fully upon the Adonis that he has carefully freed.

If you want many more bits along these lines, this passage comes from the delightful [**Strange Nations**](https://www.drivethrurpg.com/en/product/200950/strange-nations-a-worldbuilding-resource) by [R. James Gauvreau](https://www.patreon.com/c/WMBsaltworks/posts), made available under the terms of the Creative Commons Attribution Share-Alike 4.0 International license.  We covered some of [Gauvreau's narrative fiction]({% post_url 2022-12-17-rjg %}) a few years back for the Free Culture Book Club.

You'll notice that the premise---I assume accidentally, since nothing else in the book nods to this sort of thing---closely tracks with Free Culture.  In many ways, publishing *starts* the process, rather than ending it, handing it off to the community.  Rather than "consumers," Free Culture empowers us to bring out what we see buried in the art, looking to incrementally perfect it.

And not only do we have the power and authority to improve on what the artist created, but we have the power and authority to improve on what we *wish* that the artist created.  Remember (the second half of) that second freedom as I phrased it, the freedom "to create derivative works that say what you want."  Imagine the (completely fictional) corporate version of this for a moment.  Since I have already invoked *Star Trek* a couple of times, imagine the possible fan works that lavish praise on the franchise, *but* rework the central concept to remove the military/colonial premise.  Or imagine remaking famous 1980s action films without the jingoistic warmongering[^FmwLCG].  If it got any significant attention, the studios would do their best to stomp such a thing out immediately, because you can't tell *them* where the heart of their franchise lies.

[^FmwLCG]:  I never cared for Chuck Norris or any of his work myself, but since he died the other day and produced a lot of those "we should appreciate the Vietnam War more" films as star vehicles for himself, I couldn't resist getting the timely jab in.

For a given Free Culture project, however, you absolutely can do exactly that.  If you find one of the (relatively rare) stories that serve as a platform for hate, but still find some interesting aspects to it, you can build something that actively flips the original message on its head.  I won't name names or link anywhere, because I want to do a "best (so far) of" post later in this series, but consider two comics that you might recognize from their descriptions.

- One started with a specific and narrow political agenda, but over time expanded to regressive views including explicit transphobia.  Later creators could---and one actually did---remake the comic into one denouncing such views and introducing queer themes of all sorts, drowning out the hate with its own messaging.
- Another railed against famous brands getting new faces and stewards that didn't look like the same straight, cisgender, Christian white men pervasive in Western media during the early twentieth century.  While it hasn't happened, another creator could return to that world and interrogate why the protagonist feels so threatened by people seeing a Black woman as a role-model.

I mean, yeah, you could go the other way, too, and rewrite progressive Free Culture content to carry regressive messages of bigotry.  But honestly, what about "create art that empowers anybody to build on and revise it, because we want to connect" actually sounds like a conservative, reactionary, or even libertarian project?  I hate to tell you, but if you enjoy Free Culture---and probably any Free Whatever, software, hardware, or anything else---and actually sit with that feeling, it'll infect you until you start caring about the treatment of people who look and think nothing like you.  We welcome your voice and your contributions, but the ideas will change you far more than you change us...

Recently, along these lines, I retroactively started making this point in the book club posts.  When I talk about *what works less well*, rather than leaving it open to interpretation that I meant it as an absolute criticism---despite the term "book club," some people inevitably see it as a series of reviews---it states that I want to highlight aspects that an adaptation might consider improving.  The actual quality of the work doesn't matter for those posts, because somebody can come along at any time and make it better.

Anyway, to put this all another way, to support Free Culture in the highest-effort way possible, become a creator, using other Free Cultural works as raw material.  Free Culture feels like the only space where you can get away with talking about [polishing a turd](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/polish_a_turd) as a compliment.

## Coming Soon

As always, I have probably overlooked many ways to support the people who make Free Culture things, but hyping it up, paying for things, helping out, adapting, and pulling out the best of a project for something new, those should get you started, at least, and I would hope might inspire some ideas in between.  At its best, we can encourage creators to create more, when they might otherwise need to work longer hours or would question whether anybody cares.

And once again, we'll end here, so that this doesn't become some unhinged manifesto.  But at some point---I hope not in another five months, but definitely not too soon---we'll talk about the other half of this topic, the responsibilities that well-behaved creators have and should have in Free Culture, to best protect the four freedoms in ways that resonate with the broader philosophy.  We should have some additional "guest appearances," too.
