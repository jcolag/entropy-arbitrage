## Move Along Home

For context, this episode aired after [*Birthright*]({% post_url 2025-03-13-birthright-2 %}) finished off, with no **Next Generation** episode for the following week.

 > **JAKE**: Dress uniform?
 >
 > **SISKO**: I haven't worn this in almost three years. I must've lost a little weight. Feels kind of baggy.

In case anybody still believed that clothing came from replicators, Sisko has carted this around, unused, since---assuming that our years mostly match up to their years---probably the assorted *Saratoga* funerals, including Jennifer's.  Three years our time would put the event in the tail end of **TNG**'s third season, [*Sins of the Father*]({% post_url 2023-08-24-sins-father %}) through [*The Best of Both Worlds*]({% post_url 2023-10-26-both-worlds-1 %}), the latter bleeding into the fourth season, so those funerals probably wouldn't have happened more than three months after three years past.

Without Jake asking about (speculating, here...) the portrait of Picard embroidered into the lining of the uniform's seat or some more television-friendly clue, though, we'll never know exactly what he means.  The grief would probably fit with his losing weight, though.

 > **SISKO**: Wasn't it just a few weeks ago that you were playing with model starships?

If only Sisko had known that the "children's toys, but for adult men (because actual children don't recognize any of the brands that we can afford and women mostly see those brands as red flags)" industry would explode not long after this aired...

 > **SISKO**: Nog. So all you know about women you've heard from the Ferengi boy.

Nog will, in fact, turn out to carry around at least some of the toxic approach to gender roles imposed by his culture, sure.  However, I can think of plenty of humans and Vulcans (to start) who we've seen who don't have all that dissimilar a view of women from the Ferengi.  Hell, the series started in [*Emissary*]({% post_url 2026-02-26-emissary-1 %}) with O'Brien asking Sisko "have you ever *worked* with a Bajoran woman" to foreshadow the fact that Kira...has strong opinions.  In [*Dax*]({% post_url 2026-04-16-dax %}), Bashir all but stated explicitly that he applies Big Tech consent rules to sex, classifying answers as "Yes" or "Maybe Later."  Dax herself made a big production about how much she loves the creepy leering that her new body gets her in [*Babel*]({% post_url 2026-03-26-babel %}).  Odo had his weird "men lose their identities in relationships" sitcom-style rant in [*A Man Alone*]({% post_url 2026-03-19-man-alone %}).  Add in all the horrible comments and actions that we saw on **TNG** from pretty much every significant male character who acknowledged the existence of women, and I'd call Nog among the least-destructive options for advice about women, myself...

 > **SISKO**: First contact is our most important mission, Jake. It has to be handled delicately. You might say it's sort of like a first date with a girl. You want to show a certain respect for---

I mean...always show a certain respect to women, as people, regardless of whether you hope to sleep with or impress them, **right**?  If not, then his advice probably won't differ much from Nog's, frankly.

Also, not to nitpick, especially since I didn't bother to quote the line, but if the Vulcan ship made contact with the Wadi, then why treat *this* like a first contact?

 > **BASHIR**: I'm positive I packed my dress uniform. I can't understand it.

Not only do they not replicate their uniforms, but apparently they *can't* replicate them.  And while I realize that they haven't bumped him to a recurring role yet, [*Past Prologue*]({% post_url 2026-03-12-past-prologue %}) went pretty far out of its way to make sure that we knew about the tailor on the station, who presumably (no innuendo, here...) has Bashir's measurements from the whole suit fiasco.

By the way, you might notice that the Wadi ship has more than a few design details that the show will later use for the *Defiant*, for people who get excited about that sort of thing.  It looks close enough that, for a moment, I forgot that they wouldn't introduce the ship for a couple of years...

 > **FALOW**: I am Falow, Master Surchid of the Wadi.

If Falow looks familiar, then you might recognize [Joel Brooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joel_Brooks), successful character actor for decades.

 > **SISKO**: First contact is not what it used to be.

I know, right?  They all showed up sober, modestly clothed, not under the control of a TRS-80 that The Ancient Ones forgot in some disused crockery, not on the run from some worrisome military force, with no overt plans to steal weapons so that they can exterminate a competing power, and not planning to secretly elevate some sad child to frivolous omnipotence.  Bashir made the right call.  Why even bother getting dressed up...?

 > **QUARK**: Commander, I'm a host. A host is an ambassador of good will. The more good will that I can generate, the longer they'll stay, the greater my profits. I think we're talking the same language here.
 >
 > **SISKO**: Not quite.

Admittedly, I have spent a lot of time trying to parse out the Federation's economy and why everybody (other than the occasional aristocrat lecturing the poors on how nobody wants for anything) seems to avoid the subject.  However, with that caveat, it *sounds* like the same language to me.  They both want to impress the Wadi to encourage trade.  Starfleet might see information and eventually permission to deploy the occasional military base as more valuable than cash, at the moment, but everybody sees this ideally ending with freighters lining up on both sides of the wormhole.

 > **FALOW**: Then, we really have nothing to wager...except this.
 >
 > **QUARK**: Get these folks some drinks

This probably has much more to do with the writing than anything else, but this reads like a con job to everybody, no?  "For currency, we only thought to bring some leftover Lincoln Logs, a watering can of toilet wine, and a giant sack of some boring old gemstones that you probably wouldn't care about."

 > **SISKO**: With all due respect to the procedures and to McCoullough, I have had it. This is as far as duty takes me. Keep them happy. If they ever get tired of playing dabo, let me know. Good night.

Well, now I want to see what they expected to happen.  It seems highly unlikely that nobody has ever bothered to game out what to do if the envoy gets distracted, since that feels like the single most probable outcome when dealing with somebody from a new culture.  I mean, you can't even guarantee that newcomers will have the same sensory input, let alone the same priorities.

 > **BASHIR**: Perhaps we're being given some sort of behavioral test, like a laboratory rodent who must work his way out of a maze.

Do they still do that sort of thing frequently enough that everybody would recognize the reference, even the one who has spent her entire life on the fringes of an oppressed society?

 > **JAKE**: The computer says he's not on the station.

Seriously, why does the computer not *notify* anybody when it unexpectedly can't detect people?  If you absolutely must violate everybody's privacy by tracking them all the time, this seems like the single use that justifies it, especially in space.

 > **JAKE**: School's closed while Mrs O'Brien's back on Earth. Nog and I were going over to airlock three and watch the Bajoran transport passengers come on board.

The O'Briens ran away back in [*Dax*]({% post_url 2026-04-16-dax %}).  I can't help noticing that, despite the big production made of the station falling apart without Miles at that point, they seem to have gotten along quite well for three episodes...

 > **PRIMMIN**: I heard that Wadi party went on all night.
 >
 > **ODO**: How long have you been a security officer, Lieutenant?

This feels like it calls back to all the times in the original series when officers would roll their eyes at and walk past a colleague in clear distress, with the exception that Odo points out the complete dereliction of duty this time.

 > **PRIMMIN**: Yes, Constable.
 >
 > **ODO**: And don't call me Constable. I'm Chief of Security.

I mean, sure, but as I sort of (admittedly poorly) got into last time with [*The Passenger*]({% post_url 2026-04-23-passenger %}), English has two kinds of title, job titles and honorific titles, or in military terms, rank.  The latter work as names, the former don't, in that you wouldn't write Primmin saying "well, Chief of Security, based on my investigation," and so forth.  That doesn't excuse Primmin's lapse, because he should know that they don't have a relationship where he gets to use affectionate nicknames, but it (still) makes no sense that Odo operates in the chain of command of two cooperating military groups and doesn't get a rank that doubles as an honorific.

 > **DAX**: Come on! Just say the rhyme.

I don't know if this deals more with the writing or the culture, but especially given that the episode started with Sisko trying to come to terms that he no longer raises a small child, it defies all reason that "old man" Dax happily played along with the game while the single father in the group, who has almost certainly had to do far sillier things in front of his colleagues, saw skipping around with a chant as some mortifying act.  Maybe I have this confused with **Bluey**, I don't know, but with what we've seen of Sisko, both his centering of family and his general interest in people over protocol, I'd expect him to have pushed Dax out of the way to get into alien-hopscotch.

It especially feels weird with Dax's disinterested delivery.  Even Kira gets into it by the end, but Dax makes it feel like a lecture with a professor who didn't have time to prepare and so will read a few pages from the textbook to the board, so we can't dismiss it as Dax's enthusiasm for her newfound youth...

 > **DAX**: A game. Home is often the destination in a game.

She needs a big old "<sup>Citation Needed</sup>" on this, no?  I can think of a handful of groupings, such as the [pachisi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pachisi) family---a subclass of the cross-and-circle subclass of race games---safe haven sports such as baseball and cricket, and hide-and-seek, where at least some players have "home" as a goal.  But "often" feels tough to justify when games include everything from guessing a number to simulated empire-building to field hockey to contract bridge.

 > **KIRA**: How did you know?
 >
 > **BASHIR**: Scientific method. Actually, I took a guess. It didn't seem like I had much to lose.

We do get *small* glimpses of a viable person in there, from time to time...

 > **ODO**: Well, I'm not in Starfleet. Set the coordinates to midway down the second deck corridor. Is it against Starfleet rules to press a few buttons?

This must have Primmin so excited.  Not only has somebody (almost voluntarily) included him in something, here, but somebody wants to do some crimes without sending him out of the room like Picard and his ilk always do...

 > **DAX**: If you were hurt, I'd leave you behind.

Starfleet really does like sacrificing its officers.  It feels like, in preparing this era of the franchise, the watched all the security officers die on missions and the whole "needs of the many outweighs the needs of the few" rhetoric from [**The Wrath of Khan**]({% post_url 2022-03-24-twok %}), and decided that they needed that as an official policy.

 > **KIRA**: Court-martial me.
 >
 > **SISKO**: I can't. You're not in Starfleet.

Ha!

By the way, I feel like I've said this a lot in watching this series, but did this script start out as more personal for Sisko?  The episode started by dropping those heavy hints that he still can't even *talk* about the funerals for his colleagues and wife, beyond things that happened about three years ago, and now we have Dax pinned in pretty much the same position as he found Jennifer in *Emissary*, with a similar urgency to leaving Dax behind.  You could even imagine the analogy of his failure here (even with Kira's support) getting him closer to no longer blaming himself for surviving Wolf 359...

 > **QUARK**: Oh, that's right, you were here for the grovelling.

Other than a great line, it does seem weird that they let the Wadi off the hook, here.  Even accounting for the cheating, "you cheated me, so I'll abduct four people entirely unrelated to you and torment them" feels like a pretty serious diplomatic problem...

## Conclusions

We didn't even get Chula rules, let alone much insight, but...

### The Bad

We see regressive views on dating, as well as an assumption that a Ferengi kid will give dangerously sexist advice, despite plenty of worrying gender politics among the humans.

Starfleet seems to instill unrealistic expectations of first contact scenarios, including a belief that other cultures will stick to the Starfleet script.  They also continue to emphasize that teams need to leave colleagues behind if helping them will slow things down.

The surveillance computers still don't report when officers unexpectedly drop off the grid.  And people see every manager failing to show up for work and shrug their shoulders.

### The Weird

They keep (at least) ceremonial clothing for long periods, carrying uniforms from posting to posting.
