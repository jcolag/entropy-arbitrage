I do want to point out---and I always mean to write a full post about this---that even before Trump, we started to see the signs of Silicon Valley's interest in authoritarian governance.  During the Bush administration, then through Obama's, we saw companies systematically attack the Bill of Rights.  The Constitution doesn't bind corporations, but consider that you have probably signed non-disparagement contracts limiting your speech about an employer or vendor, non-compete contracts limiting your freedom of association, terms of use denying you access to systems if you defend yourself from their invasive actions depriving you of "arms," had [software installed on your computer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sony_BMG_copy_protection_rootkit_scandal) without your knowledge or consent, had your files searched, and so on down the line, accustoming people to not "needing" civil liberties, even as they managed more of our lives.  In that sense, why would it surprise anybody that these companies would side with *governments* with the same view of civil liberties?

## 9:02 -- Fri 05 December 2025

{% embed https://images.theconversation.com/files/701498/original/file-20251110-56-29jazm.jpg|A hand with a pen writing in a notebook|false| %}

[<i class="fab fa-mastodon"></i>](https://mastodon.social/@jcolag/115667326887771044) [Writing builds resilience by changing your brain, helping you face everyday challenges](https://theconversation.com/writing-builds-resilience-by-changing-your-brain-helping-you-face-everyday-challenges-265188) from The Conversation

 > In other words, externalizing emotional distress through writing fosters safety. Expressive writing turns pain into a metaphorical book on a shelf, ready to be reopened with intention. It signals the brain, ‚ÄúYou don‚Äôt need to carry this anymore.‚Äù

Hashtags:  #Writing #Psychology #Emotions

In late 2021, I noticed that I found myself constantly surprised at a new week or month, and couldn't really account for anything that I worked on.  As an experiment, I started writing a nightly "diary entry," really a semi-structured text file, and interestingly, it works (similar to what this article describes, stress-wise) in proportion to how seriously I take the process.  If I push myself to handle the day's events from memory, then the memory "sticks" and I feel better.  But if I "phone it in"---looking through recently updated files to figure out what I worked on, checking streaming service histories for what I watched, blowing off the actual comments for the day, and the like---then it doesn't seem useful.
