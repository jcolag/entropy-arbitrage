Hashtags:  #USPol

The article inexplicably doesn't touch on the traditional aspect of this, by the way:  Support for breaking up (among liberals and progressives, at least) the company plays into almost every right-wing plan.  It says that no, conservatives really don't deserve a functioning government, and we all lied all this time as they insisted.  It says that a pluralistic democracy can't work.  And maybe most importantly, breaking up the country would split up government power---which, yes, gets misused when we elect terrible people---leaving each open to capture by special interests.  Do you want people on the new borders to live a few miles from toxic waste...?

<i class="fas fa-square"></i> [The long shadow of Paul Ehrlich’s ‘Population Bomb’ is evident in anti-immigration efforts today](https://theconversation.com/the-long-shadow-of-paul-ehrlichs-population-bomb-is-evident-in-anti-immigration-efforts-today-279151) from The Conversation @TheConversationUS@newsie.social

> Demographers and economists in the decades after World War II [rejected the idea](https://digitallibrary.un.org/record/3926522) that Ehrlich was promoting to millions of readers: that population growth is the primary cause of environmental degradation. Instead, the expert consensus showed how pollution and resource depletion are [driven far more](https://archive.org/details/closingcirclecon0000comm) by extraction and overconsumption than by head count.

I have mentioned this many times over the years on the blog, that the idea that we had overpopulation risks from *those people* didn't originate with Ehrlich, but he certainly popularized it and kept it in the mainstream for decades, and we never really contended with its influence.
