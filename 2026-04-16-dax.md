## Dax

For context, this  episode lands between [*Face of the Enemy*]({% post_url 2025-02-20-face-enemy %}) and [*Tapestry*]({% post_url 2025-02-27-tapestry %}).

That said, I should note that this episode probably won't give us much, the main plot split between two non-Federation legal regimes while our main cast wishes that they had something useful to say.

 > Station log. stardate 46910.1. Chief O'Brien has escorted his wife back to Earth to celebrate her mother's hundredth birthday. In the meantime, the rest of us are trying to keep the station up and running.

Fingers crossed that they don't show us any of that subplot.  O'Brien in Japan dealing with a mature woman sounds like a *lot* of offensive comments, combining everything that he had to say in [*Q-Less*]({% post_url 2026-04-09-q-less %}) last week, [*The Wounded*]({% post_url 2024-01-25-wounded %}), and probably any appearance of Troi's mother...

 > **BASHIR**: Perhaps we ought to call in a Romulan repair service.

He makes this seems like a wild idea, but...yes?  This actually sounds like an effective use of their time.

 > **BASHIR**: I can think of better ways of keeping you up. And they're more fun than drinking Klingon coffee.

I'll rant about this entire thing next, but I do want to point out that fun sex---I assume that he means sex and not that sad-looking concentration video game from [*A Man Alone*]({% post_url 2026-03-19-man-alone %}), because television (and real-life creeps) always mean that---has a firm requirement of enthusiastic participants, unless you don't care about your partner(s).  If enthusiasm doesn't matter to him, then surely he could replicate something that would work at least as well as "sure, go ahead" or whatever response he expects here.

 > **BASHIR**: Oh, well, good night then. Not necessary, Julian. But not forbidden, either.

Ugh.  Seriously, *no means no*, dude.  Stop trying to find the loophole...

In surveys, more than four out of five women report having experienced some sexual harassment or assault[^2CnR0S], and that starts with some creep trying to find their loophole.

[^2CnR0S]:  More than two out of five men *also* report experiencing sexual harassment/assault, which I point out down here to (a) preempt the dismissal of the figure as overblown, since we have a group often reticent to admit vulnerability in the same order of magnitude and (b) give some idea of the scale of the problem that almost two-thirds of adults---assuming that people don't underreport---has to deal with this crap.

To look like a responsible adult, I'll point people who need more information to the [National Sexual Violence Resource Center](https://www.nsvrc.org/), around for over a quarter-century as an offshoot of the oldest coalition against sexual assault, the (recently renamed to stop traumatizing people) Pennsylvania Coalition to Advance Respect.  As it turns out, both groups promote Sexual Assault Awareness Month in April.  As the commercials used to say, "you're soaking in it."

It feels like I've overdone this snide comment, but the fact that we do have the awareness month, with [Denim Day](https://denimday.org/why-denim)---commemorating an Italian court *the year before this episode* overturning an attacker's conviction because the victim wore tight jeans---in two weeks provides a nice opportunity.  And that dovetails here, because those four-out-of-five women (and the men that I mention in the footnote) all have stories of some creep trying to find the loophole that justifies their action.  Wearing tight jeans, not outright forbidding following her home, seems mature for their age, maybe they'll change their mind afterward, all serve the same purpose of ignoring what the victim wants.

Also, I don't know how much the rest of the episode has to do with the Federation, so I might as well use the space for something useful.

 > **SISKO**: It's working. I'm increasing the flow to the EPS wave guides.

It seems worth pointing out that, a lot like Kirk throughout the original series, Sisko seems substantially better at O'Brien's job than O'Brien.  I don't actually know what any of his nonsense lines should *mean*, but you'll notice that he calmly solved the problem.  He didn't jump around shouting "bloody'ell," blaming Cardassian technology, or explaining that they haven't had the time to actually do all the underlying work, and he probably would've mentioned it in a report eventually.

 > **TANDRO**: My name is Ilon Tandro, special envoy from Klaestron Four in charge of this extradition procedure.

You might recognize Tandro as [Greg Itzin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregory_Itzin), with a substantial career under his belt including another **DS9** appearance, a late **Voyager** appearance, and a mirror-universe admiral at the end of **Enterprise**, but probably gets the most attention for playing one of the presidents on **24**, which I've never watched, so please don't ask follow-up questions...

 > **TANDRO**: You'll find that to be a valid warrant for her arrest. The relevant treaty between Klaestron Four and your Federation allows for unilateral extradition.

I get that.  I even agree that, often, you want your allies to have the right to extradite criminals to their territory for prosecution.  However, what kind of messed-up government agrees to a treaty where your trading partner gets to send in the A-Team[^msd6LS] to physically capture them and drag them across the border without notifying any agencies.

[^msd6LS]:  Oh, go ask your parents...

I mean, surely, this would've gone faster by sending the cops to Sisko's office with the warrant.  Or even better, call ahead and demand that Sisko take her into custody.

 > **SISKO**: He never mentioned he'd even been to Klaestron Four. Strange.
 >
 > **ODO**: Not so strange considering these charges.

Also, not so strange considering that it happened thirty years ago, and he has done a lot of more interesting things.  I've gone to places that I don't talk about, because "I toured [Biosphere 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere_2) after normal operations ended" (to pick one example from roughly thirty years ago) barely qualifies as a story at all, let alone an interesting story, and it only comes up in conversation in moments like this one, even if the gift shop coffee mug does look pretty nice[^HKUl1O] on the shelf...

[^HKUl1O]:  I actually have two, one normal blue mug with a white sketch of the facility on it, and a green glass equivalent with metallic gold outlines.  The blue sits next to the Stargate-inspired terra-cotta Horus mug that I got at the mall after seeing the film.  Riveting story, right?  Now you see why I don't tell the story, and why Curzon might not tell his story.

 > **SISKO**: You don't have an extradition treaty with Bajor. I think that's why you tried to abduct Lieutenant Dax. You were afraid the Bajorans would refuse extradition.

I appreciate the attempt to explain the situation, but this in no way explains how that treaty got signed in the first place.  Nobody suggests that the extraction operation violated anything, making it almost certain that the agreement specifically allows this.

And by the way, since I really don't expect to have much to do for most of this episode, I might as well say how much I enjoy this scene, with how Sisko and Kira work together.  Actually, a lot of this episode flows well, despite not really working on plenty of other levels.

 > **RENORA**: This will be an informal hearing, so I'm going to start with some informal advice. I am one hundred years old. I do not have time to squander listening to superfluous language. In short, I intend being here until supper, not senility. Understood?

The judge, you might recognize as [Anne Haney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anne_Haney), who we last saw in [*The Survivors*]({% post_url 2023-05-18-survivors %}), but also worked pretty much until her 2001 death, maybe most prominently as the Oval Office secretary in **The American President**.

 > **SISKO**: You say the warrant's in order. But the name on the warrant is Dax. Certainly that doesn't refer to Jadzia Dax, a female, but to the now deceased Curzon Dax, a male. This young woman wasn't even born thirty years ago.

The transgender allegory sadly deflates when you ask questions like whether she bears responsibility for pre-transition crimes.  It does make a good argument here, because this sacrifices the life of the new host, but it still feels...icky, when you can't answer the metaphorical answer with anything other than *yes*.

 > **ENINA**: Curzon Dax was not responsible for the death of my husband.

And you almost certainly (by now) recognize Mama Tandro as [Fionnula Flanagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fionnula_Flanagan), who we met/will meet in [*Inheritance*]({% post_url 2025-07-31-inheritance %}) playing Data's mother, where I got a bit deeper into her career.  I mostly recognize her from multiple unrelated **Murder, She Wrote** appearances, myself.

 > **PEERS**: Because another Trill is involved, the Trillian government requested that I be present during the extradition.

Last one, you might recognize Peers as [Richard Lineback](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Lineback), who we saw in [*Symbiosis*]({% post_url 2022-10-13-symbiosis %}) as the one not played by Kirk's son.  Like everybody else, I guess, he'll also end up on **Enterprise**.

Also, did they phrase it "the Trillian government" as a reference to [**The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Hitchhiker%27s_Guide_to_the_Galaxy), where (one of the handful of surviving humans) Tricia McMillan takes that name?  I'd say no, but given that this episode directly revolves around a young female scientist---an astrophysicist, no less, and Dax will later recount receiving the same degree---pursued by an awkward dolt with boundary issues, it seems like a shockingly good fit.

 > **TANDRO**: Minister, through how many hosts have you survived?
 >
 > **PEERS**: The symbiont within me has survived through the adult lifetimes of seven hosts.

I suppose that this has nothing to do with the episode, because we somehow have two non-Federation legal systems at work with the Federation playing bit parts, but "the symbiont within me has survived through the adult lifetimes" seems to conclusively make Sisko's point, making him the worst witness for Tandro to bring in.

Actually, let's raise the issue behind this, hinted at in the (not quoted) exchange directing Bashir to hunt for evidence:  How does the Federation not have a body of case-law, here, or at least a literal Trill law providing a definition?  This echoes [*The Measure of a Man*]({% post_url 2023-01-19-measure-man %}), where we found out that nobody had ever considered the legal status of a non-biological creature before somebody wanting to strip Data down for parts.

In other words, does the Federation not guarantee universal civil liberties?  Putting these two episodes---plus the sequels to *The Measure of a Man* where we find out that "the truth for all time" actually didn't create a binding precedent---together suggests that every cohort has some legal obligation to justify their literal lives.

 > **SISKO**: He probably wasn't the ideal Trill. He drank a little too much. He could be more interested in women than maybe he should've been.

That seems like a bizarre way of putting things.  How much interest *should* a person take in women?  Sisko presumably means looking for sexual partners to the detriment of his responsibilities, but...maybe say that instead of implying that normal people don't care about women.

 > **TANDRO**: Commander, Starfleet officers do have the reputation of being honorable, and I judge you to be an honorable man. As an honorable man, do you think this crime should go unpunished?

Leave it to the antagonist (in a snappy blazer, no less) to become the first person to call Starfleet officers honorable...

 > **SISKO**: I prefer no crime to go unpunished.

I recoiled at this, too, but realize that at this time, we had retributive justice in full swing, with---as evidenced by the aforementioned **The American President**---the phrase "soft on crime" one of the most dangerous insults in politics, as opposed to something that apparently you now feebly call the Pope for disapproving of illegal wars.

For better context, months after this episode, the disastrous [crime bill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Violent_Crime_Control_and_Law_Enforcement_Act) would make its way through Congress with full-throated support of many people living in the affected communities.  Despite the fact that every criticism has borne out---excepting the Violence against Women Act that got folded in, which constantly faces a reauthorization fight because it works---the bill had wide support, higher among Black voters than white, with people imagining criminals everywhere and seeing policing as the only way to stop them.

In that sense, having Sisko see crime as a thing to punish rather than solve and rehabilitate fits, even though the line makes my skin crawl knowing how that belief has panned out.

 > **SISKO**: Then what is it that's holding you back? Help me. Damn it, if you were still a man.

This feels less defensible, persisting the idea that men solve their personal problems with fists, but that we should treat women as some entirely separate kind of thing.  It does almost become a joke about Sisko's temper, maybe another partial callback to *Q-Less*, but it doesn't look good.

## Conclusions

As suggested, because the episode largely straddles between foreign and Bajoran law, rather than the Federation, we don't get too much out of it, despite bulking it up with somewhat famous guest stars.

### The Good

Sisko returns to the tradition of commanding officers able to do the jobs of their reports.  And Starfleet does have some foreign supporters.

### The Bad

They treat calling experts to repair foreign equipment as a joke, rather than the way to get exotic equipment fixed.

Women not interested in a sexual relationship apparently need to game out every possible malicious interpretation of ever phrase uttered and action taken.  We also see mention that normal people don't take an interest in women.  And violence against men has a different connotation than violence against women in identical contexts.

When the Federation signs extradition treaties, they allow foreign governments to violently abduct people accused of crimes.  And we get a strong impression that the Federation doesn't protect the rights of its citizens equally.

The Federation has returned to supporting retributive justice.
