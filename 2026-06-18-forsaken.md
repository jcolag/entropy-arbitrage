## Previously...

I initially outlined the project [in this post]({% post_url 2020-01-02-trek-00 %}), for those falling into this from somewhere else.  In short, we attempt to use the details presented in *Star Trek* to assemble a view of what life looks like in the Federation.  This "phase" of the project changes from previous posts, once again.  **Deep Space Nine** takes place long after the original series and contemporary with/following **The Next Generation**, so we shouldn't expect identical politics and socialization.  Maybe more importantly, I enjoy the series more.

## The Forsaken

For continued context, this episode landed between [*Rightful Heir*]({% post_url 2025-04-24-rightful-heir %}) and [*Second Chances*]({% post_url 2025-05-01-second-chances %}), which---as many of you know---both probably had some influence on this series as it continues.

As maybe broader context, I remember this episode treated in the media as something of a "goodwill tour."  I have mentioned before that, because fandom mortifyingly hasn't changed much since [**The Animated Series**](/blog/tag/st-tas), many loud fans decried **Deep Space Nine** as absolutely not *Star Trek* as they saw it.  The complaints ranged from the (gasp) lack of travel to the less-striking designs to the moral ambiguities to, yes, dislike of showing a Black man (not always the term that they used) in charge.  At least one **Next Generation** performer indulged this, insisting that Roddenberry would have hated a series where people needed to deal with non-technical problems.

Now, I don't know if they made this decision behind-the-scenes because of this or because somebody liked the story idea and Majel Barrett had the time, but I remember coverage at the time framing this episode as the franchise itself asserting the show's validity by having the "first lady" come to play with the newer cast.  Lending credence to the idea, couple of years later, Barrett would also feature in a central episode of **DS9**'s competition **Babylon 5**, at least partly to tamp down the absurdly vitriolic rivalry---sadly sometimes amplified by the studios and (ahem) at least one chronically online creator---between the groups of fans.

 > Station Log, stardate 46925.1. We have been honored with a visit by a delegation of Federation ambassadors on a fact-finding mission to the wormhole. Fortunately I have just the officer to take them off my hands.

I have to admit, while I chuckle at the reveal as much as everyone else, this log entry makes the episode feel like a leftover script from **The Next Generation** more than something for this show.  Unlike Picard, who repeatedly made the point that only he and people who aspire to have his career do *real work*, while everybody else---politicians, ambassadors, administrators, laborers, and so forth---utterly *bores* him while they waste his time, Sisko often seems to delight in showing that he gets along with everyone, even if he occasionally has a mischievous motivation to it.  And Sisko also has a much broader job that often includes diplomatic work, meaning that he has a natural way of connecting with the ambassadors.

 > **TAXCO**: Madam Ambassador.

You might recognize our first ambassador as [Constance Towers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constance_Towers), from plenty of classic films, an extensive theater career, and (after this) a long run on **General Hospital** reviving a character[^Uj6TWq] originally played by Elizabeth Taylor.

[^Uj6TWq]:  Nobody cares, but seeing the character name in her Wikipedia page struck me as familiar and confused me, because I didn't watch daytime dramas in 1997 when Towers joined the cast, what with the office job during the day, but I *did* watch **General Hospital** because it aired as I got home from school and occasionally got pretty weird as if always testing the waters to go full-on **Dark Shadows**, which it would actually do around this time in spinning off the **Port Charles** series that I've never seen.  Anyway, Taylor asked for a role in maybe the era's biggest media event, the (ill-advised) wedding of Luke and Laura.  The resulting character comes in as the widow of the antagonist played by John Colicos, who you'll remember as playing Kor on the original series and later in this series and Baltar on **Battlestar Galactica**.  In any case, I needed to go on that dumpster dive to make sense out of my mention of Towers, so I present it here rather than letting it go to waste...

 > **LOJAL**: What are you recalibrating?

And you might recognize Lojal as [Michael Ensign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Ensign), still a few years from his most famous role as Benjamin Guggenheim in **Titanic**, but also showed up pretty much everywhere as minor characters, including the government minister in [*First Contact*]({% post_url 2024-02-15-first-contact %}), the episode, not the film.

 > **BASHIR**: Everything. It's a sweeping recalibration of all systems.
 >
 > **LOJAL**: I cannot speak for the others, but I would be fascinated to observe this recalibration, Doctor.

That does actually sound fairly intense.

And I appreciate the bit of [game theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Game_theory) in the gag, here.  Using recalibration of any single thing as your excuse, that sounds boring enough to pass muster.  But then you need to avoid picking a specific thing that might trigger somebody's interest.  To avoid that, Bashir lurches in the other direction, which now gives the impression that either something happened to *require* adjusting everything at once or that the crew found an approach that makes doing everything at once more valuable, which sounds worth checking out...

 > **TAXCO**: Are you actually suggesting we indulge in one of those disgusting Ferengi sex programs?

It occurs to me that, throughout **The Next Generation**, most prominently and explicitly in [*The Perfect Mate*]({% post_url 2024-10-03-perfect-mate %}), they made it abundantly clear that Starfleet officers primarily use holograms for sex.  But put the same thing in the hands of the Ferengi, and it spontaneously becomes a disgusting technology and worse use for it.

 > **LWAXANA**: Yes, and I know where it hurts the most, you little troll. Now, I want this room sealed, and I want everyone in it strip-searched until you find my brooch.

I realize that the A-plot needs to move, but this "crack down on crime at all costs" nonsense feels wildly out of character for Troi, no?

 > **COMPUTER**: Cardassian specifications accept operating efficiency within twenty percent.
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: Well, I don't. Anara?

I suppose that we should leave this for somebody else's posts, but how do the Cardassians even function, if they need all their equipment to tolerate eighty to a hundred twenty percent of normal power?  It must cost a fortune to harden everything that way.

Also, can they not update the specification?  That sounds like a configuration file somewhere...

 > **O'BRIEN**: It's a bloody inefficient system, and I'd trade it in for a Federation model tomorrow if I could. But it's all we've got. Do me a favor. Keep an eye on the exhaust plasma temperature. Computer, increase deuterium flow by five percent to reaction chamber two.

Didn't they establish this as an efficient system that happens to have degraded by thirteen percent?  Or does his hatred of Cardassians figure into his assessment?

 > **O'BRIEN**: I don't want to read your tech manual. Fine, we'll do it ourselves. Re-initializing reactor two. Directing plasma stream to conduits one-four-three, one-four-four, one...

In the post for [*The Nagus*]({% post_url 2026-05-07-nagus %}), among other episodes, I started questioning O'Brien's competence.  This deepens the puzzle, because I'll tell you that, if I ever had a colleague with O'Brien's track record of always falling behind on work and blaming the equipment, and we happened on that person shouting about doing everything from scratch instead of spending time reading the documentation, I feel pretty confident that we'd start calling that person an *ex-colleague* as quickly as possible...

 > **SISKO**: Root canal?
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: It's engineering shorthand, sir. I'll have to get into its guts and rebuild her from the ground up.

Can I point out that "a rebuild" more commonly serves as similar shorthand, directly says what it means, and has one fewer syllable, or does that come off as too petty...?

Less petty, every engineer that I have ever seen decide that they needed to tear everything down and start it all over from scratch left behind either a worse mess than they started with or never completed their project and so only got significantly less real work done.

 > **SISKO**: How long will that take?
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: Well, I'm still not that familiar with Cardassian technology, but I'd guess no more than two, three years.

To O'Brien's credit (who said that?), while that seems incredibly low to me, every engineer who I've seen come into a project with this attitude predicted that they'd take *nine months*.  Every.  Single.  One.  I guess that, if it works for making a new person[^hDbEhq], it probably works for software, too.

[^hDbEhq]:  Yes, I know that human gestation comes closer to forty to forty-two weeks.  Nobody rewriting code knows that, though...

Actually, it occurs to me that it seems weird that O'Brien needs to *guess* at this.  I have to imagine that he (or Sisko) knows how long it takes Federation engineers to build the computer systems from scratch for a facility their size, and this ill-advised project should have a similar scale.

 > **SISKO**: Chief, I want you to do what you have to do.
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: Thank you, sir. I'll get right on it.

I hate *everything* about this conversation, too.  O'Brien went full passive-aggressive surly teenager when Sisko asked him to justify allocating tens of thousands[^apwhD3] of engineer-hours, effort that could go to anything else, to O'Brien's personal side-gig.  And after watching the snit, Sisko approves the project.

[^apwhD3]:  Three years minus weekends and a couple of weeks' vacation, thirteen hours per day falls a bit short of ten thousand hours, so multiple people on the project gives us tens of thousands.  We could quibble over the shift length and number of engineers, but the outcome probably doesn't change much.

O'Brien could have said that the current computer adds however much overhead to every project, so this will pay for itself in however many months/years, but instead *pouted*.  And it worked.  And he perks right up when he gets his way, suggesting that he did it to manipulate his boss.

Like I said, I hate this scene.

 > **BASHIR**: And this is Ops.

And seriously, why does Sisko not want in on this?  Especially the eye-rolling genuinely feels like a Picard or Riker scene hastily repurposed.

 > **VADOSIA**: That's it? That's the whole thing?
 >
 > **BASHIR**: Well, yes, it is.
 >
 > **VADOSIA**: I was expecting more somehow.

Trekkies get so tiring, don't they, with all the claiming to love the philosophy, but then whining about special effects...?

 > **BASHIR**: The young woman over there has over three hundred years experience, Ambassador.

Yeah, but mostly experience in, like, art and carousing and the like, no?  I seem to remember that [*Dax*]({% post_url 2026-04-16-dax %}) made a big point about how Jadzia took all the science classes...

 > **LWAXANA**: I've never been with a shape-shifter.

Following up on my comment about the sudden personality changes, I tend to cut Troi a lot of slack in the franchise.  She has some aristocratic background that seems to have involved people not pushing back on her requests, but she generally seems to use her privilege to find outcasts to support them.  And she makes Picard uncomfortable.  All good qualities.  And I even like where this relationship ends up.

All that said, trying to seduce somebody at work, where they not only need to stay but have some politeness obligation, always feels like the worst possible thing that a person can do.  And sure, she only sexually harasses a cop who worked directly for fascists, but still...

 > **O'BRIEN**: Well done, computer.

You know if I didn't have 2026 as context for this episode, I might have overlooked this line, because O'Brien has little to no emotional regulation, so a wild mood swing barely registered.  But in 2026, when we all see privileged people act like this every day with LLMs?  O'Brien sees a computer that politely follows instructions in an intelligent-looking way, because he didn't create all the instructions, as a broken tool for him to dispose of and rebuild at his pleasure.  An *obedient* computer, however, he now sees as a companion worthy of his emotional investment.

 > **SISKO**: Have you thought of letting her catch you?

I don't want to diminish the literal textbook sexual harassment happening, here.  However, I do like that Sisko (correctly, I think) reads the situation as Odo mostly objecting to Troi's assertiveness and takes that objection off the table.  And I wonder if the conversation plays out differently if Odo responds here with a disinterest in Troi, rather than seeing mating as a nuisance.

 > **ODO**: Procreation does not require changing how you smell, or writing bad poetry, or sacrificing various plants to serve as tokens of affection. In any event, it's all irrelevant to me.

Seriously, did the Cardassians raise him on 1980s sitcoms?

 > **ODO**: I don't eat. This is not a real mouth, it is an approximation of one. I do not have an esophagus or a stomach or a digestive system. I am not like you. Every sixteen hours, I turn into a liquid.
 >
 > **LWAXANA**: I can swim.

Actually, I think she started to change my mind, here.  As a telepath, who made a production of her inability to read Ferengi but hasn't said a word about her ability to sense Odo, and especially this line, it starts to look less like sexual harassment than...well, therapy (ironically), provoking him into dealing with his pent-up feelings of isolation.  Once trapped, after all, she only talks about getting to know him.

That still doesn't excuse interfering with him at work, mind you---never do that, even if you think that you know better---but the story has more of a shape than **TNG**'s usual "sigh, a cougar on the make" approach.

 > **O'BRIEN**: If we were on a starship, I'd reroute the EPS power flow in a couple of hours but with this computer, there's no telling how long it'll take.

And he already hates the computer again, because he didn't get what he wanted.

 > **LWAXANA**: My daughter and I were once trapped aboard a Ferengi cargo ship, and it was dreadful. Well, all right, it wasn't actually dreadful, it was mildly lamentable. And it was all because of that loathsome Daimon. Well, actually he wasn't altogether loathsome. He was just slightly repulsive. But he did have a certain charm, in an insufferable sort of way. Of course, he was totally at the mercy of his uncontrollable passion for me, which means he wasn't all bad now, doesn't it? You know, it wasn't all passion. There was some negligible commercial interest involved, but oh, the passion, that was perfectly real, and kind of sweet, in a way. He was so helpless. At first, it was totally a question of expediency when I made love with him. What are you looking at?

If you don't recall [*Ménage à Troi*]({% post_url 2023-10-12-menage-troi %}), have a recap...

 > **BASHIR**: Ah. So now you take the same perverse pleasure in doing it to me.
 >
 > **SISKO**: Exactly.

Hazing does seem in keeping with Starfleet tradition, sadly.

 > **SISKO**: It was a simple misunderstanding over his attempt to coax a young Ensign to his quarters against her will. But...Don't hit one of them, Doctor. I'm not nearly as understanding as Curzon was.

Whoa.  For maybe the first time in the franchise, somebody has taken an actual stance against sexual assault.  He didn't insist that his guy wouldn't do it, or that something analogous to sexual assault seemed bad in certain contexts, but that he punched a powerful person for pressuring a woman to join him in private quarters.

 > **O'BRIEN**: Sir, when you work with a computer as much as I do, you get to know it. They're all very different. Working with the *Enterprise* computer was like dancing a waltz. With this computer, it's always been like a wrestling match, 'til we started downloading the probe.

Or---here me out, here---on the *Enterprise*, he only had to push the transporter button, so everything already worked, and so he assumed that the computer did it all for him and not dozens of engineers.  But as a man-child incapable of handling responsibility, putting him in charge makes him think that he needs to fight with the computer instead of understanding the overall system.

No, I don't know when this post became lessons in engineering, either...

 > **O'BRIEN**: It came here when we downloaded the probe's files. If we upload those same files back to the probe, it might go with them.

...*What*?

"I downloaded some malware, so let me submit a copy of the file back to the server and how that my original loaded copy vanishes"?  I realize that I shouldn't expect any understanding of how file transfers actually work, *especially* since this feels like the first time in media history that a character has used "download" and "upload" as the right verbs.  But you "move" digital files by copying them.  Copying them back doesn't make them go away...

 > **COMPUTER**: Stand by. Unable to complete requested function. Please abort and retry.

No, computer, you say [Abort, Retry, Ignore?](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abort,_Retry,_Fail%3F) during this period...

 > **O'BRIEN**: It's not as easy as I thought it would be, Commander.

Shocking that his plan went nowhere...

 > **LOJAL**: I've been reading the reports of your Chief of Operations, Doctor. They gave me the impression that he was a competent engineer.

O'Brien must have quite the talent for creative writing, then, because every episode makes him seem like the absolute worst person to have in charge, here.

 > **ODO**: I was always very self-sufficient.
 >
 > **LWAXANA**: I'm sure you had to be, to survive, being so different from everyone else.

Far too many people need to have that conversation.

 > **ODO**: My way of trying to fit in. I found I could be entertaining. Odo, be a chair. I'm a chair. Odo, be a razor-cat. I'm a razor-cat. Life of the party. I hate parties.

I don't generally bother to look at Odo's lines, since he doesn't have Federation citizenship or even any cultural connection to the Federation, plus most of his lines tend to frame even his work as some weird unofficial part of the Bajoran militia.  However, I wanted to draw attention to this, because we have seen similar lines from almost every outsider.  Spock, Data, and Worf especially have their moments talking indirectly about how their social connections often only saw them as novelties to exploit.  Without a Starfleet commission, though, Odo gets to say it bluntly.

 > **O'BRIEN**: The computer's not responding. None of the fire suppression systems are coming online.

You could almost believe that slowing the computer to a crawl on a space station might pan out badly...

 > **O'BRIEN**: It's not paying any attention to me.

They could've named so many episodes after this line.  The guy absolutely has Main Character Syndrome...

 > **LWAXANA**: My hair. Nobody's ever seen me like this.

...Haven't we, though?  They don't have much lighting, so maybe she has a crew cut or something, but I feel like most of her **TNG** appearances went with hair that at least *looked* like what we see.  Do we toss this in the same category as twentieth century humans reacting to Spock or Worf far out of proportion to their actual appearances?

 > **O'BRIEN**: I don't see why not. It's happy. It's not bothering us anymore. It seems the humane thing to do.

I can't help noting the similarity to [*Elementary, Dear Data*]({% post_url 2022-12-08-dear-data %}), where O'Brien treats this creature, which nearly killed everybody as it lashed out, with more respect and dignity than almost every other non-human who he has dealt with, other than Tosk in [*Captive Pursuit*]({% post_url 2026-04-02-captive-pursuit %}).  And like Moriarty in the former episode, they make a big show of keeping the novel creature in the computers, but it'll never come up again.

Honestly, especially with Barrett doing the voice for so many years across shows, it seems pretty weird that the franchise took until the tail end of **Discovery** to have the computer as an occasional character treated like a member of the crew.

## Conclusions

Quite a bit of the episode feels off, but we'll push ahead regardless.

### The Good

Sisko does a fairly good job of standing up for women's rights.  He both refuses to condemn an assertive woman and admits that he once assaulted an ambassador who tried to push a young woman too far.

### The Bad

We have returned to the idea that people with administrative or diplomatic jobs mostly waste time and energy.

Bias against Ferengi continues, as their work and activities get derided as disgusting for doing what we've routinely seen among Starfleet personnel.

While almost certainly applied to the wrong character, we see that at least some in the Federation have a strong "Law and Order" bend, wanting to see aggressive steps taken to catch petty thieves no matter the cost to the innocent.

O'Brien hits almost every bad-engineer-and-worse-manager stereotype available, refusing to make clean configuration changes or read the manual, using onerous jargon for plain-language concepts, picking arbitrary numbers for work estimates, and so forth.  He even makes it clear that he prefers his computers subservient to correct.  But he apparently heaps praise on himself in reports back to Starfleet.  And yet somehow, he gets what he wants after openly sulking.

People still haven't figured out that going to somebody's place of work to flirt with them reads as dangerous and predatory, rather than adorable.

Analogous to the unfortunate obsession with status that we've seen elsewhere, we find that at least some parts of Starfleet have a specific tradition of processing the pain inflicted on them early in their careers by inflicting it on people early in their careers.
