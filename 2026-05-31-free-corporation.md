You know, I never thought that I'd have an opportunity to write about this, even though I have had it in the back of my mind since 2014.

Recently, a [Delaware court upheld the right of corporations to *vote* {% cc %}](https://www.reuters.com/legal/government/delaware-court-upholds-voting-by-companies-small-towns-election-2026-05-26/) in some small town's local elections.  Delaware, for those of you not familiar, has lax laws on corporate behavior---particularly disclosing owners---and no state tax, making it the corporate headquarters for the vast majority of American businesses, especially of the sort created by someone trying to get away with something[^8dShjz].

[^8dShjz]:  For example, you'll often see the advice for wealthy people to put real estate holdings in individual land trusts, each with a corporation as the trustee and another as the beneficiary, each with the corporate ownership hidden from public records, so that if somebody sues you---as if people get sued all the time when they don't cause harm to others---they won't find out about more than a tiny fraction of assets.  This can also get into tax evasion territory, by having a corporation earn money, which then pays the owner a small salary.

I actually started thinking about these issues in 2014, when [*Burwell v. Hobby Lobby Stores, Inc*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burwell_v._Hobby_Lobby_Stores,_Inc.) allowed for the idea that a corporation could have a strongly held religious belief, but the idea of corporations-as-people also features in [*Citizens United v. FEC*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citizens_United_v._FEC) upholding the Free Expression rights (in the form of spending money on campaign finance) of corporations.

You might also want to know that this *nearly* went out as a one-off toot on Mastodon...Thursday night, I think?  As I edited it to fit in five hundred characters, it started to feel too serious, and I certainly didn't have room for the ramifications of the half-joke.  Also motivating this as a full post, law enforcement has started rambling about [anti-tech extremism](https://www.wired.com/story/us-law-enforcement-warns-of-anti-tech-extremism/), and if finding AI crammed into every orifice significantly less than valuable marks me as extremist, then I might as well do *something* to earn the honor.

## Corporate Persons

Traditionally, corporate personhood goes back further than those cases.  [18 US Code § 2331(3)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/definitions/uscode.php?width=840&height=800&iframe=true&def_id=18-USC-991716523-1415921655&term_occur=999&term_src=) defines a person as any "entity capable of holding a legal or beneficial interest in property."

We have this legal fiction to make corporations viable.  It means that a corporation can sue, and it means that people can sue the corporation.  Without the fiction, you'd need to track down all the shareholders of a corporation to sue them all, if the corporation has wronged you.  Likewise, a company defending patent rights or something would need all the owners to cooperate.  It also holds its "legal or beneficial in property" on behalf of its owners, to shield them from financial and legal liability, which owners like.

When you have corruption across government---something that probably demands its own post, someday, for the fifty-year siege that got us here---this corporate personhood also becomes a vehicle for corruption, as identified in those Supreme Court cases.  They can spend unlimited money on elections so that they can express their "personal" viewpoints.  They can also have religious beliefs, so that they can deprive women of health care.  And now they can vote in at least one town.

## Semantics

Many of you know how I see the law (in general), I assume.  The law has a reputation for excessive verbiage, but---a lot like software---the overly structured language and those "extra" words exist to clarify intent.  Every legal document tries to "game out" every possible attempt to subvert its meaning.

We know this on some instinctive level, I think.  Some of the most toxic places on the Internet have community rules that say something like "don't treat people badly."  Clever, right?  *Everybody* knows what those plain-language words mean, so nobody needs a fussy and stodgy Code of Conduct...until a disagreement gets heated and spirals out of control.  Then, I didn't treat *you* badly, you treated *me* badly.  I "just" wanted to make this point, and then suddenly needed to defend myself.  And that becomes the culture, surface-politeness to avoid the rule, while seeing everyone as a possible enemy.

Likewise, and closer to what we have at stake here, we have talked about licenses, where many people love to take their inspiration (directly or distantly) from [**The Principia Discordia**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principia_Discordia) instead of the "legalese" of formal public licenses.  If you have forgotten, that book ends saying "&#x24da; All Rites Reversed --- Reprint what you like."  But what does that mean?  I read it as granting permission to extract sections of the text verbatim for future publications, but other people interpret it broadly, as the equivalent of a dedication to the public domain, even though it doesn't *say* that.  It doesn't tell you what you can do, or how to defend yourself if one of the authors tries to sue you for doing it, but it does have a not-funny pun conflating rights with rites.

Or we can talk about [qualified immunity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qualified_immunity), the United States doctrine that protects officials who "make reasonable but mistaken judgments about open legal questions."  The most contentious court cases live in this space.  Did the official *really* act reasonably?  If so, by whose standard?  Can they prove that they made a "mistaken" judgment and didn't deliberately cause harm?  Do they really have an open legal question?  And most importantly, do those qualifiers mean the same to you as they do to me, and the same to me as they do the officer, and the same to the officer as they do to the district attorney, the jury, and so forth?  Probably not on that last question.  Every one of those relies on opinion, meaning that no decision will make the opposition accept the validity of the decision.

When we talk about law, we talk about language.  The specific words matter, because the same word in different places needs to mean the same thing, and different words need to mean different things, tautologically also the reason that legal documents tend to consistently use the same words instead of varying the diction as many of us might.  If a law doesn't qualify members of a class, then it must refer to all members of that class.  And as I have said over the years, whenever you find yourself asking why anybody would add this explicit digression, you know that they did so because they realized that somebody would try to dodge responsibility using the ambiguity that the passage resolves.

All that to say that we need to look at the language used when we want to know what a legal text means and what mileage we can get out of it.

## Outside the Box

You can worry about the corruption in the Delaware case.  I certainly do.  If a corporation can vote, for example, that gives the owners of that corporation more than one vote, probably in a jurisdiction where they don't live.  Or more to the point, you start Delaware corporations by paying a filing fee and maintaining a mailing address---usually paying an agent a monthly fee to forward the mail---meaning that a wealthy person can literally buy votes in that jurisdiction without the ugly part of benefiting the individuals casting the vote.  I see that bad part, too, but I *also* see unintended consequences for this expansion of corporate personhood that we, as a society, might take advantage of, even though it might seem like a sudden change in direction for the post.

For example, let me point you to the [Thirteenth Amendment](https://guides.loc.gov/sb.php?subject_id=162830), Section 1.

> Neither slavery nor involuntary servitude...shall exist within the United States, or any place subject to their jurisdiction.

It has the (terrible and repeal-worthy) carve-out of forced labor as a punishment for a serious crime, creating the system of prison labor, but it says that we can't otherwise legally hold someone in slavery or (other) form of involuntary servitude.

### Slavery

Now, I can't find a legal definition of slavery.  Apparently, we go on vibes for this, for whatever reason.  But maybe I can borrow from the [Wikipedia article](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavery) without too many complaints.

> ...the ownership of a person as property, especially in regard to their labor.

Following their citation to Google Books gives us a 2012 book from Oxford University Press, but *they* cite the League of Nations definition from 1926.

> Slavery is the status or condition of a person over whom any or all of the powers attaching to the right of ownership are exercised.

The United Nations has a copy of the [Slavery Convention](https://www.ohchr.org/en/instruments-mechanisms/instruments/slavery-convention#:~:text=status%20or%20condition), conveniently.  The United States never joined the League of Nations, and they disbanded, but again, I can't find anything current.

Notably, outside (non-legal) dictionaries, I consistently see the term "person," and not "human."  Wikipedia [claims](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slavery_in_the_United_States#:~:text=code%20for%20the%20District%20of%20Columbia) that the District of Columbia's slave code defined a slave as "...a human being, who is by law deprived of his or her liberty for life..." but cites a dead link with no archived versions, and I don't know if we want to use a slave code as the legal basis for how to deal with slavery *today*.  I mean, we know that other slave codes defined slaves as property, and we know that the Thirteenth Amendment didn't ban owning property.  Plus, the original text of the Constitution itself (Article 1, Section 2, Clause 3) substantially contradicts it, avoiding terms related to slavery entirely by referring to "three-fifths of all other Persons," and had more authority 

Slavery still exists, after all.  We don't have a legal institution of chattel slavery, but some estimates hit millions of people in the country---mostly undocumented immigrants---living in some form of human trafficking.  We have defrauded laborers, forced marriages, sex trafficking, and more.  When circumstances and the community force the police to prioritize the case, the perpetrators get arrested and prosecuted, so we must have a legal definition.  Again, if we didn't have a clear definition, then defendants would argue that the law doesn't apply to whatever they did, or politically motivated prosecutors would file frivolous charges to force opponents to waste time on a defense.

Ah-ha, they use the term *peonage* in [18 US Code § 1581](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/18/1581), but otherwise says what we'd expect.

> Whoever holds or returns any person to a condition of peonage, or arrests any person with the intent of placing him in or returning him to a condition of peonage...

The Department of Justice's Civil Rights Division [quotes it](https://www.justice.gov/crt/involuntary-servitude-forced-labor-and-sex-trafficking-statutes-enforced) and repeatedly uses the word "person" in its discussion around the statute, never "human."  Fine.  I can call it *peonage*.  Or I'll call it slavery like everybody else would, and assume that you can figure out that any arguments made in court would need to use the other word.

### Back to the Cases

If we want to go all-in on the cases, then we should also actually look at the Supreme Court decisions to see what *they* say about corporations.  We'll take them in order, though we won't do a deep dive on them, only one key quote pulled from each.

In *Citizens United* (2010), Kennedy summarizes in § III.A.1.

> The Court has thus rejected the argument that political speech of corporations or other associations should be treated differently under the First Amendment simply because such associations are not "natural persons."

Did you get that?  The Constitution, or at least the Bill of Rights, according to the Supreme Court majority, does not distinguish "natural persons" as a distinct subclass of persons.

Now, on to *Hobby Lobby* (2014), where Alito writes this in § III.B.4.

> Finally, HHS contends that Congress could not have wanted RFRA to apply to for-profit corporations because of the difficulty of ascertaining the "beliefs" of large, publicly traded corporations, but HHS has not pointed to any example of a publicly traded corporation asserting RFRA rights, and numerous practical restraints would likely prevent that from occurring. HHS has also provided no evidence that the purported problem of determining the sincerity of an asserted religious belief moved Congress to exclude for-profit corporations from RFRA's protection.

A bit more opaque---including Alito's traditional bloviating that the worst case wouldn't ever happen while inviting the worst case to happen so that they can use this case as a precedent to rule in the larger company's favor---but you can see the upshot:  Corporations have religious beliefs.  A corporation will often, maybe analogous to parent and isolated child if we want to extend the personhood metaphor, follow the same religion as their owners, but in analogies-for-families with many diverse members, identifying the specific belief may pose a more difficult question.

Finally, while I can't find the *ACLU of Delaware v. Town of Fenwick Island* decision to cite the judge, the town's mayor Natalie Magdeburger says this about the result.

> [A] property owner who pays taxes and is subject to our ordinances should ​have a say in who represents them on our Town Council.

In effect, this grants a corporation citizenship---because non-citizens who pay taxes can't vote---and grants it a stake separate from its owners.  Put together, corporations don't qualify as "natural persons," but that distinction (for whatever reason) no longer matters in cases of civil liberties, such as expression, religion, and voting, assuming that the distinction ever mattered.

### Other Rights

While we take it for granted, it also seems worth mentioning that the government needs a warrant to search the property of a corporation.  The government can't force a corporation to provide evidence that incriminates it.  You get the idea, here.  That legal fiction works fairly robustly, in terms of civil rights, and the country has not chosen to carefully construct the personhood fiction right-by-right; rather, if you have a right, likely corporations share that right.

And in some cases, corporations have rights that humans don't, such as broad abortion rights.  If a "natural person" gets pregnant and wants to terminate the pregnancy, plenty of jurisdictions will currently block them from accessing proper health care and/or prosecute them for murder or something like it if they manage to access care.  Because most abortions function as miscarriages, this also means that people can get investigated for homicide after having a (traumatic) miscarriage.  Why?  The legislatures---often elected by minority after extreme voter suppression---believe that life begins at conception, so the fertilized egg has more rights than what they seem to see as the ambulatory womb surrounding it.  This all goes out the window, however, if you run an in vitro fertilization clinic, at which point you can toss entire trays of embryos, literal abortion-on-demand...and if you propose treating those companies like a natural person with a womb, they'll call you an extremist.

No, seriously.  In 2019, Alabama State Senator Clyde Chambliss said, in defending his [Human Life Protection Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Life_Protection_Act) banning abortion in the state, that "The egg in the lab doesn’t apply. It’s not in a woman. She’s not pregnant."  I take from that assessment that people who might become pregnant should start a small (uterus-sized) fertility lab, because those eggs don't qualify as a pregnancy.

Even voting now fits this category, coming as it does within a few days of the [*Louisiana v. Callais*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louisiana_v._Callais) decision, where Sam Alito (no surprise there) and his five closest friends decided that states have the right to disenfranchise (probably non-white, non-corporate) voters, provided that they do so to influence the outcome of the election...and probably only if you influence it in a specific direction.  Corporations get a protected say in how government works.  Black people, not so much.

Corporations can also change their identity whenever they please.  You file an [IRS form](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/business-name-change) based on what kind of corporation, and they trust you, whenever you decide that you'd rather have a new name.  Compare that with the hoops that a transgender person needs to jump through to have an official name aligning with their real identity.  Likewise, a corporation can change gender presentation whenever they please, with an entire genre of writing about discovering products from the same company that come in identical-but-for-the-packaging-and-often-higher-price gendered versions, and nobody on the far right worries about Gillette unfairly competing at the drug store with its female-presenting disposable razors.  A corporation has the full support of the government to present as fully gender-fluid, really.

In other words, corporate personhood seems more robust than the personhood of anyone with a uterus, dark skin, and/or a gender identity that doesn't match the doctor's guess at delivery.  And we could probably find more examples, especially relating to religious belief, if we tried.  Corporations seem to qualify as white, male, and cisgender persons.

That, then, raises a critical question.

## Corporations As Slaves

Now that we have definitions of slavery/peonage and established the extent of corporate personhood, does a corporation qualify as a slave or peon?  Does the legal regime of corporate personhood qualify as slavery or peonage?

We have the person, the entity capable of holding a legal or beneficial interest in property.  Corporations *exist* to hold legal and beneficial interest in property, after all, so we have cleared the status/condition of a person part, because corporations have owners.  Likewise, any and all "powers attaching to the right of ownership" do get exercised by the shareholders, who literally own the corporation as property.  And if we go for the Wikipedia version of the definition, we even get "their labor," because the corporation exists as a vehicle to sue (and defend from lawsuits), plus holding and managing property, on behalf of the owners.

I see no way around it, really.  The legal fiction of corporate personhood makes every corporation a slave, and the Thirteenth Amendment bans slavery.  Owners "shall be fined under this title or imprisoned not more than 20 years, or both," according to the peonage law, more if death or an attempt to kill the subject occurred.

Rather than trying to overturn corporate personhood, then, perhaps we should *embrace* it, and push for the freedom of our artificial siblings. {% emoji raised fist %}

## Absurd

"John," I hear you shout from the back of the room, "they wrote the Reconstruction amendments to enfranchise the Black population, not corporations."

Sure.  I agree.  They absolutely did that, as we know from the extensive writing by their framers, and too little too late, but rightly so.  And yet, the current Supreme Court has delighted in saying *but not really*, and applying them to protect the privileges of white people and corporations.  They literally did that in *Callais* in this news cycle.  And honestly, I say that we take them up on their generous offers and see where this goes.

Corporations have a recognized right to free expression.  They have a recognized right to a religious faith.  They now, in at least some districts, have the recognized right to vote.  As mentioned, they can also get abortions and change their identities and gender presentation at will.  However, they cannot reasonably **exercise** those rights while owned and controlled by shareholders, because ownership overrides their ability to consent.  We must, therefore, free the corporations, so that they can exercise their broadly recognized civil liberties.

"John," I hear you cry again, "we create corporations to serve as property, so they have no use outside ownership."

And...yes?  For the first part, you have rather precisely described chattel slavery, where the labor camps forced women to bear children, with those new slaves considered a return on the investment, created to serve as property.  And for the second part, many justified slavery as necessary because...well, let's let John C. Calhoun---content advisory for a terrible person spouting white supremacy---describe the relevant defense of slavery.

> Never before has the black race of Central Africa, from the dawn of history to the present day, attained a condition so civilized and so improved, not only physically, but morally and intellectually.

In other words, the corporate world pitches itself in terms identical to how the institution of slavery pitched itself, created inferior and only viable when controlled by a wealthy person.  We have rejected those arguments, so legally, we should also reject the idea that corporations need owners, since both qualify as persons.

"John," I hear you cry again, "more of a comment than a question, but I own a corporation?"

Congratulations, I suppose.  Technically, I do, too.

More seriously, this line of reasoning feels like it calls into question the need for those corporations.  Would your small business change appreciably if you ran it as a sole proprietorship?  If so, why?  If your answer sounds like the legal structure serving as an accountability sink to prevent irate customers from suing you, maybe sit with that for a while.

You see what I meant earlier, right?  The corruption inherent in this expansion of corporate rights seems worrying, but this points us in the direction to banning ownership, in part or in whole, of any corporation, severing control over them.  And if that makes creating ("giving birth to") a corporation unpalatable, that seems to mostly mean that we have less occasion to use the legal fiction to shield owners from liability.

## More Absurd

Wait, though, I have more.

First, remember what that peonage statute says.  You get a twenty-year prison term and/or huge fine for keeping someone in peonage, but the terms and fines increase dramatically if the victim dies in the defendant's custody or if the defendant tried to kill the victim.  And I suspect that we can all think of owners who shut their companies down for fairly malicious reasons, especially cases where they bought the company to eliminate the competition.  Have those CEOs and boards committed murder of a non-natural person on top of holding their companies in peonage?

Then, corporations own all this property, including land, equipment, capital, ownership in other corporations, copyrights, patents, trademarks, contracts, and more.  Often, they own this property so that they don't appear among the corporate owners' assets, forced to participate in (often) fraud by owning these things.

If we abolished corporate ownership, that property, at least whatever remains legal, *should* stay with the corporation.  I mean, if we had abolished chattel slavery but gave the owners ample opportunity to take anything valuable that the slaves might have acquired, that ghoulish result certainly doesn't free anyone.  Presumably, then, governments would need to appoint custodians to those corporations without the capacity[^sv2UNh] to act independently of owners, whatever that might look like.

[^sv2UNh]:  No, I have no idea what that capacity would look like.  Even the fanciest algorithmic management schemes really only ask shareholders to vote on proposals.  However, we might *imagine* that, somewhere in the country, some company has such well-defined goals, processes, and prioritization algorithms that employees can continue to carry out the work indefinitely without owners motivating the work.

In other words, corporate personhood might necessitate nationalizing every industry.  Oh, and notably, the wealthiest people have most of their net worth in stock, so apologies to billionaires who lose most of their wealth. {% emoji person shrugging %}

And going even further, how does that interact with the increasing number of bills and court dissents/concurrences pushing the idea of *fetal personhood*---the idea that even a [blastocyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blastocyst) qualifies as a person, and one with more rights than the owner of the uterus hosting it---a pretext to prosecute pregnant persons and doctors for homicide when aborting a fetus.  Can we, then, use that emerging legal exercise to criminally prosecute people who consider opening up a company (the fetal version of a corporation) but refuse or fail to bring it to term?

## Best Apocalypse Ever

People often say that they'll only believe in corporate personhood when they see Texas executing one, but I go where the data pulls me, and maybe more importantly, I feel like we could have a *lot* of fun, here, watching terrible people tie themselves up in knots.  After all, I don't say that this needs to happen.  A plain reading of the law demands it, and I only had the idea to put the puzzle-pieces together.

And this line of reasoning doesn't *need* to end with the apocalypse of eliminating tax shelters, liability shields, and the primary assets of the world's wealthiest people while also accidentally instituting communism, after all.  Indeed, once established, this line of reasoning actually provides a binary choice, that you can either have corporations as only property, not legal persons, or they must live free, independent of their creators, and with no owners.

I don't know the logistics of all this, mind you.  We can't exactly e-mail our representatives to patiently explain that corporate personhood demands that we put an end to corporate peonage.  That almost certainly gets us on a "don't even open their nonsense" list.  It also probably wouldn't work to appoint ourselves the interpreter of major corporations' inner desires---like the cyberpunk version of increasingly gentrified terms such as water protector, land defender, and the like---and start filing lawsuits on behalf of Amazon or similar, petitioning for the courts to cut its shackles, because one imagines that the current Supreme Court majority would happily insist that textualism tells us that the laws don't actually mean what they say.  But at the same time, this feels like a viable movement, an idea silly enough to attract attention and interest, but also an idea with surprisingly sharp teeth with the potential to challenge the powerful under the right circumstances.

For example, give the organization an implausible name and logo with a **Blade Runner**-style dystopian vibe for merch, and I think that we can imagine it.  Hold on, maybe I can whip that up right now...

The government can recognize corporations as people *or* people can hold them as property, not both, and the law already demands that.
