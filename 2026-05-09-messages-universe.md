## Messages from the Universe Graveyard

The Itch page has the following description, minus one line that belongs elsewhere.

> Explore a dead MMO and leave messages for other players to find.  
>
> - 100+ abandoned and glitchy rooms to discover
> - 20k+ words, mostly in messages
> - Social (?) interaction
> - 0 NPCs, enemies, combat encounters, skills, items, classes, crafting recipes... (None of that works anymore. Sorry, RPG fans.)
>
> Interactive fiction made for [Shufflecomp 2023](https://itch.io/jam/shufflecomp-2023). Ranked 1st in "game goodness" out of 15 entries, which is pretty cool.
>
> You can spend anywhere from a few minutes to a few hours in this game.  
>
> Interactive Fiction Database entry [here](https://ifdb.org/viewgame?id=d5k4zk54m4iqxhvc).

The GitHub repository has something similar but distinct to say.

> Contains source code for Messages From the Universe Graveyard, an interactive fiction game originally made for [Shufflecomp 2023](https://itch.io/jam/shufflecomp-2023).
>
> The game was made with [iffinity](https://github.com/zehanort/iffinity), a Tweego-like interactive fiction engine by Sotiris Niarchos.
>
> Needed but not included in the repository due to being build outputs:
>
> - export/index.html
> - src/meta/crumblingcastle.js
>
> They are generated with `build`. The files in `src` are the actual source code of the final iffinity project, while the files in `script_files` get compiled by `build` into `src/meta/crumblingcastle.js`, which is loaded into the final project (as a story script).
>
> Image and audio assets aren't included in the repo since they'd make it unnecessarily large, but they come with the downloaded version of the game you can get on Itch.
>
> **In other words, `build` takes all the changes you've made to the source and applies them to the new index.html output. Open index.html after running `build` to see the new version of the frontend.**

Now you know what I know, maybe more, since I probably only skimmed most of that...

## What Works Well?

You'll find a lot, most of it seems interesting, and it (almost) carries something like a story.  I started mostly skimming when it became clear that we don't necessarily do anything, but I'd call the descriptions interesting.  The images also, and this probably helps, feel cohesive.

Unless I missed something, I also appreciate that the main problem doesn't get an explanation, unless I missed something.  It hints at the nature of things, but doesn't demand that we "stop and think" about (I assume) pretty much the same plot twist from [**Forgotten**]({% post_url 2020-07-04-forgotten %}).

## What Works...Less Well?

{% include lesswell.md %}

Maybe this reflects more on me than the game/story, but the experience feels significantly worse for not having a way to distinguish between messages left behind by characters and those left behind by players.  You can even see it *in* the messaging, where (I believe) one of the early players keeps complimenting the likely clues on getting so far so fast.  But also, I don't know if any of that has more (or less) relevance to the story than the person venting about their loneliness or family drama.

Also, I don't really understand the "puzzle design," here.  While you can guess a bunch of destinations by guessing numbers that a person on the modern Internet would likely pick, and make your way to six destinations, I often found clues to rooms in either messages left by prior users---which you could argue "spoils" the puzzle by providing most of the map---or in the places that I found *from* that room.

## Opportunities

You can find the [source code on GitHub](https://github.com/kadw108/Messages-From-the-Universe-Graveyard), which has included somewhat regular updates as recently as last year.

## What's Adaptable?

The setting itself could probably work in another context.  And if you can separate the user-generated content from the character journal entries, as I mentioned earlier, you do get some indications of the background and a few recurring characters.

## Six Years Hence:  Orang-U

I mentioned last week that, as these things turn out, the 2026 calendar overlays neatly on the 2020 calendar.  For example, that means that [finishing up with **Avalon High**]({% post_url 2026-05-02-avalon-high-3 %}) came exactly on the sixth anniversary of announcing the book club.  As such, for probably as long as I find it fun (and only the first posts in a run, if I broke it up), I'll highlight the project from six years ago.

Ideally, I'll have something to add to what I said then, but I can't promise that some of these updates won't stretch far beyond a reminder that a post or a series of posts exist(s).  Like this week, sometimes I'll probably also use it as an excuse to talk about the project overall...

This time through, six years ago today, we *really* started the book club with [**Orang-U: An Ape Goes To College**]({% post_url 2020-05-09-orangu %}).  In the time since that post, I have discovered that co-creator [Matt Lee](https://mat.tl/) tried to launch a Free Culture Foundation at least twice before launching GNU Social, the federated social networking system that evolved into the Fediverse of today, including apps such as Mastodon, Write As, Castropod, and so forth.

Lee's website says that production on the *Orang-U* sequel(s) started sometime last year, so we may soon (for liberal values of the word) have more to the story.  The [project website](https://orangumovie.com/) also shows that Lee recently tried working the original film into an almost-silent horror(?) short built on still-images, [**Dreams into Nightmares**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gOcD0IdIRCI).

I have probably said this before, but it might bear repeating that, six years ago, my vision for these posts looked more like the Sunday rant-style posts, posting irregularly when I learned about some new project.  Even then, I worried about running out of possibilities and "needing" to talk about works only available under non-commercial licenses, spreading beyond actual Free Culture to keep the idea active.  Instead, with a gap of only three weeks---when I conflated the license of a preservation effort with that of the underlying project---we have talked about the works of at least two hundred creators[^oJMhx] across three hundred eleven posts (plus the announcement) in six years.

[^oJMhx]:  Counting the *Full Title* or *Full Titles* lines from the quasi-structured data at the top of these posts gives me a hundred ninety posts with something different.  That only gives us a lower bound, though, because several weeks dealt with anthologies, where we might have blown through half a dozen separate creators, and some projects came out of collaborations between multiple creators.

Over six years, I haven't needed to stretch the definition of Free Culture[^wVU1Q2], haven't needed to talk about reference material and journalism---important, but hard to talk about an encyclopedia, for example, in a "book club" format---and haven't even needed to keep rushing back to [Blender Open Movies](https://studio.blender.org/films/) or return to any creator's other work yet.  In other words, even as a vocal proponent of Free Culture, the amount and variety of material out there impresses me and I hope that it impresses readers, too.

[^wVU1Q2]:  Actually, I may have stretched the idea slightly from my original vision, by occasionally including once-commercial works where the creator or owner later gave it an explicit public license or equivalent statement.  As I recall, I started out dismissing such projects because it can feel like it sends a message that Free Culture only needs you to slap a license on something after discovering that it won't rake in the cash...

Anyway, that all started six years ago today with **Orang-U**, which while I don't think that I'd ever call it *good* except with massive qualifiers (budget comes immediately to mind), I enjoy it at least partly for the number of story ideas that it crams in.  Even though other projects probably provided more technological artifacts that a team could use to adapt or expand the story, this floats non-human college students, people who sometimes know about the film, demonic law firms, weird television shows, haunted mass transit, and more, which feels like more of an invitation to play in the sandbox than (for example) unedited footage might.  It also feels like more of a rejection of how an established studio would try to streamline the story into something with a fixed marketing demographic.  In some (I hope complimentary) ways, it feels like having a conversation with a toddler, where in moments of mischief, they see an entire fictional world that comes off as entirely unhinged to the rest of us as they describe it, but also has a shocking consistency and internal logic.
