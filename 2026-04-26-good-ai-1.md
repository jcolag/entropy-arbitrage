As a word of warning, this post exploded in size as I tried to zero in on what I wanted to say, so I'll break it across three weeks, so that I don't go dumping ten thousand words on people on a Sunday morning.

Over the past few weeks, I have seen a surprising number of "Open Web" types---no, I won't name names, but you can probably all figure out whose blogs I read, and they numbered at least three that I can remember---say something that I find at least mildly perplexing.  Over and again, I see people who I feel should know better talk about how, really, the AI bubble's problem comes from a centralized market seeing people and the Internet as a resource to strip-mine, and that we can solve this with "Good AI," the same thing that the big companies offer to and inflict upon us, but with us having seized the means of production[^KVGVFO].

[^KVGVFO]:  I might argue the means of counter-production, given that studies continue to show nobody but Nvidia getting anything useful out of the bubble.  And rather than seizing anything, because nobody seriously thinks that the public wants to use anything built around mass-pirated works, not so much "seizing" as painstakingly reproduce in an ethical manner.  But you can barely fit "painstakingly and ethically reproduce the counter-productive means of wasting money and time" on a protest sign, let alone get people to follow you with it...

![A drawing of a robot with non-articulated legs but gorilla-like arms, plus a smiley face drawn on its face](/blog/assets/Ink-and-Marker-Robot-Illustration-81.png "He seems friendly enough, but...")

And, look, I can almost buy the first half.  If LLMs spilled out of some hobby community or thousands of tiny companies, the situation wouldn't look nearly as absurd or bleak.  The individual backers would put resources into fighting each other instead of us.  They'd compete on benefits to adopters instead of screaming at us to hurry up and use their disappointment machines already.  And regulation could keep them in line.  This would improve the landscape dramatically.

## Can We Not

However, the second half feels like complete fantasy, a hand-wavy wish that we participated in an economy where consumers had all the power, and a completely different set of technologies took hold, which...sure, dream big, but also, let's not pretend that it might happen without organizing the largest social movement in history *at the same time* that we also need the largest social movements in history to deal with half a dozen actual existential threats that people can actually see before their eyes every day, not to mention the more systemic risks that blend into the background.

Also, this sort of wishing cedes ground to the LLM-hype.  It presumes that we all desperately (secretly?) want a world where every tool, every object in our lives with a microprocessor, runs a chatbot as its primary user interface, the Genuine People Personalities of the Douglas Adams books.  Why, they seem to ask, would we *not* want doors[^zpeuNW] that sigh with satisfaction when somebody walks through them and elevators ignoring the occupant because they predict problems in following directions.  Or worse, it presumes that the marketing has predicted our future, that (like printed books) LLMs will become pervasive, and when it comes to news saturated in deepfakes, vibe-coded infrastructure, and AI psychosis, we only get to influence the subscription rates.

[^zpeuNW]:  Apparently, they didn't keep reading to the point where a future encyclopedia defines the GPP-boosters as "a bunch of mindless jerks who were the first against the wall when the revolution came."  The readers also apparently missed the constant and blatant satire, somehow.

If *we* had the technology---instead of Open AI, Google, Anthropic, and their ilk---they seem to cheer, then Inkscape, your BitTorrent client, your printer, your password manager, your electric kettle, and heck, even [grep](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grep) would have a perky chatbot asking if you'd like a summary of your session written as a series of haiku or clever tips guaranteed to work...at least in some future version of the application after consistent hallucinations cause users to [bully the development team into adding the feature {% cc %}](https://www.holovaty.com/writing/chatgpt-fake-feature/).

## Disclosure

And sure, I have engaged in a similar "nibbling around the edges" [Generative AI Wish List]({% post_url 2025-06-08-ai-wish-list %}), where I came up with a small handful of ideas.

- Securing consent from the originators of training data, á la the [Common Pile](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.05209), and now the [Common Corpus](https://huggingface.co/datasets/PleIAs/common_corpus)[^gg0erl] which proved it possible.
- Reduce energy costs and hardware requirements after DeepSeek proved that possible.
- Stop the industry hype.  I assure you that you won't "get left behind" for not letting the machine write bug-ridden code for you at breakneck pace.  (You *will* fall behind as that code piles up faster than you can validate it, though.)
- Stop pushing other trendy technologies because the keywords match.  I get it.  You have a *lot* of Python and blockchain training data.  That doesn't make either a perfect tool for all or most jobs.
- Search for errors[^vmemka] instead of making them.
- Stop the sycophancy.  A bot telling me that it loves my idea for adapting **Welcome Back, Kotter** into an operetta in the style of Gilbert and Sullivan[^8P9X6H] or whatever doesn't improve the idea or get it closer to (fictional) production.
- Improve continuity in conversations.
- Announce failure instead of guessing at answers.
- Adapt more classical AI.

[^gg0erl]:  I have, admittedly, not used either LLM, so I can't speak to its relative quality.  I only know that those projects prove that you can train a viable LLM without pirating every book and repeatedly scraping every website.

[^vmemka]:  It seems worth a mention that, in the past weeks, a couple of the companies have excitedly published that their new models found such-and-such critical vulnerabilities that "nobody" ever spotted before.  Notice that they don't tell you whether smaller models of the sort that you'd run at home found the same issues---people have, in fact, replicated the results---or how many false-positives and low-priority issues humans needed to slog through before finding the big problems.  That gives more of an "infinite monkeys with infinite typewriters producing **Hamlet**" impression than a "Claude can replace experts" impression.

[^8P9X6H]:  Back when these systems still seemed amusing, I used that as one of my standard tests of the bots' capabilities, in the early days when they could do an interesting job of writing.  I never got anything even approaching viable results.  Early ChatGPT could do a semi-credible **My Favorite Martian** as a 1930s radio drama, more obscure in both aspects, I would think.  But for the operetta idea, it couldn't keep the six main characters, the urban setting, the style, and the idea of a light opera together in any single attempt.

In other words, the "Good AI" idea definitely nags at me on some level, too.  As I have said before, I like the broader artificial intelligence field, and have managed to get local LLMs to do productive work more effectively than I could've with conventional programming.

And while my old ideas seem fine if uninspired, and I don't object to them happening, you'll notice that they fall into a small handful of categories.  First, companies should behave more ethically.  Second, change the chatbot prompt to something that'll actually *help* instead of promoting engagement with the product.  And finally...fundamentally change the product to something else.  In other words, exactly the "change the economy and the technologies in use" problem that I complained about.

Instead of that limited vision, similar to the "open source and magic pixie dust" ideas that I have dismissed elsewhere, I want to try to put together a more cohesive vision of what we could have and might even get to.

## Sure, the Usual

Let's get the core principles out of the way in one shot.  Any "good" system in the AI-assistant vein *must have* the following properties to have a chance of not becoming yet another feudal system where users get occasional access in exchange for training the models.

- Correctly licensed training data, tagged in such a way that the model can cite what it has drawn from.  We do know that [models store their source material {% cc %}](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.02671) in *some* form, after all, so they could certainly handle attribution and identify licensing entanglements.
- Reproducibly trained/built, so that interested researchers can build on that base and take advantage of improvements.
- Optimized for lower-end commodity hardware.

Mind you, those three changes don't solve the real problems *at all*.  Nothing in everybody's wish list takes care of LLMs turning in work riddled with subtle errors.  It doesn't make them less addictive to people prone to those issues.  And it certainly doesn't do anything for the atrophy of persistence and skills.  It does exactly squat for the fact that software doesn't and can't care about its audience.

Plus, we have seen repeatedly that adhering to licenses, creating a reproducible build system, and aiming for home computers still leaves *plenty* of room in software for exploitation and other terrible behavior[^mCCxb_].  However, I don't think that we can solve those real problems without those changes.  They create stable ground on which we might build.  Without them, we leave our fates in the hands of a couple of billion-dollar multinational corporations and hope that they suddenly grow consciences.

[^mCCxb_]:  Yes, I will get back to my *Defining Free Culture* posts (relatively) soon, and that idea will feature, at least somewhat.

From here, ideas become somewhat speculative.  I know that the Big AI model doesn't work unless you own Nvidia.  I don't care for the "home game," running small models on [Ollama](https://ollama.com/), though I find it (generally) less objectionable than the big models.  And I have certain preferences in how I work that other people may not share.  But I have no idea what "getting this right" actually looks like, or if the possibility even exists of getting it right.

## Not Much More Than Meets The Eye

Before we get going, I should probably mention that almost all major LLM models currently use the [transformer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformer_%28deep_learning%29) approach.  In short, and informally you can describe it this way.

- Take the input string and turn it into a list of numbers ("tokens"), which flattens and simplifies the request.
- Use that list of numbers to calculate a semantic vector, an arrow---a strength/distance and direction---in the abstract semantic space, which the LLM people call an *embedding*.
- Repeatedly transform (hence the name) that vector.
- Reverse the embedding process on the resulting vector to get a list of tokens, and then turn the list of tokens to text.

In other words, as I have described it elsewhere, you estimate the meaning of the request, perform some linear algebra---changing the direction and length of the arrow---to turn a request into a response vector, and turn the estimated meaning of the response into text.  You can see that every step flattens meaning through approximations.  The tokens represent chunks of words out of context.  Embedding condenses all ideas, from "hello" to a five-thousand-word specification describing requested software, into a single arrow.  The math presumes that changes in semantic direction have consistent (congruent) angles to them.  Producing text from the resulting embedding can produce *some* variety, but it doesn't make sense to risk the credibility of the response, so it'll go back to the "autocomplete" idea of picking the most likely chains of words.

And this should make some sense.  Google devised transformers for machine translation, a space where you ideally want text in your source language distilled to an approximate meaning, and that meaning expressed in the target language.

And I should mention that, to the extent that I find this technology valuable, embeddings make up a *lot* of that value.  In addition to a cheap translation to get the broad ideas of text in other languages, this drives "search, but I can't remember the exact terms that I need," "these two articles probably talk about similar topics," and more, because it turns arbitrary text into a vector in (generally 1,024-dimensional) space, which you can compare using something like [cosine similarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosine_similarity).

### So What

Anyway, I wanted to provide that outline for a handful of reasons.

First, actually describing transformers directly shows that my frequent characterization of them as "turn text into arrows, modify a request arrow to a response arrow, and turn the arrow back into text" doesn't really exaggerate the process.

It also demystifies the technology.  You wouldn't do it at this kind of scale (the "large" in Large Language Model), but you can do all this on paper.  I don't know if things have changed much, but when I went to junior high and high school (off in another century), they treated the dedicated linear algebra class like a form of exile[^S9HCsa], the class that you took to fulfill the state mathematics requirements when you "couldn't hack" the mainstream classes.  And in that sense, it means that we all understand what we have in front of us, at least in broad strokes.

[^S9HCsa]:  I should call this out explicitly as an unfair characterization.  Linear algebra, both the matrix math and solving systems of equations, undergirds a lot of engineering, first off.  But also, students performing poorly, especially in a math class, frequently have a motivation (teaching) problem, where they feel like their success doesn't matter to anybody.

In addition, knowing the structure imposes some limits on our discussions.  For this context, it doesn't make sense to ask for (a favorite example of mine) an AI chatbot that will warn of similar structures that often get fixed in later iterations, because LLMs don't do that kind of categorization work that has sat at the core of machine learning for decades.  That would require a fundamentally different concept instead of trying to find the "good" version of the current tools.  Likewise, we can't suggest that LLMs stop flattening nuance, because it does so at the structural level.

And probably finally, it serves as a reminder that no amount of sycophancy will make these things your friend or collaborator, because you can't treat a conversation as a subclass of translation.  They don't think, because you can't treat thinking like a subclass of translation.  In that sense, Google didn't invent the transformer, [Anatoly Dneprov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatoly_Dneprov_%28writer%29) did in his short story *The Game*, which uses the "Portuguese stadium" thought experiment, a stadium full of people who each have transformation rules that, when combined, respond usefully to questions in Portuguese, questioning whether any part of this process *understands* the language.  About twenty years later, John Searle floated the similar [Chinese Room](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_room) which only used one person.  We don't believe that the people in the stadium or the rule-writers know Portuguese (except coincidentally), nor do we believe that the person locked in the Chinese room knows Chinese, so we shouldn't believe that ChatGPT gets you.
