## Babel

We have the final episode airing during **TNG**'s winter hiatus.  After this, the *Enterprise*-D crew picks back up with [*Ship in a Bottle*]({% post_url 2025-02-06-ship-in-bottle %}).

 > **JAHEEL**: Mister O'Brien.

Probably not the level of star power that we often find roaming the backgrounds of these shows, but you might recognize Jaheel as the late [Jack Kehler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Kehler).

 > **O'BRIEN**: I'm afraid we've gotten a little bit behind in our maintenance schedule.
 >
 > ...
 >
 > **JAHEEL**: Your best, Mister O'Brien, hardly seems good enough.

Right...?  I almost pointed this out myself.  The show tries, to its credit, to make it seem like O'Brien's team has far too much to do---the Cardassians sabotaged the station when they left, and nobody designed the station for the new traffic---but he blames it on falling behind on maintenance.  In other words, nobody has bothered to make sure that visitors don't get trapped in airlocks.  And that means that either he hasn't managed his team at all well *or* he hasn't tried to make the case to Starfleet and/or the Bajoran provisional government that this incredibly important outpost needs more experienced engineers.

 > **O'BRIEN**: Have you checked the EPS convertor?
 >
 > **DAX**: Yes. First thing.

...Also, though, that doesn't sound like her problem.

In some work environments, you can build the sort of relationship where workers do the initial diagnostics and attempted repairs when something goes wrong, sure.  But these two don't have that relationship, as far as I know, and a lot of people in O'Brien-like positions---I have the most exposure to folks in the IT department---get offended and/or worried when people try to do their jobs, either seeing it as disrespect to their experience/process or a sign that they'll have twice as much work to do after you make the problem worse.  And you almost *never* find somebody in that kind of position accuse the worker of not doing that work, because then why do we pay you?

 > **KIRA**: That's more like it. You look like you could use some sleep.
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: Who has time for sleep? I'd settle for five minutes of peace and quiet.

And this answers the question above:  We have a clear case of both mismanagement and refusal to request proper resources.  Rather than allocate work carefully, get more accomplished, and---remember that we saw this same problem in [*Emissary*]({% post_url 2026-03-05-emissary-2 %})---make his bosses aware of the problems, he instead wants to testosterone his way through the problem, burning himself out along with his team until nothing gets done.

...Not that I have ever worked with *anybody* like that.

 > **QUARK**: It's these replicators. If I don't get them fixed soon, they'll destroy my business.

Wait.  How widely have replicators spread?  [*The Wounded*]({% post_url 2024-01-25-wounded %}) seemed to at least hint that even civilians have had the technology for most of O'Brien's life, and we have seen them scattered around the station.  If Quark has a functioning business because of the quality of replicated food, and anybody can get a replicator, then how does this business function at all?

I could certainly believe that O'Brien grew up wealthy, which would greatly reduce the likely number of replicators, but it otherwise seems odd.

 > **DAX**: Being female. I haven't been one for over eighty years. All this attention.

Similar to Bashir's disinterest in boundaries, and actually a lot like Troi on **TNG**, this show will spend an unfortunate amount of time explaining that we should find Dax attractive.  Did they think that we'd need this reinforcement after years of watching teen "coming out of her shell" films, and not consider that the twenty-something-year-old, conventionally attractive TV star might appeal to someone?

 > **DAX**: Actually I find it quite enjoyable.

Right, and it also wants to push the idea that some women really do enjoy it when men ogle them.

Spoiler:  If you ever meet the actual Jadzia Dax, the extraterrestrial character and not the performer portraying her, ogle away.  For anybody else, **stop looking for reasons to make people uncomfortable**.  You can look.  You can appreciate.  Don't stare.  Definitely don't leer or ogle.

Anyway, this all lends credence to the extrapolation that the writers originally envisioned this role as Kamala from [*The Perfect Mate*]({% post_url 2024-10-03-perfect-mate %}), because the scene makes quite a bit more sense if they need to introduce the pheromones to a new audience and pitch this as "newly single" as opposed to "newly a woman."

 > **KIRA**: You're the one who could use a little help.
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: Oh, no, I'm fine, really.

Does Starfleet not understand that sick people do terrible work?  Send this nitwit to his quarters for a few days.

 > **JABARA**: Yes?

You might recognize the recurring Nurse Jabara as [Ann Gillespie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ann_Gillespie), who mostly worked in "soap operas"---daytime and **Beverly Hills, 90210**---and who we last saw as an unrelated character in [*Pen Pals*]({% post_url 2023-03-02-pen-pals %}).

 > **DAX**: Isn't aphasia typically associated with some kind of cranial trauma?

I should probably mention that, at the time, [aphasia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aphasia) largely felt like science fiction, even though it affects millions of people, usually due to strokes rather than direct cranial trauma.  These days, the disorder has celebrity faces like Bruce Willis, demystifying it a bit.

 > **QUARK**: I thought the front wheels were sticking a bit.

Not at all related to our little project, here, but how the heck does Odo create wheels?

 > **KIRA**: That's not possible. All the food on the station is replicated, and all the replicators use biofilters. They automatically screen out contaminants including viruses.

Seriously?  I don't want to tell them *not* to filter contaminants out of their food, but if they assemble the food atom-by-atom---which Kira confirms later---what could they possibly filter out?

 > **BASHIR**: We've converted some empty crew quarters into a hospital ward. He'll be well looked after.
 >
 > **SISKO**: I'll take him there myself. Come on, Jake. You're going to be all right.

Do I smell a metaphor, here, with Sisko risking getting further infected by comforting the ill of a sudden epidemic and not sweating it?  It seems maybe about a decade too late, but this does feel reminiscent of the push (still ongoing, at this point) to get people to finally stop ostracizing AIDS---as in the immune collapse potentially caused by HIV that we can now increasingly get under control---patients.

Despite the strong vibe, it still seemed like a stretch to me, so I looked up a rough timeline of AIDS-related stories.  At this point, the epidemic had started to become "real" news.  Jeremy Irons wore an awareness ribbon, and widely beloved people like Howard Ashman and Miles Davis died in 1991.  In 1992, Muppet puppeteer Richard Hunt, Isaac Asimov, Robert "Mike Brady" Reed, Anthony Perkins, Paul Jabara, and others died of AIDS-related complications, and a Miss America chose AIDS awareness as her platform.  We wouldn't know for sure that all of them died from those complications for years, but stories circulate, and families have since confirmed them as the stigma lifted.

Wait a second, though, *who*?  Less famous than many others on the list, [Paul Jabara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Jabara) acted, sang, and wrote music that you almost certainly know.  But I included him in the list, because the aforementioned nurse shares his name, either to signal exactly this intent or as one heck of a wild coincidence a couple of months after the man's death.

To bring this as close to full-circle as I can without doing any research on the actual production history, on **The Next Generation**, original series writer David Gerrold submitted a script for *Blood and Fire*, a fairly shallow metaphor for the AIDS epidemic, which the producers rejected[^lTV03E], and one of the fan productions later produced.  And while I certainly don't know if the writers intended this as a homage to that script---or even if they really did mean to evoke AIDS---I will point out that most people recognize Gerrold's name as the writer for [*The Trouble with Tribbles*]({% post_url 2020-11-19-trouble %}) and [*More Tribbles, More Troubles*]({% post_url 2021-09-16-troubles %}), and come the fifth season, guess what episode the crew will need to coincidentally revisit...

[^lTV03E]:  The idea that nobody wants "politics" in a franchise that openly railed against war and authoritarians, and literally had an episode explaining that the United States Constitution only has meaning if it applies equally to everybody in the United States, didn't appear with the Internet...

That said, the fact that the problem starts in O'Brien, the most "mainstream" character in the main cast, does speak against an intentional allegory for AIDS.  While *I* don't think of O'Brien as a sympathetic character, I find him unsympathetic in a fundamentally different way than people found the archetypal person with AIDS, gay and (in the earliest days) Black.

 > **BASHIR**: He hasn't responded to neural stimulation. I've given him thirty ccs of corophizine to avoid secondary infection, but so far I've been unable to lower his fever.

It looks like we have returned to the land of brand-name pharmaceuticals[^lHqRBK].  I expect that the *co-* prefix should imply some combination---again, possibly a reference to the "drug cocktails" that doctors have used to reduce the effects of HIV---and antihypertensives can take a *-dralazine* suffix, which includes the *-zine*, but none of the rest of this tracks.

[^lHqRBK]:  I dread the chance that, someday, I'll rattle off some generic name stems in conversation, and then need to explain that I know a handful of them while trying to understand *Star Trek*'s pharmaceutical industry, like a totally normal person would do...

## Conclusions

Fully half the episode sidelines everybody from the Federation, so again, we don't have too much, but still some useful information.

### The Bad

We get a strong impression that O'Brien dangerously mismanages the engineering crews, thinking that his team can muscle their way past a lack of resources, with no priorities, and not bothering to update management.  He also seems to think that his colleagues should fix their own problems.  And this seems consistent with the organization, which seems to think that having sick people on the job won't cause problems.

And we still have name-brand drugs in use.

### The Weird

Replicators no longer seem widespread, and may not work as advertised.
