## A Man Alone

Since last time, nothing happened, because **The Next Generation** remains on winter hiatus for a bit longer.

 > **DAX**: Julian, you and I have to have a talk about Trills and relationships.
 >
 > **BASHIR**: Fine. We'll do it over supper. Quark has found me this delightfully dry champagne estate bottled on Korris One.

Ugh.  I forgot how much "badgering the young woman (with an old-man brain) no matter how many times she rejects him" that we need to endure in the early seasons.  Episodes of **The Next Generation** such as [*The Price*]({% post_url 2023-06-22-price %}) did try to prepare us for it by showing us how their culture socializes women into blaming themselves for how men treat them, but that doesn't mean that I enjoyed it then or enjoy it now...

 > **DAX**: That's all. I've been trying to master it on and off for, oh, a hundred and forty years. Would you care to try?

As a point of comparison, I can't find a closer equivalent to this sort of puzzle from the 1880s---a hundred forty years ago---than [disentanglement puzzles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disentanglement_puzzle) that apparently did become fairly popular at the time.  I suppose that this seems more analogous to [brain training](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brain_training) exercises, but that field only stretches back about a hundred years, so you won't find many people telling you that they've tried to solve it for almost a century and a half...

 > **DAX**: Just sit comfortably, and release your tension into my hands.

Actually *touching* people who you don't intend to sleep with?  What sort of bizarre science fiction television show have I stumbled into, here?

 > **O'BRIEN**: We made the decision together.
 >
 > **KEIKO**: Not true. That's not true. You decided and asked me to agree with it.

For the record, fans remember this as the franchise's healthy, mature relationship...where Miles pushed his wife into something and declared it her decision.  Oh, but stress or whatever.  Nope.  This matches to how he treated Keiko starting with her introduction in [*Data's Day*]({% post_url 2024-01-18-data-s-day %}), characterizing her hesitation to get married as "childish, selfish, irresponsible."

 > **ODO**: I'll never understand the humanoid need to couple.
 >
 > **QUARK**: You've never coupled?

"The silliest woman can manage a clever man; but it needs a very clever woman to manage a fool!"

Wait, sorry, I thought that they wanted to do the "do you enjoy *Kipling*" joke.

Look, I told you all that I'd have less to do for a lot of this show...

 > **ODO**: Choose not to. Too many compromises. You want to watch the karo-net tournament, she wants to listen to music, so you compromise. You listen to music. You like Earth jazz, she prefers Klingon opera, so you compromise. You listen to Klingon opera. So here you were ready to have a nice night watching the karo-net match and you wind up spending an agonizing night listening to Klingon opera.

This has nothing to do with the post as such, but does *anybody* believe that Odo watches sports?  Everything about him, especially so far, has the vibe of somebody who either doesn't socialize beyond Quark or goes to wherever people invite him and complains about all the things he could've done with that time.

Also, did he get impressions about relationships from 1970s sitcoms?

 > **QUARK**: Not at all. I know for a fact, when she was a he, you know, before she got this new body, they were old friends.

Worth pointing out, here, that the aggressively misogynist outsider has demonstrated far less transphobia than either of the Crushers did in [*The Dauphin*]({% post_url 2023-01-26-dauphin %}) and [*The Host*]({% post_url 2024-04-11-host %}).  Not *great*, but "transitioned, now a woman" still shows far more progress than the franchise has shown us so far, and doesn't say great things about the Federation for the Ferengi to do significantly better...

 > **SISKO**: Dax, did I ever tell you about the meals my father used to cook for us when we were children?

Notice another kick away from **The Next Generation**, talking kindly about a parent and (presumed) siblings.

 > **SISKO**: It won't be that way with us. It's just a little uncomfortable right now.
 >
 > **DAX**: I suggest you that allow yourself to be comfortable with your discomfort. Time can do the rest.

And to follow up on the Quark comment, Sisko understands that he has a problem, and that he'll overcome it, so not *everybody* in the Federation acts like such a complete creep.

 > **O'BRIEN**: We'll be sending runabouts through the wormhole. I'll see to it you get to go.
 >
 > **KEIKO**: I don't need favors from you. I just need to be useful.
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: Just think how much more attractive this place could be if you planted some trees and flowers on the Promenade. You could even create an arboretum from the new specimens we bring back from the Gamma Quadrant.

Seriously, look at the absolute condescension on display, here.  He pushed her into joining him far away from her support system---often, as I have mentioned, a prelude to abuse---used emotional blackmail to get her to agree to stay when he says that *maybe* he won't get demoted if they leave, suggests that he can pull strings to get her a toy-career, and also maybe she could use her advanced botany education and experience (at least a decade, at this point) to become...a florist, pretty much, landscaper if you stretch.

 > **KEIKO**: Do you really want to raise your daughter in this place, Miles?

You remember, the daughter that she had in [*Disaster*]({% post_url 2024-06-13-disaster %}) that took almost two hours into this series to even get a mention?

 > **ODO**: His name is Ibudan. He used to run black market goods through here to the surface during the Cardassian occupation, gouging his fellow man who needed medical supplies and so forth. Some Bajorans actually considered him a hero, but I saw him let a child die when the parents couldn't afford the drug that would've saved her life. A few years back, he killed a Cardassian officer who wanted a payoff to look the other way. He went to prison for murder. I sent him there.

For the younger readers, back when they produced this, the majority of people had never heard of such a thing as withholding non-palliative medical treatment---especially "drug in-hand that could save a life" treatment---based on available funds, so this made him sound like a shady outsider rather than every health insurance executive about to get a raise.

 > **BASHIR**: You said, *try* to rise above, which suggests you don't always succeed.

The Academy seriously needs a "no means no" class.

 > **KEIKO**: This isn't like a starship, Miles. The kind of freedom children have on the *Enterprise* just won't work on a space station. There are too many ways to get into serious trouble here. What this place needs is a school.

I often feel like one of the few fans who appreciate Keiko's position, but this conversation makes extremely difficult.  A space station with a large civilian area needs *more* rules than a military vessel where almost everybody in charge likes sexual harassment and dangerous science experiments get left on the floor?  If she suggested that the kids might get cosmic-tetanus from or fall into space due to the damaged metal infrastructure everywhere, I could understand it.  But this sounds suspiciously like she doesn't think that the commander's kid should spend time around "those people," whether she means Bajorans, Ferengi, or civilian workers passing through.

Also, it doesn't say much about Federation education, if she thinks that schools exist to keep kids away from the general population.

 > **ZAYRA**: It's just that the murdered man, Ibudan, I spoke to him yesterday about an hour before it happened.

You might recognize Zayra as [Edward Albert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Albert)---the son of Eddie, as in the **Green Acres** guy---who...probably appeared in everything at some point, honestly.

 > **SISKO**: I don't want to talk about this now. It's this Ferengi boy, Nog. Jake won't be having anything more to do with him.

What happened to wanting "to co-exist and learn" from [*Emissary*]({% post_url 2026-03-05-emissary-2 %})?  For all Sisko knows, "this Ferengi boy" will buck stereotypes, join Starfleet, and become so prominent in history that they'll honor him by naming a ship after him and people will still talk about him centuries later.

Don't look at me like that...

 > **KEIKO**: You know, I've never actually been a teacher, Commander, but it's something I've always thought about doing.

Has the Federation de-professionalized teaching?  Back in the day of the archetypal one-room schoolhouse, communities would often hire people with no other options---wanderers and ex-convicts stand out in histories---to teach.  But for well over a century, I believe that you at least need to commit to getting certified and a degree within a certain amount of time to teach children[^ITd_9P].

[^ITd_9P]:  For teaching adults, any restrictions on who can teach generally revolve around the institution's reputation.  A college can pull some random person off the street to teach a graduate class, but people will probably drop the class, and the accreditation board might want that explained if it affects many students.

 > **KEIKO**: I know I'll have to come up with an innovative program, but I can't imagine any parent not welcoming the news of a school opening here. Thank you, Commander.

Again, for the younger readers, back when they produced this, people overwhelmingly saw formal education as a good thing.  You'd stumble across the occasional parent, often a religious zealot, who kept their kids home to protect them from learning about reproduction and evolution---this will become important in a few months, I believe---but we didn't have billionaires trying to convince tens of millions of parents to pour money into private schools or challenge public schools on every topic.  Unlike the idea of keeping medication away from a dying child, "can't imagine any parent not welcoming the news of a school opening" probably didn't raise many eyebrows.

 > **ROM**: You want my boy to go to a Federation school?

We met [Max Grodénchik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Grod%C3%A9nchik) in [*Captain's Holiday*]({% post_url 2023-09-07-captain-s-holiday %}) playing an unrelated character, for those keeping track of such things.

 > **QUARK**: Nobody knows him like I know him. Let me tell you something. He's an ill-tempered, over-bearing crosspatch. But he was no Cardassian collaborator and he's no killer.

I mean...Odo *literally* collaborated with the Cardassians, no?  He had an actual job carrying out police work under Cardassian law, and didn't use his position to sabotage the regime.  That would seem to make him a Cardassian collaborator, no matter what Quark says.

 > **SISKO**: The man we have in charge of a murder investigation is the prime suspect, Major. Those people have a right to complain.
 >
 > **DAX**: Even if he's innocent, it could still seem like a conflict of interest, Kira.

Yes!  Decades of television making internal investigations of police officers seem like some absurd imposition, when you actually want to do that specifically to protect the reputations of the officers as much as protecting the community.

 > **BASHIR**: Let's see what a standard electrophoretic analysis tells us.

Somebody studied their science.  Generally speaking, especially when talking about genetic material, the term refers to [gel electrophoresis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gel_electrophoresis), which pushes charged particles through the medium to spread them out by mass for further investigation.

 > **SISKO**: Care for lunch?
 >
 > **BASHIR**: Sure.

Again, Bashir has no idea that people don't want to spend time with him.  Although it also seems impolite for Sisko to have asked only one person in the group...

 > **SISKO**: I guess I'll have to find that out. If I were to tell you some of the mischief he and I got into. There was a time, I was no older than you are, we were at the running of the Rujian Steeplechase. Dax loves the races. Anyway, there were these gorgeous seven foot Ruji twin sisters that Curzon knew, and he and I...I guess we won't be doing that again soon, will we?

That depends on her preferences, I imagine.

 > **SISKO**: You don't understand, Doctor. Dax used to beat me regularly at bare fist Juro counterpunch.
 >
 > **BASHIR**: Well I guess you won't be doing that again soon, either.

Can we tamp down on the sexism any?  I have known quite a few women who spent a lot of time practicing martial arts, even back then.

 > **O'BRIEN**: I want to see you standing in the Promenade tomorrow morning ringing your school bell.

For the record, Keiko blew up her entire career to take on a more stereotypically feminine job for lack of anything to do, so her loving husband...*gloats*, by insisting that she play her new role more theatrically.

 > **SISKO**: Do you? Justice? Really? Is it justice you're after or just some way to express your anger, your fear. Look at yourselves! In an hour, you'll regret what you've tried to do here. Do not condemn this man because he is different than you are.

I wish that they would've connected this with his condemnation of Nog, earlier.

 > **BASHIR**: if you know to look for it. We've matched the victim's gene-sequence with the fellow in the jar here. They're definitely both clones.
 >
 > **ODO**: What happens to this one?
 >
 > **BASHIR**: In about two days, he becomes a living, breathing member of Bajoran society.

Compare this to...almost every other use of clones in science fiction, honestly.  What should they do with him?  No debate or confusion, they treat him like a person and move on with their lives.  The fact that he didn't grow organs from inside a womb doesn't matter.  Although he might have some trouble looking like a murderer.

And we'll skip the actual *Scooby-Doo* reveal.

## Conclusions

We got quite a bit out of this one.

### The Good

We finally see some reasonably social behavior, including casual physical contact (not intended as seduction) and kind words about family.  Sisko also admits that he needs to deal with his (metaphorical) transphobia.  They also see clones as people, rather than horrific aberrations.

The crew understands that law enforcement can't investigate itself.

### The Bad

We see a *lot* of misogyny here.  Bashir ignores Dax's boundaries, and O'Brien treats his wife's career as a mildly amusing hobby to tolerate.  And Sisko and Bashir seem to believe that a woman can't have sex with other women or fight.  Moreover, we see that the foreign culture that centers misogyny has less of an issue with people changing genders than any Federation citizen who we've met so far.

We also see some casual racism.  Keiko keeps insisting that they live someplace unsafe for children and wants them away from the civilians, while Sisko outright blames his son's behavior on a new Ferengi friend.

The Federation's education system also seems problematic.  Keiko suggests that they need a school to isolate the station's children, and teachers apparently don't need any sort of credential or experience.
