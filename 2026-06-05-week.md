## 9:03 -- Tue 02 June 2026

{% cw Incarceration, Death Penalty %}
{% embed https://i0.wp.com/otherwords.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/04/shutterstock_765745765.jpg|A prisoner in front of a tray of sodium thiopental and equipment|true| %}

[<i class="fab fa-mastodon"></i>](https://mastodon.social/@jcolag/116680647606178228) [There’s No ‘Moral’ Death Penalty](https://otherwords.org/theres-no-moral-death-penalty/) from OtherWords

> Yet the fundamental act has never changed. The state still kills. The train keeps moving. Even when jurors change their minds. Even when victims’ families plead for mercy. Stopping the train requires courage — especially from elected leaders who have the power to do it.

Hashtags:  #Incarceration #DeathPenalty

I have said this before, and will probably end up writing a full post on the topic someday, but a big problem comes from the fact that the carceral state doesn't have a "mission" or "goal" that we can measure it against.  Does imprisonment or execution reduce the crime rate?  No.  Does it help victims?  Also no.  Does it rehabilitate offenders?  Again, no.  Does it teach offenders an important lesson?  Certainly not if you kill them.  Does it inflict pain on offenders?  Yes, but we want to do that slightly less.  Do we want any of those, some combination, and/or something else?  Nobody knows, so we have no idea whether to change any particular policy, which seems like a heck of a problem when you get to irrevocable discipline such as execution.  And therefore, we nibble around the edges trying to make horrible acts like executions feel less horrible.
