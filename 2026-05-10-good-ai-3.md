If you came into this group of posts without the background, the [introduction post]({% post_url 2026-04-26-good-ai-1 %}) described that I have seen a few prominent open web advocates---people whose opinions I generally respect---trying to pitch the idea of "Good AI," often seeming to mean the Big AI services, but slightly more sustainable and ethical.  It makes no sense to me, as you might guess by my post about why I [don't use LLM chatbots]({% post_url 2026-01-18-using-ai %}).  I followed that up kicking around [possible technical improvements]({% post_url 2026-05-03-good-ai-2 %}) to LLM services that might mitigate the problems that we see emerging in studies.

![A drawing of a robot with non-articulated legs but gorilla-like arms, plus a smiley face drawn on its face](/blog/assets/Ink-and-Marker-Robot-Illustration-81.png "Kill'em (dashes?) with kindness...")

These posts represent me trying to work through the possibilities to find a set of modifications that might result in a system that helps people.

## Restrictions and Acknowledgements

I make the assumption, here, that the (deliberately unnamed) people who I mention must see the same studies that I see, showing that LLMs have addictive qualities for at least some users, cause an over-confidence when scheduling work, take longer than manual work, produce subtly error-riddled results, and sap the users of both skills and motivation.  Seizing the means of sarcasm-quotes *production* solves a legitimate problem of guillotining the power of Big AI, sure, but it doesn't solve the problems with the technology, and doesn't make it *good*.  I realize that my fellow Long Islander Lee Zeldin would disagree with me as he tears away environmental regulation, but you don't have "Good Pollution" because local companies dump the highest quality waste products into the aquifer.

I do think that those first steps---usually something like ethical sourcing and citing of training data, reproducible training, and targeting cheap commodity hardware---can enable improvement.  But if we only shift people's attention from ChatGPT to a hypothetical Free Software LLM run by Zabulon down the street, that won't magically fix the deskilling or poor quality results.  Seizing the means can nudge us towards a better path, but *only* doing that accepts the toxic framing that this bubble---which already shows signs of bursting quickly, as prices rise, problems become more apparent, and pundits start testing the waters for the next bubble---shows us some inevitable future where humans keep hitting {% key Sure, Whatever %} button while computers exist to extrude combinatorial stacks of existing work-slurry with fingers crossed that the next remix of other people's work does what you want.

Therefore, this series of posts tries to step beyond "open source and not an outright blight on the environment" to find real changes to both the technology and our culture, that might---I freely admit that I don't have anything more than guesswork, here, and hope to inspire better ideas more than I imagine fixing the industry---improve the tools.

Last time, we took a look at possible improvements to the technology to make them less toxic.

- Audit training data for bias
- Ditch the chatbot
- Add sidebars searching for confirmation
- Limit the range of topics
- Stop interacting after ten minutes and take breaks
- Measure output by information density

This time through, we'll look at the potential changes to the cultural side, fixing us instead of the tools.  Or, rather, we'll *start* to do that, because I realized that---unlike the technology adjustments---advocating for societal changes requires background and explanation, meaning that the original version of this (part of the) post quickly blew through six thousand words, convincing me to chop it in half.

And I emphasize again that everybody should consider this completely hypothetical, speculative at best.  After all, I don't know for sure what can make this stuff work well for us, or even if we *can* make it work.  If I did, I'd happily raise piles of money---and if somebody wants to fund that, you know where to find me, I guess---and put all the [Roko's basilisk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roko%27s_basilisk) clowns (more on that next time) out of business while employing people who need the work.  But I can certainly look at the problems that I listed before, experiment to feel out the pain points that might cause those problems, and work from there.

## Rejection

I have said this before, probably multiple times, but if you release *anything* created by an LLM, I don't trust you or take you seriously.  Nobody should trust you or take you seriously, in my opinion.  I don't see any other solution.  Because linear algebra doesn't care about you, your audience, or your goals---except to the extent that it can manipulate you into engaging further---then it will **never** create anything genuinely fit for purpose.

You don't trust the people behind that fictional website[^AOlWue], right?  They definitely didn't care about anything other than capturing your attention for a moment while they serve up ads.  Whoever "wrote" it left behind their placeholder text and generic clip art.  And everybody in the quality chain signed off on it without reading it.  They did far more search engine optimization than reading.  The text contains some English words, but only coincidentally.  Everything about this feels bad, completely careless.

[^AOlWue]:  I hope that at least the techies in the audience appreciate the work that I put into the IDNA URL...

How does this sort of example differ from an AI-generated page?  It takes more of your time and attention to *discover* that they didn't care.  You'll get the same impression, eventually, but it'll waste your time getting there while you realize that it keeps repeating ideas and overusing the same boring grammatical flourishes that trainers identify as the mark of good writing.

And more to the point, if they put this kind of "work" into how they present their project, why would you think that they run any other part of their enterprise any differently?  Phone it in here, and I have to assume that you phone it in everywhere else.

### The Boring Parts

Singling anybody out seems cruel, here, but because they phrased it perfectly, I want to point you to a comment at the end of a [book covered for the Free Culture Book Club]({% post_url 2025-05-17-pilogy-4 %}), emphasis mine.

> I’d say that around 30% of the book is written by ChatGPT, *mainly the boring parts*, because that’s what it’s good at writing.

As I said then, if those parts of the text didn't move you (hypothetical-you, now, not that specific author) to write them---in fact they drove you away from writing them, because you considered them so boring---then why the heck would you inflict such a thing on the audience?  If you didn't find it interesting enough to write (or draw, or program), then I won't find it interesting enough to read (listen, examine, use), nor should you.

### Helping Hands

I mentioned one difference between my careless website example and AI-generated work, but another distinction comes to mind, in this context:  Bad or boring from a human source, we can *fix*.  We can sit down with the creator, if we have some relationship with them, and urge them to focus their attention on the parts that matter.  We can teach them the parts that they find mystifying.  In effect, we can improve their work.

An LLM, though?  You could introduce better training data to get more technically adept output, but it'll still never care about anything more than keeping you in the session, because **it can't care**[^QQVdFY].

[^QQVdFY]:  And if you could design a hypothetical algorithm with plenty of linear algebra that could care about things and people, then we need to question what rights it should have under the law, because you don't want to enslave a caring intelligence.

And if you don't have anybody to help, you can't help.

### Learning the Hard/Only Way

And maybe appropriately, this also ties into the loss of skill and persistence, because we only learn by doing the parts that we wouldn't otherwise care about.  I suspect that every academically successful student learns this eventually.  If you had allowed teenage-me to create my own curriculum with no restrictions, I probably would've taken all science and math classes, because I found that fun, and I probably would've learned pretty much *nothing*, because I also read that sort of material in my free time.  If I had an AI chatbot letting me phone it in, I might well[^gp6_PR] have taken advantage of it all the time.

[^gp6_PR]:  I can't say for sure, because I wanted to respect certain teachers, suggesting that I would've put serious effort into this social studies class or that English class if slacking off felt bad.  That said, the English class with a bizarre running theme of privileged boys and men overcoming mild adversity while aggressively ignoring homoerotic subtext, that year I already phoned it in, no LLM required... {% emoji yawning face %}

Instead, I took my science classes, but state requirements and common sense also forced me to spend time on reading, writing, history, physical activity, health, economics, visual art, music, a foreign language, and so forth; my schools also required home economics (sewing and baking, primarily), shop (woodworking, metalworking), and (different school) a heavily recommended typing class.  Without those adjustments to my priorities, I wouldn't have learned about *most* things that I enjoy doing, plus I would've failed to get so many things done that needed to happen, because I wouldn't have known where to start.  Whenever I engage in those various gratitude rituals, the anonymous people who made those decisions almost always land fairly high on my list of people deserving my thanks, in addition to the teachers (between me and those administrators) who insisted that I care.

Even in the most immediate sense, if the liberal arts sort of classes in high school didn't make me write incessantly on so many topics, I doubt that I would've ended up writing thousands of answers on Quora or decided to move that sort of writing to a space of my own such as this blog.

### Who Cares

Anyway, if we need to read through three paragraphs (or the equivalent for the medium) to realize that the author, the developer, the artist, or the bridge-builder didn't care, then why should we take them seriously at anything else?  For a concrete example, consider Denver Gingerich (of the Software Freedom Conservancy) talking about the so-called [Eternal November](https://sfconservancy.org/blog/2026/apr/15/eternal-november-generative-ai-llm/), specifically this.

> We're all tempted — as some were in 1993 — to shun these “newbies” and reject their contributions aggressively.

For clarity, I actually agree with the thrust of the article overall, and have even said similar things, myself, that using the technology to get people excited about programming (or writing, or art) but "harvesting" them before they lose what little they've learned and their ability to keep at a goal would help a lot of us; I even have an entire section about it in this post, next.  But the particular line that I quoted mischaracterizes the problem, because **the LLM users don't contribute** to anything.  They somehow believe that the most-hyped technology in history has gone unnoticed by the team, and dumped "the boring parts" of what they want on the team to figure out[^fw-dsd] how to make it work.

[^fw-dsd]:  This causes a second huge problem, in that every team also now needs to find a way to distinguish the legitimate people who have a real passion for the project (and so would behave better with education) from the people who want credit for a pull request on a famous project that they can add to their work history, probably because an LLM told them that they need a beefy GitHub profile to apply to some companies.  One group might care about outreach, but the other absolutely won't.

Roughly twenty years ago, when somebody new would join a forum and ask a "frequently asked" question, some jerk would invariably reply with a link that encoded their keywords as a search string for Google.  You can still find plenty of copycat "let me Google that for you" websites somehow hoping that the joke will come back.  You can, I hope, see the problem with the joke.  The respondent put in effort---probably exactly the same effort that it would've taken to answer the question directly, sometimes more---to make the newcomer feel unwelcome, while *not actually answering the question*.  Offering LLM-generated output as "help" takes this a step further by not even waiting for the question, instead producing "answers" that require other people to fact-check and otherwise validate the work, and answering it for people who (in most cases) actually already knew how to do the work.

We may never get back to a world where people-in-general refuse to use these things, like we'll never get back to a world where people-in-general avoid junk food, for exactly the same reasons:  Convenience and tricks that manipulate you into something like instant satisfaction.  Unlike people who eat junk, though---the cookies that you ate don't affect *my* digestion or metabolism, so I can't rightly tell you not to do it---but we absolutely can create a social norm that using LLM-generated output in front of other people makes you look careless and incompetent.

## Search and Rescue

I gestured at this somewhere in the last section, and it also relates to the [session limits]({% post_url 2026-05-03-good-ai-2 %}#session-limits) suggested last time.  However, to say it explicitly, we know the following about the current LLM systems.  I have cited studies describing the effects in other recent posts, particularly that how-I-use-AI linked earlier, so I'll take them as given, here.

- A chatbot will almost always give you a useless and banal response, but *might* give you a hilariously wrong or impressively correct response.  At the beginning of the AI hype cycle, [Janelle Shane {% cc %}](https://www.janelleshane.com/) often wrote about how most of her (humor) work involved having her models generate hundreds-to-thousands of responses, then manually looking for the funniest dozen or so.  When Shane does it, the curation with intent creates art.  But when the LLM does it by its nature, it creates a "variable ratio schedule" in operant conditioning terms, making the interaction more compelling, and triggering addictive tendencies.
- Prolonged chatbot use leads to atrophied skills or a failure to learn those skills.
- Non-trivial sessions with a chatbot makes users less likely to stick with a difficult task...such as learning.
- Worn-down users get caught in a feedback loop that sends them down self-destructive paths.

This puts the technology in a dangerous category, probably most analogous to stimulants.  For example, for almost a hundred forty years, people have used methamphetamines for performance enhancement---at least partly due to unwarranted confidence boost, I should note---risking addiction, self-harm sometimes due to delusions, sensory problems, and neurological problems that all degrade performance.  Sounds familiar, right?  Sometimes doctors will prescribe a commercial version, though, for certain conditions, most notably (and oddly) ADHD.

Yet, we know (instinctively) how to mitigate the worst effects of the stimulants but not the chatbots, even though they have the same process with the same caveats.

In other words, we need to *find* people who believe that they get useful help from their LLMs before the worst effects hit them, break the addiction, and build on what they've learned.

As I have said, and as Gingerich's article (almost) says, sparking enthusiasm in different fields could make LLMs a priceless technology maybe even almost worth all the other problems, **if** we can convince the users to drop the AI assistants at the right time and teach them to do better for themselves.  If we stop them too early, then they won't believe that stopping will make their work easier, and they might lose enthusiasm.  If we catch them too late, then the LLM will have burned them out, wiping out their enthusiasm.  We want the crest, not people with addiction finally realizing that they need to seek help after hitting their notional rock-bottom, recruitment rather than Gamblers' Anonymous...

## Education

Speaking of rescuing people, have you seen those studies indicating that people often rate art-like AI extrusions more highly than they do actual art created by humans?  I believe that two reasons sit behind this result, one common and one more complex.

### Veterans of Retail Wars

For the simpler answer, I present the analogy of the [Cola Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cola_wars).  For the sake of the argument, ignore the fact that most of us today drink far less soft drink than we did back in the 1980s.  Prominent in the "war," commercials trumpeted the "Pepsi Challenge," where blindfolded participants reliably picked Pepsi Cola over Coca-Cola, shocked that they had a preference, let alone a preference for the brand that far fewer people bought.  Why?  I mean, sure, they cherry-picked their results for more dramatic commercials.  But people also replicated the results.

The answer came with [New Coke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Coke).  Pepsi has/had a sweeter and sharper flavor profile that draws attention, and Coca-Cola tried to replicate that to recapture their market share.  But the launch failed to an embarrassing degree, because what people prefer in a single sip, with instructions to judge it, doesn't map to what people would drink regularly.

In other words, the taste test went in an unexpected way, because testers tried to pick the product that gave the strongest cola-like signals in the abstract.  In the same way, LLMs pack their output full of art-like signals, so people might "prefer" AI art when glancing at two options, even though nobody would put an LLM-created sculpture in their living room.

### Appreciation

More subtle but related, over the last few decades, we have cut funding for arts education and tried to convince everybody to not care.  People no longer learn the craft of art, nor do they learn art appreciation.  I have had to teach myself art appreciation[^6tyuU_], to the extent that you can say that I know anything about it at all, to give you an idea of how long we've neglected this sort of education.

[^6tyuU_]:  If it helps anybody else out there, I have come to see the effort as trying to work out the artist's intent (beyond aesthetic) and whether they would have achieved that intent.

As such, most people don't have the foggiest idea what makes good art, and so they rely on those art-like signals that LLMs pack into their output.  As I've mentioned in a variety of posts, they always "write for posterity," even though they have nothing to say, padding out their work with structure that looks serious, literal pretense.

Even long before LLMs, we would often see this problem with poetry.  People often "prefer" meaningless doggerel, because it rhymes and uses fancy words, and so seems more poetry-like.  But they'd dismiss something like Allen Ginsberg's *Howl* or Elizabeth Bishop's *One Art*, which don't do those things at all, but pull a receptive reader in.

If we want use out of LLMs, then, we need to start teaching people how to judge things, whether code, food, poetry, or paintings.  We needed to do that anyway, as I assume that most people reading here would agree, but I don't see how anything works without doing it, now.
