## Captive Pursuit

Having finally ended **The Next Generation**'s winter hiatus, we finally have a bit of context, in that this aired between [*Ship in a Bottle*]({% post_url 2025-02-06-ship-in-bottle %}) and [*Aquiel*]({% post_url 2025-02-13-aquiel %}).

 > **SISKO**: You're telling me the contract you signed requires you to---
 >
 > **SARDA**: I guess I should have read it more carefully. I'm not a legal expert, Commander
 >
 > **SISKO**: I'm not a legal expert either, but I can assure you, after I talk to Quark, he won't hold you or anyone else to this provision concerning the exchange---

This entire exchange seems to imply that the Federation allows people to sign away their autonomy to an employer, and that most people somehow don't know about it.  That, or they don't have laws against (textbook) sexual harassment.  Either way, the phrasing seems to insist on one or the other:  Quark *won't* hold her to the contract, not that, on a site administered by the Federation and/or Starfleet, he "can't."

Oh, and if Sarda looks familiar, [Kelly Curtis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelly_Curtis) plays her, as in Jamie Lee Curtis's sister, and the daughter of Tony Curtis and Janet Leigh.  She already had a minor film career at this point, but would soon hit the main cast of **The Sentinel** for a season, among other appearances, and has shifted behind the scenes.

 > **SISKO**: Welcome to the Alpha Quadrant. I'm Commander Benjamin Sisko of the United Federation of Planets.
 >
 > **TOSK**: Alpha Quadrant?

This seems like the goofiest way to greet a foreigner who ends up in your lap accidentally.  The hypothetical civilization on the other side of the wormhole *probably* doesn't enumerate parts of the galaxy with Greek letters, and it almost certainly doesn't start naming them with the one where they don't live.

Oh, also, I don't think that anybody knew him at the time, but Tosk might look familiar as portrayed by [Scott McDonald](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scott_MacDonald_%28actor%29), who has had a fairly significant career since then, including---in our past as we go through these one show at a time---Troi's handler N'Vek in [*Face of the Enemy*]({% post_url 2025-02-20-face-enemy %}), in addition to another role that we'll see in a couple of years, and then recurring Commander Dolim in **Enterprise**.

 > **SISKO**: Agreed. Why don't you meet him by yourself at the airlock, Mister O'Brien? He might find that a little less intimidating.

Really?  I have to say, sending the one officer who we know of with post-traumatic stress and deep-seated bigotries to casually handle their first contact does seem like a bold choice, if one likely to create an international incident.

 > **O'BRIEN**: That's your name or your species?

Speaking of creating unfortunate incidents, this has serious "which of you does the man things[^PlDBke] in the relationship?" energy.

[^PlDBke]:  Don't confuse that with Man-Thing, the swamp monster from Marvel comics and films, once star of the comic given the never-not-hilarious title **Giant-Size Man-Thing**.

 > **TOSK**: You wear a weapon?
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: Defensive purposes only. It may not seem hospitable, but when you go into an alien ship for the first time, you never know what might be in there. You may even find someone who can make himself invisible, you know what I mean?

Yeah, you never know when you might find somebody who you can't see, because you'd need to *murder them*.  Normal, everyday ways of interacting with people, you know...

 > **O'BRIEN**: Well, sometimes when I walk along here I feel like we're the flea markets of the sector. But I guess our real job is to keep an eye on the wormhole. There's traffic going in and out all the time now.

Sorry, but who the Hell has a problem with *flea markets*.  I haven't gone to one in decades because I don't know of any near me, but I don't think that I have ever heard somebody object to the presence of one.  It seems especially strange on a show like this, given how flea markets---especially at the time---implicitly revolve around community (everybody goes to the fixed location for a limited duration) and sustainability (most merchandise comes second-hand).

 > **O'BRIEN**: As the Vulcans say, we're here to serve.

Do Vulcans say that?  I don't think that I've seen a Vulcan say that, and it doesn't sound like any of the Vulcans who we've met.

 > **TOSK**: Show me where the weapons are stored.
 >
 > **COMPUTER**: Habitat ring, level five, section three. Access restricted to security clearance seven and above.

Well, that seems like terrible security practice...

 > **O'BRIEN**: It's hard to say, sir. You hate to prejudge anyone.

Sure, except for the---as he'll start calling them soon enough with regularity---"bloody Cardies," and the Bajorans.  And again, he seems less than thrilled interacting with the Japanese, beyond his wife.  Other than them, you hate to prejudge.

 > **O'BRIEN**: Like a ram-scoop.
 >
 > **TOSK**: Ram-scoop?
 >
 > ...
 >
 > **TOSK**: Piece of cake. Ram-scoop. Arva nodes.

Seriously, even the foreigners from the other side of the galaxy, they force them to learn English?  Or maybe their translation system deliberately provides literal translations of idioms so that we can have "fun" scenes like this.

Oh, and by the way, [ram-scoops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bussard_ramjet) exist, a 1960-ish idea for an already-moving spacecraft to catch interstellar hydrogen in pretty much a giant funnel, to feed a fusion reaction.  I won't bother pointing out that you probably don't find them deep in the engine...

 > **O'BRIEN**: I was joking, Tosk.
 >
 > **TOSK**: I cannot tell, O'Brien.

It certainly didn't sound like a joke.  And we know that Colm Meaney knows how to deliver jokes...

 > **TOSK**: What is R and R?

Again, clear evidence that they force the foreigners to deal with their own language.

 > **TOSK**: You sleep a full third of your rotation. You rest and relax while you are awake. Alpha quadrant has far too much downtime.
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: My wife would laugh at that. She's barely seen me in the last three weeks.

Does she dislike it, though...?

 > **BASHIR**: People tell doctors things they wouldn't tell anyone else. If you could talk him into having a routine medical examination, maybe I could---

Given that they cut him off to make the point that, no, Bashir doesn't make anybody feel more comfortable, and they've stopped paying any attention to him---I'll grant you, a funny bit---it seems bizarre to even have him in this scene.

 > **SISKO**: I don't see that we have much of a choice. We can't hold him. If he wants to tell you the truth, fine. If not, wish him well and send him on his way.

This makes so much more sense than the "we can't hold them" nonsense from [*Past Prologue*]({% post_url 2026-03-12-past-prologue %}).  They'd like to know more, but they have no authority over him beyond his activities on the station.

 > **KIRA**: You may need this.
 >
 > **ODO**: Thanks, Major, I know I never use them.

Not relevant to us, but honestly, when you *hide behind people* with guns for protection, you use guns.

 > **HUNTER**: What a disappointment. And after such an entertaining beginning.

You might recognize the lead hunter-whatever as [Gerrit Graham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerrit_Graham) from pretty much everywhere, most notably from his work with Brian De Palma, but also behind the scenes writing songs and scripts.

 > **SISKO**: Centuries ago, people on my world engaged in blood-sports, killing lower species for pleasure. A few cultures still do. But even they wouldn't consider hunting a sentient being.

It seems worth pointing out, here, the contrast to Picard's usual speeches in this vein.  Over and over, we have seen Picard make wild claims about how humans used violence back in the depths of time, but no modern human would resort to such a thing, often shortly before blowing somebody up or punching them, and so I see no way of believing him, let alone a reason to do so.  By contrast, this identifies the problem narrowly, tells us that the practice does still persist, but humans don't murder whatever-sentient-means-in-this-context people as a sport.

Next, humans still engage in blood-sports, so Picard can sit down and shut up about how humans have outgrown violence.

Finally, back to that last thing that I mentioned, *sentient* feels like such a bizarre term to use, and I don't know why science fiction in general has adopted it as "the" word to use, here.  In biology, the term means what it sounds like, a creature with the capacity to sense, think, feel, or otherwise perceive.  You could make a good argument for the sentience of trees, because they perceive the Sun in some fashion, so at that point, the term doesn't mean much.  In newer and more progressive science fiction, you have probably started to see its replacement with *sapient*, deriving from Latin for discerning or wise.  Much like I try to use "culture" or similar instead of "race" (and definitely never "species," since that doesn't account for the increasing mixed parentage) for foreign groups, and try to use terms like "foreign" or "extraterrestrial" over "alien" (alienness suggests some level of universality, whereas the other terms have a more relative connotation), I have tried to consistently use terms like *conscious* instead of either, because (a) we actually mean that, and (b) nobody needs to reach for a dictionary to find the definition.

 > **SISKO**: I can't judge what is right and wrong for your world. But on this station

I mean...he *can*, but nobody on the other side has an obligation to listen to him.

 > **O'BRIEN**: Shut up, Quark.
 >
 > **QUARK**: More trouble with the little woman...
 >
 > ...
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: There is nothing wrong between my wife and I. If there were, I sure as hell wouldn't want to talk to some barkeep about it.

Yeah, that doesn't sound like domestic violence at *all*, in a "definitely sounds like domestic violence" kind of way that everybody ignores while he rants.

 > **O'BRIEN**: It's a hunt, did you know that? They're chasing Tosk in a hunt. Everybody's playing by the rules. They're playing by their rules, we're playing by ours, and he's caught in the middle. Of course, I suppose if the Ferengi don't like the rules, they just change them...
 >
 > **QUARK**: ...
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: Of course! Change the rules. Why didn't I think of that? Thanks, barkeep.

Changing rules?  It seems really bad when the Ferengi do it.  But it also seems pretty great when humans do it, because we should apparently trust humans and not Ferengi.

 > **O'BRIEN**: Glass jaw. Now I know why you wear a helmet. Up here.

By "change the rules," O'Brien apparently meant to bypass the chain of command and assault a foreign national.

 > **O'BRIEN**: The Cardies made these conduits from two meter thick duranium composite. I've never seen a scanning device that could penetrate it. Until now.

Sorry, my mistake, earlier.  The slur for the Cardassians starts in the episode where he talks about not prejudging people.

 > **O'BRIEN**: No. No, I don't think so. One day as a Tosk is enough for me, and I've got a wife and kid that wouldn't fit too well into this lifestyle.

He didn't exactly care about that when he dragged them across the galaxy for this gig...

 > **TOSK**: Will your Federation punish you for helping me?
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: Maybe. But if I know my Commander. Hell, those guys wanted a hunt. I just gave them one. Go on. You'd better get out of here while you can.

Did he really suggest that he could steam-roll over Sisko's authority?

 > **SISKO**: So in effect you assaulted him. Our first contact with a new species.

Don't forget about punching him.  That *did* happen.

 > **SISKO**: Save it. You ignored your duty to Starfleet. You took off your comm-badge so you could ignore me. You even ignored the Prime Directive by interfering with their damned hunt. Another stunt like this and your wife won't have to complain about the conditions here anymore. Do I make myself clear?
 >
 > ...
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: I knew I couldn't override all the security seals on the station, and I figured once you and the Constable located us, well, it'd be over. We'd find ourselves locked behind some force field somewhere.
 >
 > **SISKO**: I guess that one got by us.

I almost raised the issue when Sisko told Odo to take his time, but this wraps the entire idea up nicely.  Sisko (rightly, I think) agrees with O'Brien on the morality of helping Tosk escape, but O'Brien also did this in maybe the worst possible way that endangered at least a dozen people not involved in the situation.  And he did it, as I mentioned, thinking that Sisko would look the other way on a rogue action that could have started a war, for all anybody knew.

## Quick Commentary

Ignore the fact that you could describe the plot of this episode as "[*The Most Dangerous Game*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Most_Dangerous_Game) stumbles onto DS9" for a moment.  Knowing where the series goes, I want to draw attention to one (segmented) line that I believe most people have ignored, despite it not only strongly foreshadowing upcoming plot points, but also doing what every *Star Trek* story arguably needs to do.

 > **HUNTER**: But he is sentient only because we have made him sentient. He has been bred for the hunt. His entire reason to exist is the hunt. To make it as exciting, as interesting, as he can. Obviously, you do not comprehend.
 >
 > ...
 >
 > **HUNTER**: Abuse? We *honor* Tosk. They are the symbol of all that is noble and courageous. They train and condition themselves all their lives for this event. They're proud of their role in our culture.

Because **The Next Generation** became so popular---for reasons that still mystify me beyond "we couldn't find anything else on in 1987"---productions have largely forgotten about it.  But as I have probably said many times before, "good *Star Trek*" prioritizes one thing, social satire.  Roddenberry pitched it as **Wagon Train**, evoking the tropes of Westerns, especially [Manifest Destiny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifest_destiny), but looking more like [**Gulliver's Travels**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gulliver%27s_Travels) in its most memorable moments.

At its best, the franchise takes its "strange new worlds" and uses them to hold up a mirror to the worst people, to let them hear what they sound like, and to lecture them through a proxy.  Think of [*The Omega Glory*]({% post_url 2021-01-14-omega %}), for example, has Kirk scream at the white dudes that (their alien version of) the foundational documents of the United States, elevated to sacred texts that only the powerful have permission to read, "must apply to everyone, or they mean nothing."  Sure, it comes off like a goofy episode with a nonsensical plot and some atrocious acting, but it nails the tone that I mean, literally telling white Americans that you can't store freedom in a box to dole it out only to the anointed few on important occasions and still call the country free...not that I have any particular situation in mind since they wrote it.

In the same way, I'll talk about this more as the series go on, but "the hypothetical civilization," as I called it earlier in the post, will eventually emerge as the Dominion, which serves as a proxy for the United States (if not the West in general), and it starts here.  The hunter[^IsvlAs] insists that they breed the Tosk and have made them intelligent, functionally describing chattel slavery with a "White Man's Burden" cover story.  We'll later see their foot-soldiers (controlled through narcotic addiction) and middle-management, with similar quasi-religious claims that their leaders created them and made them intelligent enough to serve.

[^IsvlAs]:  I'd call it a missed opportunity to name him after Zaroff, but I suppose that the original story didn't lose copyright protection until six years ago, making that feel dicey.

I doubt that anybody would call this their favorite episode, but rewatching it, the importance of it finally dawned on me after over thirty years.  And honestly, if this show has one *massive* flaw, I'd call it never engaging with this (almost certainly bogus) origin story by finding evidence of the ancient Tosk (or Vorta[^j7sXow] or Jem'Hadar, who we'll meet in upcoming seasons) space-faring civilization.

[^j7sXow]:  Recall the Romulans' term for a mythical paradise from [**The Final Frontier**]({% post_url 2022-04-14-tff %}), *Vorta Vor*.  Total accident, I assume, but imagine the episode in the midst of the war, where the crew fights to release evidence of prehistoric Vorta contact with Vulcans, disproving the uplifted-assistant assertion.

## Conclusions

The episode goes to some weird places, but we squeeze a bit of information out.

### The Good

Sisko puts honest effort into threading the needle between doing the morally right thing and recklessly violating protocol, allowing the action while reprimanding it for the .

### The Bad

Federation labor law doesn't seem to have any way of preventing on-the-job sexual harassment.

We see an almost pathological presumption of conformity.  Everybody almost certainly expects foreigners to understand English and local cultural references.  They "joke" about endangering newcomers.

They also take weapons onto foreign ships, expecting an ambush from hidden creatures.  But they ignore potential signs of domestic violence.

The computers have programming allowing anybody to locate weapon caches.

Some human cultures continue the practice of killing animals for entertainment.

And we see racialized double-standards, with an offensive thing done by a foreigner appropriated as a good thing for humans.  This happens alongside repeatedly assaulting a foreign national and using a slur for other foreigners.

### The Weird

Apparently, flea markets have aggressively gone out of style.
