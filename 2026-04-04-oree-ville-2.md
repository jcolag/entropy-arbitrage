## À l’orée de la ville

The primary page for the book describes it as follows.

> Professionnel de l’usurpation d’identité grâce à Civi, son assistant IA, Füerzò est un pirhack. Grâce à ses compétences, il prospère en détournant tout ce qui lui est nécessaire dans un monde où la misère sociale est exacerbée par des monnaies inégalitaires et une surveillance automatisée et absurde. Déconnecté de cette sombre réalité, Füerzò reçoit une mission dans un hôpital qui va changer sa vie et sa vision du monde. Quelles alternatives s’offrent à un pirhack lorsqu’il est arraché à son hyperconnexion ?

In English, that says the following.

> A professional identity thief, thanks to Civi (his AI assistant), Füerzò is a pirate. Thanks to his skills, he thrives by diverting everything he needs in a world where social misery is exacerbated by unequal currencies and automated excessive surveillance. Disconnected from this dark reality, Füerzò receives a mission in a hospital that will change his life and his vision of the world. What alternatives does a pirate have, when he is torn from his hyper-connection?

And for context, we even get an author bio, this time.

> En pleine reconversion professionnelle après deux ans au service du CNRS en tant que chercheur en géologie, Allius a profité de son récent temps libre pour concrétiser une de ses passions de longue date : l’écriture. Mordu de fantasy et de science-fiction, c’est naturellement qu’il se tourne vers le dernier genre dans lequel il peut exprimer tout son engagement à travers son premier texte publié : À l’orée de la ville.

Again, we can look at it in English.

> In full professional conversion after two years serving the CNRS as a researcher in geology, Allius took advantage of his recent free time to realize one of his long-standing passions: writing. Fantasy and science fiction enthusiast, it is natural that he turns to the latter genre in which he can express all his commitment through his first published text: *At the edge of the city*.

We don't generally get background like this, so bravo!

## What Works Well?

This run of chapters "feels" better, I think.  We start with something like interesting action, where things happen and have consequences.  And that carries through the five chapters.

And more, a couple of passages finally provide enough context---I could complain about not making this clear from the start---to indicate that some of the "redundant" passages or the abrupt changes in direction come from the book having two (so far) protagonists.  Chapters (mostly?  I forget) have two...*things* in each, which I can now identify as subheadings, in this form.

> Anorkae
> ↓
> Füerzò

One runs in one direction, and the other has the names reversed.  Having ignored them as maybe some external information or "atmosphere" of some sort, it didn't occur to me that the directions described tells the reader who currently has whose attention.  As such, reading seems much more coherent...and wish that (again) I would've liked to have had that insight at the start of the book instead of the middle.

## What's Adaptable?

Using witches as an identity for a female-led anti-capitalist resistance movement out of sympathy with persecution than a connection to magic has its precedents---including the real-world [Women's International Terrorist Conspiracy from Hell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Women%27s_International_Terrorist_Conspiracy_from_Hell)[^89AmdW]---but we now have one in Free Culture fiction, as well.

[^89AmdW]:  Unrelated, pre-pandemic (maybe even pre-Trump), I attended a local event that managed to lure in former WITCHes as well as former (local offshoots of) [Janes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jane_Collective) as fellow attendees.  It took a lot of effort to tamp down giddy squealing, but I really enjoyed talking to them...

Also, it technically got a brief mention in the first third of the book, but the "ultranet" has appeared frequently enough now to understand it as Internet-like.
