If you don't know Robeson's work, I recommend fixing that.  In his time, coming out of the Harlem Renaissance, he became fantastically popular primarily singing progressive-tinged patriotic songs...and as a professional football player, too, because why not, I guess?  Due to his activism work, such as his involvement with [We Charge Genocide: The Crime of Government Against the Negro People](https://www.crmvet.org/info/genocide.htm) for the government's inaction on lynchings, far-right politicians branded him a Communist, and when he refused to formally and publicly renounce Communism, they had him blacklisted and revoked his passport (eliminating his ability to secure work overseas), so he largely vanished from the public eye.

## 9:05 -- Tue 26 May 2026

{% embed https://storage.ghost.io/c/a3/8c/a38c83f8-bcf7-4a9e-a1e8-14d4368e6b10/content/images/size/w2000/format/webp/2026/05/googleai.png|Google executive standing in front of screen that says Google Search is AI Search|false| %}

[<i class="fab fa-mastodon"></i>](https://mastodon.social/@jcolag/116641019976762952) [Google is its own worst enemy](https://disconnect.blog/google-is-its-own-worst-enemy/) from Disconnect

> Just ignore how the prevalence for the AI system to output false answers — or hallucinations, in industry speak — meant Google was generating millions of inaccurate Overviews every hour. Its ambition was clear: it wanted more users to spend more time on Google and far less visiting other parts of the web.

Hashtags:  #Google #AI

Who the heck aspires to *spending time* with their search engine?  I have probably mentioned before that I only had a narrow window when Google's search still turned up useful results for me, presumably because when I need to search, I need relatively obscure material, so I probably investigated alternatives and saw the problems earlier, but that doesn't make their bizarre decline *less* frustrating.

Worse, we still don't really have a good alternative.  All the centralized, generalist search engines rely on somebody else's index or multiple indices, meaning that when Google eventually leads the way in pulling their API, the other big companies will follow, and then every "independent" search engine collapses.  I have typically called [YaCy](https://yacy.net/) the best alternative, decentralized and relying on the collective indexing across nodes, but you can see right on their front page that they consider ChatGPT a viable support option, and have similar wide-eyed excitement about LLMs in the forum, suggesting that even *they* might want to move in Google's direction, assuming that we don't [already see that behavior](https://community.searchlab.eu/t/yacy-release-1-941-the-ai-release/3376) in current versions.

In theory, anybody could set up an open source search engine user interface, seed the index with [Common Crawl](https://commoncrawl.org/) and start spidering on top of that to fill the void, but you'll need over a hundred terabytes of storage to get the compressed download, then however much more to decompress it, multiplied by whatever redundancy and scaling.  It looks like new drives currently---largely because of the AI bubble---cost at least twenty bucks (US) per terabyte[^UNcSBB] unless you want to buy used, so we already have investments of tens of thousands of dollars for the version of this running in your back room, before writing any code, getting the Internet connection for both users and spiders, supporting users when things go wrong, and placating angry companies when your spider moves too quickly for their tastes...

[^UNcSBB]:  Cloud storage will apparently run about a quarter-to-half that per month, and then you'll need to also cover "egress costs" for actually using the data, so it doesn't seem worth considering as an option.

[Mwmbl](https://mwmbl.org/) looks promising, but mostly in an "early days" way, where if (for example) you [search for my name](https://mwmbl.org/search?q=John%20Colagioia), it turns up some social media profiles (mostly unused, including at least one from defunct sites), one of the small puzzle games on the site, and then a plenty of material from other people named John, because surely I didn't really care about *both* keywords.
