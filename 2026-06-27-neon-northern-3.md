## What Works Well?

The sections on the networking (the NSNN) probably come out as the showpiece of this world, feeling mostly thought through and described thoroughly.

## What Works...Less Well?

{% include lesswell.md %}

The editing actually gets worse in the adventure, where the table of contents bears no clear resemblance to the organization of the booklet.

And more concerning, the adventure doesn't really go anywhere.  It provides a map and ways to get around, emphasizing that the players need a source of light, but then doesn't indicate where one might find it, or provide any actual goals, apparently figuring that everybody playing will guess at the intent.  They *probably* need to escape, but not saying so feels weird, especially with references to killing.

## Six Years Hence:   Won't You Come In?

Continuing to check back in with projects from early in the book club, [*Won't You Come In?*]({% post_url 2020-06-27-comein %}) comes from the broader *Ascension Epoch* fictional world.

I haven't kept up, but checking their website now, I note a couple of changes.

- They no longer keep their stories behind a paywall.
- Nothing on the site references any Creative Commons license anymore.
- The project no longer describes itself as "unapologetically Christian and Libertarian."

Actually, on that last point, I should probably go a bit further behind the scenes than usual, even though I need to mostly go on memory, beyond my notes quote that characterization but the post not mentioning it.  Until preparing for that post, I had never *read* any of the stories from the project, but early versions of the website---remember, I had gone on a hunt for Free Culture fiction somewhere around 2003--2010 for some project or other---included a fair amount of background on the project, both their intents and the world's history, such as having the United States dissolve after losing the Civil War.  Why not mention this in the original post?  I would *guess* that I either misplaced my notes or didn't want to expose what might sit behind the paywall then in place, but I can't recall.  Actually, I may have also dismissed it because I can't find those early versions of the website in the Internet Archive, making this a fairly literally unfounded accusation.

Anyway, I bring it all up, because it clarifies the obsession that the introductory story has with "bad neighborhoods" and "thugs," which the rest of us tend to recognize as code for over-policed neighborhoods that have certain racial demographics due to decades of red-lining.  And before anybody complains about my characterizing Christians in this way, which you'll actually notice that I *didn't* actually do, maybe put that effort into demanding that your notional siblings stop using you as a human shield for their bigotry...

However, the middle item actually seems like the most relevant issue for us.  If *I* wanted to use their material for something, I would prepare to spend a lot of my time scouring the Internet Archive for the last time that the project identified a public license, since the license won't cover any changes or additions made after removing the declarations.

This might make it the first Free Culture fiction project that I know of that has abandoned its license.  They can't revoke the license on material already covered as stated in §2(a)(1), but it does mean that you now need to understand versions of each text.
