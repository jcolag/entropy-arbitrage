At the risk of constantly returning to the subject of the lack of use from large language models---and I hope to keep this one short---early in June, I started seeing a strange shift in sentiment.

![A humanoid robot bust missing one arm from the shoulder down and torn off around where a person's ribs would sit. The remaining arm and the face suggest a resigned attitude](/blog/assets/6156869386_376c5d13c9_o.png "Some robots pay for this kind of treatment, I hear...")

After years of screaming at us to "get on board or get left behind," and after the massive backlash especially from software developers, a bunch of people---half a dozen blog posts that made it to my screen, as somebody who doesn't seek this nonsense out at all---took it upon themselves to inform us that we've all gotten it (and them) wrong.  In reality, they now tell us, we have all overlooked the *best* way to do our work with LLMs.

## The Cunning Plan

Despite the breathless excitement (and aforementioned screaming) about networks of LLMs talking to each other---the alleged "improvements" that the companies keep trying to sell---while pulling information from the Internet and scrubbing through your files, we should *really* use these tools more carefully, they tell us.  I keep seeing the same recurring process recommended, now.

- Think carefully about the specific next step required in the program.
- Ask the LLM to generate that tiny snippet of code.
- Carefully review the snippet, with a willingness to discard it and start over, to ensure that it serves the purpose.
- Carefully integrate the snippet into the overall program.

And...OK, yes, you *can* do that, and doing that will certainly produce better results than "vibe-coding" your trite idea and pouring money into advertising---sorry, "growth hacking"---to get people onto your completely insecure app that'll leak all their private data.  But this (allegedly) improved process raises a huge question.

## Why

Seriously, why the heck would you ever want to do such a thing?

As I have pointed out repeatedly, the actual code takes the least effort and time out of a project.  Writing code *feels* like the hard part when you first start learning to program, because you only have that one part to focus on.  And for the first couple of years of your career, you might only get assigned those tasks, assuming that you otherwise have a fully staffed team and nobody notices you picking up the other parts of the job.  If you haven't yet taken to programming, it certainly looks near-impossible in some ways, too, or at least mystifying.

However, compared to the rest of the project---coming up with the idea, selling it to stakeholders, planning on how best to release it, estimating and scheduling the work, planning/designing what needs to happen, marketing the new thing, testing it, selling it, and getting it into the hands of people with money (assuming that you need to pay the bills), supporting the product, and fixing and supporting the changes---the code takes the *least* time and money.  And with some experience, most programmers don't really even "see" the code as they write it, any more than we write prose by envisioning a sentence parse tree and searching for words that mean what we want and fit in certain slots.

### Paying the Piper

And since I mentioned cost, that brings up the relatively high salaries that software developers can command.  This comes from a few factors, I suspect.

Primarily, as mentioned, even developers don't spend most of their time churning out code, because the project doesn't call for that.  The *other parts* take more time and energy.

Then, I don't want to use the term "accountability sink," here, but as the people who wrote the code, our reputations hinge on it working.  When things fail, a single developer never *caused* the problem---every failure results from a flawed system, not an individual---but you can trace the faults directly to that person[^ExJ6z1], and most companies stop there in assigning responsibility, because it takes less effort to reprimand one employee than it does to investigate to find the reason-for-the-reason-for-the-reason-et-cetera for the failure and overhaul processes to mitigate it.  And that kind of accountability, in turn, costs money.

[^ExJ6z1]:  Early in my career, most of my teams had an informal ceremony before a release where each of us needed to affirmatively state that we had no worries about the release.  Most directly, this gave us the opportunity to make the case for expanding the schedule.  But it also carried an implied lesson that we all put our reputations on the line with every release.

Related to both the previous issues, as I have mentioned in previous posts talking about AI, even though code represents the least effort and also creates costs for the organization rather than assets, it turns the rest of the work into something that you can ship, making it a fairly literal "necessary evil."

Finally, as many other people have said for decades, we play the role of professionals, even though we don't go through any licensing process.  Good software engineers will explain the consequences of certain decisions and what it would cost to mitigate those consequences.  They/we---if you'll pardon me classifying myself as *good*---make sure that you can rely on the schedule for planning other projects, as the scope and team changes.  They/we build the infrastructure that allows for continuity when people come and go on the project, contingency plans for when things go wrong, and so forth.  And, because all the non-software tasks funnel into the software work, good engineers also become coordinators at the other end, as the only people who have relationships with the entire project as an ordinary part of the job.

## Bubbles and Pin Pricks

I bring this all up, because it feels like an admission that the bubble accomplished nothing useful.  When you shift the messaging from "our tool can do everything, so you don't even matter" to "use it to work the way you would ordinarily work, but slower and more expensive, with an unreliable tool," it feels like begging people to keep paying attention.

"OK," they seem to say, "vibe-coding actually made a complete mess of things even as we told you it worked wonders.  But you can still pay for the tools, using them like an expensive and unreliable boilerplate generator..."

This seems to match the business side.  Believing that they have the world hooked on their ~~dangerous stimulant~~ text-generating technology that treats conversations like a form of language translation, the AI companies have started [jacking up prices {% cc %}](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/05/the-token-bill-comes-due-inside-the-industry-scramble-to-manage-ais-runaway-costs/), because they have aggressively subsidized usage so far, meaning that every [AI enterprise loses orders of magnitude more money {% cc %}](https://isaiprofitable.com/) than it makes in revenue...except for Nvidia selling to all of them.

And then we have the possible endgame:  Something like a dozen AI companies (including xAI, already accomplished) plan either an IPO or an IPO-like stock offering to specifically funnel money into their AI divisions.  Meanwhile, pretty much every analyst confidently points out that these companies *still* don't have any way of turning a profit, still run on the "we only need to ignore the law, have someone invent a working fusion reactor for us, and have a useful product manifest from the aether to succeed" premise that I've talked about since the beginning, and rising costs have gotten companies to finally start admitting that their massive AI bills haven't produced any value.

### A Certain Musky Odor

I don't want to call this a [pump and dump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pump_and_dump) or a [bag-holder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bagholder) scheme (or both) in the making, because I have no inside information that would let me know and would hate to make unfounded assumptions, to say nothing of the legal liability in doing so.  But it certainly *looks like* one of those schemes to either grab some cash before they shut down or keep the lights on long enough for another investment round.

Personally, I can't make sense of the "no, keep using it even though it has made your company demonstrably worse by every metric; you'll figure it out" sentiment without that assumption.

None of these companies, after all, knows how to turn a profit.  Or, maybe more to the point, they have an inkling in how to bring in money, but not through what we would consider traditional business.  For example, SpaceX (now part of xAI along with {% x %}, a perfectly normal company {% emoji eyeroll %}) made it as far as it got, pre-2025, with [thirty-eight billion dollars in government funding](https://www.congress.gov/119/meeting/house/117956/documents/HMKP-119-JU00-20250226-SD003.pdf).  Sam Altman has talked about the dream of using [UBI and AI subscriptions](https://www.businessinsider.com/openai-sam-altman-universal-basic-income-idea-compute-gpt-7-2024-5) to "fix" the economy, in effect turning his service into a national-level utility subsidized by the government.  Anthropic claims to have profits coming soon, but they project that seemingly based on a quarter when [companies accidentally spend half a trillion dollars {% cc %}](https://www.axios.com/2026/05/28/ai-spending-roi-enterprise-costs) on Claude as they raise prices and ignoring everybody cutting back.

You might notice a theme, there, even as you check out the smaller AI groups:  They want their profits manifested by having authorities declare their "service" a necessity, then either pay for it directly out of national budgets or have you pay for it with your government cutting you a check to cover for the tiny detail that nobody has a job because of their service.

And expect them to roll that money---such as from the IPOs---right into [even more lobbying {% cc by-nc-nd %}](https://www.citizen.org/news/one-in-four-federal-lobbyists-now-work-on-ai/) to jockey for those big contracts and utility-like status.  Oh, and by the way, we also need to cut their taxes, because they *create jobs*, which you can tell by the number of jobs that they want to wipe out and how depressed the economy seems with their convincing CEOs to cut employees. {% emoji person shrugging %}

## The Point

I don't know if I actually had a real goal in writing this, other than that I *needed* to talk about this "use AI slowly and carefully" idea.  Honestly, I like the plan that they outline, except for the part where it uses an LLM.  Think carefully, write a small amount of code, review that code against its context, iterate until it makes perfect sense, and integrate it.

Anyway, I hope that this means that I don't need to write any more AI posts.  These people exhaust me...
