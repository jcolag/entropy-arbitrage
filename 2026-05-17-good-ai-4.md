If you came into this group of posts without the background, the [introduction post]({% post_url 2026-04-26-good-ai-1 %}) described that I have seen a few prominent open web advocates---people whose opinions I generally respect---trying to pitch the idea of "Good AI," often seeming to mean the Big AI services, but slightly more sustainable and ethical.  It makes no sense to me, as you might guess by my post about why I [don't use LLM chatbots]({% post_url 2026-01-18-using-ai %}).  I followed that up kicking around [possible technical improvements]({% post_url 2026-05-03-good-ai-2 %}) to LLM services that might mitigate the problems that we see emerging in studies.

![A drawing of a robot with non-articulated legs but gorilla-like arms, plus a smiley face drawn on its face](/blog/assets/Ink-and-Marker-Robot-Illustration-81.png "Kill'em (dashes?) with kindness...")

These posts represent me trying to work through the possibilities to find a set of modifications that might result in a system that helps people.

## Restrictions and Acknowledgements

Surely, the (deliberately unnamed) people who I mention must see the same studies that I see, showing that LLMs have addictive qualities for at least some users, cause an over-confidence when scheduling work, take longer than manual work, produce subtly error-riddled results, and sap the users of both skills and motivation.  Seizing the means of sarcasm-quotes *production* solves a legitimate problem of guillotining the power of Big AI, sure, but it doesn't solve the problems with the technology, and doesn't make it *good*.  I realize that my fellow Long Islander Lee Zeldin would disagree with me, but you don't have "Good Pollution" because local companies dump the highest quality waste products into the water source.

I do think that those first steps---usually something like ethical sourcing and citing of training data, reproducible training, and targeting cheap commodity hardware---can enable improvement.  But if we only shift people's attention from Gemini to a hypothetical Free Software LLM run by Ramakanta down the street, that won't magically fix the deskilling or poor quality results.  Seizing the means can nudge us towards a better path, but *only* doing that accepts the toxic framing that this bubble---which already shows signs of bursting quickly, as prices rise, problems become more apparent, and pundits start testing the waters for the next bubble---shows us some inevitable future where humans keep hitting {% key Sure, Whatever %} button while computers exist to extrude combinatorial stacks of existing work-slurry with fingers crossed that the next remix of other people's work does what you want.

Therefore, this series of posts tries to step beyond "open source and not an outright blight on the environment" to find real changes to both the technology and our culture, that might---I freely admit that I don't have anything more than guesswork, here, and hope to inspire better ideas more than I imagine fixing the industry---improve the tools.

Two posts back, we took a look at possible improvements to the technology to make them less toxic.

- Audit training data for bias
- Ditch the chatbot
- Add sidebars searching for confirmation
- Limit the range of topics
- Stop interacting after ten minutes and take breaks
- Measure output by information density

Then, last time, I started looking at some social changes that might help.  This time through, we'll finish looking at the potential changes to the cultural side, fixing *us* instead of the tools.

I emphasize again that everybody should consider this completely hypothetical, speculative at best.  After all, I don't know for sure what can make this stuff work well for us, or even if we *can* make it work.  If I did, I'd happily raise piles of money---and if somebody wants to fund this, you know where to find me, I guess---and put all the [Roko's basilisk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roko%27s_basilisk) clowns (more on that later) out of business while employing people who need the work.  But I can certainly look at the problems that I listed before, experiment to feel out the pain points that might cause those problems, and work from there.

## Humanize

In many ways, I think that you can see the current AI industry as "the final project" of a curriculum kicked off in the backlash to the Enlightenment.  Early in the blog's run, I wrote about how the Enlightenment---initially little more than a couple of dozen writers hopped up on coffee writing each other letters, at its core, speculating about collective governance that adapted its policies based on the extent to which they achieved broadly useful outcomes---largely [overthrew the global social order]({% post_url 2020-09-06-enlight %}) of competing absolute monarchs seeing the world as their property, as these newer ideas spread to influence millions.  I have mentioned since that post that, in the centuries since, you really only have [three types of countries]({% post_url 2021-05-30-winning %}), democracies, autocracies propped up by oil money, and irrelevant autocracies[^CBzVh6].

[^CBzVh6]:  And we can see this classification at work directly in the United States today.  The far-right wants to replace democracy with petrodollars, but at a time when renewable energy has become cheaper than oil.  What do they do?  They back resource-hungry technologies such as cryptocurrencies and AI while starting wars to lock up the oil supply, delivering record profits to oil companies.  But that still can't get them to what they want, so they have *also* given up on global leadership, remaking us---to the extent that the administration succeeds---more into North Korea than Saudi Arabia.

As that earlier post continues to recount, some people looked at this new philosophy of constitutional governance, civil liberties, broad tolerance, scientific thinking, and social progress, and they had instant objections.  Looking back, we could **imagine** them maybe objecting to the limited vision as we would today, how their philosophy often quietly carved out exceptions in that tolerance for *those people*, rather than agitating for support of the rights of women and non-white people.  We could totally imagine that, and too many people have come to confuse "didn't go far enough" with "did something bad."

They didn't do that, though.  Instead, the [Romantic movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanticism) demanded emotional truths and adventure, looking back to what they imagined as [chivalry]({% post_url 2024-03-17-chivalry %}) as a model, urging a return to social hierarchies and struggle, a mythical utopia to retreat to from progress based on empirical results.  Where the Enlightenment started the West on the path to recognizing that our humanity rests in our relationships and cooperative efforts, the Romantics saw the rugged individual standing against the world.  You know, *this* jackass.

![Wanderer above the Sea of Fog, my go-to illustration of the Romantic movement](/blog/assets/Caspar_David_Friedrich_-_Wanderer_above_the_sea_of_fog.png "For the record, I actually like this painting, but it also feels emblematic of everything wrong with society since")

Every libertarian, every aspiring "good guy with a gun," and every gentrification or colonization advocate sees himself in that coat[^ILlMJP].

[^ILlMJP]:  I should note that I oversimplify this a lot, because the artistic and political aspects of the Romantic movement sometimes diverge in weird ways.  For example, when talking about the movement, probably nobody comes to mind faster than "mad, bad, and dangerous to know" Lord Byron.  But when he joined the House of Lords, he scandalized the country by passionately and vocally opposing the death penalty for Luddite frame-breakers, then supporting the removal of legal restrictions on Catholics, publishing poems a decade later that feel like Enlightenment anthems, and seems to have fought in the Greek Revolution primarily so that he could get his money directly to victims of the war, but also played tiny parts in ushering in a democratic Greek state before his death.

The movement's name has changed over the intervening centuries, but the obsession with fictionalized versions of the Roman Empire and Medieval Europe and how people imagine gender roles and power politics of those eras, not to mention that "rugged individualist" who allegedly loses out by participating in society, remain right at the heart of everything.

### Maybe-Crackpot History

Personally, I see this conflict as ongoing and central.  For one way of looking at this, if you overlay the emergence of the Enlightenment on the so-called [Second Hundred Years' War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Hundred_Years%27_War)---since they both largely end with the Atlantic Revolutions---and follow the results of the individual conflicts into the future, then you start to see most wars (including cold wars) and political conflicts as part of this same continuous struggle.  Or in other words, you could argue that we have fought a single world war that started within a decade or two of 1700, starting as a regional conflict between mainly England and France, but spiraling (through the ugly magic of imperial colonization) into a fight between autocrats and democracies around the world.  Even if you ignore that single-war framing[^c51Djc], though, you can still see that the divide between "equal rights and rule by consent of the governed" and "some people have a birthright to status" drives a lot of conflict, underpinning everything from border disputes to how much we should care about borders at all to who gets to use which bathroom to whether the wealthy should pay taxes to whether we at least have the right to stop billionaires from directly abusing people.

[^c51Djc]:  A framing so admittedly fringe that I can't find anybody else proposing it, even among the tiny slice of historians who buy into the Second Hundred Years' War idea.  My reasoning stems from noting (as others have, including Winston Churchill) the global scope of the Seven Years' War (1756--1763) and probably a laundered version of Don H. Doyle's argument that Napoleon III's and the UK's interest in the American Civil War (1861--1865), plus the Confederacy's interest in recolonizing South America, nearly made *it* a world war, itself.  Putting those together with the "official" two world wars and the Cold War, not to mention the wars between and how each plants the seeds for the next, suggests a narrative frame that the Nine Years' War (1688--1697) started as a territorial war against imperial French expansion, but spread across the world as pushes for and fights against democracy and social progress, sometimes with opposing armies on the same notional side.  I mean, even the current-or-recent-depending-on-who-you-listen-to war on Iran makes far more sense as two authoritarian governments going to war with each other in order to justify stifling democracy *at home* than as either party wanting anything from the other, or historically, the Soviet Union under Stalin siding against the Axis.

And maybe more notably for some people, the Enlightenment also revived interest in thinking about class conflict that marked a surprising amount of surviving writing in the ancient world.  I don't want to say that the thinking subsided, because the 1300s alone saw a handful of high-profile labor revolts and Shakespeare touches on class issues a few times, but writing about the recognition of a class struggle seems mostly quiet between Ancient Greece/Rome and Jean-Jacques Rousseau.

### Labor Force

I say that LLMs feel like an attempt to complete this regressive project of rolling back the Enlightenment, because it claims to do what those regressives have tried to do for decades if not centuries:  They'd really like us to set aside the "horrors" of industrialized work in the factories and offices, because that work doesn't count to them.  Instead, they think that men should do "real work" (think mining, construction, military service...or having enough money for an idle lifestyle) while women handle unpaid domestic chores (primarily incubating fetuses).  And by automating (or claiming to automate, at least) intellectual and physical labor, what jobs remain...?

That gives us another way to bring the fake-AI technology under control, though, valuing humans as individuals.  This echoes other things that I have said over the years, so it probably won't surprise anybody to see me talking about it now.  But we need to value intention and interest, not volume of work.  And, when applicable, we need to value actual human effort[^lOLbNr].

[^lOLbNr]:  The idea makes me a bit uncomfortable, by the way, because this comes precariously close to asking people to thank their abusers.  It takes *serious* effort to send unwanted gifts, monitor a person's activity, and control their lives, but we don't want to value that kind of labor.  And we don't want to say that we should only value labor that does what we ask and pleases us, because that steers right back into productivity.  In other words, use your judgment, here.

Notably, we have seen this before, and repeatedly.  The [Luddites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luddite), socialists, the labor movement, pushes for social progress, and more have all had their roots in the ideas of the Enlightenment and concerns about capital keeping the benefits of industrialization away from workers by refusing to value the humans in the system.

### The Household

I should say that, when we talk about labor, we also need to remind people that stay-at-home parents clean, cook, provide child care, and perform other duties, not only all at the same time, but "on call" around the clock with few if any days off.  In the United States, we have a national minimum wage of $7.25 per hour, unchanged for decades, though many states have done better[^bXih7u]; every hour worked past forty (other than "exempt" workers, mostly management) gets paid at one-and-a-half times the hourly rate.  If we treated such parents as minimum wage employees, that would cost $87,704[^hGazMR], about what they'd get paid---and with far better hours---as an entry-level software engineer, a registered nurse, or an air traffic controller in a lot of the country.

[^bXih7u]:  Some states also do worse, keeping a lower-than-national minimum wage on the books in case they can convince Congress to repeal labor law.

[^hGazMR]:  Since overtime gets billed weekly, we start with 168 hours per week.  Subtract forty as billed normally, and multiply the remaining 128 by 1½, paying the equivalent of 232 hours of work per week.  Divide by seven to get the hours per day, and multiply by 365 and again by the minimum wage to get a prospective annual salary.

As *women's work* (in the eyes of the worst people), however, it "doesn't count," allowing for this obsession with forcing women to do it and convincing them that the "tradwife" lifestyle would appeal to them more than personal autonomy and legal rights.

As a corollary, start seeing the obsession with "declining birth rates" for its eugenicist and misogynist nature that demands that white women have an obligation to carry some wealthy white man's baby, yes, even when they pitch it as a problem of not having enough caregivers for the aging population.

### Care and Attention

Housework matters, though, because *caring* matters, leading to the other half of this stance.  A superficial scribble or a blurry photograph has more value than a pristine AI-generated image, because nobody cared about the latter.  Our culture has somehow come to see enthusiasm and sincerity as childish or carrying some suspicious subtext, so why should it matter if a computer strung together words or pixels to build a probable response instead of someone actually doing the work to satisfy some need or urge?  The cynics keep trying to convince us that humans don't really do anything more than that anyway, the most extreme versions of this idea telling us that we even only do it all on autopilot on the path to mating to spread our genetic material[^Pki-iQ].

[^Pki-iQ]:  Notice that, besides literally dehumanizing all of us, it also tries to normalize eugenics.  And you might notice that many of the same billionaires telling us that AI will replace (or micromanage) all the knowledge workers also happen to have plans for baby-ranches where they can sire many children like retired horses.

We need to actively and maybe formally reject such thinking.  Enthusiasm and care, rather than statistically probable choices, get the job done, and we should celebrate that, never letting anybody forget it.  We always needed to reject that cynical thinking, but this seems like the most effective time to do it.

*Do things that excite you*, in other words, and share that excitement with people.  When people share their excitement with you, as long as it doesn't impose on you, encourage them instead of rolling your eyes.  If you suppress any part of your personality, make it that junior high school bit that finds enthusiasm ridiculous or suspect.

I should mention the irony that so many people objected to the term "content" when referring to art, treating unique human creations as undifferentiated extrusions to spread evenly across rental and streaming services.  But when we create software that fairly literally extrudes undifferentiated *content* as a rental service, suddenly people want to call it art, apparently more worthy of the term (to them) than actual art, probably because the entire [Big Tech narrative obsesses over humanizing the bots](https://globalvoices.org/2026/04/16/there-is-no-connection-but-human-why-it-is-vital-to-value-human-creativity-in-the-age-of-ai/), whereas we want to ["make space for imagination"](https://globalvoices.org/2026/04/16/on-centring-the-human/)[^csNt0u].

[^csNt0u]:  I recommend that entire series of "Don't ask AI, ask a peer" posts, by the way.

Incidentally, check the [previous post]({% post_url 2026-05-10-good-ai-3 %}#education) for why I suspect that people feel more comfortable calling some image generated from some "ArtStation 4K HDR hyperrealistic" prompt *art*, while the rest of us make *content*, and how we might fix that.

## Actual Work

As most of you know, I come from the software industry.  As such, a lot of my thinking has gotten shaped by decades of needing to get the best possible product out the door, given constrained resources, at the most useful time for the company, alongside the team.  And that has taught me one thing:

**The code barely matters**.

I mean, yes, the code matters in the literal sense that, without it, nobody's computer does anything.  But the code matters in the same way that your choice of stationery matters when you write somebody a letter, largely conveying the message without contributing much.

For those of you not working in software, fear not, I'll circle back to apply this lesson elsewhere.

### Caring Again

Echoing [that earlier section](#humanize), all the *caring* matters.  The customer(s) care(s) about specific aspects of their data and their processes, which the software needs to express to have any use.  We need to understand our predecessors, what they cared about, and what they did about it, because that explains the current situation.  The sales team cares about priorities that (when they get it right) means money coming in that keeps the development team employed; the marketing team cares about a competing but complementary set of priorities that looks at the longer term.  If we don't care about colleagues, then we burn them out and probably need to replace them---costing emotional energy, time, and money---while they pull themselves back together.  We care about deadlines, because colleagues and customers plan around them, but they also *represent* cares, and so understanding those underlying cares often makes it possible to juggle priorities.  Once you get to the code, you care about design that the team can implement and maintain in reasonable amounts of time.  The team needs to care about quality or, like with the customer priorities, the software doesn't do what it needs to do.  We write documentation because we care about our successors not having as much trouble understanding us as we did our predecessors.  And I've probably skipped a bunch of other places where this work centers on caring about individual people.

Or if you want a more macho noun instead of caring, think about the project's *scars*.  Every time that the team failed to care about one of those things properly, it caused damage.  The project embodies all the imperfect attempts to recover from that damage.  The code pales in comparison to the value of all that.

### Parts of Parts

The code carries a distillation of some of that care, sure.  But it represents the smallest percentage of the work.  Early in the blog, I wrote about [estimating projects]({% post_url 2021-01-27-estimate %}), where I floated a typical ratio of 2:1:3, that for every unit of time spent writing code, you'll almost certainly spend twice that in designing the application and three times it in testing/fixing.  Notice that *only* includes the implementation side itself, quietly ignoring the even-more-substantial time spent gathering all the requirements.

Because of all that, the code accounts for *maybe* a tenth of the effort on the project, and then only in cases where the project has a known goal and scope.  The code matters because you can't ship caring, but it also doesn't matter because it wouldn't take long to replace it.  And we recognize that in the industry by talking about "software engineering," not "coding."  Engineering involves taking ideas and getting them to market reliably and at scale[^ExPxeZ], not remembering the syntax of a `for` loop or the structure of a `details` element.

[^ExPxeZ]:  I mean the scale of the project and team, here, not scaling the throughput of a project.  The latter has its own specialized discipline that only applies to some work.  But a team that doesn't have a plan---not necessarily formal---in place to add a new feature or a new colleague doesn't do anything like engineer.

It also means that writing code faster *increases* costs, rather than decreasing it, because it floods and overloads the rest of the system.  You reduce that tenth (or less of the project) but now need to multiply the other ninety percent (or more) of the project to make sure that the code does what everybody needs it to do.  I went through why in my post about the [Software Crisis]({% post_url 2026-03-29-software-crisis %}), for people who find that confounding, but in short, complexity in software comes from interactions, which rise exponentially with lines of code, so more code means repeatedly doubling the number of things that could go wrong.  And making sure that it doesn't go wrong requires more and better communication.

### Set Your Watch by the Screams

I should note that everybody in the software industry over the age of around thirty already knows this, regardless of whether they know that they know.   Every five years or so, a new bloc of countries emerges as the place for every company to outsource their development work on the cheap.  I have watched companies cut staff for contracting houses in East Asia, South Asia, Southeast Asia, South America, and Eastern Europe, in roughly that order.  I probably missed whatever came after Eastern Europe, since they've had a lot on their plates in recent years, calling back to that whole democracy-versus-autocracy fight.

No, I won't play the Ugly American game here, trying to undermine overseas workers with half-compliments.  You know, the "they do credible work, but..." something that has nothing to do with their ability to turn in usable code, that quickly gets generalized to everybody of that ethnicity.  The *companies* often bordered on fraudulent---a surprising number of them would even reassign developer identities to cover up staff turnover---but the developers did what they could, given the constraints.

They turned in a lot of mediocre work, mind you, but they also had weird constraints such as working in inconvenient time zones, having bosses that saw them as disposable, and often not having continuity on the project.  And I have worked with locals who did worse than mediocre with fewer constraints.

And yet...off-shoring always[^UBzaGd] *increases* the cost of getting working software out the door, even though the price of developers has dropped on paper.

[^UBzaGd]:  The exceptions come from where the company directly hires the offshore team, complete with a management structure.  Almost nobody wants to spring for that, though.

Sure, some of that surcharge comes from outright mismanagement.  Whenever somebody objects to declining code quality, someone with more authority will point out that they can afford to have the cheaper developers re-do the work several times before they hit the same price as having it done right the first time.  And that means slipping deadlines and plummeting morale.  But that doesn't account for a consistent problem.  (When people say that AI stands for "Absent Indians" or sometimes "Absent Indonesians," they don't *only* mean that the Global South did most of the reinforcement training that makes "it's not just an X---it's *Y*" an unfortunate normal part of all our days...)

The real problem comes from misunderstanding where the value lives.  Cheap code increases the cost of management, because teams with different cultures need to coordinate with few if any overlapping hours.  It increases the cost of testing, because nobody knows in advance what might have gotten caught in miscommunication.  Because it "costs nothing" to have them work something, other teams need to plan for late delivery of what they need.  And if any part of the organization has the stability to build any institutional knowledge around the product, the company needs to "rent" that knowledge instead of having somebody in-house and on-salary to ask, tying the project to that offshore team while prices inevitably rise[^Ofq1Db] and undo the initial cost savings.

[^Ofq1Db]:  Either the economy in the remote country strengthens, bringing their currency closer to Western currencies, or their software industry matures to where people start opening companies and hiring your contractors away, rapidly increasing demand for that labor.  It can't *not* happen.

In other words---say it with me---*the code barely matters*.  The value of software lives everywhere *except* the code.  Cut corners on the code itself, and you'll only move the price somewhere else in the development chain, and not at a discount.

### Arts and More

Now, other fields might not work exactly the same way---the complexity model differs, you have fewer large teams, and companies don't generally search for ways to cut their poetry costs---but I know that they come pretty close.  I know that, when a person creates art without a unified vision of what they want, they often need to do more work, ping-ponging between the equivalents of implementation, testing, and design, and needing to redo all three.  Those ratios might change for different media, but implementation still always has the smallest value except to the extent that you don't release anything else, the actual concrete instance of the art only mattering because it embodies the ideas behind it, and because you can't get your ideas into my brain without some transmissive medium.

And much like code only matters because of all the care that led to it, art only matters because of the experience that went into it.  We inappropriately valorize artists who live(d) in pain, because we recognize this value of experience, and our empathy makes pain the quickest experience to identify as potentially important.  But even lightweight television shows can resonate and stick with us, when the experience that goes into them feels more profound than somebody needing a paycheck.

### The Point

When I talk about recognizing "the actual work," I mean exactly this sort of thing, the caring, the scars, or whatever other description that you want to dangle from it.  When somebody has AI create something, they treat the artifact like the entire work.  The art doesn't arise from experiences.  The code doesn't come from caring about the needs and motivations of many people.  And it shows.

We can only really fix this by building (rebuilding?) a culture that understands that the value never sat in the mechanical bits, but in all the social and experiential aspects around the craft.

## Togetherness

The previous two sections have talked about focusing on and honoring enthusiasm and care (to over-simplify), so you can probably see what I have in store, here:  We need to work and organize as groups.

You can have collections of instances of LLMs "work together," yes.  The excitement about "agents"---not the definition historically used for the term in this context, but whatever---centers on this.  Dawkins deluding himself into believing that he has Claude socializing with other instances of itself also centers on this silly idea.  But you don't really get anything new from combining these tools, because they don't have experiences.  At best, you spend ten times as much to rent ten times as much GPU time at once.

We, on the other hand, complement each other and learn from one another.  Together, we can get more of the actual (caring) work done, out of proportion to scaling up what we can do individually.  Caring about each other helps us to recognize the important parts of the work and emphasize the human aspect of it all, improving the chances that LLMs do useful things for those teams.

And yes, I don't necessarily want to say it out loud, but you all know *exactly* what I mean by organizing as a separate activity from cooperating.  LLM companies wouldn't have had any chance of steam-rolling through industries if a strong labor movement could have threatened to shut operations down in those industries instead of hoping that helping the steam-roller might preserve their jobs.

## Stupidest Basilisk in the Room

Calling back to a couple of sections, here, we also need to talk about [TESCREAL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TESCREAL)---raised in [last Thursday's post]({% post_url 2026-05-07-nagus %})---the toxic cocktail of Silicon Valley's worst ideas.  As the Wikipedia article puts it, the ideas include [Transhumanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transhumanism), [Extropianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extropianism), [Singularitarianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singularitarianism), Cosmism, [Rationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rationalist_community), [Effective Altruism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effective_Altruism), and [Longtermism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longtermism).

Nobody identifies as an adherent of the whole mess, and those who openly follow individual bits make a show of focusing on "reasonable" parts[^JguNEP].  But you can see evidence of those ideas everywhere, the idea that individual people don't actually matter, only the *future*, often their future or some hypothetical future of "the species" (in which they expect their many children to have power).

[^JguNEP]:  By the way, Effective Altruism gets *pretty* toxic, but surely they meant Effective Accelerationism, right?  The idea of unfettered technological progress serving as a panacea feels far more TESCREAL-relevant than stubbornly refusing to donate to charities that pay their staff or burning a year or two at a terrible company to amass savings...

And that brings us to the inane basilisk idea that I have mentioned for a couple of posts, now.  We have people who, because they read a poorly written forum post, believe that a possible future super-AI might torture a simulation of them if they don't help bring the super-AI into existence.  And they place more value on the wellbeing of that imaginary digital copy of themselves than on the billions of people who they gleefully externalize their costs onto, such as you and me.

Let me repeat that:  Many wealthy people supporting AI do so, at our expense, in order to protect a pretend computer model of them from torture in some distant future, because to them, a possible-but-unlikely avatar with their personality demands more empathy than every real person.

Notice that this dumps out of and feeds TESCREAL.  The scary dragon, the blackmail threat, only works as a viable belief because of a prior belief in The Singularity™, Cosmism[^Xuypwf], Rationalism (in the "come up with scientistic-sounding reasons for whatever we wanted to do" sense), and Transhumanism.  And it justifies sacrificing everybody (else) today, because some indeterminate long-term future might provide a better life for the descendants of any surviving humans, which happens to coincide with the needs of growing a company's profits.

[^Xuypwf]:  Honestly, Cosmism in the Russian sense imported to the United States already accounts for a lot of the cocktail, deriving as it did from galaxy-spanning science fiction.  We probably could've lumped it all together under that banner, since we no longer talk about its early Soviet origins...

Right.  They invented their own emotionally stunted version of [Pascal's wager](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal%27s_wager), where the "bad" column says that their god extorts them into funding LLMs, at the expense of everything else on the planet, by threatening some hypothetical digital poppet...and they want us to take them seriously.  They want to see ourselves as gullible for not kowtowing to the evil extortion-god, not them for falling for the silliest confidence scheme.

### Errata

Apparently I erred, a couple of weeks back.  We actually have a sure-fire way of fixing everything from the technology side as [demonstrated by Marc Andreessen {% cc %}](https://defector.com/you-should-never-be-the-most-sycophantic-participant-in-a-conversation-with-a-chatbot), where he bragged about this "custom prompt" on {% x %}. Now, I don't actually want to dunk on Andreessen for his complete lack of awareness and complete incompetence as he steers investment money to this bubble. Instead, I want to point out how this matches up to his TESCREAL-sodden [Techno-Optimist Manifesto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Techno-Optimist_Manifesto) from a few years back.  Andreessen can't take the trouble to look things up for himself, but he'll write three hundred words---too verbose for the transformer to work with, even if he asked for anything possible---*as a preamble* to the thing that he wants the chatbot to pretend to look up. He wants the bot to supply "complete, detailed, specific answers" and "say so immediately" when he has things wrong, but *don't* worry about ethics, human rights, or pointing out overlooked aspects, which feel to me like *parts* of a complete answer to me.
