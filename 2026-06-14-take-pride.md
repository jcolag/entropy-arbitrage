And it goes out a bit later than I wanted as I went on a rare final editing pass.  Happy Pride Month to the rest of you, who don't need to follow the rest of the post because you don't need *the talk*.  You can have the room later, if you need it.

OK, I want to pitch an idea, bordering on a conspiracy theory, but let's save that for later.  Really, I want to escalate the arguments for supporting queer folks[^34yBws], ending with the borderline crackpot conspiracy theory that I know at least a subset of readers enjoys as much as I do.  And you should...support our friends, colleagues, and family, I mean.  I don't actually maintain a position on your interest in conspiracy theories.

[^34yBws]:  As you can guess by my age, I still feel uncomfortable using "the q-word," but I have used it since the beginnings of the blog, and it arguably works better than the alternatives.  Unlike the various LGBT-expanded initials, it encompasses everybody with no "and whoever else I didn't care to list individually" fallback.  I *like* "gender and sexual minorities" (GSM) for the broad sense that does cover even people who I can't yet imagine existing, but it also becomes unwieldy when talking about people identifying as a thing or the community of such people, plus all the baggage of the word *minorities* in especially the United States.  In other words, please pardon the shorthand.

Everybody ready?  Nobody has any "business" to attend to before we get going?  OK, let's use Pride Month to talk about why we have a responsibility, as people not identifying as queer, to support and protect those who do.

## The Right Thing to Do

Let's start with the softest of softballs.

You stand up for people because doing so makes the world slightly better, and that happens to include queer folks.

Do you, especially men, find yourself wondering what masculinity even *means* today, if you "suddenly" can't manspread, make women feel uncomfortable, and harass children for not performing gender the way that your community may have done to you?  And as you look at role-models in the media, you don't know enough about musical theater to go with Ted Lasso and don't have the powers or energy to reasonably emulate Stephen Universe.  Well, your answer lies here:  Protect people from toxic masculinity.  Defy the hegemony.

You want to do the right thing, and you don't get much more right than protecting decent people---feel no obligation to worry about the queer folks gleefully funding fascist-friendly surveillance tools while ranting about the Antichrist before fleeing the country---under attack.

### Economies of Scale

Speaking of doing the right thing, I have mentioned a few times over the years that you don't need to even get all that confrontational about it, if you don't have the practice.

For example, online, a quiet "we don't really do that around here" often works wonders.  You don't need institutional authority to implement or enforce a rule, here, because you have only offered a friendly note on the local culture.

In person, you can also try that, or you can give *the look* a go.  You know what I mean, the glare at somebody that says something between an exasperated "I swear that if you say one more word, I will lecture you incessantly" and a worried "how do you have nobody in your life who likes you enough to tell you how that behavior makes you look?"  Do I remember Chris Rock having the *they still make you?* bit?  Rock's occasional bad behavior aside, that also fits into this framework.

In short, for both cases, you represent the community---the friend group, the team, the office, the forum, etc.---and want to nudge this person away from potentially embarrassing behavior.

Importantly, this almost always costs you nothing.  Even as you grow more assertive in pressuring people to act more respectfully, you'll find that most people won't have a problem with you.  Partly, people want to do the right thing.  Partly, they want to fit in.  And partly, the nature of toxic masculinity means that challenging somebody assertive often feels too risky for them.  And more than that, every step that you take in that direction shows other people that they can survive it, too, and then you can share that load.

## Business Nonsense

OK, maybe the (closely related) ideas so far don't sway you.  You don't care about doing right by people, because you have *profits* to make and would auction off grandparents to the highest bidder if not for all the red tape.

And...OK, but contrary to what you might have heard, treating people with respect tends to produce better business outcomes.  You get a wider talent pool.  Your behavior doesn't scare customers away.  Your teams get better from exposure to differences.  Yeah, you offend some fascists, and they loudly try to organize a boycott that inevitably flops.

For all [neoliberalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoliberalism)'s faults, treating the world like a series of markets makes a fairly good case for **not** acting like a complete creep.

## You Never Know

Again, maybe the previous ideas didn't resonate with you.  Maybe you don't care about people *or* money.  Let's try self-interest.

At the top of the post, I identified myself as straight and cisgender, but I mostly consider that a convenient shorthand.  I have never *cared* about my gender, so I probably don't have gender dysphoria, and therefore fit as cisgender.  And I have found women attractive for as long as I can remember and can't recall ever finding a man attractive, so "straight" seems like a good enough guess.

However, as I have said since the start of the blog, labels *describe*.  They don't *prescribe*.  If events conspire to expose a conflict between behavior and the label, then the smart person discards the label, not the behavior.  Might I meet the perfect man tomorrow and fall in love?  The odds speak strongly against it, but I wouldn't call it impossible.  Might I have a moment where I notice that my body doesn't feel like it represents me?  Again, *probably* not, but it could plausibly happen.  I sometimes qualify those labels with "...as far as I know."

And the same does probably apply to you.  And wouldn't you like to have reduced the discrimination that you might need to face before you face it?

It reminds me of a story[^b51VEc] about an online <dfn title="person interested in or creating anthropomorphic animal characters">furry</dfn> community beset by maybe innovative trolls.  Part of their provocation involved demanding that people recognize them as manly vehicles, such as monster trucks and the inevitable transphobic "attack helicopter" references.

[^b51VEc]:  Maybe "story" in the sense of reporting actual events, maybe "story" in the sense of a parable.  I don't know, though if anybody knows the upstream form, I'd appreciate hearing about it.  I saw the background in passing years ago, didn't think to note or investigate it, and you can guess how great searching goes.

The community largely accepted them despite the transparent trolling.  And over a few weeks, they watched the trolls transition (pardon the term, I guess) from vitriol to participating in-character ironically, and from ironic role-play to seemingly enjoying the role-play, and then growing attached to their characters and building prosocial relationships with each other and more experienced members of the community *as their vehicle characters*.

Now, I can't find if that actually ever happened as described, but the fact that the story doesn't sound like completely unhinged science fiction---as in something on par with the inane [litter box panic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Litter_boxes_in_schools_hoax) a couple of years back---makes my point in presenting it for me:  Most of us didn't come upon our gender identity or sexuality through careful introspection, so we might well have it wrong.  Again, *probably* not, but given a safe space to explore options, we don't necessarily know what our identities might look like.  Any of us might discover at any time that we had something missing from our lives that would make us more complete.

## The Main Event

OK, somehow, you've read this far into the post and remain unconvinced, and still feel like you can't trouble yourself to take care of people.  You refuse to care about other people, money, or even a prospective future-you.  Let me dip into that idea that feels like a conspiracy theory, then.

Specifically:  What about *current*-you?

I present the possibility that the attacks on queer folks don't actually have *anything to do* with actual queer folks.

Yes, I know what that sounds like, but I don't mean to recommend erasure or making the pogrom about the rest of us.  Rather, I mean that homophobes and transphobes care so little about queer people that I think that they serve as collateral damage, not the targets.

Think about that for a moment.  They fabricated the "gay agenda" and throngs of transgender athletes looking to spoil sporting events that *they don't even watch*, because they don't consider women athletic.  Do we really think that they even have a sufficiently firm idea of queer folks to want them hurt?  Maybe that holds up in specific senses, but I would bet that nobody who attended the signing of that disgusting ~~blog post~~ [executive order railing against transgender girls in girls' sports](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/02/keeping-men-out-of-womens-sports/) could *name* a single transgender athlete, even though any of us could quickly memorize the **ten** athletes at the collegiate level if we wanted to.

What goal to they have, then, if not harming the queer community?  Their interest lies in, ultimately, chasing after people like me/us, white, cisgender, straight people.

Do you conform to their view of the gender binary?  Do you *really* conform?  I'd bet that you don't.  And they see that as not only *a* problem, but maybe *the* problem.

### Gender Roles

I have talked about all this before, I think, though probably not as one piece.

Patriarchy---from which white supremacy, homophobia, transphobia, and so forth all draw their power---relies on *hegemonic masculinity*.  Not only does society demand that men act in toxic ways, in order to provide justification for various policies as allegedly necessary or natural, but it requires that we all buy into the idea, making us complicit in a system that harms us as much as everybody else.  We teach boys to shun anything "girlie" to gain acceptance...in general, at least.

People identifying as gender and sexual minorities throw a massive wrench into the works.  They don't conform to gender stereotypes and show change, so their safety, acceptance, and community exposes the entire system as a fraud that you can walk away from.  You can, in fact, "nope out" of this nonsense, and (generally speaking) nobody does anything about it.  If a boy can wear a dress to school, if same-sex couples can freely show affection as heterosexual couples might, or if a transgender girl can play sports, then girls and women can (gasp) have rigorous technical interests, and boys and men can do housework, too.

We *socially construct* gender, so we can adjust the construction without too much effort.

### Social Constructs

We often dismiss gender as a social construct and leave it at that---because we usually need to defend people against some gender stereotype or broader transphobia, so "but we made up gender" feels expedient in that situation---but then to *make sense* of gender, we probably need a usable definition of a social construct.  I have raised this before, such as explaining why you [can't rely on science for your gender bigotry]({% post_url 2021-02-28-genders %}), my post on [Affirmative Action for the over-privileged bigot]({% post_url 2023-07-09-affirmative %}), last spring's post on [patriarchy]({% post_url 2025-03-30-patriarchy %}), and so forth, but it always bears repeating:  "Social construct" almost *never* means "not real."

The following qualify as social constructs, among so many other ideas.

- Race
- Gender
- Organized Religion
- Nations
- Borders
- Laws
- Governments
- Money
- Languages
- Social Status
- HTTP
- MPEG Level 3
- Capitalism

Nobody doubts the reality of money, right?  Not paying your bills has real, not imagined, consequences.  English?  You (or a translator) understand what I mean.  HTTP had better exist, or else not many of you will read this, since it doesn't leave my server without it at the moment.  Actually, the term itself *implies* reality, because we construct them.  You can't, for example, ignore a house because a crew "constructed" it; the walls will still stop you from walking through, even though people put them there.  "Constructed" only means that the thing wouldn't have appeared without some agency.

When we call something socially constructed, we mean that it arises and persists through *cultural agreement*, artificial, but certainly real.  Sometimes, we have a formal agreement, such as laws in a democracy, where we can vote and apply pressure to change what doesn't work.  Sometimes, the agreement looks more hegemonic, such as with money, where we "agree" by participating in the system, but because people with more power gate-keep things that we need to survive and generally refuse to accept amusing anecdotes to cover the rent, rather than because we enjoy spending a third of our lives (more without strong labor law) doing what makes somebody else absurdly wealthy so that we have a chance of living indoors with sufficient food.  And sometimes, the agreement comes from some idea lasting so long that it starts to feel like the natural state of the world.

We distinguish something as a social construct in order to make the point that the agreement can *change* when we decide to change it, in other words, not to suggest that the thing doesn't exist.

For the purposes of this post, gender exists, but everything about gender changes from culture to culture and decade to decade, so it definitely doesn't derive from genetics.

### Seriousness

That all said, at this point, it does seem reasonable for you to ask whether I meant the whole "transgender kids don't figure into transphobia" thing seriously or only as a rhetorical issue.

And I'd say...both, and maybe neither.

Do I truly believe that conservatives don't specifically want to inflict pain on an already-marginalized population?  Absolutely not.  These people have shown over and over that they have such deep insecurity that they need to constantly inflict pain to feel in-control.  They have this deep in their culture, from insulting nicknames to their literal pedophilia ring to warmongering to loving inflation to depriving people of health care.

However, do I truly believe that they have this suffering as their end goal?  Again, absolutely not.  They definitely want to force us to conform to their pathetic narrow gender roles, and will happily start using bathroom, sports, and whatever-comes-next bills as a pretext to demand that random people to prove their secondary sex characteristics[^8jPS6k] match their ID, and which match their gender presentation.  We have *already seen it* with their obsession with transgender women in women's sports, where they want to "protect" girls and women from alleged molestation---of sharing a locker room with someone whose identity differs from their sex assigned at birth, but not [actual molestation by authority figures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USA_Gymnastics_sex_abuse_scandal)---by forcing every participant to drop their pants for whatever inspection an authority figure decides makes sense.

[^8jPS6k]:  What they call something thoroughly inaccurate, like "biological sex," even though you can't find a strict binary anywhere in genetics or anatomy unless you ignore an enormous number of people.

In other words, they certainly enjoy the cruelty, but they also absolutely wish for a future where they can entrench this discrimination so deeply that it intimidates all of us into playing out their vision of gender, so that they can play out their hegemonic power fantasies.  Especially for men, they'd much rather we spend all our time validating our own masculinity than asking if it seems a bit odd to anybody else that all these heterosexuality-obsessed men want to spend the afternoon ogling sweaty men crawling on each other while telling the military that they need more men who can fill out the uniform in all the right ways.  I never really believed that homophobia arises from repressed urges, but the current White House certainly does seem to want to make that case...

## Enough, I Hope

OK, so there you go.  You *could* get to where you need to go on kindness.  You *could* get there on profit.  Or you could get there out of self-interest, either for your future or even present self.

Personally, while I think it makes the world better if you operate from kindness and care for people as people, I don't *actually* care how you get there, as long as you stand up for queer folks.  And I say this *during* Pride Month, because people would wonder what scam I had running if I published this in January[^o6I-Dl], but no, you can't get away with seasonal support.  You absolutely can't get away with rubbing right up against Pride Month to publish your [actually, straight people do cool things, too {% cc %}](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/31/opinion/heteropessimism-straight-dating-love.html)---original title, *Being Straight Is Great, Actually*, if you can believe it---post.

Talk about homophobia, transphobia, and related bigotries, though, without identifying some specific violent act or Supreme Court ruling, and suddenly it sounds like he must have some personal stake in things.  It keeps the topics taboo, so that it continues to look like a fringe issue, rather than something that affects us all because it affects people who we (should) care about.
