## Emissary

Picking up where we left off, at the first relevant point after the Cardassians leave Quark's bar.  It'll probably go faster than the first half, because I never get these splits right.

Oh, speaking of this going faster, if you do find this post a bit thin, I should note that my [February newsletter](https://buymeacoffee.com/jcolag/entropy-arbitrage-newsletter-february-2026) went out earlier this week, which I mention here because it includes a follow-up to my comments in the [*These Are the Voyages...*]({% post_url 2026-01-29-these-are-voyages %}) about the unfortunate consistency of the franchise, "pitching" seven hypothetical shows that a *Star Trek*-like media empire could try that would expand the audience beyond "military pretending to explore."  And if you want more, in my *Project Previews*, I also outline a (mostly abandoned, but still on the web) project that I wanted to position as an "open source *Star Trek*," into which those proposed shows could also plausibly fit.  And I assume that some non-trivial number of readers here might enjoy either of those.

 > **CARDASSIAN**: Leave it to Starfleet to ruin a fine day.

Starfleet still has its wonderful reputation, I see...

And we have some *extreme 1990s* morphing technology on display.  I forget if it had become embarrassing by this point...

 > **KIRA**: What the hell is happening out there?
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: I don't know. They're just gone.

Check out the look that Kira gives him.  It seems fairly clear that she asked a rhetorical question, and it seems like he answered to hear the sound of his own voice.  Given his comment about working with "Bajoran women" in [the first half]({% post_url 2026-02-26-emissary-1 %}), the look makes me wonder if she has seen him do *anything* useful so far.

 > **SISKO**: This is what my planet looks like. You and I are very different species. It will take time for us to understand one another.

Wait, no demands?  No threats?  I feel like I started watching the wrong show.

No, really, try to imagine this scene playing out with Picard for a moment.  The omnipotent creatures separate him from his partner, accuse him of trying to do them harm, and start asking him intrusive questions about his emotional trauma.  Can we imagine the captain---politely and with fascination---talking about the work involved in building relationships?  I can't, if only because this feels like a checklist of everything that Picard hates:  Non-humans with power over him, suggestions that he restrict his behavior, and a need to deal with his emotions like an adult.

Honestly, compare this directly to Picard's behavior in [*Encounter at Farpoint*]({% post_url 2022-05-19-farpoint1 %}), since the plots have such clear parallels of omnipotent entities demanding that the leader justify his people's history to allow further travel.  Picard took a defensive, belligerent stance immediately, treating the trial as an absurd imposition.  Sisko, though...well, I'll talk a bit more about his approach as it unrolls, here.

 > **O'BRIEN**: The *Enterprise* is still the nearest starship. They could reach us in two days.

Not really relevant, but since we have a fairly thin post on this half of things, I could understand never having closer ships than the *Enterprise* on the show centering on the ship, but this raises questions about the Federation's and Starfleet's density, as well as what purpose Starfleet actually serves.  And we might as well look at the numbers...to the extent that I can work them out.

Near the midpoint of [**First Contact**]({% post_url 2025-12-18-first-contact-1 %}), Picard tells (will tell) Sloane that the Federation includes a hundred fifty planets across eight thousand light years.  Assuming that we don't have many inhabited planets among older stars, then we can probably limit the space to the [thin disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thin_disk), with a thickness of around a thousand light years.  Figure that, if Picard cited a diameter, then that gives us a rough disc-shaped volume of fifty billion cubic light years[^oGxS4a] and an average of one planet for every third of a billion cubic light years.

[^oGxS4a]:  In theory, Picard could plausibly have meant eight thousand *cubic* light years, but an average of twenty light years' radius makes the more distant real-world stars implausible.  Or you could have a non-contiguous Federation, but then it ends up covering something closer to Picard's eight thousand light year diameter.  He could also mean the square light years across the width of the thin disc, I guess, which would give it a radius of around fifty light years, but that still doesn't account for the named stars.  And yes, I know that the writers mostly picked star names out of a hat without any regard for the galaxy's map.

[Stellar density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_density) (at least in our neighborhood) clocks in at somewhere around one star for every two hundred fifty cubic light years, so the Federation seems extremely sparse, especially since we now believe that [planet-hosting stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet-hosting_star) show up so often that planets probably outnumber stars, and about one in five Sun-like stars have Earth-like planets, though writers at the time would never have guessed numbers like that, since it took the Hubble Space Telescope for the search for exoplanets to expand from a list that people could memorize to thousands.  Add the technology to modify or create an ecosystem, as we saw in [*Home Soil*]({% post_url 2022-09-15-home-soil %}), and the ratio looks especially sparse, unless the majority of colony worlds don't actually become part of the Federation---Picard's count of a hundred fifty planets---for whatever reason.

I could only find tertiary information on Starfleet's size for [*Thine Own Self*]({% post_url 2025-09-11-thine-own-self %}), but I gathered there that the fleet might consist of a few to tens of thousands of ships.  Averaging those sizes to ten thousand ships for a rough estimate, that gives us one ship for every five million cubic light years.  That certainly tracks with only ever having one nearby ship except during specific crises, but makes it seem impossible that anything would ever get done, given how many logistical activities have relied on the *Enterprise*'s presence on top of their military and scientific work.  And it really calls into question Picard's comment about how much he misses exploration work in [**Insurrection**]({% post_url 2026-01-01-insurrection-1 %}), because who has the time[^p8uJZH] to cross the border into new regions?

[^p8uJZH]:  And speaking of time, that doesn't even consider---because I don't feel like hunting down their "official" warp factor formula---whether the *Enterprise* even has a chance of getting between Earth and the border in a reasonable amount of time.

 > **ODO**: Major, I was found in the Denorios Belt. I don't know where I came from, no idea if there are any others like me. All my life, I've been forced to pass myself off as one of you, always wondering who I really am. Well, the answers to a lot of my questions may be somewhere on the other side of that wormhole. You coming?

While not directly relevant to the Federation---and maintaining that distinction will probably become difficult over the next few years as we go through this series as the crew unifies---it seems worth comparing this line to the many times through **The Next Generation** where Data broached this sort of topic, only to dance around the fears, resentment, and wishes for connection, reinforcing the idea that Data didn't feel like he had the liberty to speak his mind.

 > **ALIEN-PICARD**: The creature must be destroyed before it destroys us.

The creatures sure do have Picard's patter down.

And no, I *won't* nitpick how the creatures can understand an imminent threat without understanding the past and future.

 > **O'BRIEN**: Damn it, I didn't ask for an opinion. Just tell me whether or not we can get enough thrust with only a partial field established.

Say what you will about the franchise, but it absolutely *nailed* the tone of every LLM product decades before anybody created one.  Rather than doing the job as requested, it will instead write you a five-paragraph content-marketing essay about the topic, I guess.  If it starts dividing the world into tapestries and ghosts, explaining that "it's not X --- it's *Y*," and so forth, we'll know...

 > **DUKAT**: Really? And I suppose you'd also tell me these are not the lifeforms that have sent the Orbs, or that your Commander Sisko is not negotiating for their technology. I thank you for your concern, but I think we'll see for ourselves.

Again, we see the Federation's reputation.  And this time seems more interesting than what we generally see, painting the government as desperate for new technology, as opposed to the usual accusations of warmongering or angling for political advantage.

 > **SISKO**: No, this is the truth. This day, this park, it was almost fifteen years ago. Far in the past. It was a day that was very important to me, a day that shaped every day that followed. That is the essence of a linear existence. Each day affects the next.
 
No, hearing them call fifteen years ago---incidentally, less than half the time between when this episode aired and today---"far in the past" doesn't make me feel old at all, and I don't know why you'd ask...

 > **PAST-JENNIFER**: I've heard Starfleet officers don't want families because they complicate their lives.

I started responding to this by suggesting another reputation issue, because Jennifer describes something more akin to [Peter Pan syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Pan_syndrome), rather than the dangers to everybody on a ship or similar.  But as I thought about it, except for Crusher and Sisko[^fK2lwj], we have only rarely seen successful parents in Starfleet, but we have seen plenty of Kirk's "second star to the left, and straight on 'til morning" energy from [**The Undiscovered Country**]({% post_url 2022-04-21-tuc %}), and I have noted multiple times how much the social dynamics on the *Enterprise*-D feel like an affluent junior high school.  Maybe, then, Starfleet does primarily hire the perpetually immature...

[^fK2lwj]:  No, I don't count O'Brien.  First, we haven't seen him as a father, yet.  But also, he won't exactly have the best parenting track record.

 > **SISKO**: That's not true. We're aware that every choice we make has a consequence.

 > **ALIEN-JAKE**: Then how can you take responsibility for your actions?

 > **SISKO**: We use past experience to help guide us. For Jennifer and me, all the experiences in our lives prepared us for the day we met on the beach, helped us recognize that we had a future together. When we married, we accepted all the consequences of that act, whatever they might be, including the consequences of you.

Revisiting the different "strategy" as I hinted earlier, I have to assume that other people have noticed this before me, but Sisko increasingly acts like a mentor or (idealized) parental figure.  He hasn't gotten defensive or taken this personally, even though reliving his memories *makes* it personal, and teaches a lesson on taking responsibility for our lives.

You can see this especially in the baseball discussion, which cleverly subverts both the insistence by sports fans that non-fans only need the rules explained to them more clearly *and* the trope of extraterrestrials bonding with humans over our culture, to build (the start of) an example of responsibility in an uncertain world, "set a strategy for all the possibilities as best you can."

 > **SISKO**: That may be the most important thing to understand about humans. It is the unknown that defines our existence. We are constantly searching, not just for answers to our questions, but for new questions. We are explorers. We explore our lives, day by day, and we explore the galaxy, trying to expand the boundaries of our knowledge. And that is why I am here. Not to conquer you either with weapons or with ideas, but to co-exist and learn.

I don't know if I have anything to say about this bit, but apart from one of the better inspirational speeches that actually carries any content to it, it occurs to me that it *actually* works in the episode because Sisko almost certainly didn't believe any of it an hour ago.

 > **O'BRIEN**: What shields?

Did he not brief her?  Maybe he wouldn't need to have such strong opinions about working with Bajoran women if he gave them the information that they needed.

 > **O'BRIEN**: Doctor, you ever studied your military history of the border wars?
 >
 > **BASHIR**: Yes.

 > **O'BRIEN**: You ever heard of the Setlik Three massacre?

Didn't that war happen something like a year ago?  Two years, sorry.  [*The Wounded*]({% post_url 2024-01-25-wounded %}), almost a year before this episode, starts with an assertion that they signed a peace treaty "nearly a year" before that.  I raise the question because that doesn't seem like something that would already show up in military history.  I get the sense that you can only really get propaganda for quite a while, since participants from all factions still have reputations to protect and everybody remembers their emotional reactions.

 > **O'BRIEN**: You know what they do to their prisoners, sir.

We've heard that sort of opinion vented about at least---I might miss some---Klingons and Ferengi, and probably the Romulans.  And yes, TV viewers would have seen Picard's torture by the Cardassians only about two weeks prior, so the line wants to gesture at that.  But still, the fact that *every* major power allegedly does horrible things to prisoners reeks of either propaganda to justify violence or "every accusation a confession."

 >
 > **ALIEN-JENNIFER**: You exist here.
 >
 > **SISKO**: I exist here. I don't know if you can understand. I see her like this every time I close my eyes. In the darkness, in the blink of an eye, I see her like this.
 >

 > **ALIEN-JENNIFER**: So you choose to exist here. It is not linear.

I admittedly don't understand much about trauma, but this sounds like almost every description that I've heard of people trying to cope.  And I have seen multiple studies over the years indicating that we can treat post-traumatic stress by revisiting traumatic events and---carefully; don't try this at home, as they say, don't imagine that I have a medical degree, a license to practice medicine, or enough knowledge about your situation to give you anything even approaching advice---retelling the story in ways that allow the patient to build up broader and less consistent memories of the incident, which dovetails with that description.

Well done, in other words.  {% emoji applause %}

That said, we again have a massive condemnation of the Federation's psychological health system, if a few nosy questions during an alien abduction got him on the path to wellness after about two and a half years of suffering.  Did *nobody* in the fleet bother to ask him about his feelings in all that time?

 > **O'BRIEN**: Major. Remind me never to get into a game of Roladan Wild Draw with you.

I can't help but think that they intended the awkward tone, here.  Normally, when any production pulls out a line like that, it feels conciliatory, or at least respectful.

In this case, though, maybe shaded by his other comments in this episode, it feels like he mostly wants to feel relevant.  I mean, for starters, what about their working relationship so far suggests to him that she'd like to sit down with him to socialize in the near future.

And maybe that brings this into focus:  He finds her intimidating, and doesn't want to show it, because he also sees her as inferior, a "Bajoran woman," an undifferentiated type.

 > **BASHIR**: Yes!
 >
 > **KIRA**: Too soon for a victory celebration, Doctor. Mister O'Brien?

We have already probably said too much about Bashir's relative disinterest in people who he claims to want to serve, but this bit seems telling, Starfleet's alleged best-and-brightest treating this emergency like a theme park ride or action film to excite him, rather than seeing a sign that a bunch of people probably already need his help.

In fact...

 > **ODO**: Look, Doctor, maybe I should find you someone...
 >
 > **BASHIR**: Hold it there.

I have to give him credit for *something*, here, so demanding that Odo get past his insecurities to save lives does start to justify his inclusion.

 > **SISKO**: Casualties?
 >
 > **BASHIR**: Thirteen injured, Commander, and no fatalities.

Again, notice that he dives in looking for consequences.

 > **PICARD**: Well, you've put Bajor on the map, Commander. This will shortly become a leading center of commerce and scientific exploration. And for Starfleet, one of our most important posts.
 >
 > ...
 >
 > **PICARD**: I'm not sure that I can. Are you certain that's what you want, because we cannot afford to have an officer who's---

Oh, shut up...

That critical sentiment voiced, this scene feels like a serious let-down.  After all the apparent rejections of "business as usual" for the franchise, we watch Sisko endure a lecture on the new importance of this post---notably erasing the Bajorans from the list of beneficiaries, I might note---with the patronizing implication that Sisko doesn't deserve the "exile" if it might have benefited his career.  And in response, Sisko more or less begs for the job that he already has, suddenly treating Picard with some deep reverence.  No "I learned to forgive you for your participation in an assault on the Federation, and can only hope that you can learn to forgive yourself" or pointing out that he made a handshake deal with an omnipotent power, and all that commerce, science, and militarism probably falls apart if he leaves.

Speaking of that last bit, by the way, other than racism, I can't think of any possible in-universe reasons for the Federation not see Sisko as one of their examples of role models who changed the galaxy, based on his performance here.  Like, when they do the "Einstein...Surak...Cochrane" thing, the guy who personally made it possible to cross the galaxy in a few minutes has to sit fairly close to the top of that list, no?  Heck, given that (as I pointed out last time) he did all this while chasing a two-hundred-year-old legend, you'd expect Picard to eat this up and beg for Sisko's autograph, not apologize for failing to get him fired in a timely fashion.

 > **BASHIR**: So, where can someone practice with his phaser around here?

And...right back to not caring about people.  It seems relevant that they put this scene close to Picard's final lecture on acting like a proper officer of his Starfleet.  Picard, Bashir, and O'Brien fit together in their way, and the show seems to emphasize that.

## Conclusions

As I probably should have guessed, they did front-load the information that we care about into the extended episode, but I wouldn't call it useless.

### The Good

Sisko continues to show that the typical Starfleet officer who we've seen throughout **The Next Generation** doesn't represent all of Starfleet and the Federation.  Some do lead with care, empathy, and responsibility for consequences.

Despite problems, we also do see the potential for the mainstream of Starfleet to overcome their self-absorption...if only briefly.

### The Bad

The Federation continues to have a terrible reputation, both at home and abroad, though these criticisms seem more grounded.  And we see at least some in Starfleet living down to those expectations, treating their non-Starfleet colleagues like tokens on a board only grudgingly worthy of anything like respect, and treating combat as an entertaining spectacle.

We get an indirect reminder of how stifled certain classes of people feel in the Federation, showing a more open alternative beyond their borders.

Health care seems content to ignore people in emotional pain who wouldn't need much treatment.

Starfleet appears to claim all benefits from newly discovered phenomena, ignoring the needs of the locals.

### The Weird

The Federation seems to cycle recent actions into their military histories.
