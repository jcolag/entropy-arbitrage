## Previously...

## Q-less

For context, this episode sits between [*Aquiel*]({% post_url 2025-02-13-aquiel %}) and [*Face of the Enemy*]({% post_url 2025-02-20-face-enemy %}).  And as we'll learn soon enough, the episode also serves as a sequel to [*Qpid*]({% post_url 2024-03-21-qpid %}), continuing the stories of both guest stars from there.

 > **BASHIR**: Starfleet medical finals. Gets them every time.

I have to admit, I don't know what to make of this scene.  It seems important to this project *somehow*---the posturing, the sad attempt at flirting that somehow seems to work, and so forth---but I can't really identify anything useful in it beyond it continuing to show Bashir as self-absorbed, but also a funny performance.

 > **SISKO**: Chief, we have to get this hatch open. Lieutenant Dax and Ensign Pauley are trapped in there.
 >
 > **KIRA**: The runabout barely made it back through the wormhole. By the time they docked, power levels were near zero.

Please, engineers, never design doors (for living things) that require power to open, something that we also saw in [*New Ground*]({% post_url 2024-07-18-new-ground %}) before you try to hypothesize about damage.  You can make them difficult to open and have the powered parts of it assist, but needing power at all means trapping people.

 > **O'BRIEN**: Vash?
 >
 > **VASH**: That's right?
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: Miles O'Brien, from the *Enterprise*.

Why the heck does O'Brien recognize her?  I suppose that she (probably) used the transporter...once(?), but so do a lot of people.  *We* recognize her because we watch every scene from both her prior appearances, where she brightened up stories that could have flopped, but it doesn't make much sense for O'Brien to have that perspective.

Worse, he expects her to recognize him, as if they spent hours exchanging stories over coffee, and not that she spent a few hours on the ship two years ago (as they'll remind us later) intent on embarrassing Picard, nowhere near O'Brien...

In other words, this reads like a cashier from a Walmart in some town that you can't remember while on the road a while back rushing to your hospital bed and asking how that deodorant worked for you.  I mean, do we need to invoke the specter of [*Galaxy's Child*]({% post_url 2024-02-22-galaxy-s-child %}), here?

 > **VASH**: I'd hardly call the Gamma Quadrant uncivilized. Some of the cultures I've encountered have histories that date back millions of years.
 >
 > **BASHIR**: Really? I'd love to hear about them. I mean, we have no idea what's beyond the wormhole.

Agreed.  I would love to hear more, too, especially with what I mentioned last week about [the quasi-religious political oppression]({% post_url 2026-04-02-captive-pursuit %}#quick-commentary) that we'll see over the next few years.  Do the cultures with millions of years of history happen to overlap at all with the cultures claimed to have recent artificial origins?

Also, what do they mean by "civilized," in this context?  I ask, because I don't think that I have ever seen an explicit or implied definition that included historical persistence.  Most definitions seem to range from language and non-familial community[^InKb7O] to arts and sciences.  For example, archaeologists today probably wouldn't consider nomadic clans foraging for food civilized, no matter how long they've done it or even if they had ancestors who lived in cities.

[^InKb7O]:  By that, I mean something like the typical ancient cities.  If a bunch of people with familial ties settles a large area, that comes off more like a dwelling for an extended family.  If a bunch of unrelated people travel together, that looks more like a caravan.  Put together, though, unrelated people in a fixed settlement, you now need a common language and system of governance.

That aside, it does show that they think of civilization as inherently local, unable to imagine the idea of sophisticated people so far away.

 > **VASH**: So now the Daystrom Institute is interested in me. Well, that's ironic.
 >
 > **SISKO**: Professor Woo seemed especially eager to speak to you again.
 >
 > **VASH**: Did he really? I suppose he told you that he suspended my membership from the Institute's Archaeological Council?
 >
 > **SISKO**: On two occasions. Something about the sale of illegal artifacts.

Notice the less-than-subtle condemnation of elite education, here, that their ethical standards mysteriously dissolve when they can get benefits out of the deal.  Sure, she broke a bunch of laws and smashed through ethical standards, but you know, maybe she could sell *us* some artifacts before the government acknowledges it as smuggling.

Oh, also, I've surely mentioned it at one other times that they've mentioned the outfit, but one assumes that they named the Daystrom Institute after the scientist responsible for hundreds of deaths by setting his experimental AI loose in [*The Ultimate Computer*]({% post_url 2021-01-21-ultimate %}), so maybe the ethical lapse shouldn't surprise us.  Might as well attend [Union Carbide India Limited Technical College](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bhopal_disaster).

Plus, I guess that we've seen that they all support a vision of archaeology where privileged people pillage heritage sites to stock elite museums.

 > **VASH**: Well, when it comes to choosing between science and profit, I'll choose profit every time.

Don't they still insist that people don't care about money, though, despite constant evidence to the contrary...?

 > **VASH**: You know, I haven't been back to Earth in, it must be twelve years.

Does the Daystrom Institute sit on Earth?  I don't know why I assumed otherwise, but it seems like a "put it somewhere else to make it exotic" kind of thing, I guess.

 > **O'BRIEN**: Well Sir, Vash and Captain Picard were friends. Close friends, if you follow my meaning. Seems they met on Risa a few years back. I think she must be a special woman, being friends with the Captain and all.

Look, if you *must* gossip, you tell the damned Robin Hood story, not this "say no more" garbage using her to titillate.

 > **SISKO**: Somehow she doesn't seem to be his type.

I would've gone the other way with the comparison, myself.  In my experience, most people make an exception in their "type" for pretty, funny, and talented.  But "grumpy fascist preaching from his ivory tower" generally takes a harder sell.

 > **O'BRIEN**: The Captain likes a good challenge, sir.

Does he, though...?  I seem to recall that he whined about needing to interact with her.

However, you'll notice what the two lines suggest.  When people gossip about men "liking a challenge," they mean finding a way to *control* an independent woman.  And I point this out especially because that fits a bit too neatly with Picard's obsession with horseback riding, where the trainer starts by "breaking" the horse.  Tellingly, nobody ever means the phrase as the man liking people in their lives who *challenge them*, an actual healthy and non-creepy thing.

Sisko also, by finding this surprising, strongly suggests that Picard comes off as the sort of person who chases docile women who fall well within conventional Western beauty standards.

 > **O'BRIEN**: You've obviously never slept on a Cardassian mattress. If you need anything, just ask the computer.

They haven't replaced the *mattresses*?  That sounds disgusting even if the previous tenants didn't oppress the natives or trash the place before leaving.

 > **Q**: Really, Vash, I can't believe you're still pining for Jean-Luc, that self-righteous do-gooder.

Takes one to know one.  The pining for Jean-Luc part, I mean, not the self-righteous do-gooder thing.  Even then, I'd call both of them self-righteous, certainly, but only one of them manipulates events to help people...

 > **Q**: How perfectly vile. If that's the kind of company you kept before meeting me, it's no wonder you ended up with Picard.

At least somebody finally made the right comparison.

 > **BASHIR**: So I thought I'd contribute to your good health by buying you dinner. Quark's makes a delicious couscous, and the company, I hope, might be mildly entertaining.

It seems worth pointing out, especially with how much I'll need to complain about Bashir for a while, that he *does* know how to conduct a reasonable flirtation.  She seemed interested in him, for a protracted period of time when she didn't have an obligation, and so he asked her out.  That makes all the other incidents *far* worse, mind you.

 > **SISKO**: A powerful and extremely unpredictable entity. I was at a Starfleet briefing on him two years ago.

What does that meeting even look like?  "OK, so we've known about this guy for a few years, kind of a weirdo, does a lot of god-like things.  You know the Borg?  He might have pointed them in our direction, that sort of thing.  And he likes dressing up in our uniforms.  Did Picard tell us anything more than that, Madge?  OK, then, let's eat."

Seriously, you'd think that they'd issue immediate and public advisories on the omnipotent flake claiming the right to litigate human history, not hold quiet briefings a few years later.

Oh.

Wait.  A little over two years ago, from their perspective, means [*The Best of Both Worlds*]({% post_url 2023-11-09-best-both-worlds-part-2 %}).  Starfleet may well have conducted that briefing to plan the stand at Wolf 359.  But...actually, more on this in a bit.

 > **O'BRIEN**: I believe they actually met in Sherwood Forest. It was one of the little jokes Q played on the *Enterprise* crew.

Again, you *lead* with that story, not the juvenile "tee-hee, maybe Picard had sex, but I couldn't say."

 > **Q**: I'll tell you anything you want to know, Commander. Just answer one question. Is Starfleet penalizing you or did you actually request such a dismal command.

Not really important, but this line feels like it seriously undermines Q's big omnipotent-and-omniscient claims, trying to goad Sisko by bringing up an issue that he resolved.

Well, it would undermine the claims *unless*...well, back in a bit on this point.

 > **Q**: Or what? You'll thrash me? Shall we settle this mano a mano?
 >
 > **SISKO**: What?

Best delivery ever?  And it continues with Sisko's eye-roll after knocking Q down.

 > **SISKO**: I'm not Picard.
 >
 > **Q**: Indeed not. You're much easier to provoke. How fortunate for me.

What about "punch him in the face repeatedly until he retaliates" sounds easier to provoke than Picard, exactly?  I don't remember anybody punching Picard in the face.  That would've made the series much more appealing, so I'll go back and watch it if I missed an episode...

OK, those bits that I said that I'd return to?  It has nothing to do with this post or the episode-as-written, but if Sisko's briefing involved Wolf 359, then this episode would make much more sense as Sisko still having trouble dealing with his wife's death.  The snipe about getting exiled out here would then read as a provocation to get Sisko to lash out at the other person---more or less a friend of Picard's, no less---with direct responsibility for the deaths of his wife and colleagues on the *Saratoga*.  And that would make Sisko seem easier to provoke.

If I squint, I can see that plot as the skeleton of the episode, and then scribbled out as they realized that they could bring Vash back and needed more attention on the supporting cast.

 > **SISKO**: Decompression protocol. Mister O'Brien, raise the confinement shields.

I commend O'Brien's patience, here, carefully waiting for Sisko's command before saving all their lives.  And by commending his patience, I mean I wonder what he has wrong with him beyond ineptness that he didn't reflexively do the thing that the *decompression protocol*---something that he almost certainly wrote or approved---says to do in exactly this situation.

 > **O'BRIEN**: Tritium? Isn't that pretty toxic.
 >
 > **DAX**: Only when it's highly concentrated. We'd only use a very small amount.

This---and the follow-up later with O'Brien complaining about maybe a handful of people dying in a few years as they crank up that concentration, as weighed against their impending destruction in minutes---feels bizarre.  They plan to mildly endanger people, because something worse might eventually happen.  They apparently can't tell people about the danger.  But when death seems certain, they take time to have a reasoned debate about the potential long-term effects.

Also, unless they created a new substance that happens to sound exactly like [tritium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritium)---a heavy, and so radioactive, hydrogen---doesn't have much toxicity in the air, only emitting the occasional electron (beta particle), usually at relatively low speeds that can't penetrate skin.  If you drink a lot of heavy water, that can get worse, but even then, not much, both because the beta rays still need to get up enough energy to penetrate a membrane and because water doesn't stay in the body for a long time.

 > **Q**: Earth. Oh, don't get me wrong. A thousand years ago it had character. [Crusades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crusades), [Spanish Inquisition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_Inquisition), [Watergate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watergate_scandal). But now it's just mind-numbingly dull.

Invite him over, I guess, because we have the [Crusades](https://theconversation.com/what-is-crec-and-how-does-it-shape-pete-hegseths-religious-rhetoric-279637), the [Spanish Inquisition](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/04/this-easter-president-trump-reaffirms-america-as-a-beacon-for-christian-liberty/), and [Watergate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_prosecution_of_Donald_Trump_(election_obstruction_case)) all happening on top of each other at the moment---oh, plus the [oil crisis](https://thehill.com/policy/international/5822104-iran-strait-of-hormuz-closure-ceasefire/)---and I suppose that at least *somebody* should enjoy it.  "May you live in interesting times" indeed...

## Conclusions

We don't get too much out of this, but still some.

### The Good

People actually do understand how to conduct respectful romantic overtures.

### The Bad

We see the return of doors seemingly designed to trap people in an emergency.

O'Brien carries on the idea that women face a strong expectation of friendliness and affection, even for men who they've barely met.  Their lives also provide fodder for tawdry gossip.  We also see suggestions that most men don't value independent companions.

Elite universities ignore major ethical lapses when they have something to gain.

O'Brien continues to foul up his job, nearly killing people.  And they don't seem to have any reasonable understanding of risk to people.

### The Weird

Civilization has some oddball definition.

Starfleet apparently doesn't dispose of and replace old mattresses immediately on taking possession of a facility.
