## Duet

For broader context, this episode landed between [*Timescape*]({% post_url 2025-05-08-timescape %}), featuring the crew's poor treatment of pets and foreigners, and closing out their season with the first part of [*Descent*]({% post_url 2025-05-15-descent-1 %}), bringing back the Borg, who (arguably) kicked off this series.

For the sake of getting the pathetic excuse for a joke out of my head, I gather that this episode has nothing to do with the [identically titled Fox sitcom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duet_%28TV_series%29) probably best known for providing the first acting role to Ellen DeGeneres, distantly followed by the theme song with singers scatting in lieu of lyrics, and I've had the latter stuck in my head for weeks as I saw the post title dangling there in my editor.  Now, having admitted that we don't actually have any joke to mine from there beyond two different shows having the same title, I can move on with my life.

 > **KIRA**: We never cared what we did, as long as it annoyed the grown-ups. All inner deflector shield sub-systems are showing equal intensity.
 >
 > **DAX**: Power balance levels are reading normal. I was a champion window breaker. On a dark night with a few rocks, I was deadly.

Please make it the worm...

 > **BASHIR**: I'm not aware of it. I'll check the computer.

Wh...Why even bring this up?  I mean, points for not *pretending* to know about it, I guess, but I have to imagine that everybody would assume that the doctor would look up a patient's condition before treating him.  In the future, I mean.  Plenty of doctors today probably guess, especially with the pervasiveness of medical racism.

 > **MARRITZA**: Problem?

You might recognize the war criminal (of whatever importance) as [Harris Yulin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harris_Yulin), who appeared a *lot* from 1970 to his death a bit more than a year ago.

 > **SISKO**: So that makes him a war criminal, just being there?

I mean...yeah, right?  If you work for the occupying government and spent time at a labor camp, then you didn't do so because you got lost on the way to pick up self-stealing stem-bolts to fix the wobbly dining room table.  You had war crime-y stuff written in your day planner.

 > **KAVAL**: And to you, Commander. I trust you're in good health. Things are going well on the station?

Less prolific than Yulin, but you still might recognize Kaval as [Ted Sorel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ted_Sorel), probably best known for appearances on **Guiding Light** and **Law & Order**.

 > **SISKO**: If it turns out that he is a criminal, then he'll be yours, and you're welcome to him. Until then, he's just a traveler under suspicion.

I feel like this line needs more to it, like background or explanation.  Like, who decides whether the term "criminal" fits, here?  In our world, that term comes out of conviction for a crime, but even as authoritarian as I see this Federation, it seems like a massive reach to imagine that they routinely try and convict suspects in absentia before trying to arrest them.  Sisko *could* mean that he won't arrest unless he thinks that Bajor can secure a conviction, a reasonable position to take if Sisko believes that a not-guilty verdict might get blamed on him or if he believes that the government might convict anybody no matter the evidence, but that doesn't make sense to us if we don't see him discussing evidence with the prosecutor instead of recapping the plot with this random minister.  I could even imagine the treaty with the Cardassians making this worrisome, but then that needs to come into the conversation instead of me inventing it.

Without any of those, it gives the line the sense that Sisko won't allow the arrest of a suspected war criminal unless he gets *the vibe* about Marritza, something convincing him on an emotional level to make the arrest.  And yes, a lot of policing absolutely gets done that way---one of the issues that makes racial stereotypes so dangerous---but it looks pretty bad for the whole institution if you say it out loud...

I guess that calls back to our original question about Bashir happily talking about not knowing about a disorder and the association with racism.

 > **MARRITZA**: My compliments on your replicators, Major, But this sem'hal stew could use a little yamok sauce.

I absolutely do not understand how the replicators work on this show.  But then, I've said as much since [*Babel*]({% post_url 2026-03-26-babel %}), I think, and probably brought it up in [*Progress*]({% post_url 2026-06-04-progress %}) when the kids created the Great Yamok Sauce Famine.  Still, I feel compelled to ask why he couldn't order his stew with the sauce...

 > **MARRITZA**: I told you, you'd be disappointed. Actually, I would have preferred to avoid military service altogether, but I had the good fortune to be posted to the records office at Gallitep, and I turned out to be an exemplary file clerk. In fourteen units of service, I never misplaced or lost a record. I received numerous commendations. Gul Darhe'el himself called my computer filing system a masterpiece of meticulous exactitude. Well, there you are. My secret's out. My crimes laid bare. I await execution.

Not relevant to our post, but since we'll do this sort of thing for most of the episode (and quite well, I might add), it does concern me that we don't follow up on this.  Meticulous record-keeping at a forced labor camp means direct participation in war crimes, making them more efficient if nothing else.

The analogy to draw, especially given that this episode will lean heavily into the Cardassians as allegorical Nazis---other episodes will have them borrow heavily from other governments committing ethnic cleansing, but this episode draws a lot directly from the [Nuremberg Trials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuremberg_trials) and its dramatizations---it seems worth gesturing at World War II itself.  While they have never faced consequences for their actions, International Business Machines (IBM) built a lot of the "locator file" infrastructure that enabled and managed the Japanese American internment camps.  And while weakly disputed, evidence has come to light that they did pretty much the same for the Holocaust.

You could argue that nobody at IBM took any specific action to harm a person, or that if they didn't do it, somebody else would have, but that doesn't make the company less complicit.  And since at least 1948, the [Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genocide_Convention) makes complicity a punishable act.

In other words, I don't know what Bajor's relevant treaties and conventions say, but on Earth, he has given Kira exactly what she needed, but they treat it like a boring tangent or a likely lie to cover up some "actual" war crimes.

Oh, and this gets more at the writing, but as I recall, we'll find Kira doing this sort of thing surprisingly often for a couple of years.  As if the character understands the dramatic pacing needs of an episode, she'll repeatedly ignore fairly clear evidence of whatever she needs to investigate until the final act...

 > **DUKAT**: Commander, I was led to believe the Federation guarantees the safe and unrestricted passage of all travelers visiting your station.

We all remember Dukat (and his hilariously one-sided rivalry with Sisko) from [*Emissary*]({% post_url 2026-02-26-emissary-1 %}), I imagine.

 > **DAX**: Even if he is just a file clerk?

Well, I guess that Dax made my tangent about file clerks and IBM relevant, because it sounds like the Federation doesn't see complicity with genocide as particularly interesting, either.

And I guess that tracks.  As I pointed out in [**The Next Generation**'s summary]({% post_url 2026-02-19-ng-summary %}), the *Enterprise*-D crew planned ("will plan," I guess, since some of that still sits in this episode's future) seven genocides, assuming that I didn't miss any (trying to follow through with some), wanted to watch two cultures go extinct, and didn't see any problems when somebody confessed to wiping an entire culture from history.  Starfleet will reprimand them for this exactly once, because they *failed* to exterminate the target culture.  And if they don't have a problem with directly engaging in genocide, then I don't suppose that they'd find the genocide's support staff troubling at all either.

 > **NEELA**: Aye, sir.

Last of the guests that I can find anything useful on, you might recognize Neela as [Robin Christopher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robin_Christopher), whose primary body of work came from---more daytime dramas---**All My Children** before this episode, before moving her character after this to **One Life to Live** and **General Hospital**.

She'll come back next time, but don't get too attached...

 > **ODO**: He says his name is Gul Darhe'el.

Extremely not relevant to us, but they use Gul as a *title*, an honorific, right, kind of like Starfleet's Captain?  Or does Cardassia choose its commanders from people whose parents named them Gul?  I'd say that I don't care which, but I admit that the latter would have much more in-universe storytelling potential.

No, I don't mean the equivalent of Starfleet hiring Lou Tenant.  Well, OK, *maybe* I mean that, too, because we all know that I absolutely have that sense of humor.  But I really mean that, in the United States after the Civil War (and for longer than we probably want to think about), many white people made a point to only call Black people by their given names (or ethnic slurs), in a culture where they addressed everyone else as Mr., Miss, or Mrs. So-and-So, or Sir/Ma'am for variety, plus or minus job titles.  Among the main Federation cast, imagine dialogue populated with Chief O'Brien, Doctor Bashir, Lieutenant Dax, and *Ben* in charge of it all...although maybe you don't need to imagine it, since that sort of thing happens with women.  Anyway, that sort of behavior prompted a tradition among some Black families of naming their kids *for* honorifics, forcing white people to call them Sir, Miss, Princess, Mister, and so forth.

Anyway, now I want the story about the Cardassian underclass naming their kids Gul or Glinn or whatever, and the military not knowing about it so their computers automatically send them paychecks and orders...

 > **MARRITZA**: That's not true. I am alive. I'll always be alive. It's Marritza who's dead. Marritza, who was only good for cowering under his bunk and weeping like a woman. Who, every night, covered his ears because he couldn't bear to hear the screaming for mercy of the Bajorans.  I covered my ears every night. I couldn't bear to hear those horrible screams. You have no idea what it's like to be a coward, to see these horrors and do nothing. Marritza's dead. He deserves to be dead.

...This doesn't make him less complicit.  Crying yourself to sleep after a hard day's work at the genocide shop doesn't make you less a part of the genocide.  I don't know why nobody in the episode sees this.

Don't get me wrong.  I see the character as sympathetic, and I appreciate the plan that he'll momentarily explain and see why it would undermine Bajor at least as much as it helps to heal.  But looking at this as a story about the need for accountability for war crimes, nothing about this episode makes the case for letting Marritza go free, while everything that he tells us makes a strong case for prosecuting him.

 > **MARRITZA**: For Cardassia. Cardassia will only survive if it stands in front of Bajor and admits the truth. My trial will force Cardassia to acknowledge its guilt. And we're guilty, all of us. My death is necessary.

Finally.  At least *somebody* understands complicity, even if it falls on the person here with the least authority to do anything about it.

## Conclusions

As mentioned, we spend most of our time with the big confrontation, but some of it spills over.

### The Bad

We seem to have swung to the other end of the recurring anti-intellectual stance, where they consider ignorance an interesting topic of conversation.

Everybody seems shockingly, if you'll pardon my appropriating the phrase, soft on war crime.  It starts with the Bajorans, but the two Starfleet characters who get much attention keep emphasizing that, to them, support staff at a forced labor camp doesn't count as committing war crimes.

### The Weird

Sisko raises many questions about law enforcement procedures in the Federation, but with nothing like an answer.
