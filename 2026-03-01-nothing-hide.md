## Hiding and Fearing and So Forth

I'll skip the background, here, because you almost certainly know it.  Either that, or you've come to this post much later than I published it, and so have a *different* background that you care deeply about, but I couldn't envision in 2026.  It doesn't really matter, because every instance of this problem looks identical:  Somebody spies on a population, and when people object, they say *if you have nothing to hide, then you have nothing to fear*, or some grammatical variation.

The situation goes back at least far enough that people recognized it when Henry James used it in [**The Reverberator**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Reverberator) (1888), a comedy novel about tabloid journalism.

> If these people had done bad things they ought to be ashamed of themselves and he couldn't pity them, and if they hadn't done them there was no need of making such a rumpus about other people knowing.

Upton Sinclair used the modern phrasing of the justification outright, with powerful people opening and reading his mail, in 1917.

## Everybody Hides

Often, when somebody tries to answer the "if you have nothing to hide..." argument, they try to find a list of things that most people do have to hide.  And while I acknowledge that instinct, it seems a bit simplistic to answer that way.  Those "universal" things to hide generally come in two categories, financial information and biological functions.  If you really think that you have nothing to hide, they say, then publish your credit card information or pictures of your bathroom activities.

We can usually ignore the first category, because almost everybody treats financial information differently.  If you pry into my personal life and use that information, then only you and I care, but if you pry into my *financial* life and use that information, then you have probably committed a felony.  Even if I explicitly show you all my financial data, if you use it in some way without my explicit permission, it will cause you problems.

And we might also ignore the financial side, because all of us probably spread our financial information across the world without much thought to whether we can trust a given vendor with our data.  I mean, seriously, when you go shopping, do you verify the security of every online store and their payment systems?  Do you go through a multistep process to make sure that the clerk can't get access to your credit card information?  Or do you trust that most people won't actually take advantage of you and, if they do, the people running the networks won't hold you responsible for the fraud?

And for the more personal information, I realize that people have different tolerances for embarrassment, but for most people, somebody learning intimate details doesn't really cause damage after a certain age.  Like, I wouldn't *supply* it to anybody, but (granting that I have more privilege than many) if somebody wanted nude pictures of me or descriptions of my last bowel movement, that sounds like *their* private shame[^I8-zx1], not mine.  And I say almost exactly the sorts of things in private that I do in public, so hacking into my e-mail won't turn up anything juicy.

[^I8-zx1]:  For example, I always get a laugh out of the ransom-spam that shows me a password that I used twenty years ago and declares that it has recorded me pleasuring myself to pornography, and if I don't pay up, they'll publish the video.  And first off, no, you don't have any such thing for a variety of reasons.  But also...who would *care* if such a hypothetical video got out?  I like to imagine my friends meeting up to discuss something like that.  *Oh, no, John has genitals.  Whatever shall we do?*

## Reasons to Hide

Ready for a different direction?  Let's play [the Pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid_%28game_show%29)!

- Victims of abuse looking for help
- People adhering to unpopular religions
- Queer folks
- People *questioning* their sexual orientation or gender
- Inventors about to change a market
- Politicians about to announce a new initiative or campaign
- Undercover police officers
- People under witness protection
- Whistle-blowers
- Journalists reporting on corruption

People who have something to hide for legitimate reasons?  *Yes*.  {% emoji clap %}

At least under certain conditions, such as intolerant communities or at a point when a rival can spoil a plan, everybody on that list and more might, in fact, have something to hide.  They all[^i3fN3r] live legitimate, perfectly legal lives, but their lives (or at least careers) rely on secrecy.  They don't need secrecy because *they* need to do something wrong, but because someone might want to inflict wrong on them.  Specifically, they all have legitimate, reasonable fears resulting from exposure.

[^i3fN3r]:  For certain values of "legitimacy," in some cases involving official power...

Rather than worrying about my own privacy when hiding things, to me, this makes more sense as a refutation of the "nothing to hide" argument.  That list has enough diversity that you can play different political blocs off each other.  This group might want to expose all the queer people, but they probably have deep concerns about undercover cops, for example.

And critically, we can't selectively protect the privacy of individuals in these categories without "outing" them.  That leaves us with only two options, hanging everybody out to dry or protecting everybody's rights regardless of need.  If not, well, let's look at a quick example.

### Wheat and Chaff

If you want to suggest a compromise, that we only protect the secrets of official people, such as our undercover police officer, because you don't worry about the other groups, then consider the following hypothetical capture of network communication, with my apologies to George Bernard Shaw.

> **A**: And how are all your people down at Selsey?  
> **B**: Jub gbyq lbh zl crbcyr pbzr sebz Fryfrl?  
> **A**: Never you mind. They did.  
> **B**: Ubj qb lbh pbzr gb or hc fb sne rnfg? Lbh jrer obea va Yvffba Tebir.  
> **C**: Oh, what harm is there in my leaving Lisson Grove? It wasn’t fit for a pig to live in; and I had to pay four-and-six a week.  
> **C**: Oh, boo—hoo—oo—  
> **A**: Live where you like; but stop that noise.  
> **D**: Come, come! He can’t touch you: You have a right to live where you please.  
> **E**: Park Lane, for instance. I’d like to go into the Housing Question with you, I would.  
> **C**: I’m a good girl, I am.  
> **E**: Do you know where I come from?  
> **A**: Hoxton.

OK, quick quiz:  Which speakers have official authority to hide their secrets?  Gosh, I wonder who...

This extends the care-about-others argument.  If only a small group can protect themselves, then they stand out, making them *targets*.  We protect ourselves to camouflage the people who need protection, even---especially---if we have nothing to hide.

In the same way, if you only *sometimes* have something to hide, then you should hide as much as you can, so that you don't draw attention to your private moments.  If you use [Tor](https://www.torproject.org/) for some purposes, then you should use it for as much traffic as you can.  If you encrypt some files or messages, then you should encrypt as much as you can.  When only The Bystander---character B in the lines extracted from Shaw's **Pygmalion**---has permission to use [ROT13](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ROT13), it doesn't take much effort to pick out his activity.  And if The Bystander only encrypts the most sensitive messages, then you know where to focus your attention.

Borrowing from military aviation, which borrowed from agriculture, we have the idea of [chaff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaff_%28countermeasure%29), countermeasures that disrupt search (radar) operations by overwhelming the operator with irrelevant signals.  The more of us adopt privacy norms even when it doesn't matter, the harder it becomes to target somebody who needs privacy.

## Motivated Reasoning

If I haven't convinced you with an appeal to empathy---and the last decade or so has shown that some people hate nothing more than civic-mindedness and empathy---consider the words[^jdusr0] of Armand Jean du Plessis, [Cardinal Richelieu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_Richelieu)[^xGcZzG].

[^jdusr0]:  People dispute whether he said it, actually, but *somebody* said it, and it doesn't matter for our purposes who.

[^xGcZzG]:  Amusingly, one of the times that I mentioned this quote on Quora, many years ago, somebody commented on my answer to complain that I should try to find a similar sentiment spoken by a *real* person.  They apparently had only encountered the name in the *Three Musketeers* books and adaptations, and so assumed that Dumas invented him as a plot device...

> Qu'on me donne six lignes écrites de la main du plus honnête homme, j'y trouverai de quoi le faire pendre.

Or we could translate it to English, I guess.

> If you give me six lines written by the hand of the most honest of men, I will find something in them which will hang him.

The day that somebody *wants* to target you, any information that you couldn't hide will prove remarkably useful to them, even if you haven't done anything wrong.  To pick the classical criminal concerns, if someone who you've ever known happens to know someone associated with a terrorist---and keep in mind that certain administrations love to redefine "terrorism" to whatever behavior personally worries them---if your location history shows you've driven too close to a bank robbery or a murder, or if you have ever bought something that you can't really afford, that provides a toe-hold to build a terrorism, bank robbery, murder, or tax evasion charge against you.

Do you *really* have nothing to hide, then, even from some future adversary who desperately wants to discredit you and has the time and resources to pore over everything?

This line of thinking brings (back) us [Blackstone's ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blackstone%27s_ratio), that "the law holds that it is better that ten guilty persons escape than that one innocent suffer."  I prefer Benjamin Franklin's version of a hundred guilty persons, but either way, it makes the point that we protect everybody's rights, and maintain such a high bar of evidence, to prevent the modern Richelieu from hanging innocent people.

## Name Games

Since "age verification"---to get back to the motivation for revisiting these ideas---sounds like a superficial, benign subset of invading privacy, we should probably talk about *how* you verify somebody's age:  Unless you don't mind getting a lot of cases wrong in both directions, you collect official ID from people.

That doesn't limit the privacy transgression to age.  And when that [collection of IDs has a data breach {% cc %}](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c8jmzd972leo), *everybody* has access to all that intimate information.  Even further, your "age" now links your relationships and every word that you say with you.

Note that this also excludes people who, for whatever reason, don't have government-issued identification.  I know, sometimes, they present some goofy AI-driven thing where they use [neo-phrenology](https://www.peppercarrot.com/en/miniFantasyTheater/014.html) to guess your age.

In turn, the current situation reminds me a lot of the so-called [nymwars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nymwars), when social media, led primarily by Facebook and (now-defunct) Google+, demanded that users only run accounts under their government-recognized name.  They insisted that only the name on our official identification could keep us civil, as if they have never met a bully.  Study after study showed that this didn't work and, unsurprisingly, actually pushed *good* people away from the sites, because their identity didn't match their official name---pen names, changing identities, and so forth---or because their real names opened them up to harassment off the site in addition to what they received on the site.  And because Silicon Valley software developers don't understand that people with different backgrounds exist, the policies also actively drove people away by banning them from the site for the crime of...having a name that looked weird to somebody; Wikipedia has collected a [sampling of people banned](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facebook_real-name_policy_controversy) by Facebook for such reasons.

This led to a lot of reactions.  For example, if you develop software, then you have probably read (or had someone beg you to read) [Falsehoods Programmers Believe about Names {% cc %}](https://www.kalzumeus.com/2010/06/17/falsehoods-programmers-believe-about-names/)[^yqJqmb], which inspired [similar lists](https://github.com/kdeldycke/awesome-falsehood) on almost any topic that changes depending on your culture or location.  It also led to the like of [4chan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4chan) and its successors, to disprove the idea that anonymous people had no accountability; say what you will about the site's effect on the rest of the Internet, but participants over there did generally stay accountable to *each other*, "despite" anonymity.

[^yqJqmb]:  I believe that I had already adopted the convention when this article came out, but if you've ever noticed me pedantically referring to somebody's *given name* or *surname*, it comes from similar experiences with first/last name fields in databases that made a user's life difficult, and making that distinction leaves room for the cases where the person needs their *laqab*, *call name*, *teknonym*, *oriki*, or the other couple-dozen other name-types that I have some awareness of.  Some kinds of names have specific cultural significance as a surname does in most of the West, others don't.

## Alternatives

By the way, Discord has heard your concerns.  And they helpfully announced that [most users won't actually need to submit an ID {% cc %}](https://discord.com/safety/how-discord-is-building-safer-experiences-for-teens), because they have a totally-not-creepy alternative.

> We leverage an advanced machine learning model developed at Discord to predict whether a user falls into a particular age group based on patterns of user behavior and several other signals associated with their account on Discord. We only use these signals to assign users to an age group when our confidence level is high; when it isn't, users go through our standard age assurance flow to confirm their age. We do **not** use your message content in the age estimation model.

Yes, the idea that they *watch everything I do* sure does sound less intrusive.

Tangentially, why would they not include message content in their AI-driven phrenology?  The service doesn't work if they don't save all your messages, and to the extent that you can realistically infer anything about somebody by their behavior, age from their language use seems near the top of the list.

Anyway, they also decided to [postpone the rollout {% cc %}](https://discord.com/blog/getting-global-age-assurance-right-what-we-got-wrong-and-whats-changing) after a series of related embarrassments.  But don't let that fix anything, because the same thing will happen again in a few months with less scrutiny.

Finally, I should note---like many others have, I assume---that Discord users[^IHhOuJ] really have one clear chance to stop this, beyond making your voices heard:  Don't verify your age if asked.  Yes, you'll have the inconvenience of restricted access.  But if large numbers of people accept that inconvenience, then Discord becomes less convenient for every user, threatening their business model as people look for alternatives.

[^IHhOuJ]:  I doubt that anybody cares, but if you want an example of my situation with Discord, I currently find myself signed up to about two-dozen servers, of which I only really check in on a couple and haven't participated more than a handful of times in the past year or two.  Some of those communities will probably try to migrate to another platform, especially those revolving around some Free Software project, but probably not all.  As such, I admit that I have an easier time accepting restricted access than somebody else might.
