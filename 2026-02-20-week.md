## 9:06 -- Thu 19 February 2026

{% embed https://images.theconversation.com/files/717291/original/file-20260210-56-a8ddhm.jpg|A man stands over a seated woman and gestures toward a computer while the woman gestures over her keyboard|true| %}

[<i class="fab fa-mastodon"></i>](https://mastodon.social/@jcolag/116097676868932112) [Distrust and disempowerment, not apathy, keep employees from supporting marginalized colleagues](https://theconversation.com/distrust-and-disempowerment-not-apathy-keep-employees-from-supporting-marginalized-colleagues-274502) from The Conversation @TheConversationUS@newsie.social

> When popular messaging, like on social media, and organizational interventions misunderstand the causes of people‚Äôs inaction, they risk exacerbating frustration and tensions. Interventions need to account for their audience‚Äôs true perspectives on what makes allyship difficult.

Hashtags:  #Work #Identity #Inequality

This tracks, in my experience.  In a "classical" workplace, people quickly get the impression that their opinions don't matter and that the power get cut more slack than everybody else.  If it feels like the options begin at nobody paying attention and end at retribution---in many cases, reporting harassment gets the *victim* reassigned somewhere irrelevant to minimize the chance of contact with the (more powerful) offender---then it becomes a matter of choosing battles.

To go a bit beyond the boundaries of the article, over the years, I have found two ways of building the motivation to push past that.  First, when you have some formal or informal authority, get used to the flat phrase "we don't do that here."  It minimizes the feeling of judgment, and it doesn't expose anybody to much risk.  This often works well in smaller communities online, where anybody with some history in the group can get away with making an observation about the group dynamics.

For people who feel like they have less of a right to get involved, embrace your disbelief and disgust.  In a lot of cases, you can get a lot of mileage out of the slow head-shake when the offender looks to you for a reaction.
