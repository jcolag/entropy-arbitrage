## The Nagus

Since last time, **The Next Generation** hasn't had a new episode, so nothing happened.  And this episode probably won't have much for us, since it focuses primarily on the Ferengi and a couple of personal relationships.

 > **JAKE**: What's the Gratitude Festival?

This feels like the sort of thing that you figure out from context, no?  Two words, both plain English...

 > **JAKE**: Sounds great. When do we leave?
 >
 > **SISKO**: This afternoon, right after school.

Wait.  With school back in session, how would Jake not already know about the Gratitude Festival?  During his three weeks off, and at his age, we might imagine that his busy girl-watching schedule revealed in [*Move Along Home*]({% post_url 2026-04-30-move-along-home %}) didn't have the capacity to include hearing anybody talk about it or introducing himself to anybody.  But we know that Keiko recruited Bajoran kids into the school at the end of [*A Man Alone*]({% post_url 2026-03-19-man-alone %}), and she made a huge show about building a curriculum that worked for the diverse backgrounds of the potential students on hand, so I can't imagine it *not* coming up there.

Early in my career when I worked at companies that had large international customers, I *personally* had a pile of calendars on my desk from around the world that I'd check every morning, because if someone from Bulgaria---example chosen to fit the day that this post goes out, not to reflect an actual incident or customer---showed up to learn about the product, having somebody on the tour of the office who might remember to say something about [Radio Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_Day) helped them to unwind a bit.  If *I* could do it for the entire world, it seems wild that their computer doesn't have upcoming local holidays *where they live* front-and-center of every display, since that impacts which shops they'll have open, who needs leave, and a dozen other critical issues.

Actually, I have a better example.  Years back, I contributed to a lot of Kickstarter campaigns for projects.  I lost count of the number of teams who sheepishly posted about their schedules massively slipping because they somehow didn't account for factories shutting down for a week or two during Chinese New Year festivals, leading to them losing their reserved spot at the assemblers, packagers, or shippers...

 > **KRAX**: I am Krax and this is my father, Grand Nagus Zek.

You might recognize Krax as [Lou Wagner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lou_Wagner), probably most visible as the mechanic on **CHiPs**.  We also briefly saw him as the (barely relevant) Ferengi commander in [*Chain of Command*]({% post_url 2025-01-23-chain-command-1 %}).

And while he doesn't speak for a while, you presumably recognize the Nagus as [Wallace Shawn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wallace_Shawn), who...do I really need to do this?  He played Vizzini in **The Princess Bride** among other roles, has written award-winning plays, published essays, shows up at political and activist events, and probably a bunch of other things.

 > **O'BRIEN**: Sorry I'm late. The Transporter in Ops needed a minor adjustment in its upper molecular imaging scanner, but never mind that. All right, let's settle down. Let's settle down! Jake! Nog! Nog! Thank you. Now we only have two more weeks to get through before Mrs. O'Brien returns from Earth, so that shouldn't be too difficult, right? Right. Let's take out our homework PADDs. We'll begin with the older students. Your last assignment was to write an essay defining the term "ethics." Let's start with Nog.

Ah, I remember my school-days, when the teacher needed to take a trip, and so their surly, racist spouse covered the class as a side-gig...

Sorry, wait, that never happened, because it shows a complete disrespect to the profession of teaching, the students, and the substitute's "real" job.  But I guess that we'll need to watch O'Brien "demeaning" himself by doing his wife's job.

Also, if he has the time for this, doesn't that reiterate that he does a terrible job of running the operations side?  When he left for his vacation, shortly before [*Dax*]({% post_url 2026-04-16-dax %}), everybody pitched in to keep the equipment from falling apart.  Now, more than a month later in 1993 terms and a bit less in ours, he only has minor adjustments to make and has time to spend hours pretending that he knows how to teach.

 > **O'BRIEN**: Hold on. You're saying Vulcans stole your homework?!
 >
 > ...
 >
 > **O'BRIEN**: Any idea why?

Honestly, the franchise has made "logic" such a conversation-ending term that Nog probably could've gone with that and left O'Brien nodding in agreement.

 > **SISKO**: Chief, I just heard that airlock nine is jammed again.

Wait, maybe O'Brien moonlights as a saboteur?  For the first batch of episodes, nothing worked.  Then they had two full episodes with no Miles and no engineering problems, not even a token whack-the-console moment.  He shows back up, and while he wanders around grading essays, airlocks start crapping out again.  Could that happen coincidentally?  Sure.  But if I managed somebody who oversaw that kind of coincidence, I'd look *really* close at his work logs...

 > **O'BRIEN**: You know how it is, sir. Kids, there's nothing they like better than to take advantage of a substitute teacher. I always did.

He always sounds like such a peach when he talks about his personal life...

 > **O'BRIEN**: Well, I'd find a way to separate them, sir. That Nog's a bad influence.

Oh, great, we can now get our racism in stereo, this week...

 > **SISKO**: That's because your daughter's three. Wait until she's fourteen.

Again assuming that their years look like our years, Molly apparently skipped a year and a half since her birth in [*Disaster*]({% post_url 2024-06-13-disaster %}), airing about seventeen months before this episode.  I get it, mind you.  We'll meet her in about a month, and as I recall, they find a fantastic child performer for the role and the age of a tertiary character doesn't matter to storytelling.  But since she'll feature in a time travel story later in the series, it seemed worth mentioning.

 > **ROM**: Nog, I never want you to step foot in there again. Now go to your room. And no studying. My apologies, Nagus. It's those Federation do-gooders, always sticking their ugly noses in other peoples business.

We always get the sense (until it becomes explicit) that Rom doesn't actually believe this, but it does seem worth mentioning that the Federation has the sort of reputation where everybody finds this entirely believable, much like nobody questions disparaging the Ferengi.

 > **SISKO**: Oatmeal lumpy again?

Do I not understand replicators?  It doesn't matter since I don't care about the fictional technology, but if you assemble food molecule-by-molecule, surely you replicate the starch molecules individually swollen with the cooking liquid, making lumps impossible.

 > **JAKE**: But you're the one always saying that we should make friends with other cultures.
 >
 > **SISKO**: I believe that, and I'm glad you believe that too. But human values and Ferengi values are very different. We've never been able to form a common bond.

I keep coming back to this racism, even skipping a couple of lines, but this time through seems utterly bizarre, considering that they both want a stable, safe community, and the Ferengi only stayed on the station in [*Emissary*]({% post_url 2026-02-26-emissary-1 %}) because Sisko openly played on Quark's concern for family.  The Ferengi act more openly on their misogyny, but the Federation quietly loves its patriarchy, too.

Maybe the 2020s make "capitalism or no" feel like an absurdly petty difference when we have [TESCREAL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TESCREAL) flakes wringing their hands about their libertarian AI god torturing simulations of them, which somehow hurts them, if they don't burn the world down to create their libertarian AI god.  Yeah, I don't get it either.  But it makes the point that you can have people with completely incompatible values that go well beyond what we see in the Ferengi.

Also, I have to appreciate that Jake referred to the Ferengi as another culture.

 > **DAX**: Aubergine stew. Looks delicious. So where's Jake?

Impressively, most cultures have an eggplant-based stew, and they generally work pretty well.

Also, asking about Jake makes this scene feel surreal, or maybe obnoxious, like Dax deliberately interrupted family dinner because she felt like playing chess...

 > **NOG**: There are four...teen
 >
 > **JAKE**: Fourteen planets.

I guess that this answers the question of whether they actually read.

## Conclusions

We don't get much new out of this, beyond literacy...

### The Bad

People don't keep track of the local holidays where they live and find them completely opaque.

We see a fairly complete disinterest in education from the adults.  We have O'Brien taking over his wife's class on breaks from his normal job.  And he brags about taking advantage of substitute teachers as a child.

Speaking of O'Brien, his return also seems to reinforce the idea that the station's technical problems rise from his poor management.

Mostly, though, where the episode focuses on the Federation, it does so in the form of bigotry against the Ferengi.  They can only see irreconcilable values, bad influences, and crime, unless explicitly shown otherwise.  And the Federation similarly has a reputation for directly interfering with people's lives.
