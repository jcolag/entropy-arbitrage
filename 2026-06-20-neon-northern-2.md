## What Works Well?

While (as I'll mention in the next section) we mostly have a numbers-and-mechanics section that tells us little about the world beyond that they use some (mildly) offensive terms for certain groups of people, we do get one quasi-narrative section in the "dungeon" example framed as a mini-adventure to explain how things work.

## What Works...Less Well?

{% include lesswell.md %}

A lot of this chunk of the book has sections with nothing more than dry tables for randomly generating the type of thing.  Often, the section doesn't even warrant a description of the thing to create or why.  And importantly, a lot of that defies the sense that the early book gives that everybody plays in approximately the same world.

And speaking of generating things randomly, I should point out that, while the book never comes out and says so---again, this section of the book has a tendency to not say much of anything, in fact---it gives the strong impression that characters will mostly stumble across people to fight.  Yes, it comes as the default assumption of the format, and that doesn't really help, especially when it fills it "Bestiary" with *people*.

## Six Years Hence:   Elephants Dream

Continuing to revisit projects from early in the book club, we come to [**Elephants Dream**]({% post_url 2020-06-20-dream %}), the first Blender Open Movie.  And...let me explain why I picked this one.

Most likely, I have hinted at this all along, but in the early days of the book club, I expected things to fall apart quickly.

Today, I never have *much* remaining that I know about---except for music, where you can find *so many* albums, if you can stretch the definition of "narrative" a bit---I have consistently had at least some ideas for the next few weeks, and usually have confidence that we'll generally see at least a computer game, a role-playing adventure or setting, some form of motion picture, a novel, an album, a comic, and some shorter-form writing such as a short story or poetry before I go start going back through the list.  Six years ago, though, this felt like a complete fantasy, and I thank the many creators for proving me so wrong on this.

Specifically, six years (and a couple of months) ago, I started with a list with maybe a few dozen Free Culture projects that I had considered using for something else, not necessarily vetting the licenses as well as I would today.  At the time, I figured that after exhausting that list, which took so much work to assemble in the first place, the book club would hobble along as an irregular series adding a post whenever I ran across something new.  When I didn't have something to talk about for the book club, maybe (I imagined) I could fill Saturdays by talking about software that I use often, or maybe it'd look like the Sunday posts where I would *like* to publish something every week, but if I don't, no big deal.

Got that?  OK, so that brings us to probably the most famous Free Culture productions, what they used to call "Blender Open Projects," but now seem to call (yawn...) [Films](https://studio.blender.org/films/).  By 2020, they had produced a dozen such short films, mostly all animation except for one.  I could have tried covering them all in one post or splitting them over a couple of posts, but **Tears of Steel** and **Big Buck Bunny** don't even have the same sorts of things to talk about, let alone have a combined cohesive story that I could talk about at once.

And I decided there that, as a solution to both problems, I could give each film its own post, using it to fill in when I couldn't find anything else for the book club.  That philosophy fit with prior posts, such as [the Floraverse]({% post_url 2020-05-23-seeds %}) and [**Jathia's Wager**]({% post_url 2020-05-16-jathia %}), where the creator worked on more---years of comics or a short sitcom, in turn---that I didn't do more than gesture at in the post, leaving open the likely possibility that I'll return to all the more prolific creators someday.  It also made sense, because I see more value in introducing you (the readers) to as many new creators as I can find (because you can usually click through to the creator's website and explore their other projects) than I see in hammering on the same long-running (and often already-well-known) projects.

In any case, if I wanted to cover each film from the Blender team separately, then I should start with the first one, **Elephants Dream**, even though nobody particularly *likes* it beyond showcasing that Blender can do professional work.  I definitely didn't care for the paper-thin story or the characters with Jim Henson mannerisms but no personality to speak of.

By the way, for the first few years, I considered changing my rules briefly in late March to talk about **Spring** around the equinox, but I usually found myself in the middle of a novel or forgot, so that never happened, and so it made more sense to keep to those rules when it also meant spreading attention around.

Since the post went out six years ago, the Blender team has put out another five short films, with **Singularity** dropping about a month and a half ago.  If you follow them on social media, such as the [Blender Studio Mastodon account](https://mastodon.social/@BlenderStudio), they release previews through most of a film's development process.  Probably importantly, especially starting with the justifiably famous **Sintel**, except for the occasional project made (like **Elephants Dream**) primarily to demonstrate the technology, storytelling has taken the spotlight in many of these films, even though they all serve primarily as bait to bring in monthly subscribers who get more convenient access to the models that go into each film.
