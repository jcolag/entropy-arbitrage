I apologize that this goes out so late.  Let me explain the background.  For once, though, a late post shouldn't weigh too heavily on people.  Although yes, the big disastrous current events *do* have me thinking about the possibilities of not having generation after generation of these complete buffoons.

Shortly after publishing last week's post about celebrating International Women's Day and [Women's History Month](/blog/tag/womens-history-month) by [abandoning patriarchy]({% post_url 2026-03-08-iwd %}), largely by understanding what feminism describes and attacking gender inequality by undoing the thousands of years of discrimination against women, it occurred to me that such discussions always leave an unanswered question:  Without patriarchy, what role do men have?  What do we *do*, exactly?

![Three seated men in three-piece suits and top hats](/blog/assets/6649802661_3a01bbae6b_o.png "I think that I may have worked with the guy on the right...")

And I can't claim that the time has actually come to write that post, because I don't really have the answers.  I think that my point includes the possibility that nobody has those answers, because we've made toxic masculinity look like a load-bearing construct in society, impeding our vision of a world without it.  Likewise, strict gender roles got us into this mess, so replacing toxic masculinity with...slightly *less* toxic masculinity will only provide opportunities to make it toxic later, so if a lot of advice sounds like "do whatever you want, but don't act like a creep," well, now you can envision that ideal.

And yes, I planned to have this post out early this morning, then utterly failed to do it as I mostly rewrote it.

## Towards a Solution

Realizing that we can't really tell every boy and man "do whatever you want as long as it doesn't look like *that*" probably won't work, both due to a lack of imagination and the impossibility of enumerating every possible terrible behavior, I do want to think a bit about the possibilities of a "transitional masculinity," a middle path, so to speak.  By middle, I mean that we currently have two extreme paths.

The incumbent extreme, pathological path says that we should define ourselves as men through a complete opposition to women, as outlined last week.  At the other extreme, we tell young boys and men to---in effect---do whatever you want...but also never forget that we'll judge you if you get it wrong, and people still supporting the patriarchy will judge you harshly if you get too far out of line for them.  I don't want to suggest that **nobody** follows the latter path, but it definitely takes a certain uncommon kind of person to see that as a viable way to live and not a version of [Pascal's wager](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal%27s_wager), but with a choice between wide condemnation and slightly narrower condemnation...but also maybe wider condemnation, who knows?

Because of that, I want to outline the start of a framework, something that helps map out the space between the two broken paths.  Or maybe you can think of it as the social equivalent of nicotine patches for men addicted to toxic masculinity, a covert way of getting people to healthier places.

While this holds true across all posts, more than the usual I'd like to invite people to build on this, contribute comments, and otherwise help improve it for the people who need it.  I would bet that I won't come up with any original ideas, rather collecting what I've heard over decades, yet this space absolutely needs innovation.  With that, let's try to dig in.

## Anger

Let's start at the most prominent issue.  Many have said it before, but under patriarchy, society permits men two emotional states:  Disinterested and enraged.  Everything else, they want us to stifle it as quickly as possible.  We can put some anger to useful ends, but consistent or amplified anger stresses out the body, which reduces immune function, increases inflammation, damages the cardiovascular system, and more.  And for all that harm, who does it serve?  Not the people around you, because it makes you less safe to associate with.

Therefore, deal with your anger.  Work out what triggers it and why, so that you can deal with those underlying causes.  Some people need therapy to deal with those issues, other people don't.  Find what works for you and get to those---probably trauma related, because we don't exactly have a healthy society---roots.

And for the rest, figure out how to channel your remaining healthy anger into something productive.  You all know this.  I have to imagine that every children's television series works to make the point that yes, you have a valid emotion, but no, it doesn't give you the right to throw a tantrum or otherwise act out, so *use* it.

For children, this advice has to almost feel like a joke, because they don't have a wide range of socially acceptable activities that help.  As adults, though, we can usually go for a walk or run, talk out what bothers us, or write about it.

After you have dealt with your anger, then you can start working on experiencing *other* emotions in ways other than suppressing them until they turn into anger.

Admittedly, that still doesn't feel like a satisfying answer, though.  Work on myself?  Deal with trauma to protect my health?  Ugh, what, does John also think that I should eat more broccoli[^gAJ8MN]?  Nobody wants to write an action flick screenplay about a dude getting healthy.  OK, then let's try to find some ideas that read more as "active."

## Media

Again, we'll start small.

I don't care what media you enjoy, but stop *promoting* the media that doesn't reflect your values, or made by people who don't share your values.

Again, you don't need to hunt down projects by unassailable creators with feminist, anti-racist messaging, though you'll probably enjoy at least some of it.  But at least shut up about how you wish that they'd bring back the show with all racist allegories where the performers have spoken out about abuse on the set.  And no, I don't necessarily mean the one that popped into your head, because *so many* of these exist.

On indie social media, a series of interlocking polls have floated around with the premise of, given a selection of people wearing t-shirts celebrating a given media franchise, which of them would you trust most[^TZcmAg].  You can think of this as a variation of that.  When you talk about a show, sports, or whatever, what does your interest in it say about you?

[^TZcmAg]:  The results inevitably get skewed based on popularity, so don't worry about the details.  For example, *Stargate* seems to over-perform, when compared to whether you should trust somebody excited about military adventurism constantly seeking to defend the homeland from invaders, not to mention the constant misogyny.  I loved the film and can enjoy the shows, to some extent, but I certainly wouldn't trust somebody wearing the merch, nor would I recommend that other people trust that person...

Again, we like what we like.  You might *eventually* want to purge your consumption habits, because you no longer find some things worth your time.  But to start, at least stop making references to the book series written by a bigot.  Again, I don't want to single out anything in particular, because so many of these exist, even though your mind jumped to "that one."

If you really need some guidelines on properties to watch out for, try these.  If a sport centers on the performance of masculinity, and its management and executives like to cozy up with right-wing causes, that presents a problem.  When a creator seems to spend more time justifying their hatred of "those people" than writing new material for their fans to enjoy, you probably have a problem.  When performers talk about mistreatment on the set, you probably have a problem.  If the material jokes about sexual assault or has characters threaten it, if it shows the existence of a certain kind of character as an existential threat, or if it insults men by comparing them to female animals or female anatomy, then you probably have a problem.  And yes, for that show that crossed your mind earlier, if it delights in condemning sex workers (among many other problems), then you almost certainly have a problem.

Consider an example:  If a creator openly denies the humanity of some people, and you quote their words, talk about using their ideas to understand the world, and otherwise remind everybody how much you love the franchise, how do you think that looks to the dehumanized targets of that creator?  Do you really want people to see you that way?  And you can fix that at the low cost of *shutting up* about the media franchise.

Yeah, that sounds like more introspection.  But you get to stick it to billionaires, in a small way, by getting off their hype-teams, and that feels action-adjacent.

## Defending

I know, you want something that satisfies the desire for confrontation trained into you since childhood.  You want action that people will *tell stories* about.

Fine.  Stand up for people (in general) with less privilege, then.  When a friend or colleague makes an inappropriate---based in race, gender, class, religion, sexual orientation, whatever---joke, for example, make it clear that you disapprove.  Oh, suddenly the responsibility for epic battle between good and evil should maybe go to somebody else?  I never said that this wouldn't take work.

Honestly, privilege has a fun little feature that you can take advantage of:  The people who throw it around never have any confidence, because they know that they didn't earn their positions.  We feel naked without it, but whenever we use our privilege, we have the nagging sense that we shouldn't.  You can see it as pervasive in how aggressively right-wing personalities desperately want to silence oppressed groups.  They don't want to hear about slavery, patriarchy, transphobia, employers not treating workers fairly, or much of anything else, because it *makes them feel bad*, and privilege almost always means not needing to notice things that would make us feel bad.

This gives you a convenient route to practice standing up for underprivileged groups.  Give them *the stare*.  You know the one.  The "not cool" stare.  The stare that you'd give a toddler about to do something dangerous that they want you to find funny, or a grandparent who has grossly misused slang.  Show your disappointment and don't back down, because that makes it clear that they went too far.  Most of the time, you'll see that recognition and concern, and you've had your first success.

In online spaces, you want to get used to the phrase "we don't do that around here," the polite way of telling them to take it back to 4chan.

Remember, patriarchy demands conformity, so you don't need much authority to make it clear that your community has norms with higher standards.  In both cases, you only want them to fit in better.

As you build confidence there, you'll find your voice to say things such as, hey, if we want better decisions, maybe we shouldn't make the decisions where members of the group don't feel comfortable.  Or if we really want the best candidates, maybe we shouldn't screen for people who want to play video games with us all night[^PzFqS1].

[^PzFqS1]:  I haven't actually heard that one in over a decade, but I walked out of one job interview, because they kept telling me about how they'd work all day, then have all-night gaming sessions, sometimes until they had to start work the next day.

### Not Too Far

Rein it in, though.  You only want the best for the group, never to make yourself the focus of somebody else's story.

Don't position yourself as anybody's champion.  If somebody wants your help, then they'll ask for it, and we'll get to why that hasn't happened yet in a bit.  When you push your way into defending a specific person, you give the impression that they can't stand up for themselves, and make yourself the center of attention, exactly the opposite of supporting people.

Now, sometimes you need to redirect attention, and you make as good a center as anything else.  Generally, this has a physical or near-physical nature to the problem.  For example, when you have men delighting in violating women's boundaries, getting the focus off the targets gives them the opportunity to get out of harm's way and find help.

As a rule, though, if you matter in the retelling of another individual's day, then you probably haven't actually helped them.

## Listening

Whenever I think about this topic, it reminds me of an interview that late celebrity chef Anthony Bourdain gave shortly before his death.  The interviewer asked about how the [#MeToo movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MeToo_movement) has affected him, since he had at least one person in his life who featured in the evolving story as both abuser and victim.

He had an extensive answer that expressed actual empathy for the victims, and went into his regrets for leaning into a hypermasculine persona[^jdGseA] in a way that may have helped normalize tantrums in his industry.  More than that, though, he talked about how his ignorance of the problem started him down the path of introspecting, trying to find the aspects of his persona that kept victims from confiding in him, so that he could work on fixing them.

[^jdGseA]:  I admittedly avoided Bourdain's work because of that persona, always finding him off-putting and irritating.  After seeing that interview, I gave him a second look, and started to notice that it does come off as much more of a satirical act.  That certainly doesn't make it healthy---we have all seen people who take the wrong lesson from satire, and you can't inflict that on colleagues "as a bit" without it feeling real---but I can better understand his intended audience.

Like I said before, for many privileged people privilege means never hearing about anything that might make us feel bad about our position.  Consider how often less privileged people in your life have confided in you.  Do you believe that the research has their statistics wrong because it doesn't match your (lack of) anecdotal evidence, or do you think that your readiness to dismiss the problem might have convinced people that it'd waste their time to tell you?

If you can remember ever telling somebody that "you probably just misinterpreted him," or worse, then you have shown two things.  First, you will protect the reputation of somebody with privilege over the victim.  Second, as a result, people shouldn't trust you with those concerns, because you'll do the wrong thing.  If you want to fix the culture, then you need to fix that image.

And this takes work.  I once wrote a [post about redemption arcs]({% post_url 2022-07-24-redemption %}) making the point that, no, you can't find a single redemptive act that fixes your reputation.  You need to build a new reputation by consistently doing the right thing, even when it probably doesn't matter, and even when people don't believe you.

Become the person who hears people when they confide in you, refuses to judge them for what happened or how you think they understood the situation, and keeps their secrets.  When people talk about safe spaces, they mean exactly this, that when they talk about bad experiences, people won't tell them why they probably have the wrong impression.

## Advocating

If you have a platform, then you have a responsibility to use it.

I talked about this almost five years ago, actually, encouraging people to [do the work]({% post_url 2021-06-06-do-work %}) before identifying their beliefs or positions in the community.  In particular, I started with the then-famous story of a Texas valedictorian who swapped her administration-approved speech with one condemning anti-abortion laws.

That doesn't really matter so much as, if people might listen to you, then use that attention wisely.  In that post, which seeded some of the thinking for this week, I made the point that we can't afford to "leave *women's issues* to women, *racial issues* to racial minorities, and the gender/sex issues to the folks who identify somewhere in the LGBT+ space," because part of privilege involves people not listening to them.  But they might listen to you.

## Transgressing

I'll throw in one more short idea, which I talked about a bit last time around:  Because patriarchy requires strict gender norms to work, you can sabotage a lot of it by not conforming.

This amuses me, since we have so many possible aspects of gender to opt out of in different parts of our lives, and we can often *justify* them in different ways, if somebody really objects.  For adult men, think of the classic case as getting a manicure.  I don't have that kind of patience, myself, but in that, you have a socially acceptable means of presenting yourself well if maybe a bit stuffy for some circles, and you walk away with polished nails.  You get a slightly subversive action, but in a way that reads more as aspiring to (or having) wealth than "wearing cosmetics," which makes it a bit easier for the man who happens to like wearing nail polish.  To push that further, you can also find entire lines of not-quite-cosmetics for men (sunscreen or moisturizer with a slight tint, for example) that further help blur that once bright line.

Back in school, girls made up most of my friend group.  Because we define masculinity as a rejection of anything feminine, platonic friendships across gender lines hilariously reads as not-heterosexual, lest boys get the impression that girls have their own inner lives.  And because my choice led to other choices in classes to take, you have my weird list of female-coded hobbies, such as baking and sewing.

I highly recommend that, if you still attend school[^nUzBMq].  Not only to you diversify your friends and (depending on your school) learn something day-to-day useful, and not only do you make life easier for your gender non-conforming peers, but when "the guys" accuse you of homosexuality, you get to laugh in their faces for thinking that surrounding yourself with women makes you less heterosexual than their manhandling each other to jockey for dominance.

[^nUzBMq]:  As an adult, having friends of other genders still has personal benefits.  Nobody really cares that you scheduled lunch with a woman, though, so it doesn't really erode any societal boundaries.
