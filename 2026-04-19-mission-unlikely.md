* Ignore for ToC

A while back, Free Culture comics zine **Fodongo** put out a [call for comics](https://fodongo.ca/coll/LaRana/bonanza/bonanza.html) for a one-off collection and e-zine of comics revolving around the frog mascot, [La Rana](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rana#Spanish).  Now, I don't really draw---I keep promising myself that I'll take the time to learn, but that hasn't happened yet---but you all know that I'll drop a lot of what I have going on to support a Free Culture project when I can.

![The titular frog, perched on the Earth, covering Canada and the northern United States](/blog/assets/la-rana-2-1.png "Top of the world...")

Therefore, I decided to give it a shot, but minus the part where I actually draw anything.  Not only could I support a fun project and entertain myself---and hopefully some readers---but I realized that, with some work, I could also build it out of Free Culture works, saving everybody the trouble of looking at my sketches.  I started by digging through La Rana's appearances, cutting the character out of each scene, and vectorizing the resulting image, giving me a small but robust library of poses and expressions.

From there, I started brainstorming ideas that would cover a story that could feel complete in three pages, entertain me, and fit inside that library of poses.  The context could change the interpretation of what the character did, and I could maybe modify an occasional image in the library, but I wouldn't do much to *add* an entirely new pose.

As an aside, because other frogs will get mentioned in the post, I'll try to consistently use La Rana as the formal name of the character including the definite article, rather than as a presumed generic noun that happens to mean "the frog," or referring to the character as the frog.  Awkward, but it feels necessary for the context.

## La Rana at the Mountains of Mild Irritation

My first idea actually tried to attack current events, coming up with this rough outline.  The actual outline uses fragmentary phrases, so I cleaned it up for readability here.

- Page One
  * Open on **La Rana** at a hotel, watching television.
  * La Rana has become anxious.
  * La Rana goes outside into winter weather, walking across a frozen pond.
  * La Rana falls through a crack in ice.
- Page Two
  * La Rana falls into a massive ice cave.
  * We see a pictorial sign etched into the wall:  Stars align, Cthulhu rises.
  * Through crack in roof, we see stars in the alignment from the sign.
  * **Cthulhu** rises from the water.
- Page Three
  * La Rana screams.  Cthulhu stares with a question mark thought, confused.
  * La Rana explains things from the news, flailing arms with tiny pictures around their head.  Cthulhu grows anxious.
  * La Rana and Cthulhu attend an ICE protest.

Ultimately, before I started working on it, I decided to discard the idea for three significant reasons.

- Showing the ICE protests seemed to require the [Portland Frog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portland_Frog) to land the joke, which has clear copyright entanglements.
- It seemed too serious for a project soliciting "the sillier, the better," the idea that horror-icon Cthulhu can't manage the news makes me laugh, but then turns too sincere.
- The first explicit guideline says "no hate," and while the story doesn't *express* any hate, ICE and the Department of Homeland Security embody hate and xenophobia---they exist to pick and choose who "deserves" to live in the United States based on visibility---and I didn't want to force our editor to split that hair on our first time actually working together after years of talking to each other.

Oh, right.  I forgot about the fourth major reason.  [N. Toyohito](https://fodongo.ca/cont/ntoyohito.html)---whose work you might recall from [**Antumbra**]({% post_url 2024-08-17-antumbra %})---put together the "parent" zine's [Vive la Révolution](https://fodongo.ca/comics/iss21/iss21.html) cover and ran [*Rebellious Cocktail*](https://fodongo.ca/comics/iss20/iss20_c02_01.html) in the previous issue featuring protests, making it feel like I wanted to ride on those coat-tails.

I then considered swapping the television for a computer.  Companies shoving LLMs {% emoji sparkles %} into every product would agitate the frog.  The story would continue from there as described, but ending with the frog and Cthulhu making art themselves, replacing the protest.  Wouldn't you know it, though, as I started thinking that through, David Revoy---whose work you know from [**Pepper and Carrot**]({% post_url 2020-12-19-pepper %})---published [*Everywhere...*](https://www.peppercarrot.com/en/miniFantasyTheater/038__hd.html) in **Mini Fantasy Theater**, pretty much the same joke, and the ending still felt cloyingly sincere.

"Oh, no," I scream to the heavens.  "I have similar ideas to two[^yxribP] Free Culture comic artists whose work I respect and enjoy."  I officially have the most first-world of first-world problems.  Well, I suppose that having so much money that it earns interest faster than you can give it away *actually* qualifies as the most, but this has to have a top-ten ranking.

[^yxribP]:  Actually, make that three Free Culture comic artists whose work I respect and enjoy.  I wouldn't know this until I sent in my final comic, but **Fodongo** creator/editor and friend of the blog Emiliano (Jectoons) Carrasco mentioned as we chatted about my abandoned paths that somebody else---one of the prior zine contributors---asked about the viability of setting a comic at an ICE protest.

## She Loves You, Iä, Iä, Iä

OK, I said.  Maybe the slice-of-life aspect, the anxiety from current events, provided such a convenient and comfortable path that everybody better at this than me would get there first, no matter what conflict it used.  Maybe, then, the comic needed to start with a structure that no rational comic creator would try.

And you know what no rational comic creator would try, right?  Yeah, no rational comic creator would mash up a postmodern action movie with a hard-boiled detective.  I had in mind something of a thematic joke where none of the characters would speak in the comic, but the scenes have heavy, stylized narration that might not always reflect the action.

- Page One
  * Open on **La Rana** plummeting from the sky.  "I bet you're wondering how I got into this situation.  It all started...with a dame."
  * *Three Days Earlier*.
  * (Everything past this point would have had narration, but with one exception, I didn't get that far in development, so most won't try to create it here.)
  * La Rana sits at a desk in a decrepit office.  Someone knocks at the door.
  * **Cthulhu** enters, wearing a hat and veil, possibly with panels stacked to imply the male gaze.  "She had tentacles that reached all the way to the window, the kind of girl who makes the stars align and gnaws on sanity like a cosmic beaver creating a dam of incoherence," and so forth.
- Page Two
  * Cthulhu describes their problem, but in an incomprehensible language that barely registers as dialogue.
  * La Rana sits back for several panels, narrating irrelevant things and probably not listening.
  * Cthulhu puts money on the desk.
- Page Three
  * Antics (TBD) ensue.
  * La Rana finds the whatever-she-wanted.
  * Back to La Rana falling.  "Wait, so how *did* I get into this situation.  And did the dame pay me?"

This structure could've replaced Cthulhu with anybody, but I still had a couple of vectorized images from planning the first idea, and I find the idea funnier this way, so tried to keep it around for a few more iterations.  For the office background, I had the idea of cutting something shoddy out of cardboard and snapping pictures of it on a grassy patch.

In this case, though, that final section didn't come to me.  I could've lifted the main plot from any hard-boiled detective story, but the idea of filtering hundreds of options based on the small library of La Rana's gestures didn't appeal to me.  And most such stories hinge on dialogue and relationships, which I can't cram into one page.  It didn't so much go on the shelf as get shifted to the back-burner, a fun idea that might need too much work or more pages.

## Libera Liltings

This idea didn't last long enough to get a breakdown or real plot, but given that La Rana's existing stories (except one) come available under the terms of a CC0 public domain dedications, it sparked the idea of having them collect more famous "funny animal" comic and cartoon characters in the public domain.  I had made up a list of such characters not too long ago, from Gertie the Dinosaur to Toby the Pup, so options definitely existed, here.

Again, a main plot didn't come to me to motivate this, but the comic would have had our frog-friend go around recruiting Mickey Mouse, Flip the Frog, and so forth, and they'd form the "Libera Liltings" studio, a brand meant to evoke Looney Tunes, Merrie Melodies, Silly Symphonies, Happy Harmonies, and a bunch of other classic anthologies of various fame.  More studios had such a brand than you'd expect.

I backed off this, because either La Rana gets pushed into the background as one of many characters---we only have three pages, remember---*or* it looks like any number of Muppet movies including **The Muppet Movie** itself, a frog traveling around collecting weird characters to put on a show.  In fact, at one point, this became something like using La Rana in place of various frogs in popular culture, including Kermit, which I think made it clear that the overall premise didn't quite work for me.

## MISSION: Unlikely

The idea of collecting characters, though, grabbed my attention, reminding me less of a talent show than the original **MISSION:  Impossible** series.  The show had the premise---though not in practice, because the regular cast had such good chemistry---of each mission recruiting a different set of agents specialized in the needs of the case.  That started to make sense, and it only needed a plot that absolutely would *not* warrant some big secret mission.

That brings us to what I actually created.  Hunger made a good motivation, so I had a plan for a "heist" to get food from a campsite.  The natural area returned to the idea of working with other "funny animal" characters, which also provides a convenient punchline as the weird antics progress.

This [comic went live](https://fodongo.ca/coll/LaRana/bonanza/bon_01_01.html) on [March 25<sup>th</sup>](https://mastodon.social/@jcolag/116292038918466373), and I held off releasing this post so that I didn't accidentally draw traffic away from the zine...and also because I had other things that I wanted to post about.

### Page One

[![First page of the comic; panel-by-panel description below; click for the full-sized vector image](/blog/assets/mission-unlikely-01-sm.png "The setup...")](https://codeberg.org/jcolag/comics/raw/branch/main/mission-unlikely/mission-unlikely-01.svg)

Transcript/description:

**Column #1, Panel #1**:  A wooded area.

**Column #1, Panel #2**:  A campsite deeper in the area.

**Column #1, Panel #3**:  A grassy area near the campsite, **La Rana** reclined and relaxing.

**Column #1, Panel #4**:  La Rana sits up, stomach growling.  Having established the setting, most panels have no backgrounds from here.

**Column #1, Panel #5**:  The rumbling becomes full-body shaking.

**Column #1, Panel #6**:  Smaller and abstract, La Rana lies on their back looking up.  An arrow points up, between the columns, to gesture at them looking at the next panel.

**Column #2, Panel #1**:  *MISSION:  Unlikely* by John Colagioia.

**Column #2, Panel #2**:  La Rana has an idea, but only a fuzzy compact fluorescent bulb that doesn't show up.

**Column #2, Panel #3**:  A frustrated La Rana strains, kicking furiously.

**Column #2, Panel #4**:  A happy La Rana watches a human hand reach out and flip a light switch up.

**Column #2, Panel #5**:  The CFL bulb lights up, and La Rana can now imagine food on a picnic blanket.

**Column #2, Panel #6**:  A grinning La Rana marches off to the right.

I somewhat regret drawing the arrow to indicate the flow, instead of finding some more clever way of representing it.  Looking at it with the second page, I almost wonder if it would've worked better inverting the second column, so that the activity works its way back *up* the page to the title, with La Rana walking "into" the spoof credits.

That would take a bit more work to sell it, but for example, it would make the CFL bulb and the light-switch motion *part* of the flow.  You'd see the hunger move into thinking, encouraging looking up at the bulb, then something would need to adjust the tantrum to point to the excited frog looking up to the light-switch, indicating another movement up to getting the actual idea, to walking off the page.

### Page Two

[![First page of the comic; panel-by-panel description below; click for the full-sized vector image](/blog/assets/mission-unlikely-02-sm.png "Planning...")](https://codeberg.org/jcolag/comics/raw/branch/main/mission-unlikely/mission-unlikely-02.svg)

The top of the page has a horizontal line running from near the left (in a spark) to the right-hand margin, referencing the 1960s **MISSION:  Impossible** opening credits.

**Panel #1**:  La Rana, wearing square glasses, sits at a desk piled high with papers, plus a coffee mug with his own image on it, doing the Dan Briggs/Jim Phelps thing, (implicitly) looking at dossiers of public domain and Free-licensed funny animal characters.

We then see a series of eight candidates for the mission, pictures with unreadable text (their names) along the bottom, suggesting flipping through.  The candidates include Disney's *Mickey Mouse*, George Herriman's *Krazy Kat*, the United States Office of Surface Mining Reclamation and Enforcement's *Coalie* (crossed out), Looney Tunes' *Bosko*, Winsor McKay's *Gertie the Dinosaur* (crossed out), Fleischer Studios' *Bimbo*, **Mini Fantasy Theater**'s *Avian Intelligence* (crossed out), and Ub Iwerks's *Flip the Frog* (crossed out).

**Panel #2**:  The resulting team---Bimbo, Krazy Kat, La Rana, Bosko, and Mickey Mouse---march off to the right into the action.

Conveying the dossiers probably didn't quite work, here.  It might've worked better with an establishing shot of all of them on a table, and probably hand-drawing the crossing-out.  Maybe "bigger stacks" of the dossiers---more rectangles at odd angles, maybe even adding the corners of images and text peeking out from beneath---could have helped.  Speaking of hand-drawing the rejections, I briefly had a joke that **Mini Fantasy Theater**'s Avian Intelligence got crossed out multiple times, intended as a reference to the rejection of AI images in the Bonanza's rules.  Coalie also would've gotten the multiple cross-outs, because the 2020s definitely did not need a Trump-endorsed coal-mining mascot, except as a punchline.

Oh, and the original idea for the burning fuse would have used it as part of a musical staff with the first six notes of the **MISSION:  Impossible** theme.  Or maybe it would've eventually gone with five notes, so that the rhythm would fit the Morse code for M-U instead of M-I-M.

Sorry, let me take a step back, for those who don't know this nonsense.  The original TV show theme uses that M-I motif (▄▄▄ ▄▄▄ for M, ▄ ▄ for I) in the bass line repeated as its rhythm, a clever bit in a show about maybe-spies.  You could do two rounds of that for a total of eight notes, but at that point, music publishers start to get antsy and at least try to enforce copyright[^2sfF6u], but seven notes doesn't make sense, and four doesn't quite provide enough of the tune.  Six also seems silly, because you get M-I-M.  However, M-U coincidentally *resembles* M-I (▄▄▄ ▄▄▄ / ▄ ▄ ▄▄▄), feeling like we've started a second cycle, giving us five notes that provide enough context for people to get the joke, assuming that they know the theme.

[^2sfF6u]:  For a modern version of this idea done more artfully, **Wicked**---both the film and the stage versions of the musical---has the recurring word and musical motif *unlimited* sprinkled throughout the protagonist's scenes.  It changes the rhythm to disguise it, but that motif takes the first seven notes from *Somewhere over the Rainbow* from 1937's **The Wizard of Oz**.

Anyway, I chose *not* to do that, because it seemed like it'd take too long to present and belabor the point, as evidenced by needing three paragraphs to explain the joke.

### Page Three

[![First page of the comic; panel-by-panel description below; click for the full-sized vector image](/blog/assets/mission-unlikely-03-sm.png "The heist?")](https://codeberg.org/jcolag/comics/raw/branch/main/mission-unlikely/mission-unlikely-03.svg)

**Panel #1**:  La Rana uses his tongue to indicate points on scribbles across what looks like a blackboard.  Krazy Kat watches while Bosko scratches his head in confusion.

**Panel #2**:  (Montage) La Rana attempts to carefully step over a rock.

**Panel #3**:  (Montage) Krazy Kat boosts Bimbo up, though he only waves coyly.

**Panel #4**:  (Montage) Bosko extends a hand to a squirrel, but holds a brick behind his back.

**Panel #5**:  (Montage) Mickey Mouse steers a ship.

**Panel #6**:  Zooming back out to the campsite, somebody out of sight says, "Hey Hon, let's move the tent.  The critters here are creeping me out..."

**Panel #7**:  La Rana dances in a *Libre Liltings* logo, made up of irregular polygons with an increasing number of sides.

And there you have it.

The story admittedly falls apart on this page, since it doesn't really come across as any of this resembling the plan, the plan itself spinning out of control, or me forgetting the plot and filling the page.  If I had more time to put into it (before submitting), it probably would have worked better to actually show some interaction with the never-seen picnic or food storage, and include more smaller incidents, making that section look more like a montage.  For example, it *should* have looked like Krazy Kat helping Bimbo climb something, but it didn't work with the images that I had.

## Credits and Additional Comments

I should say that, despite my own criticisms of my work, I do think that this came out fairly well.  Even if the storytelling doesn't quite hold together, I think that it has the wildly frantic energy that I wanted.  In my book, that qualifies as a success for a first attempt at a comic, excepting the possibility that I scrawled some ideas on notebook paper as a child.

In particular, the branded coffee mug on the desk makes me laugh every time that I look at it, because we have gone from a stomach growling so loudly that it knocks them over to an office with a tiny phone and branded merchandise[^b6Rkql].  I also love the "wait for the light-switch" gag, including using CFLs, a technology that stayed in the public eye for probably less than five years.

[^b6Rkql]:  Thanks for this idea go at *least* partially to Emiliano himself, who couldn't make sense of La Rana sitting in open space looking to the right.  Adding the messy desk hints that the messy piles of pictures come from the desk...I hope.

I vectorized all the raster art as black-and-white line art, an idea that I picked up from Simon (Gee) Giraudot's commentary on his [*Superflu*]({% post_url 2022-12-03-superflu %}) comic, simplified the photographs---partly to minimize my file sizes, but also to remove as much detail as possible while still retaining the impression that the original image gives---all in Inkscape, and exported the final pages to 1400x2200 pixels.

Someone might also want to see the small number of photographs that I considered as backgrounds and discarded.

- [Forest](https://www.flickr.com/photos/38436453@N07/3924238584), an alternative to the establishing shot.

Ignoring the handful of stars and polygons created using Inkscape, I only drew the scribbles on the blackboard and the rock, both on the third page.

And there you go, my first attempt at comic creation, and all Free Culture, too.  If you'd like to adapt the comic for your own purposes, you can grab the [vector images](codeberg.org/jcolag/comics), which has room to grow with more comics if I get other ideas.
