### Whither Mailchimp

Did I write up the epilogue to walking away from Mailchimp, by the way?  Shortly after I sent out what I had of the December newsletter on New Year's Eve, intending to never log in there again, they sent out an e-mail announcing their new---you guessed it!---AI chatbot for their analytics.  If I tried it out and took their survey, they'd send off a decent-sized gift card.

A gift card can always get put to good use.  And other than a chat interface taking more time and thought than checking numbers, this task actually seems like a good fit for LLMs.  It should keep the bot on rails with restricted topics, and surely Mailchimp would have rigged this test in their favor, to encourage people to pay up.  And so I logged in.

The chat supplied three suggested questions.  Again, good deal, because this smells a lot like a canned demonstration, where they might have pre-processed the data specifically for these questions.

And yet, somehow...no, they didn't do that.

The bot dutifully reviewed my analytics from 2025, and reported that I have had terrible engagement---true, and fine, because I don't use the newsletter for sales---except for a massive spike over the summer among my large number of Singaporean readers.  The analytics don't show any non-noise change in the (yes, non-zero) number of people opening the e-mail or clicking links.  And I don't know how you identify where in the world people live from their e-mail address, but I don't think that I have a massive following in Singapore[^ZAe7wx].  Asking it to present the data, the bot dutifully reported that it couldn't connect to the server, so maybe Mailchimp (the site on the screen) had gone down.  It could actually do other (mostly fictional) things with the analytics data, such as strongly urging me to send separate Singapore-exclusive offers, but it couldn't *show* me the data because it allegedly couldn't access any of it.

[^ZAe7wx]:  I actually do know people there, but as far as I know, they don't look at the blog or newsletter.

Anyway, I did put the gift card to good use, after telling them that they can really only fix their AI assistant by pulling the plug.  Everybody, please stop trying to anthropomorphize graphs.  Nobody wants to talk to a pie chart, even if sometimes they remind you of Pac-Man.

## Mini-Server, Part 20

As an update to [last week]({% post_url 2026-02-16-tet-eve %}), I discovered that [Audio Bookshelf](https://www.audiobookshelf.org/) has some minimal support for non-audio books.  It doesn't have a reader---though it seems to *think* that it does, with a prominent **Read** button that goes to a page telling me that every book has zero pages---but it happily indexes and displays a bunch of common book and comic formats that makes it possible to download the file quickly.

Likewise, I apparently spoke too soon, last week.  If you create a podcast-specific library in Audio Bookshelf, then it can import an OPML file.  Or, at least, it *claims* that it can.  It has a user interface to do it.  It took a lot of permissions-wrangling for it to even *try* to add the podcasts, let alone download the files.

Likewise, it also seems fussier about the RSS formatting than I'd like, so some podcasts won't work.  However, all that said, this should mostly suffice if I ever want to jump off [gPodder](https://gpodder.github.io/) or need to rely on a backup system.  And it should work as a place to archive the handful of Free Culture projects distributed as podcasts for myself.

### Permissions

To follow up on the permission-wrangling comment, since I originally used the media drives from Windows, I left the factory's NTFS file-system in place.  Linux, then, doesn't have a way to manage permissions except when mounting the drive, meaning that every file has the same ownership and read/write/execute state.  You can run `chmod` and `chown` all you want, and it'll "succeed," but not do anything.

I could reformat the drive to make this go away, but then I'd either lose terabytes of media or need someplace to store it.  The former wouldn't improve my access to the media.  The latter requires having unused terabytes of storage available, which...no, except for the physical media that I ripped it from, but that looks like the former option.

Anyway, to keep this story from dragging on without an end, I solved the problem by re-mounting the drive for a new group, `media`, into which I put my, Jellyfin's, and Audio Bookshelf's users.  Even that didn't solve the problem, though---I guess that it tries to confirm ownership of its files---until I edited Audio Bookshelf's systemd startup script to use the `media` user group.  Restarting the application *finally* let it set up podcasts.

## Recipes

I normally wouldn't call this sort of thing a "recipe," but the first try went so well that it seemed worth recommending.

{% cook 1|Sardines and Kimchi %}
Heat @oil{1%Tbsp} in a skillet on medium-low.

Toast @pearled barley{1/4%cup} in the oil.  When it starts to smell nutty, add 3/4 cups of boiling water and simmer for ~{20%min}.  Dice @onion{1/2} while waiting.

Add the onion, @sardines{1%tin}, and @kimchi{1/2%cup} to the skillet.  Stir, and leave cooking for ~{5%min} to warm everything through.

Serve with @tahini{1%tsp}.
{% endcook %}

I don't know why I have pearled barley, but it seemed like the quickest-cooking grain that I had handy; technically I used 3 Tbsp of the barley and 1 Tbsp of quinoa.  The recipe would probably work fine with rice or any other grain.  And by the way, when I make a porridge for breakfast, I follow the same process of toasting and adding boiling water.

And no, I didn't actually measure the kimchi[^1nba7n].  I aimed for somewhere between the volume of fish and volume of barley.

[^1nba7n]:  I should probably make my own kimchi, given the disparity between how much I enjoy it and how often I eat it.  However, for the duration, I'll have the kilogram-and-a-half tub from Costco that I don't remember seeing available before.  I'd also like to make my own miso, since I like it, but don't react well to soy, and now I've gotten completely off the topic...

The pan needs a good wash after that, by the way.  But you probably already figured that out, or look forward to making kimchi-flavored oatmeal or something the next morning.  I won't judge...

## Notoboto

{% codeberg jcolag/Notoboto %}

The database code for creating the semantic search table now should seem more idiomatic, and without the pile of commented-out old code that didn't quite work.  Python's `requirements.txt` file also now made it to the repository.

As an update on this broader project, by the way, I should probably mention that the semantic searches---at least with the models that I have available and without deliberately messing around with the search results---haven't really impressed me.  For a quick example, if you read the newsletter, you might recall that I kicked around a few ideas for an 8-bit computer to run [Collapse OS](https://collapseos.org/) last year.

The project hasn't gone anywhere, yet, but I had some fun trying to work out the cheapest processing unit[^ocLlgN], and I kept decent notes on that.  Since I don't do much thinking about computer hardware, it seemed like a fairly good test.  For the search string, I went with *inexpensive processor*---an idea that shouldn't come up much more than this one note---and grabbed the semantic vector for the phrase.

[^ocLlgN]:  Not really important, but I wanted to avoid the Collapse OS ethos of pulling the necessary chips out of an existing computer, because that doesn't make for a repeatable project.  I also didn't want an SoC (system on a chip) or single-board computer, even though those can get fairly cheap, because at that point I could run the emulator and not bother with any project at all.  That provides some boundaries, but then some units with more integration come out significantly cheaper than buying a standalone CPU and memory.  That, then, becomes an interesting constraint that you want something repairable, so maybe it can sacrifice *some* repairability, if a microcontroller with on-board RAM and I/O costs less than the equivalent microprocessor alone, let alone with separate memory and I/O chips.

Searching the database for the closest vector gave one paragraph from each of the following five[^XpfmoE] notes as results.

[^XpfmoE]:  The SQLite vector extension requires setting a maximum result limit on queries.

- A long note about a lot of random things, but the only technology-relevant piece involved mentioning looking for a non-smart television.
- The preliminary notes for what became [uManage](https://codeberg.org/jcolag/uManage).
- Notes for an online managerial-type class that I took.
- An idea for a minimalist computer science course that might work via postcards.
- Finally, at the bottom of the list with the worst match, the note that literally says "cheap CPU."

It did find the right note, but like when Google started trying to guess what you secretly really meant---probably because they used a similar technology to this---it had an unnecessary amount of trouble finding a paragraph that used the closest synonyms that I could think of for the phrase.  It found four notes that no human could possibly see as relevant first.  If I had set the maximum number of results to two, as the example in the documentation does, I would've concluded that the search failed entirely.

In other words, it does the job, and demands less effort than searching for all synonyms that I can think of and trying to run some combination of `grep` commands to get there.  And I would certainly take this over, say, feeding all my notes into an actual LLM so that I can conversationally ask it to find what I need, since that gets highly error-prone as well as seeming like the most depressing possible use of my time.  But the semantic searching also has more moving parts to break---the LLM infrastructure, the vector database, and the work creating and maintaining the index---and seems prone to missing what seem like can't-miss requests.
