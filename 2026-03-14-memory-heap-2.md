This week, our [Free Culture Book Club]({% post_url 2020-05-02-freeculture %}) finishes reading **Memory Heap**.

To give this series some sense of organization, check out some basic facts without much in the way of context.

 * Full Title:  **Memory Heap**
 * Location:  <https://memory-heap.org/~avp/poetry.html>
 * Released:  2006 -- 2025
 * License:  CC BY-SA
 * Creator:  Artyom V. "avp" Poptsov (with special guest Emily Dickinson)
 * Medium:  Poetry
 * Length:  Approximately six thousand (Russian) words across fifty-three poems
 * Content Advisories:  Occasional religious and nihilistic themes

This should go without saying---even though I plan to repeat it with every Book Club installment---but *Content Advisories* do not suggest any sort of judgment on my part, only topics that come up in the work that I noticed and might benefit from a particular mood or head space for certain audiences.  I provide it to help you make a decision, rather than a decision in and of itself.

## Memory Heap

The author describes the project as follows.

> Стихи распространяются на условиях лицензии Creative Commons Attribution-Share-Alike, конкретная версия данной лицензии указана в нижней части страниц произведений. Если коротко, то лицензия позволяет любое использование работ (в том числе, коммерческое) при условии, что вы указываете автора использованного произведения и распространяете производную работу на тех же условиях (подробнее можно прочитать здесь). Буду рад, если моё творчество послужит кому-либо.

Admittedly, I can only read a handful of words in Russian myself, so in English, that reads as follows.

> Poems are subject to the terms of the Creative Commons Attribution-Share-Alike license, the specific version of this license is listed at the bottom of the pages of the works. In short, the license allows any use of the works (including commercial) provided that you identify the author of the work used and distribute the derivative work under the same conditions (for more details, see here). I would love to see my work serve anyone.

I don't have more than that, other than to reiterate my lack of Russian practice[^CEj0dB] and that I'll read these in translation.

[^CEj0dB]:  If you must know, at the risk of dating myself, after the Soviet Union officially dissolved, my high school tried to work up enthusiasm about the Russian Federation, with half an exchange program (Russian students stayed here, and then they skipped the part where anybody from here would travel), plus an attempt at a short course about Russia that mostly taught history but included weekly lessons in the Russian language.  Decades later, I can still sound words out written using the Cyrillic alphabet and get a decent sense of the meaning of short texts based on the similarity to Greek and Latin roots---we all speak Indo-European languages, after all---and can remember *maybe* a dozen words, about half numbers.

Oh, I should also add that yes, I do know that the Russian government has done some fairly miserable things, probably illegal things, recently.  So has mine.  So has yours.  That doesn't make the work of the people stuck under those governments any less interesting.

## What Works Well?

Some of these poems start to feel more personal, no longer lists of observations, but actual emotional engagement.  *Время лечит*/*Time Heals* probably provides the clearest example, here.

The poems also feel increasingly confident, with a higher proportion of pieces that rely more on metaphor than narration.
