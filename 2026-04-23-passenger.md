## The Passenger

For context, this episode aired after [*Tapestry*]({% post_url 2025-02-27-tapestry %}) and immediately before the first half of [*Birthright*]({% post_url 2025-03-06-birthright-1 %}).  Over the fence, some might find it also worth mentioning that---opposite *Birthright*---the competition launched with **Babylon 5:  The Gathering**.  The series wouldn't air for another eleven months, and I don't know if I'll put in the effort of tracking it along with **The Next Generation** and eventual **Voyager** episodes that overlap with this series.  But given the awkward ties between the shows[^Blvl7Q], it seemed worth mentioning.

[^Blvl7Q]:  For the sake of having said it and (I hope) never talking about it again:  Because I heard rumblings about the premises for **Deep Space Nine**, **Voyager**, and **Enterprise** early in **The Next Generation**'s run, and given how many characters here definitely-to-probably evolved from characters who we already knew from **TNG**, I don't believe that *Star Trek* outright copied anything from **Babylon 5**, especially given that **B5** overtly drew from so many other famous sources itself, and so you'd draw similarities to the originals rather than the fellow borrower.  However, I absolutely can believe that both shows came from an earlier kernel of an idea that didn't have much more to it than a space station next to a portal.  By contrast, **Enterprise** looks like a direct attempt to copy **B5**, down to the "harder" technology, the too-big-to-conceive wars that allow Earth's fledgling space fleet to forge an alliance that becomes a galactic-scale government, the scene-stealing sleazy alien rival, the reactionary movement on Earth, *and* the romance between a human and a member of a culture that recently tried to pen humans in, and we somehow don't talk about that, because it doesn't take place on a station with a portal...

And honestly, especially since its thirty-third anniversary hit only two months (and a day) ago, in this month's [newsletter](https://buymeacoffee.com/jcolag)---**Follow**, in the upper-right, nothing involving money unless you want more from me---I'll run a quick piece on my evolving views on **Babylon 5**, absent of any *Star Trek* references, similar to [this month's newsletter](https://buymeacoffee.com/jcolag/entropy-arbitrage-newsletter-march-2026) with my comments on the **Firefly** revival and (later) an old, abandoned project partly inspired by that show.

Actually, if I set everything up right, then members---people giving me a couple of dollars per month---can get the [preview of my **B5** piece](https://buymeacoffee.com/jcolag/maybe-faith-does-manage) as of a few minutes ago as this goes out, a *tiny* incentive for the people who can't wait until the fifth.

 > **BASHIR**: Ah yes, well, tricorders. Very accurate with live people, not so accurate with dead ones. We learn that first year medical school.

This line feels worrying.  In our world, we constantly find cases where we can bring "definitely dead" people back to life.  During cholera epidemics, it became such a widespread concern that companies produced [safety coffins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Safety_coffin).  That doesn't bother me.  Sometimes living people seem dead.  {% emoji person shrugging %}

In our world, though, when it happens, we change how we define "dead," because it makes far more sense to find another test that distinguishes dead from (apologies to William Goldman) *mostly dead* than to believe that the doctor reversed the process of death.  For example, we once declared a person dead if they had no pulse or breath, but CPR makes it clear that the definition doesn't work.  A permanent end of respiratory and circulatory activity sounds better, but then what do we call somebody on life support or packing transplanted organs?  Most people now use the end of electrical activity in the brain as the dividing line, but if doctors start saving people at that point, then we'll find the next barrier, because death *means* that you can't bring them back.

If Federation medicine says that dead people have the ability to trick tests, and that doctors can reverse death, then the words stop having any meaning...or you start treating doctors like a mystical priesthood.

 > **KIRA**: I feel privileged to be in your presence.

I've probably said it before (already in the series), but I love how nobody puts up with Bashir.  Over on **The Next Generation**, they'd have a whole arc about how somebody like Bashir needs his colleagues to *indulge* him, convincing him to stop putting up a front, because deep down, they agree that they've *never* seen anybody nearly as impressive.  Here, only he has the responsibility to fix his attitude, and I find that refreshing.

It does feel uncomfortable that they do this for the colleague with clear Arabic heritage and not O'Brien, but I'll take what I can get after seven seasons and way too many films with the *Enterprise*-D/E crew...

 > **KAJADA**: What happened?

I guess that I got all the **Babylon 5** talk out of the way too soon, because you might recognize Kajada as [Caitlin Brown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julie_Caitlin_Brown), who (among other things) would soon go on to play a variety of roles on the competition.

 > **KAJADA**: Are you sure it's the same body?
 >
 > **BASHIR**: Quite certain. It's been sealed in here ever since our arrival.

Especially given that the episode started with the story of him "reviving" a dead woman, how does this not arouse *any* curiosity?

 > **PRIMMIN**: I would appreciate it if we could go over it anyhow. It took me three weeks to get here, and I'd hate to think it was for nothing.

Look, I still don't understand the size of the Federation versus the speed of the ships, but why would the length of his trip make his request any more urgent?  And given that he got there pretty much exactly in time, then surely he did whatever he did on purpose, and could've called ahead.  But he makes it sound like he walked the entire way, and somehow that entitles him to more information.

 > **DAX**: The Federation's been working to supply them with deuridium, but even the new deposits from the Gamma Quadrant aren't enough to service the whole population. It's so scarce that some of the Kobliad have gone underground to get it.

If the Kobliad have space travel and weapons technology and the like---enough that nobody whines about the Prime Directive meaning that they shouldn't save lives---why do they need to go to the Federation for the magic material that keeps them alive?  In our time, this would feel *awfully* colonial, like the Federation controlling a needed resource so that they can extort a weaker government.

Also, it takes place a few years into the episode's future, but [**Insurrection**]({% post_url 2026-01-01-insurrection-1 %}) tells us that asking the Federation to harvest life-extending material probably won't end well.

 > **PRIMMIN**: Ah, that's okay, Major. I'll fill him in later on anything he needs to know.
 >
 > ...
 >
 > **PRIMMIN**: Frankly, isn't the security of a deuridium shipment a little over his head? I mean, he was chatting about it in the bar with that Ferengi, Quark. If I could overhear it, so could half-dozen others.

Echoing the disinterest in putting up with Bashir's self-importance, you'll notice that, before anything else, we learn that the new guy from Starfleet has strong opinions about non-humans.

 > **PRIMMIN**: It's not they way they taught us at the Academy, is it, sir. If you want my opinion...
 >
 > **SISKO**: Actually, I don't. You and I are guests of the Bajorans, Lieutenant. You don't have to forget what you learned at the Academy, you just don't throw it in anyone's face here. If you're really smart, you might even learn a few new things about station security from our Constable. Clear?

That almost deserves a round of applause.  {% emoji raised fist %}

 > **ODO**: Yes, I've noticed that. "Constable." Very cute.

Wait.  I suppose that it has nothing to do with the Federation, since Sisko points out that Kira originally called him that, and Odo doesn't work for Starfleet, but why don't they call him by his actual title?  Does he even have a title?  That seems like a far deeper issue with respect than an endearing nickname.  It seems like the rest of the Bajoran officers have ranks, and he wears (or extrudes) a militia uniform.

 > **QUARK**: Help you? But...You can't be Vantika.

Right?  Especially since we can all hear who does the voice and saw the transfer in the cold open...

Do we think that an early draft of the script tried to cast blame on Primmin, maybe?  I don't see how the secrecy serves the story otherwise.

 > **BASHIR**: The closest thing I've encountered is synaptic pattern displacement. But that's never been done by a non-Vulcan.

Did Bashir watch [**The Search for Spock**]({% post_url 2022-03-31-tsfs %}), or did yet another intimately private Vulcan ceremony, embarrassing to ever mention to outsiders, become a task performed on demand for everybody's convenience...?

 > **BASHIR**: There's plenty of room. A humanoid uses only a small portion of the brain.

No.  We do not.  We use the *entire* brain.  Running a brain takes about a fifth of the body's energy, and so the body wouldn't keep it running if it didn't need it.  The myth probably derives from misinterpreting comments about people rarely reaching their full *intellectual* potential[^JVovBH], as researchers studied child prodigies.

[^JVovBH]:  We all came here to talk about a thirty-plus-year-old science fiction show, so I certainly can't fault the logic.

In reality, we use most of the brain at all times, rotating through.  Using more would almost certainly result in seizures.

 > **PRIMMIN**: We're already stretched to the limit preparing for the shipment. Why don't I just set up the computer to track her movements and alert us if she goes near a secured area?

I can think of one reason not to do that:  It never works on **The Next Generation**, no matter how often they try it...

 > **DURG**: That's what you said about Vener Seven.

You might recognize Durk as [Christopher Collins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher_Collins), one of the Klingons in [*A Matter of Honor*]({% post_url 2023-01-12-matter-honor %}) and the Pakled captain in [*Samaritan Snare*]({% post_url 2023-03-16-samaritan-snare %}), plus (among other roles) **G.I. Joe**'s Cobra Commander and Starscream from **Transformers** in the original cartoons, and would die about a year after this.

 > **COMPUTER**: Doctor Bashir is in the Infirmary.

Why do they even *have* those badges?  Seriously, Primmin wanted to know why not track people, and here we go.  Tracking Kajada wouldn't have gone any better than Bashir.

 > **BASHIR**: Exactly. So if you care at all about the welfare of your Doctor, you will release your hold on us. You have one minute to decide.

I'd love it if Sisko went full-on [*The Ransom of Red Chief*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Ransom_of_Red_Chief), here, and pointed out that nobody actually *likes* Bashir.

## Conclusions

The episode focuses most of its attention on its plot, but it didn't go too badly.

### The Good

Sisko continues to stand up for the Bajoran crew and local authority.

### The Bad

The Federation classifies at least some people with the potential for revival as dead.

We get two examples of completely insufferable Federation officials, and direct evidence that outsiders don't appreciate it at all.  This apparently leads to ill-will even in more innocent situations like the use of nicknames.  They also seem far less curious than the situation warrants, directly endangering people.

Somehow, the Federation still believes that humans only use small fractions of our brains.

They still think that the computer does a sterling job of tracking everybody's movements, even though it requires that the subject carry around bulky jewelry.

### The Weird

This could have a far darker purpose, but the Federation has apparently gotten itself into the pharmaceutical delivery business for other cultures.
