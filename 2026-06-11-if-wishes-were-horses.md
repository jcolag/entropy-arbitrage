## If Wishes Were Horses

For context, this episode sits between [*Suspicions*]({% post_url 2025-04-17-suspicions %}) and [*Rightful Heir*]({% post_url 2025-04-24-rightful-heir %}).

 > **QUARK**: You see that thing on his hand? It's a baseball mitten. Every afternoon, the boy goes up to play. His father brought a program with famous players. You ever hear of [Tris Speaker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tris_Speaker) or [Ted Williams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ted_Williams) or Buck Bokai?

Links to the two real players in the quote, since I don't know nearly enough about baseball to say anything useful about them other than "I've heard of them, too."  Bokai, they've teased for a few episodes, now.

 > **BASHIR**: You are constantly in my thoughts. I can't even---
 >
 > ...
 >
 > **BASHIR**: I can't even concentrate.

Read the room, man!

By the way, notice the implication, that when a man "can't concentrate" on their work, a woman takes responsibility.  In our world, you find this all over dress codes, where girls (at school) and women (at work) need to wear low enough hems and high enough neck-lines, because otherwise boys/men will wander around in a daze *because of a thigh*.

We could totally teach boys to focus on their own nonsense, and that a body doesn't actually spark much interest unless it's strongly interested in you, but nah, policing girls fits better with the plans for women under patriarchy anyway.

 > **DAX**: Julian, you are a wonderful friend. I enjoy the time we spend together.
 >
 > **BASHIR**: Stop. You're driving a stake through my heart.

Yes, because we only ever accept friendship as a consolation prize... {% emoji eyeroll %}

 > **DAX**: It could be. Ships have always avoided the Denorios Belt. We have no idea how the presence of so many warp and impulse engines might affect it. The question is, is this going to cause us any problems?
 >
 > **KIRA**: We were hoping you could tell us.

Do all the Starfleet peons do this "somebody should totally look into that thing that I specialize in" nonsense?

 > **O'BRIEN**: And the messenger from the Queen watched the little man as he danced and sang and laughed. How he would soon have the daughter he always wanted because they would never guess that his name was...

I almost damned-with-faint-praise O'Brien actually doing some parenting instead of referring to the burden on Keiko, but no, apparently he needs Keiko there anyway, a back-up team ready in case the bedtime story goes horribly wrong.

 > **O'BRIEN**: Now then, shall I tuck you in with a bedtime story too?
 >
 > **KEIKO**: Only if you're tucked in with me.

And this feels like the first time when we see these two together when she doesn't seem totally repulsed by him, so...progress?

 > **RUMPELSTILTSKIN**: Don't look so distraught. I'm only here to offer you my services if you should need them.

You might recognize The Stilt as [Michael J. Anderson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_J._Anderson), known so much more as The Man from Another Place in **Twin Peaks** than dozens of other projects.  After a too-low offer to reprise that role in **Twin Peaks:  The Return**, Anderson and David Lynch got into a strange feud probably best left ignored.

Oh, and since I'll probably never have an opportunity to bring it up anywhere else, some readers might recall reports about a decade ago that researchers Jamie Tehrani and Sara Graca Da Silva used data techniques from biology and evolution, along with references across many texts, to [estimate the histories of fairy tales](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4736946/), suggesting that the Rumpelstiltskin story probably has predecessors going back over four thousand years, possibly shy of the split between the Western/European branch of Indo-European languages (that almost all have versions) and the Indo-Iranian branch (which don't).

 > **RUMPELSTILTSKIN**: A pity you're sending the ladies away. It's as if all the life just went out of the room.

...A valid criticism.  I'd rather follow Keiko and the kid than Miles, too.

 > **BOKAI**: Hello, Ben.

And you might recognize our third ball player as [Keone Young](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keone_Young), who has shown up...pretty much everywhere, including daytime dramas, a before-its-time multiracial medical drama a few years prior to this episode, and voice roles.

 > **BASHIR**: Why am I fighting this? Why am I fighting this? I have no good answer to that question.

How about the fact that, moments ago, you pretty firmly believed that she had impaired judgment?  Surely, the Federation values affirmative, informed consent more than...no?  OK, then.

 > **QUARK**: Let me guess. Another clever Federation experiment has gone awry, and you've turned the whole station into one vast holosuite.

Good to see that the Federation's wonderful reputation hasn't changed...

 > **DAX**: Julian, there's really no need to apologize. In a way, I feel as if we've invaded your privacy. We all have fantasies and dreams we keep to ourselves, thoughts that should remain private.
 >
 > ...
 >
 > **DAX**: She really is submissive, isn't she? Is that how you want me to be, Julian? So submissive?

She gets a whole monologue showing that, as a woman, she has a right to perform emotional labor to protect men's egos, then worries about submissiveness...

Also, does "submissive" even work, here?  The imagined Dax seems fairly assertive in what she wants, and even seems largely disinterested in what Bashir wants.  If that qualifies as submissive, then actual-Dax comes off as meek in comparison, happily eating lunch with someone who ignores her boundaries.

 > **O'BRIEN**: Look, figment, I'm not afraid of anybody, least of all you. Ninety seconds to coordinates.

Have you ever heard somebody lash out about their lack of fear who actually didn't fear the thing under discussion...?

 > **BOKAI**: Well, that was baseball's epitaph, wasn't it. Nobody seemed to have time for us anymore. I could've played five more years if they hadn't killed the game.

We get some minor sports history, here.  If, in roughly 2370, he died more than two hundred years prior, then they most likely had that final [World Series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Series) in 2142, since he mentions in some other line that it took place "in forty-two."  By then, other entertainment had displaced it, with (another not-quotable line) only three hundred fans in the stands.

Weirdly, despite baseball's reputation of having fans who obsess over data collection, I can't find World Series attendance record for each game per year, only the top attendance records (and, inexplicably, the total number of attendees per season across the entire league) showing that about half the 1959 series (games 3--5) brought in more than ninety-two thousand spectators in Los Angeles, which doesn't really give us much scale.

Looking game-by-game, because why compile information into a table when you can scatter it across around seven hundred pages---figuring a first World Series in 1903 and a likely average of five-and-a-half games per series, since a team needs four to win---1992's World Series would've had around fifty-two thousand spectators per game.  For 2025, the majority of the games had about forty-four to forty-five thousand spectators, with the couple of games in the middle hitting fifty-two thousand.

In other words, without all the data---and I have no interest in spending the rest of my life navigating [Baseball Reference {% cc %}](https://www.baseball-reference.com/) to assemble it, especially since they deliberately provide no API and block suspected scrapers---this doesn't tell us anything more than audiences had declined by the 1990s.

 > **BASHIR**: And Vulcans don't have the most active imaginations.

Well, that sounds racist, especially given that they seem to invent so many things...

 > **JAKE**: I couldn't understand the directions. The computer accidentally erased the instruction page.
 >
 > **SISKO**: You really expect me to believe that?

That software from some large organization not known for reasonable user interfaces destroyed the most important information?  I certainly buy it...

 > **BOKAI**: We're on an extended mission exploring the galaxy. Awhile back, we followed one of your ships through that hole in space out there. We've been watching you, trying to figure out the rules of your game.

We all see the similarity to the Prophets in the wormhole, right?  It seems odd that Sisko doesn't spot that, given that [*Emissary*]({% post_url 2026-02-26-emissary-1 %}) had him dragged around memories and imagination for an afternoon, including using baseball as a metaphor to bridge the culture gap.  I feel like people would like this episode better, in fact, if it didn't feel like a "digest-sized" version of the pilot.

## Conclusions

We get some data points outlining the decline of baseball on Earth.

### The Bad

We see some awful gender politics, here, with Bashir refusing to stop harassing a colleague while blaming the colleague for it, and treating friendship like some miserable failure.  He also feels comfortable taking advantage of a woman with possibly compromised decision-making capabilities after determining that impairment himself.  For her part, Dax scrambles to soothe Bashir's upset for having hurt her.

Dax joins the proud Starfleet tradition of officers not bothering to do their jobs.  And probably related, the Federation has a reputation for scientific experiments that endanger others when they inevitably go wrong.

People, Bashir in this case, also see no issues spreading racial stereotypes about Vulcans.

### The Weird

O'Brien apparently can't handle telling a toddler a story without his wife actively supervising him.
