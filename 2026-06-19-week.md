![A Juneteenth flag, featuring a round, red horizon/Sunrise against a dark blue background, overlaid with a traditional white five-pointed star in the center surrounded by the outline of a twelve-pointed star](/blog/assets/Juneteenth_Flag_Variation.png "Any excuse to recycle this flag...")

In the United States, our other patriotic holidays come off as rather desperate.  [Flag Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flag_Day_%28United_States%29) started as an event to rally support for the United States during our Civil War, but expanded into celebrating the trappings of nationalism, now pirated by the Trump administration to celebrate Il Generalaccio.  And [Independence Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independence_Day_%28United_States%29) hangs its **Mission Accomplished** banner and pretends that we have done nothing wrong since 1776.

By contrast, Juneteenth celebrates incremental progress, that we can and do overcome our problems and conflicts.  Certain people oppose the holiday, insisting that it celebrates the worst moments of the country's history, and I'll let you read into it what you will...

<i class="fas fa-square"></i> [Banning noise will be a disaster for statistical data products](https://desfontain.es/blog/banning-noise.html) from Ted is writing things

> But all competing techniques _also_ rely on noise addition. The [Cell Key method](https://sdctools.github.io/HandbookSDC/05-frequency-tables.html#sec-CKM_freq), used at other statistical agencies, adds noise to statistics. Swapping, used from 1990 to 2010 for the U.S. Census, also injects randomness into the process. Sampling is everywhere in statistical work<sup id="fnref:sampling"><a class="footnote-ref" href="https://desfontain.es/blog/banning-noise.html#fn:sampling">2</a></sup>. Hell, even [imputation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imputation_(statistics)) technically adds noise to the data!

Since the start of the blog, I have wanted an excuse to talk about these information-obscuring techniques that retain the utility of the data, but haven't had the opportunity.  For example, I like to frame the Census's old "n-respondent, k% dominance" rule, which says that you can't make requests so sensitive that *n* individuals or fewer contribute to more than *k%* of a key statistic.  To exaggerate an example for clarity, this would mean that you couldn't ask for "the average savings of [Little River, CA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Little_River,_California) plus pick-your-favorite-billionaire," because only one respondent---out of less than a hundred people, total---would account for so much of the money among that group that the poorest hamlet in the country looks like a rounding error.

I always want to present the techniques, because I see these techniques as potentially interesting in other fields, such as redistricting.  For example, if I can compose a demographic question about a Congressional district where the answer directly reveals information about the Black neighborhood in that district, then that feels like a bad gerrymander.  If you can ask about the KPIs of some project in a way that identifies a specific worker, then the team has a management problem.

Anyway, if the Census Bureau can't add noise, Ted tries to *avoid* making the point that they aim to either use the specific data to disenfranchise Black voters or block the release of data so that lawsuits under the Voting Rights Act have a harder time proving discrimination on the numbers.
