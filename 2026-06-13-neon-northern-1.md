## What Works Well?

It provides plenty of parts to play with.  If you think in terms of a certain famous role-playing game in use for almost forty years, then you'll probably find anything that you might expect except for the D&D-inspired bits.  Associated with it, though, it provides many tables of random generation, sometimes more tables than text.  I don't know if the street-map generation would actually work in practice, but it does give a fairly satisfying feel to think through.

## What Works...Less Well?

{% include lesswell.md %}

I always feel petty pointing this out, but the book needs editing.  It can't always decide on pluralization or possessives in lists, for example, including in headings.  The list of "ancestries" for characters not only does that, but while it starts out with expected character templates---human, android, ex-super-soldier, and so forth---we suddenly veer off into Street Kid, Wanderer, Vagabond, different types of employee, and the like, which...don't feel like they belong on the same list at all.

The section on slums seems counter to the rest of the book that I've seen, painting it as particularly dangerous because of the people, complete with fairly offensive terms for unhoused folks, which seems bizarre in a genre that revolves around fighting back against the unchecked power of elites.

## Six Years Hence:   Motherload (Stardrifter)

Continuing our look back at early projects discussed in the book club, we come to the weirdly spelled [**Motherload**]({% post_url 2020-06-13-motherload %}), an attempt at an at least partly "hard" science fiction world distributed as a combination of audiobook and podcast, which still seems unique.  Think "**The Expanse** (but interstellar) as interpreted through Neal Stephenson."  I don't actually mean that as a compliment (because I didn't care for the series or the author), but I strongly suspect that if that description excited you, then you'll dig the world.

Anyway, in the years since the original post, the original "Cavalcade Audio" website has vanished---so for a while, I had no idea whether the story continued beyond **Street Candles**, which I admittedly haven't made the time to listen to---but to write this, I found [Collins-Rivera's personal website](https://davidcollinsrivera.com/) mostly dedicated to *Stardrifter* material.  There, I can see that **Risk Analysis** and the first book of **All He Surveys** followed, plus about twenty short stories, a print-only novella written by someone else, and a brief podcast run.

From the years when I listened to the [Hacker Public Radio](https://hackerpublicradio.org/) podcast[^Pbe84x], I have also known that Collins-Rivera spent a lot of time working on the role-playing game, with quite a few episodes play-testing the game.

[^Pbe84x]:  Nothing against them, but other than the Free Culture license and community contributors talking about whatever they feel like making an episode about, it didn't really interest me *except* in the rare cases when someone talked about some Free Culture thing.

The project newsletter seems to have gone mostly dormant, and sitting on Substack probably won't win it many fans.  I subscribed, but don't think that I received any of them past mid-2023; the URL now sits in my RSS feed, in case it revives.

In any case, the broader *Stardrifter* project appears to soldier on, with much more material than we had available six years ago.
